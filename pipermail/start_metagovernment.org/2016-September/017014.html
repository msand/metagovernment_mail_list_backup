Hey, I only know what I read. And what I read, in the Kling paper but
also from many other sources, is that LQFB just doesn't do what it
claims to, which is to improve democracy by reducing the burden on
ordinary voters via a delegation system: Because so few people
actually delegate, and even those that do aren't matched
psychologically (or even ideologically) to their proxies, most
people's opinions are simply not reflected in the final decisions.

> Yes, I am not a promoter of direct democracy. I see how the AfD

Too bad about your cynicism. But you should take heart in the
statistics published by Altemeyer and more precisely by Stenner:
Although those people make a lot of noise and *seem* like a formidable
force, they're actually a relatively small proportion of the
population (around 25%, depending on threat level). The trick, in my
considered opinion, is to have the opinions of the 50+ percent of the
population who *don't* actively participate in politics properly
recorded in the voting. Neither LQFB nor misrepresentative democracy
do that, with the completely predictable result that it's the SDAPs
who end up making public policy.

> Yes, there is room for improvement in LQFB, but the astounding

It's not that I would claim that LQFB is any worse than our current
misrepresentative democracies, but to my mind it has most of the same
flaws (most seriously that policy ends up being decided by only the
most active participants) and so would not be a significant
improvement if it were implemented widely.
