> Then where am I wrong?

That you are confusing the potential of a democratic mechanism
with its naive deployments. Just because people have been using
it wrong in the first attempts doesn't mean it isn't overdue to
start using it right.

I documented most of the social failures regarding electronic
democracy in a document at http://my.pages.de/convivenza. The
result has been that the Italian Pirates have rewritten their
statutes in order to have the necessary social framework for
*more* successful use of liquid feedback. I say more, because
even under the strain of missing social regulations LQFB has
achieved several major goals:

	1. It impeded anyone from ever taking control of
	   the political party, albeit there have been
	   several attempts.
	2. It impeded anyone from corrupting party finances
	   because no individual was ever allowed to spend
	   money by themselves.
	3. It has produced an excellent party programme
	   even though the participants were in-fighting.

With the new statutes of 2015 the Italian Pirates feel more
equal than ever before. There are individuals with ideas, but
it is their ideas leading the way, not the individuals. More
than ever before the party feels like equally "owned" by each
participating member.

So, all I can say is, it works. We are experiencing it day
to day. The problem is that figuring out how to do it right
lost us a lot of participants, so now that we finally resolved
the problem of the in-fighting we have to reboot and regain
the political momentum we had in 2012.

In all of our experience, liquid democracy was one of the few
things that never failed us. It was the social failing us, and
that has to be resolved by legal frameworks, not technology.
