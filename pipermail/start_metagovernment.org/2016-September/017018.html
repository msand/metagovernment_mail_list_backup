Right, but we've already established that you're an advocate of

with you. Seems to me your only problem is figuring out how to get the
*right* 20% to show up. My problem is that I can't live in that
fantasyland because I already *know* what the 20% who show up are

Sure, I'll call it anecdotal, but it's much weaker than that: The
participants in the PP LQFB system were a highly homogeneous group and
probably contained very few authoritarians (given the PP's extremely
progressive platform), and probably a lower-than-proportional number
of high SDO and psychopathic individuals (given the PP's relative
obscurity and low prospects for becoming a major force in German
politics). I have zero doubt that your experience would have been
completely different if LQFB was used in the general population. None
of the other LQFB experiments have fared even as well even though none
of *them* even attempted a general-population run either.

> Oh wow, one quarter of the population stinks so let's try the other
> half. No, I don't think you are going to obtain much better results
> by taking the other half's prejudices and making those into law.

It's an empirical question, to be sure. But I do have the science on
my side: It's not just Altemeyer who has found that the other 50% are
far better at decisionmaking, there is a whole body of literature in
evolutionary psychology and the anthropology of LPA bands (e.g.,
Christopher Boehm's "Hierarchy in the Forest" and "Moral Origins")
that clearly show that the way we make decisions now (by delegating to
SDAPs) is not the system we evolved to function in. By creating an
environment (using technology) that is closer to the one we're
genetically programmed to function in, my claim is that our
performance will markedly improve.

> That has been proven to be wrong. It does not have those flaws.
> It helps remove fallacious choices. It impedes the promotion of
> corrupt, lobbyist or populist proposals. It impedes corruption
> as the proxies wouldn't get far with it.

Sure it helps with those things, but as it turns out those are not
even the major problems with misrepresentative democracy. The major
problem is that the people most interested in participating in public
policy decisions are SDAP and don't have the same priorities as the
neurotypicals that make up the majority of the population. LQFB does
*nothing* to help with those problems, and may in fact make them worse
because the SDAPs that formulate policy can operate without the public
and press scrutiny that is directed at our current misrepresentatives.
This is absolutely clear from the published data, which showed that
the median voter only voted 8 times in 3 years and where there was on
average less than 20% turnout for each final decision.

> There are a lot of aspects of LD that are amazing and surprising.
> This one for example works out because of the long debating times.
> "Voter" is the wrong term. It sounds like there is no debate.

Agreed. Then again, this is exactly its most fatal flaw: By requiring
more time than most neurotypicals are willing to spend, LQFB virtually
guarantees the final decision will fit the needs of the SDAPs instead
of the majority, who are not SDAP.

> Look, the discussion we are having is clearly among left-wing

I'm not an extrovert, though, which just shows how little you
understand about psychology (or me). Actually a true extrovert would
probably not even engage in a debate like this, which is really more

As it turns out, extroversion is not even a relevant personality

Same mistakes there: extroversion is not correlated with on-line

>> OK, so your point is that delegation *isn't* to make the system more
>> democratic (i.e., more closely reflecting the Will of The People), but
>> merely to make it more stable? Again, if this is the case I'd consider
>> it to be an example of false advertising.
>
> Politically stable = more democratic.

LOL! So the most democratic countries include Cuba and China, which
have far more "stable" political climates than even representative
democracies? But seriously, "stability" has nothing to do with
democracy, only with efficiency. By definition he most "democratic"
institutions are those where the broadest range of individuals have
significant influence in the decisionmaking. This certainly doesn't
apply to any LQFB run, where 20% of the participants make all the
decisions, and the vast majority have no impact at all (they only
voted on average 8 times in 3 years, and less than 15% of them even
ever delegated their votes).

> There isn't much homogenous about tenthousand pirates other than they
> probably all dislike surveillance.

That actually tells us a lot: For example, we know that they're mostly
not authoritarian for one (authoritarians *love* surveillance).

> I'm sorry if the actual way digital democracy does work is not

No, it means you're so in love with that technology that you're blind
to its obvious flaws.

Again, we can agree to disagree if you'll just admit that you don't
find it troubling that the majority of the decisions made in the PP
run of LQFB were based on the input of only 20% of the party members,
that only 15% of them ever delegated, and that the median number of
votes for each party member was only 8 in 3 years. I find those things
*deeply* troubling, but can't argue the fact that you're entitled to
your opinion.
