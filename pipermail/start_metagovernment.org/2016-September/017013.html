In LD you delegate by default unless you think you know better and
thus vote yourself. Should you have reason to doubt the honesty,
political orientation or competence of your delegatee, you
are invited to change it. As many times as you like. The result is
a leadership which is so incredibly liquid, that it can in no way
on earth be called an oligarchy.

An example of how much this works happened to Martin Haase. He was
the most delegated person in the early period of that LQFB. So
much, the Spiegel even wrote a piece on it:

http://www.spiegel.de/international/germany/liquid-democracy-web-platform-makes-professor-most-powerful-pirate-a-818683.html

But the Spiegel was wrong in calling him the most powerful pirate.
One or two weeks after the article, the professor incidentally had
a very controversial opinion on an issue. Word got around and his
delegation support collapsed. You can only be at the top if you are
indeed promoting the most intelligent answers to the political
questions of the collective. Any false move removes you from that
position in a flash. That is so totally different from representative
democracy where all the *real* power comes from the ability to do
the things your supporters would crucify you for, but can't.

You must understand that your established criteria for good and bad
are challenged by this totally different model, and your attempt to
understand it without several years of practice is like trying to
give lessons in disco rollerskating without attempts to either dance
or skate. Excuse me if your attempts to categorize LQFB by your
expertise falls flat on its feet.

Yes, I am not a promoter of direct democracy. I see how the AfD
right-wing populist movement in Germany is growing because it uses
direct democracy to re-enforce and legitimize the existing prejudices
of its members and electorate, and I think it is Trumpism in the
digital age. Trumpism legitimized by the ideal of participation.

LQFB does work because it does not work the way you want it to,
because what you want is flawed. There is no discrepancy. One
actual weakness of the early versions of LQFB was that it invited
people to do vote shopping. By that I mean to skip the entire
debating process, glance over the top one or two proposals, then
quickly vote on them. That I think was a weakness that has been
reduced by improved UX that tries to tie interested people into
the debate earlier in the process, so that they actually do get
educated. It was however never a major problem *because* the
superdelegates were able to dig deeper into the subjects and
make a counter-weight to superficial decisions.

Yes, there is room for improvement in LQFB, but the astounding
thing to learn is that according to the data we have now it is
the least bad system of governance/consensus and we should work
from that, not keep making mistakes of excess direct democracy
or corrupt representativity.

> 7) I previously said that redelegation is a flaw, but now believe that
> in the cast of LQFB it's irrelevant: The fact that there is such a low

In liquid democratic theory the transitivity of proxying is essential
to actually achieve the effect of collective intelligence that we
really experienced. The Bavarian section has later tried a different
method of selecting a choice of possible proxies rather than allowing
transitivity, and that I think has lead to concentration of power in
the most popular figures, but we don't have scientific evidence on that.

In my understanding of the Koblenz paper we have reason to assume that
the collective intelligence quality of the results we achieved had
something to do with the transitivity of votes that would sometimes
indeed bring voting power to people that deserve it even if they seem
to be of the "wrong opinion" to the individual. Simply because the
individual can't know what is the best choice.

I understand your allergy against that kind of thinking, but there is
a fundamental difference to representative democracy: In the broken
governance models we experience in western democracies we are told
that the experts and oligarchs know better, but thanks to the power
of corruption that isn't true. Whereas in the liquid democratic
model the temporary leadership arises out of actual reason and is
not corruptible, therefore it is not only okay, it is necessary to
model the failure of the individual to assert what's best for him.

This is my personal guess. Can't say if other LD proponents would
agree and I'm not in a position to write papers on this.
