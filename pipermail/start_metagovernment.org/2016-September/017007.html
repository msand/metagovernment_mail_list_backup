Humans produce social problems in *any* kind of organization they
create and it will fail whenever there are no suitable rules. Nobel
prize winner Elinor Ostrom says you can't run a working commons if
the rules the assembly makes are not enforced and respected. The
Pirates thought anarchy was cool and nobody would have to be
disciplined, and that is plain wrong. From a theoretical point of
view a political party is the most democratic form of organization.
Neither a company, nor a corporation or nor an NGO is half as
democratic as a political party with democratic statutes - but even
if that is the least worse form of organization, it still can fail
big time if participants do not understand human nature.

So whatever you have in mind to replace the current political system,
I highly doubt you will be able to deal with the social problems, so
it would turn out not to be better than what we have.

A frequent problem with criticism about LD is that apparent problems
are constructed which do not actually exist. The fact that a delegator
could have voted differently than the person he delegated to simply
reflects the situation by which the person delegated to probably
understood the matter better than the person that delegated - exactly
because they didn't have time to dig deeper and achieve expertise
or similar reasons.

LD works also because it defeats superficiality and populism. You
can't beat populism if delegation is only about being lazy to log in.
It is about understanding that your own competence can be insufficient
to achieve the common goal.

And I'm sorry if I might not find the most convincing words to relay
this notion, but how I put it in words isn't actually important. The
numbers and the experience show that the model works.
