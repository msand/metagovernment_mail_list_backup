Hi Michal,

regarding your evaluation function, you are now summing the individual valuation. I suppose you intend to also divide by the number of people voting. If you do not do it, your evaluation would raise for every person voting and it would more measure how many people do you receive feedback from, than how good was your evaluation. So you are basically taking the average.



I would suggest that instead you take the median of all the votes. 



Why? Because of the median voter theorem (Aka Black theorem). 

which states that if a group of people are voting on a 1 dimensional ordered set of possibilities such that each person has a maximum, and how much they like the result decreases as we go away from that maximum, then the vote of the median voter will be the Condorcet winner among all the maximum.



In other words if you have 100 people, and each person makes an evaluation from 1 to 10. And then you compare all the evaluations two by two (how many would rather vote 3 over voting 6? What about 4 vs 6? ...) the number expressed by the median voter will win all pairwise comparisons (which means, is a condorcet winner).



This is not true if the votes are not ordered, or not unidimensional. For example if you (or someone) would rather vote 3 than 4, but also 5 than 4, thus you have not one maximum preference but two maximum preferences then it is not true.



Now when you are casting a judgment as a rating this is usually considered true. So much that a whole form of election is based upon this (aka Majority Judgment).



So I would suggest you use the median.



Consider also that using the median is strong against strategic voting. Meaning, if you already know how everybody votes (or you guessed it correctly), and your vote is x, and the result of the vote is y (with y != x) then if you are taking the average you would want to vote for something more extreme to pull the result from y to x stronger. But if the system uses the median, this is impossible.
