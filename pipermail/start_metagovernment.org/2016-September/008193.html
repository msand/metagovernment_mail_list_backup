See my previous post. Exactly how the system is described to the early
adopters is a marketing issue: It may be that enough people would fear
being called revolutionaries and so refuse to participate that to
achieve Critical Mass you'd have to soft-peddle, e.g., by calling the
votes "non-binding" or "advisory" or something. My sense is that most
people won't object to this portrayal as long as they feel it is safe
to do so by appealing to the Critical Mass requirement (i.e., by the
time it matters, the old form of government will already have been
replaced so it couldn't prosecute anyone for treason). Hence my use of
the "beta test" terminology: Everyone should know by now that you're
not supposed to pay for or make your company (or your life) dependent
on "beta test" software. Seems to me this might even add to the caché:
Most people, especially most young people, like to be a little bit
edgy or controversial, especially if there is very little real danger.
"beta testing" a revolution might really appeal to these people, and
of course the rest will follow along (bandwagon effect, peer pressure,
etc.) even when they don't actively participate (as is the case on all
other social media, where large minorities or in some cases even large
majorities, are exclusively consumers rather than producers).
