What the above criticism sounds like to me is "Scott has never
attached boards to his arms and jumped off a cliff: He doesn't know
anything about flying at all!". In my defense I would only say I
prefer science and real (social) engineering to pulling some ideas out
of my ass and spending months or years trying to build a following for
a product that has no real scientific or engineering justification for
existing. All these claims about needing consensus or deliberation or
whatever sound to me just like "If we attach these flapping wings
directly to the pedals, we'll greatly increase the power we have
available". But just as flying is not about flapping, true democracy
(or even "good government") is not about any of the things anyone else
associated with Metagovernment is proposing. My considered opinion is
that just as flying is about "lift" and the wing shapes that generate
it (and not anything to do with flapping), democracy in general and
matchism in particular is a *scientific* theory about how different
personalities operate in current systems and an engineering theory
about how to design systems that fix the problems that are the direct
result of unequal participation rates.

> So, well come Carlo. You know I do not agree with your enthusiasm for liquid

Here, here. Now lets get down to brass tacks: Where is the science
that shows that the problem with democracy is caused by mathematically
impure decision criteria? Or that it's caused by the lack of
availability of tools that the "Activerts" can use to refine their
proposals? Even better: I struggle with the (potential) issue of
coercion in any system that requires open voting records. How do
Vilfredo and LQFB deal with this issue when the participants are not
only expected to reveal their positions, but to actively engage to get
them accounted for in the final decision?

As for the whining about the mailing list, I sense an intense irony,
perhaps rising to the level of hypocrisy, in the positions of you two,
and indeed that of all of the deliberation/consensus promoters. My
claim has long been that these systems cannot work for defining public
policy because of the "last man standing" problem, and that it doesn't
matter whether the debate is held in person, via email, or in some
fancy deliberation software system: Anyone willing to continue
espousing their positions long enough eventually gets to define the
policy, or at least water down the proposal sufficiently that it
becomes totally ineffective in achieving the original goal. Don't you
see that by even threatening to leave or withdraw participation that
you are actually proving my point?

Not that I have any problem with Disputation Arenas like this list for
scientific or religious inquiry. The last man standing problem isn't
fatal there because the goal is to discover the "truth", not come to
any sort of general agreement. But everyone has their own "truth",
largely based on their genes, when it comes to public policy. This is
why deliberation is not a viable means of decisionmaking in that
domain.
