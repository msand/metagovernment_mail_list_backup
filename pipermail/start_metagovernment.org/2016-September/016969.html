> For a deliberative system, I agree. Unfortunately my considered
> opinion is that deliberative systems and "direct democracy" systems
> are almost entirely non-overlapping fields: A direct democracy system
> that must support more than a few dozen individuals cannot even have a
> significant deliberative component, nor does it need one.
>

For the time being, you are correct.
The best way, I know of today, is to raise a national issue, and talk with
our friends. This the Deliberative Day
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deliberation_Day> concept brought by Fishkin.

But this is what we try to change in delib.org. We try to build a
deliberative system that will allow large groups deliberate together online
and Face-To-Face. To accomplish this, we use several teams of development.
One team is responsible for the methodology, the other to run ground
experiments (like the one you have seen in the video), and the last team
builds technology. We work together to find ways to broaden the number of
effective participants.

Agree: This is why we go for philanthropic sources that wants to promote

> "breakthrough" software that wasn't developed by at most a handful of

In this point I do have some other perspective. It is right that we have
one major developer how creates 90% of the code, but the other developers
are valubales They give insight to the major developer. by discussing the
different problems, to help him solve difficult problems. They also prevent
stagnation. As a team leader, I can tell that the need to attract more
coders, make me become more responsive to suggestions. So delib team as a
whole, develop and bring about new ideas in to the drawing board every
week. from there 50% are implmented.
