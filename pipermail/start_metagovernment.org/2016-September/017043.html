Hi Michal,

there are a few points.

First of all if you speak about an index you are speaking about a single
value that somehow is summarising what is going on. Of course this is a
very limited form of information, and of course you will lose a lot of
interesting and sensible information.

You can double that up by taking a measure of the spread. For example if
you take the average and the standard deviation. But if you really want to
know about those subgroups, the tool to use is not the average (which just
will give you a wrongly aggregated measure, subject to the whims of single
people) but you should try to cluster the points.

Now, I have to admit it has now passed 17 years from when I studied those
tools. And it was brief. But I would point you to https://en.wikipedia.org/
wiki/Cluster_analysis as a starting point. But of course if you do this you
are not looking at a good decision *index*, but something else. But it
might be still very good, or maybe even better.

If instead you want to have an index, then you are aggregating all those
data. And it is not a matter of getting people to agree on a result, but it
is a matter to have a number that can correctly represent everybody. Now
whatever is the system you use, you probably want to have a system that
does not depend on the whims of a single person. That's what the solidity
against strategic voting is about.

Look into clustering :-)

> Hi Michal,
> regarding your evaluation function, you are now summing the individual
> valuation. I suppose you intend to also divide by the number of people
> voting. If you do not do it, your evaluation would raise for every person
> voting and it would more measure how many people do you receive feedback
> from, than how good was your evaluation. So you are basically taking the
> average.
>
> I would suggest that instead you take the median of all the votes.
>
> Why? Because of the median voter theorem (Aka Black theorem).
> which states that if a group of people are voting on a 1 dimensional
> ordered set of possibilities such that each person has a maximum, and how
> much they like the result decreases as we go away from that maximum, then
> the vote of the median voter will be the Condorcet winner among all the
> maximum.
>
> In other words if you have 100 people, and each person makes an evaluation
> from 1 to 10. And then you compare all the evaluations two by two (how many
> would rather vote 3 over voting 6? What about 4 vs 6? ...) the number
> expressed by the median voter will win all pairwise comparisons (which
> means, is a condorcet winner).
>
> This is not true if the votes are not ordered, or not unidimensional. For
> example if you (or someone) would rather vote 3 than 4, but also 5 than 4,
> thus you have not one maximum preference but two maximum preferences then
> it is not true.
>
> Now when you are casting a judgment as a rating this is usually considered
> true. So much that a whole form of election is based upon this (aka
> Majority Judgment).
>
> So I would suggest you use the median.
>
> Consider also that using the median is strong against strategic voting.
> Meaning, if you already know how everybody votes (or you guessed it
> correctly), and your vote is x, and the result of the vote is y (with y !=
> x) then if you are taking the average you would want to vote for something
> more extreme to pull the result from y to x stronger. But if the system
> uses the median, this is impossible.
