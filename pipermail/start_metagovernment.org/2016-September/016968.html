> Ciao Matteo.

> On Tue, Jul 26, 2016 at 10:37:53AM +0900, Matteo Martini wrote:
> > I have been involved in direct democracy for almost ten years now
> > and collaborated with a few projects, worked on some of them and saw
> > how they have been developed and how they failed
>
> I was part of the initial installation of Liquid Feedback in Berlin
> 2010 that pulled several political topics out of a magician's hat
> that are still being talked about everywhere in the world, at a time
> when the Piratenpartei was only aware of itself as a digital civil
> rights platform. The Piraten who won the 2011 elections agreed that
> the principle of Liquid Democracy was the key ingredient to their
> success. I documented this in Italian at http://my.pages.de/laricetta.
> The original voting result at https://lqpp.de/be/issue/show/635.html
> is in German, but apparently the server is down, possibly following
> legal action by party dissidents and opponents. The greatest threat
> to electronic democracy is social and political, not technical. The
> reason why the original LQFB of 2010 worked like a charm was social:
> there was hardly any participant with mean intentions. Of course
> this is not a long-term solution, so e-democracy platforms have to
> learn to deal with dominant people - they may not even intentionally
> be evil.
>
> The Berlin Pirates are attempting a new run at the elections this
> year, bare of liquid democracy because of legal threats. No surprise
> the election program looks quite boring compared to 2011. They
> attempted to follow the lead of Italian Pirates in instituting a
> permanent assembly rather than just a survey tool, but they still
> haven't solved the in-fighting issues - so the LQFB assembly has
> been impeded by people that do not want the Pirates to succeed.
> Luckily it worked in other German bundeslands, so by now several
> Pirate Parties exist that not only have LQFB running - they use it
> as an official form of permanent assembly, empowered to take real
> decisions.
>
> > As all direct democracy projects developed so far have failed.
>
> That is an assertion which I can effortlessly reject knowing several
> counter-proofs.
>
> > No one has been used at global level by, say, at least one percent
> > of the global populations
>
> That's because there are other powers at play, discrediting legitimate
> successes. Who knows why. Also, certifying the authenticity of 1% of
> the world population would be a terrific endeavour. Without certification
> it's just a big falsifiable play tool, like holding elections on Facebook.
>
> > LQFB has failed
>
> That is plain incorrect. Too late to save the Pirate movement, only
> in 2015 scientists made a profound analysis of the effects of liquid
> delegation on the democratic intentions. The paper that you should
> absolutely be aware of resides at http://arxiv.org/abs/1503.07723.
> What those non-pirate people found was that the superdelegates were
> kind of a myth, because they never really exceeded a moderate
> percentage of voting power, and that the promise of liquid democracy
> actually proved true: delegations were "stabilizing" the political
> outcome of LQFB even in the face of majorities of participants being
> distracted by other political issues or simply being busy watching
> soccer on TV.
>
> What we know now is that the liquid modeling of a representative
> approach actually works and actually reduces the risks of demagogy
> that come with direct democracy. Still, there are reasonable and
> unreasonable ways of setting up a liquid democratic platform.
> Depending on the legal architecture around it you can either reap
> the best of both direct and representative democracy or earn the
> worst of both worlds.
>
> Italian populists had a strong interest in making the Pirates look
> like they failed on liquid democracy, but the truth is the Pirates
> failed on setting up a solid legal architecture that would punish
> people's wrongdoing, so whoever publicly attacked other people
> would frequently have a strategic advantage in the inner party
> career. Many Pirates had an interest in tearing down the electronic
> democracy platform and they successfully did so, but I wrote up a
> document (in German, unfortunately) which dissects and dismantles
> all the incorrect criticism that was thrown at liquid democracy
> at http://my.pages.de/liquidspin.de. There are things worth improving
> about those technologies, but interestingly the attackers always went
> for fundamentally incorrect criticism.
>
> Let's understand: electronic democracy has many enemies - anyone who
> would prefer to have things done their way rather than having to
> listen to some thousand strangers clicking their vote from their sofas.
> And being against something is usually much easier than being for it.
> Demagogy and populism does the rest. As frequently in politics, if
> we're not going to be scientific about this, we will succumb.
>
> > Here are the five steps of the typical direct democracy project
>
> Please let's be more scientific on this mailing list. If this is
> your theory, please mark it as such. If you are presenting facts,
> please provide links to the empiric research or evidence. The
> irrationality of the debate around electronic democracy is what
> harms it the most, quite ironically. E-democracy, a victim of
> political manipulation.
>
> > 4.
> >
> >    The _decline phase_: the group accuses the public at large of being
> >    lazy, the media of being non collaborative and biased, the
> >    governments of mobbing and the other groups of sabotaging. There is
> >    little or no self-criticism about why the project is not going as
> >    planned. People start to leave the project.
>
> I don't find that very accurate. The things I experienced were
> quite totally different.
>
> > 5.
>
> Neither.
>
> > At the moment, all direct democracy projects do follow this pattern,
> > no one excluded.
>
> Incorrect assertion.
>
> > Why new developers do not listen to old developers who failed?
>
> Why do people form new political organizations without learning
> from the mistakes of previous ones? Why do people still develop
> federated chat systems when federation doesn't solve the privacy
> problem? Why do people think that all anonymity problems can be
> addressed with a bit of Tor, all data structures can be rendered
> into XML and the semantic loss of using JSON instead is suddenly
> no problem at all.
>
> Those are just some examples of how rationality is no longer
> ruling the Internet (I think it was up to 1995, when Java came
> out). Maybe it happens because the Internet is really good at
> giving the illusion you learned all you need to know in a day
> or two of surfing websites? What you actually did was absorb
