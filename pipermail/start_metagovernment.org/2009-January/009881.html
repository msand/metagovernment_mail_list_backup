>>In their article "Beware of the Big-Government Tipping Point" published in
MF> WSJ on January
>>16, 2009, Peter Werhner and Paul Ryan wrote the following "we might be
MF> approaching a
>>tipping point for democratic capitalism... a public accustomed to iTunes,
MF> Facebook, Google,
>>eBay, Amazon and WebMD is not clamoring for centralized, bureaucratic
MF> government....
>>Instead, Americans want efficient government - one that is modern,
MF> responsive and
>>adaptive." Then the authors went on building an argument why a private
MF> health care system
>>may still be better than a public one. While this may or may not be the
MF> case, focus on health
>>care misses the bigger picture - how to improve efficiency of the
MF> government overall. Yet,
>>the article lists all the ingredients of the solution: democracy, modern
MF> communication
>>technologies and individualistic, entrepreneurial population.
>>
>>Yes, the democratic capitalism is on its way to be replaced. Which part of
MF> it is going away?
>>In American context democracy typically means representative democracy -
MF> heads of
>>executive branches of power and legislators are elected, their actions are
MF> rarely controlled by
>>the public, which essentially has only one recourse - deny their election
MF> to a subsequent
>>term, and for most of the nation referendum remains a curiosity rather than
MF> a practical
>>political institution. Analysis of easily-observable technology trends
MF> suggests that the archaic
>>representative democracy is going to be replaced by a direct democracy.
>>
>>Direct democracy means direct participation of citizens in approving
MF> controversial laws,
>>judging performance of government leaders of all levels, deciding on major
MF> ways the public
>>resources are used. The laws would still be drafted and pre-approved the
MF> usual way - by
>>elected legislators and their expert aids. Only those laws that appear
MF> controversial to a certain
>>fraction of the population would be brought to a popular referendum.
MF> Performance of
>>government officials ("bureaucrats") is judged constantly, those failing to
MF> meet needs of the
>>citizens are dismissed immediately, rewards (and punishments) are directly
MF> tied to official's
>>performance. Levels of federal and local taxation, funding split between
MF> major government
>>programs is also decided by a popular vote.
>>
>>Why would this make for an efficient government? One component of
MF> efficiency is that
>>government always acts in the interest of the majority of the citizens.
MF> Bringing controversial
>>laws and fiscal proposals for a public vote before they are fulfilled
MF> ensures that government's
>>intents are consistent with desires of the public. Constant real-time
MF> public control over
>>actions of the executive powers assures that society's goals are achieved
MF> with the minimum
>>expenditure of public resources.
>>
>>What would make direct democracy possible? The key ingredient is the wide
MF> spread use of
>>the mobile communication technology. These days we trust these technologies
MF> with online
>>banking, supplying us with information and connecting with friends and
MF> families. Why
>>would not we use these technologies to express our opinions on relevant
MF> matters of public
>>life and then make sure that our demands are fulfilled? The technological
MF> trend of secure
>>mobile communications penetrating every aspect of our life is clear and
MF> unstoppable.
>>Technical and financial possibility of creating an efficient feedback loop
MF> between
>>government and the citizens exist. What is still missing is a political
MF> will and wisdom.
>>
>>Is this another utopia? Utopia is something that can't exist. Direct
MF> democracy exists now. If
>>fact, it had existed in Switzerland since 1848 contributing to prosperity,
MF> social peace and
>>efficient use of resources in this country. Switzerland has held more
MF> nationwide referendums
>>than all other countries combined. Australia holds a distant second place.
MF> In the US, only in
>>California do referendums play a noticeable role in political life.Despite
MF> a common objection
>>that bringing laws for a public vote will put too much burden on people so
MF> they would loose
>>interest in voting, Swiss experience proves that people consistently
MF> continue to vote on what
>>they perceive to be important issues. Furthermore, legislators have learned
MF> to avoid drafting
>>laws violating public interests out of the fear of loosing face or even a
MF> parliamentary seat if
>>their proposals would be struck down by a referendum. Swiss experience
MF> proves feasibility of
>>a direct democracy with a traditional voting system based on paper ballots.
MF> The modern
>>communication technology will allow for people to vote anytime, anywhere
MF> thus attracting
>>more voters and increasing the degree of their political engagement. This
MF> is also not just a
>>theoretical concept as parliamentary elections in Estonia have allowed for
MF> internet voting
>>using secure ID cards since 2005.
>>
>>American political system was a progressive one at the time it was
MF> designed. Given the much
>>more complex society with so many new changeless to handle, the technology
MF> advancements,
>>and educated and engaged public, this system is becoming archaic. So much
MF> more can be
>>accomplished by freeing up peoples' initiative not only in the economic,
MF> but also in the
>>political arena. Direct democracy will resolve the everlasting dispute on
MF> preference of
>>individual freedoms vs. "tyranny" of a strong government. It will enforce a
MF> truly people's
>>government, which always acts in the interests and under direct control of
MF> the people.
