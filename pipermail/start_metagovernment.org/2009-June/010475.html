As I read your message, we are saying the same thing using different words.
The terms of service you have quoted mean that the data cannot be shared
efficiently and effectively, which means I would be less inclined to use
MixedInk. However, I still don't care about the software programming code
used to support their service.

Regarding the issue of money, here's Wikipedia's article on local currency:
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_currency  I am certainly no expert on
that topic, but it seems to me that: a) physical locality may not be as
important as common interests, and b) local (or regional) currencies may
needlessly complicate "interoperability" (i.e. sharing of the information
embodied in "currency").  I'm not sure that anyone's interests would be
served by attempting to institutionalize eTribalism.
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tribalism

[Ed Pastore]
> Personally I think that democracy and money don't mix. Once there's a
> profit motive, there's an avenue for corruption on some level.

This is an interesting topic, but I would point out that money does
not necessarily imply profit.  Currency, ideally(?) can simply be a
unit of exchange, presumably of nearly equal goods.  What currencies
provide (in contrast on barter) in theory is a buffer of time and
space (taking place within a "market") to more efficiently match up
buyers and sellers over time....

BTW, Will be interesting to consider the ideas of a new kind of global
currency not tied to any particular state.  Consider, for example,
tying the economic system to the real world using something like gold
instead of a "floating" paper currency.  Each region could issue it's
own gold coin designs (from a fixed international and audited standard
of size and denomination) which are traded freely as regular coinage
and bills.  Wouldn't it be cool to have coins designed (and minted
presumably) from other countries (say like Namibia) finding their way
into your pocket, useable like every other, raising global geographic
as well as cultural awareness?
