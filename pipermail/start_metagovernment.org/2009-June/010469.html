Let me add that on VgtA a session does not last a single generation.
In other word, one of the "KEY" (sorry for the caps) elements of
Vilfredo is that you go through several generations, where the same
ideas are cooked over and over, while people try to integrate them one
with the other. To have that we need people committed to go through
the whole process of writing and voting a few times. The only time
where we really had it going was between me and Chris at the
beginning. Part of that was because we were really involved (we were
deciding what to do in the summer). If you can spare 120 seconds I
invite you to read the history of this question:
http://pareto.ironfire.org/viewquestion.php?q=12

This is more than just a brainstorming session.

I find the discussion about the banking crises,
http://pareto.ironfire.org/viewquestion.php?q=16
and about the meaning of life
http://pareto.ironfire.org/viewquestion.php?q=14
to be equally interesting.

The great limit I feel was that the people participating were always
changing, which made finding a stable solution impossible.
(It might become possible if the community becomes so populated that
essentially every point of view is always represented). In any case I
hope I have partially avoided this problem by adding the necessity for
users to write their email, and the fact that the system sends you an
email, if you need to vote or write on a question you have been
involved in the past.

> All of this talk about money and politics has left me wondering what we're
> trying to create, exactly.
>
> Pietro and I recently chatted about this in relation to Pareto. From the
> perspective of the User Experience, I believe that Pietro is working on
> something similar to a brainstorming session, where everyone stands up and
> shouts out ideas, giving all in attendance the opportunity to respond to
> those ideas. In my experience, a second session is required whereby everyone
> looks at the ideas presented in the first session and rates them according
> to their own personal priorities.
>
> This is a wonderful usability model, but I'm not entirely clear what the
> usability model is for some of the other projects on Metagovernment. Are
> each of us aiming at different aspects of the decision making process, such
> as primary elections, governing policies, et al? And if so, should we seek
> for some basic level of interoperability to allow the user to move from the
> brainstorming phases to the election phases, all the way down to the point
> at which these policies are enforced?
>
> Essentially, there are too many paths for a user to take towards an open
> government, and I'm having a hard time envisioning this so that I can
> contribute something of value from my own bag o' skills.
