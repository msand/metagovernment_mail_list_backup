Hi Ed,

I think that dismissing direct democracy as a yes or no process is a little bit out-dated. It is still majority rule (and the Swiss and half of the US states, among a lot of others on the way, have mob rule according to you  ) but there are a lot of improvements on the way. Liquid feedback http://liquidfeedback.org/ allows amendments and all kind of cooperation before the proposal (citizens or gevernment) is put to a vote. 
But anyway, a lot of systems will be developed in the next decades and we will evolve to some kind of government that uses the technological possibilities that are available.  One of the greatest resentments towards any kind of direct democracy i experienced is the lack of interest in politics of most people living in representative systems. Do I have really to put my time in those things? I am not interested I have other things to do.  They have a total different approach to “politics” then for example the Swiss people who are used to decide for themselves (in mob rule) and put time and effort in it. So one of the interesting developments, also incoporated in “liquid feedback”, is the liquid democracy system of free delegation. That way the possibility to delegate stays but delegation can be redrwaed at any time. And that is what we are counting on that in time the citizens will take the responsibility themselves, in most circumstances.

Consensus has not been very popular in history because it requires a sophisticated system to enable it or it requires a relatively homogenous society to use it. The best example from history I know of is the Quakers, who do use a very good consensus model: 
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consensus_decision-making#Quaker_model

But the Quakers are a fairly homogenous society, so they can rely on norms to help guide the process. And since they share many core beliefs, reaching consensus is easier.


For a more diverse community, what is needed -- and what has never been available before -- is some sophisticated mechanism to help drive consensus. That is the point of the projects here. Our systems aren't just for voting, they are for consensus-building. That requires a different approach to the process form the start. Most governance systems channel all decision-making into yes-no questions: "should we adopt this proposal or not." Collaborative governance is about finding the proposal that will work for the community... and then incidentally adopting it.

If direct democracy does not focus on consensus-building and just overlays itself onto a majority-rule system, then it is (by its very definition) just mob rule. I don't see the benefit of that.

  On Jun 11, 2012, at 4:41 AM, Paul Nollen wrote:


    yes, this seems right. But it take a lot of effort, and money if you can find it, to start an initiative and to guide it all the way to a decisive referendum. The whole process takes maybe some years, and that is a good thing, impulsive proposals are often dangerous and sparked by emotional events.  This makes that the greater the chances that someone highjacks your initiative on the way the fewer chances that an initiative will be started at all.


  In the U.S., our national legislature is creating laws continuously. Plus our state and local legislatures pass/change/repeal many, many laws each year. But referenda are limited to a few on a ballot every couple of years at best.

  Why should it be like that? Why do we need to limit the power of the citizenry to only occasionally making laws, when the legislature is doing it all the time?

  I think the requirement of consensus is all the barrier that needs to be placed on law-making. It would certainly cut down on the number and frequency of laws, and it would have only beneficial repercussions. You don't get impulsive laws from a consensus-based system.
