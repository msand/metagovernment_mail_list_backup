Hi all,

consensus is the dictatorship of only one person. 
There are a lot of  direct democracy systems around, operating for more than centuries, where we can learn from. Several states in the US celebrated 100 years of direct democracy and Switzerland has more than 13O years of experience. 
They all have developed systems from experience. Dividing a canton in two and give the people of the  communities the choice to witch canton they want to belong (Bern divided in bern and Jura). They have developed the system of “double majority” (cantons and people) but nowhere there is an evolution to a 2/3 majority, let alone a consensus. Any true “democratic” system, witch means for me “direct legislative power of the people”  has to grow bottom up. Evolutions where the people has the power to develop their own “democratic” system are therefore very important to study. 
http://www.democracy-international.org/book-direct-democracy.html  (free download in 8 languages)
http://www.scribd.com/doc/92466488/IRI-Guidebook-to-Direct-Democracy-in-Switzerland-and-beyond-2010

On Jun 11, 2012, at 4:41 AM, Paul Nollen wrote:


  yes, this seems right. But it take a lot of effort, and money if you can find it, to start an initiative and to guide it all the way to a decisive referendum. The whole process takes maybe some years, and that is a good thing, impulsive proposals are often dangerous and sparked by emotional events.  This makes that the greater the chances that someone highjacks your initiative on the way the fewer chances that an initiative will be started at all.


In the U.S., our national legislature is creating laws continuously. Plus our state and local legislatures pass/change/repeal many, many laws each year. But referenda are limited to a few on a ballot every couple of years at best.

Why should it be like that? Why do we need to limit the power of the citizenry to only occasionally making laws, when the legislature is doing it all the time?

I think the requirement of consensus is all the barrier that needs to be placed on law-making. It would certainly cut down on the number and frequency of laws, and it would have only beneficial repercussions. You don't get impulsive laws from a consensus-based system.
