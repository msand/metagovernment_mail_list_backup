Consensus has not been very popular in history because it requires a sophisticated system to enable it or it requires a relatively homogenous society to use it. The best example from history I know of is the Quakers, who do use a very good consensus model:
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consensus_decision-making#Quaker_model

But the Quakers are a fairly homogenous society, so they can rely on norms to help guide the process. And since they share many core beliefs, reaching consensus is easier.

For a more diverse community, what is needed -- and what has never been available before -- is some sophisticated mechanism to help drive consensus. That is the point of the projects here. Our systems aren't just for voting, they are for consensus-building. That requires a different approach to the process form the start. Most governance systems channel all decision-making into yes-no questions: "should we adopt this proposal or not." Collaborative governance is about finding the proposal that will work for the community... and then incidentally adopting it.

If direct democracy does not focus on consensus-building and just overlays itself onto a majority-rule system, then it is (by its very definition) just mob rule. I don't see the benefit of that.
