I agree that there are fundamental philosophical disagreements between 
us, but those differences are not at all what you have posited. Applying 
old and vague social system terms (such as "collectivist economy") to 
new social system designs that are nothing like the old designs really 
doesn't work -- it totally confuses people, and actually prevents them 
from seeing and understanding the new design. That appears to have 
happened to you in this instance.

The "propose -> vote" design pattern sets up "collectivist democracy": 
The demos makes decisions as a single huge entity.

In contrast, the "propose recursively" design pattern sets up 
"individualist democracy": Individuals and small teams participate in 
decision-making on equal footing as autonomous, independent entities, 
interacting with one another. The demos as a single huge entity plays no 
role in decision making.

You have never seen "individualist democracy" successfully operating at 
population scale. For whatever reasons, I suspect that you will never 
accept that "individualist democracy" can successfully operate at that 
scale until you have seen at least one actual demonstration of it doing 
so. (Have I mentioned that I will be really glad when we get to the 
testing phase, and past the competitive chest-beating phase?)

(You have also never seen "collectivist democracy" successfully 
operating at population scale, but for some reason that does not seem to 
have convinced you that it therefore cannot. You think that little 
tweaks such as switching from representatives to proxies, and presenting 
concise, objective, unbiased little blurbs at Point-Of-Vote can Save The 
Day.) (When we get to the testing phase, we shall see whether that is 
indeed the case.)
