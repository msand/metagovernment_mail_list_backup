> 1) Once people get comfortable with the operation of the system, they'll tend to
>  go back to sleep and not even necessarily pay attention to individual votes.

I think a good approach would be to make sure that in the limit of low participation the system falls back onto a tested design. For example, I am envisioning a system that has the current representative democracy in this limit. That is by default everybody's delegate is their elected representative. My thinking is, that if a person ever decides to change it, he is much more likely to pay attention to the situation in the future. Also, if people fall asleep, it probably means that nothing horrible is happening right now.

> The same could happen as an emergent phenomenon if delegation allows
> recursion (mine doesn't, instead it computes a weighted average of
> multiple delegate's votes to handle the situation where some of them
> may not vote on a given proposal).

Yeah, I consider recursive delegation as an option. But then again, the default setting would be no recursion. Yet, I would like to give a voter that freedom. One can also think about educating voters (at the moment when they change settings), like warning them about potential dangers of recursive delegation, or delegating everything to a single popular person.

> 2) If people are asleep, a small faction could then vote to change
> the operation of the system. This is keeping in mind that 88% of the votes
> in 1934 Germany were to elect Hitler as dictator, a vote that
> unfortunately they couldn't undo because Hitler, being dictator,
> promptly changed the rules.

This I would exclude by design: the rule that a voter retains full control of his vote cannot be changed.
> My prediction is that people are going to be even less trusting that a new server-based
 > system isn't going to jerk the around.
Let's make the system transparent, it is OK with me either way.
> Just give them the raw data (id numbers instead of names, etc.)
I was equating detaching real names from voting records to privacy. Only random checks would require revealing a small fraction of real names behind the IDs.
> But for those who are really paranoid, I still say our best bet
 > is to allow them to rely on personality-matched proxies so that they
 > can't be held directly responsible for their vote.
They would still be responsible for excepting those suggestions, so I would say they are still responsible for their votes. To the same extent that voters are responsible for how their representative is voting in Congress.
I agree with you, that at the end of the day all these ideas must be tested in practice. So why not to start with the simpler ones?

 the
