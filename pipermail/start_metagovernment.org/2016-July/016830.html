On 2016/07/22 22:51, Scott Raney wrote:
> I assume you posted this here in hopes of receiving feedback on how to
> improve it, in which case I'm happy to oblige ;-)
>
> 1) The first half of the paper is merely distraction and should be
> published separately. My prediction is that most people will stop
> reading before they even finish that section. Worse, I don't even see
> any claim (let alone support for a claim) that a direct democracy will
> actually reduce the incidence of most of those sorts of atrocities. I
> suspect it will, but certainly wouldn't be willing to stake the worth
> of my project on this. People already know our governments are
> dysfunctional so IMHO there's no need to even document this. There are
> plenty of problems other than preventing atrocities (particularly by
> the military) that a properly functioning government can solve. I
> would argue that solving small problems that affect billions is a
> vastly more important goal than preventing atrocities that might
> affect tens/hundreds/thousands of people. Most of your items are
> actually better evidence that we need to eliminate militaries or
> institute mass anti-tribalism education campaigns than anything to do
> with government.
>
> 2) You sort of gloss over the issue of open voting. You'll find a
> handful of references on the subject on matchism.org: Studies show
> that *most* people are willing to have their positions on the issues
> and their votes published, but that there is a vocal minority who
> oppose it. As it turns out, that minority also happens to be the least
> informed about the issues (i.e., they may be more concerned with
> protection against looking like fools than with any sort of
> privacy/coercion/vote buying/etc. issues).
>
> 3) Your proposed delegation system I think is under-developed (the
> same goes for every other proposal I've seen, notably including
> "liquid democracy"). This is a key component of the current proposal
> on matchism.org, where they key issue of *finding* appropriate
> delegates is dealt with (i.e., they're found by psychologically
> matching people, much the way that dating services like eHarmony
> work).
>
> 4) Although I'd agree that it's an empirical question (and there is
> far too little empiricism in this field today), I'm skeptical that
> virtual delegates (e.g, encouraging the formation of special-issue
> parties) won't just result in the system devolving back into the one
> we have now (e.g., people who vote the party line, single-issue
> voters, etc.). IMHO the trick to building a robust direct democracy is
> to work hard to decouple every vote rather than providing *any* sort
> of aggregation system. My example is that while it's perfectly
> predictable that people will vote for Hitler (or Trump) when they feel
> their resources are being threatened (it's a well-documented
> psychological phenomenon), they probably *won't* vote to approve a
> purchase order for Zyclon-B to be used on some minority group. The
> trick, therefore, is to design the system to suppress aggregation of
> power to the greatest degree possible. Political parties and your
> virtual delegates work against that goal.
>
> 5) In my proposed system I share your concerns about the vote
> counters/policy implementers gaining too much power and then becoming
> impossible to remove. I propose a simpler solution: Make them actual
> government employees and create a system such that *any* government
> employee can be removed from their position by popular vote in the
> relevant jurisdiction. I suspect that this will become a very popular
> component of my system when I get around to implementing it (there are
> certain judges and police chiefs that I'm sure nearly everyone will
> actually *enjoy* firing!)
>
> Finally I'd recommend publishing this as a web page rather than as a
> PDF. My experience has been that few people will actually read a
> multi-page PDF, but they will jump around on a web site and absorb at
> least *some* of it.
>
> I look forward to seeing the next revision, and feel free to use any
> of the ideas here or on matchism.org. I don't even expect credit: my
> major fear being that people will implement my ideas badly and set the
> field back than that attributions somehow get lost...
