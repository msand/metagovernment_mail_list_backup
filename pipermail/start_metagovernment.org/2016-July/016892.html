Well, I guess it'd be hard to call me an optimist WRT
misrepresentative democracy, but I do see that at least they're
*considering* the issue (unlike gun control in the US congress). What
you really need is a referendum system: That's what got marijuana
legalized here in Colorado. Certainly the legislature would never have
passed such a law on their own. But a referendum is a form of direct
democracy, albeit very limited (hard to get things on the ballot,
can't have more than a couple of them on each ballot (which means only
a couple per year can be considered), etc.)

This too is a social engineering problem: As it turns out, people pay
little if any attention to the posted speed limit. They could just get
rid of all of those signs and it would have negligible effect on
speeds *or* accident rates (they've actually done experiments that
show this), though of course it would have a huge impact on the
funding of police departments ;-) This is sort of unrelated to the
issue of direct democracy, but the solution to this problem is to
require civil engineers (who design the roads) to actually study
psychology as it applies to road design (which for the most part they
don't now). There are ways to give people visual cues to what the safe
speeds are (narrowing lane widths, necking down intersections, etc.)
but these are only rarely used now, and only in the most progressive
communities (e.g., these sorts of things are a big deal here in
Boulder now).

They have a thing here called "complaint-based enforcement" which
presents similar problems: The city council passes all kinds of
arbitrary laws that they don't enforce except if a
neighbor/customer/employee complains. This causes everyone to live in
fear that something they've done will get them busted. It's like a
lightweight version of the Stasi IMHO, and is incompetent social
engineering for sure (lots of stress induced, very little benefit to
the people as a whole).
