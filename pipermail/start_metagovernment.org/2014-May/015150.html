> >> If I may present a potential scenario:
> >
> > Looks good to me!  A few small comments:
> >
> > (snip)
> >
> >> - This creates small groups of advocates that could craft crazy
> proposals
> >> and gain some interest for their cause. Whatever interest they generate
> >> works against them though as their proposal becomes more popular, good
> >> citizens of the world will begin to notice more and start to investigate
> >> their potential weaknesses. This exposes any hidden agenda or truths as
> the
> >> debate carries on.
> >
> > Exactly!
> >
> > - I surmise that a proposal should only go forward to a voting stage
> with at
> >> least a 51% participation rate of all active members on the site. This
> >> should be a difficult thing to do and will require sustained interest
> in the
> >> proposal guaranteeing it remains near the top of the site to ensure it
> gets
> >> seen.
> >
> > This seems a bit high to me, but again this is an empirical question
> > and I think we'll have to wait to see how small these "working groups"
> > end up being.  I'm also inclined try to tie in some sort of rating
> > threshold (twice as many pros as cons, ratings on pro statements
> > averaging a full point higher than cons, etc.) and a greenlight from
> > the proposer(s) who may feel like they need time for additional tweaks
> > to ensure the proposal will pass the general vote rather than just
> > taking it out of their hands when it reaches some threshold.
> >
> >> - Participation does not necessary include making a post, as many users
> can
> >> not be bothered to research a strong counter argument. Participation can
> >> simply be reading the proposal a bit finding an opinion that reflects
> their
> >> view on the issue and up voting that post. This up vote increases the
> weight
> >> of that post in the discussion and makes their opinion a larger part of
> the
> >> decision, exposing it as well to possible weaknesses.
> >
> > Exactly: Merely giving a pro or con statement a rating should be
> > sufficient to count toward moving the proposal to a vote.
> >
> >> - This process creates our pros and cons list that can be drafted and
> >> presented in a simplified way to then move on to the voting stage where
> >> still more of the public will be involved. A majority of 2/3 of the
> votes
> >> take it. Everything is open and transparent the entire time.
> >
> > Again, the majority necessary to approve may need to be tweaked, but I
> > would at least *hope* that 2/3 will be achievable and so it seems
> > reasonable to start with that goal.  This will put the fire to the
> > feet of the proposers to do their best work.  One alternative we might
> > consider is to vary the supermajority required as a function of the
> > percentage of registered voters who cast a vote.  My big concern is
> > that if only 1/3 of the people vote even a 2/3 or 3/4 majority won't
> > be enough to ensure that the Neurotypicals will keep the SDAs (who
> > tend to vote in larger numbers, especially for those relatively few
> > issues they really care about) in check.  If the SDAs make up 30% of
> > the population (probably a high estimate, and not all of them would
> > vote) we'd need 2/3 of registered voters to actually vote (possibly by
> > proxy) with a simple majority rule for the Neurotypicals to play their
> > proper role in the process.  The lower the percentage of registered
> > voters who vote, the larger the supermajority would have to be such
> > that if you get down into the 30% range the vote to approve would have
> > to be unanimous or nearly so.
> >
> >> - Participation is voluntary but there can be penalties for incorrectly
> >> participating in the discussion. You can never be penalized for up
> voting or
> >> voting. This limits input without limiting quality.
> >
> > I'm opposed to "penalties" per se, but being flagged and filtered by
> > people (which an individual would be notified of) should be sufficient
> > incentive for the potentially troublesome individuals to alter their
> > behavior to confirm to societal norms.  Again, this is the way we
> > humans did it for millions of years and we ignore that fact at our
> > peril.
> >
> >> Any reason why this wouldn't work?
> >
> > Probably a thousand that we haven't thought of ;-)  What we need now
> > is a working prototype so that we can start shaping the UI and those
> > thresholds.  I'm working on it but am taking my time selecting a
> > platform (I'm doing the Django tutorial right now) to avoid having to
> > start over if I pick the wrong one to start out with.
