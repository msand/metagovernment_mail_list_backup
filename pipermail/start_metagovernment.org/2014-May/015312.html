On Thu, May 29, 2014 at 2:03 AM, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org>
wrote:

> Sorry I still can’t participate fully, but I’ve kept a partial eye on some
> of the comments flowing since my last post. A few observations.
>
> *1. We have rules; we should stick with them. *I do not like the outcome
> of this proposal, but it happened in accordance with the rules
> <http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Rules> currently in effect. If we ignore
> our own rules, then we have no governance, and that is not what we are
> about.
>
> *2. We should change the rules.* Much of what we do here is an
> experiment. Let’s learn from our mistakes and improve our rules.
> Fortunately, we have rules
> <http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Rules#Modification_of_this_ruleset> for
> how to change the rules! :)
>
> *3. The participants are the quorum.* Decisions are made by those who
> choose to participate (Scott, do you now see the sense in this?). I think
> we’ve learned here that one week is too short of a timeframe, so we should
> lengthen that. But we cannot define a quorum by a percentage of the list
> membership (that is 159 at this moment). When we think of all the decisions
> to be made in all the world, it only makes sense that it is the engaged who
> get to decide… provided there is enough publicity and wait-time for others
> to engage and change the result.
>
> *4. Decisions should stick when they are made*. This is a tough one, and
> I see a lot of people arguing with it. Shortly after the decision was made,
> the nays had enough votes to have broken the consensus. But it was too
> late. That’s the rule (as currently stated), and it is a good one.
> Otherwise, we allow what engineers call flapping
> <http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Route_flapping>. What if each time someone
> jumps into the conversation they vote the opposite of the last person? We
> could end up with rules switching on and off, back and forth, over and
> over. Governance is about consistency. If a rule passes, then repealing it
> should require the same threshold. But please note, the rule as currently
> stated is *not* that there is one week to decide, but rather that
> consensus (as defined by a percentage) must be maintained for a week.
>

To avoid flapping, we currently have a time period of one week to be
maintained by a newly build consensus.
A counter proposal could be brought up at any time and continuously be
lobbyed for until it reaches consensus which then has to be maintained for
a week.
Currently i wouldnt know how to change the rules as i feel they are fine
for now, but feel free to make a proposal :-)



>
> *5. I have no privilege*. The word “privilege” means “private law.” It
> was first used to describe the senators of ancient Rome who were a law unto
> themselves. It is the antithesis of this project. If I had felt really
> strongly about the process, I could have just jumped in and said “no” when
> I saw the emails coming in, even though I wasn’t informed. But I didn’t,
> and the rules carry. Tough for me. (I’ll note that I will evoke privilege
> in one case: if this group suddenly gets overrun by a bunch of “vandals”
> who start trying to hijack our community and pass nonsense laws. We’re not
> strong enough yet to overcome that on our own. We should get that strong,
> however.)
>
> So please go forth with proposals to change the home page. Heck, someone
> can make a proposal to restore it. I might vote for that.
>
> And also, we should formally propose changes to the rules. Please feel
> free to go ahead with that.
>
> I’d like to suggest that *only emails that start with the word “Proposal”
> be considered a valid proposal* in this community (even if your email
> just points people to a proposal in another venue). And further, if you
> think a proposal can be improved, make your own better proposal.
>


+1 for that proposal.
Every day, a lot of emails end up in my inbox, so if for some reason i dont
find the time to always immediately go through all of them, then at least i
can glance over the topics and look at the ones which say: "PROPOSAL: foo
bar baz whatever..."




>
> As for what software we use to decide on a proposal, that’s an organic and
> undefined process. *Let’s try some new things* and see what results we
> get. We can always use the discussion in this community to overrule any
> decision that a particular system makes that seems unreasonable.
