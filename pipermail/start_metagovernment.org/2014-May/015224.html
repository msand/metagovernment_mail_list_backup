Scott,
I'm appreciating the clarity you bring to these discussions. 

I've been on this list for several years and have learned so much reading the various posts, (and yet am still technically "stupid" as Max pointed out earlier) :-)~.  Nevertheless, I have a great passion for this emerging technology and I want to help in anyway I can. 

I'm often frustrated with the discussions that seemed to go round and round….and then fade away.  I don't understand why, with all of the talent on this list, we're not collaborating on a specific project. 

I like what you've put together in “The Plan”.   As a platform for facilitating discussions and decision making, I like what Loomio has to offer, however simple or baseline it may be. 

My question is this: Would it be possible to merge various bits and pieces of code that individuals on this list have written onto a platform like Loomio or Airesis? Or stated differently:  Given all of the talent and passion available on this list, what would be possible if we were to collaborate with either one of those projects?

All the best,
Steve

On May 14, 2014, at 7:29 AM, Scott Raney <scott at metacard.com> wrote:

> Rather than respond individually to a bunch of emails that seem to me
> from people unhappy with some aspect of metagovernment or another, I'd
> like to generally counter that IMHO the system is fine, it's people's
> use and expectations that may need adjusting:
> 
> 1) I've been through this forum/discussion system vs. email list many
> times on many different lists and my conclusion has always been the
> same: I prefer mailing lists, primarily because it shows up in my
> inbox where I don't forget about it like I seem to always do with
> forums.  Perhaps the best combination is what sites like slashdot and
> quora have where they email you a digest every day with the best
> threads of that day.  Not so good if you're going to be an active
> participant in a news-related story (the conversations are stale by
> the time you get to them) but great for a lurker, especially for
> long-term design issues (which is what we're all working on here,
> right?)
> 
> 2) The archives for the email list IMHO are always better than trying
> to find old stuff on forum sites, although you do have to jump through
> some hoops to use the Metagovernment list archives (e.g., using the
> site: tag in Google search).
> 
> 3) If the volume of this list is too high, I'd recommend switching to
> digest mode except for one thing: Most people don't properly trim
> their posts.  Being a mailing-list junkie for 30 years it *still*
> bothers me that people can't take 10 seconds to remove the unneeded
> stuff from a message they're responding to.  The proper technique, as
> practiced religiously back in the day for bandwidth reasons (and now
> necessary for attention span reasons) is to leave one or two sentences
> you're responding to, then put your response after that.  If this is
> too much of a burden, please reconsider posting at all: If you can't
> spend 10 seconds editing someone else's post down, do you really have
> the time to compose a message that's truly worth cluttering up
> everyone's inbox with?
> 
> 4) I don't expect metagovernment to come up with the "One True"
> on-line government system, or even to function as a "recruiting" tool.
> I'm here because this is where a lot of bright people who've thought
> a lot about the issues are.  Seems to me our role here is to ask
> questions and point out potential flaws in other people's systems.  If
> you're not working on a system, that's fine, just being a critic is a
> valuable contribution.  But to be useful as a critic you've got to go
> out and try out the systems (now Loomio and Airesis) to see what you
> like and don't like about them, and be prepared to say why.
> 
> 5) The name "Metagovernment" means a replacement government to me.
> Which means any systems we come up with should be scalable to millions
> or even billions of people.  I've nothing against decision support
> systems for smaller groups, it's just that that's not what I'm here
> for and not what I believe metagovernment.org was set up to discuss.
> This again is why I believe sorting/filtering is *the* key issue in
> any system design, and why I believe that none of the existing systems
> (or even their prototypes) are even on the right path.
> 
> 6) The Affero GPL license is the only thing any of us should accept
> for any project we "review".  Fortunately both Loomio and Airesis use
> that, as will my project.
>  Regards,
>    Scott
