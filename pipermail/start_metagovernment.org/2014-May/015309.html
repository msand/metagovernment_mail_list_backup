>>>>
>>>>> The discussion process of drafting a proposal needs to be regimented
>>>>> to an extend in order to keep the  focus directed on the project and not
>>>>> have it go off track. I think two types of posts should be highlighted for
>>>>> this process to be efficient. Posts that are highly accepted and posts that
>>>>> are highly criticised. These two types of comments usually create a good
>>>>> degree of debate and input from all sides, which is want we want. I think
>>>>> my system should work well for that.
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> To start off I want to introduce a concept for the structure of the
>>>>> forum that helps me understand the overall layout a little better. I think
>>>>> of each proposal starting out as a stem, a sprout that emerges from the
>>>>> ground. This stem then forms a few leaves (responses), the more leaves the
>>>>> more energy it absorbs and the stronger the stem becomes. The leaves might
>>>>> turn into branches themselves and host their own leaves (responses)
>>>>> eventually. Over time it becomes a full fledge tree with many different
>>>>> branches and leaves going off in many different directions shaping the
>>>>> tree's look. Every leaf is still connected in some way back to the stem,
>>>>> through a chain of branches. Every sprout has the potential to become a
>>>>> tree, every tree sits in a in a forest of other trees (proposals). The
>>>>> largest and strongest trees might eventually be chosen to be used for
>>>>> production purposes (voting stage).
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> Whenever a new leaf (response) is created the entire chain of branches
>>>>> all the way down to the stem (original proposal) is temporarily
>>>>> strengthened (raised to the top of their lists, it becomes post number
>>>>> one). However if the newly formed leaf does not acquire sufficient sunlight
>>>>> (up votes) it can quickly wither and the tree will divert more energy to
>>>>> the more successful leafs to grow that area instead.
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> Okay hopefully enough with the tree analogy. Now to get a little
>>>>> technical.
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> Each new response to a post or a new proposal starts with a score of
>>>>> zero (0). Zero is actually the best score a post can get. Upon each posts
>>>>> creation a timer starts. Every time the timer goes off, say 60 seconds, it
>>>>> loses a point. So a post that's 3 minutes old (180 seconds) will have a
>>>>> score of negative three (-3). This score ranks the order in which responses
>>>>> to a post or proposals are displayed. Closest to zero and down the list you
>>>>> go.
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> The other aspect of this formula is that every time a post receives an
>>>>> up vote the timer is increased. So instead of a post losing a point every
>>>>> 60 seconds, a post with one up vote could instead lose a point ever 70
>>>>> seconds, a post with two up votes could lose a point every 80 seconds. So
>>>>> you can see the more agreed upon posts will drop at a slower rate ensuring
>>>>> that they eventually overtake a post that generates fewer up votes.
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> Another layer is that a fresh response to a post bumps up the entire
>>>>> thread (chain of branches back to the original stem) up to the top of their
>>>>> individual lists, original proposal included. Each post associated with the
>>>>> fresh post starts back at zero and starts losing points again at the rate
>>>>> of the fresh post. So each associated response piggy backs on the fresh
>>>>> posts score and mimics it's attributes, 60 seconds timer or what have you.
>>>>> This happens until the fresh posts score becomes lower than the actual
>>>>> score of its associated posts. These posts then revert back to their score
>>>>> and timer. The timer doesn't actually stop on the associated posts it just
>>>>> pretends like it has a higher score until it doesn't anymore. The chain of
>>>>> posts cherry picks whichever response attached to it has the highest score
>>>>> and uses that number until their own score becomes the highest, which
>>>>> happens quickly if the new response receives no up votes.
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> This means any fresh insight to a proposal gives it it's chance in the
>>>>> spotlight but if it does not generate support that it falls out of sight
>>>>> and gives way to more popular conversations.
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> I do not include any down votes in this system, as it not only brings
>>>>> on unnecessary negativity but has no actual useful purpose in this model.
>>>>> If you do not agree with a post can ignore it or respond to it, however a
>>>>> response bumps it up to the top again.
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> The correct math formula is obviously not sorted out yet but think
>>>>> it's something easily established when we get some measure of consistent
>>>>> activity.
>>>>>
>>>>>
>>>>> Sorry if I repeated myself a couple times in this explanation, just
>>>>> wanted to make sure it was clear because it took some time for myself to
>>>>> fully wrap my head around. Again please with the questions, happy to
>>>>> answer. This model keeps the proposals circulating but continuously
>>>>> reverting back to the projects that have drummed up the most support
>>>>> overall. This process of giving priority to the emerging proposals and
>>>>> comments hopefully can progress proposals in the right direction.
>>>>>
