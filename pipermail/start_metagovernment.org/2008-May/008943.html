> Rather than setting up an account system, we could use existing PGP
> infrastructure. A member of a community, would set up a PGP-Key and
> send the public key to a Keyserver. From thereon, all data she sends,
> could be verified, as originating from her.
>
> This is the best solution I can think of at the moment, if we go in the
> direction of an entirely distributed system. This will probably be more
> "interesting" to implement, to say the least.
>
> > I envision metascore running on top of only a revision control system,
> > that plays probably a larger role that what you see as reflected in that

>
> The issue I see here, is that messages don't really need to be under
> version control. Implementing the scoring system on that, may also
> be more difficult. I think it would be appropriate, to separate
> discussions into a different medium than the legislation. A database
> seems to be the intuitive solution, but that may be due to our
> backgrounds. I can certainly think of how a scoring system might be
> implemented in that scenario, but there are probably other good
> approaches.
>
> One that comes to mind, would be something implemented on top of
> freenet. This is very experimental atm. and usability would be a big issue
> here, but it may be an option. All data would be "on the net", so to
> speak, including the data for the scoring system. When a client connects
> to the system, it would download the scoring data, and then retrieve other
> data, such as messages, according to the users priorities and their scores.
>
> I would like to emphasize here, that the biggest issue would be usability.
> Wrapping your head around all these concepts (PGP, DVCS, Freenet,
> Scoring) is a rather big burden for a new user, which may not seem justified
> until you've come to know why they have been put in place. It may not be
> necessary for the user to know all this, but since trust in the system is
> essential, it would be good, to keep the system as simple as possible.
>
> On that note, trust in this kind of system (once you understand it) is much
> simpler, considering there won't be any people (administrators) you need
> to trust, since there would be no servers to administer.
