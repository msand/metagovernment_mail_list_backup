what if we ask users to rank resolutions?

On Sun, May 4, 2008 at 9:59 PM, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:
> On May 4, 2008, at 2:22 PM, Aur Saraf wrote:
>
>  >> I think most of us agree with the concept of resolution scoring
>  >> instead of pure voting. Instead of everyone having a yes/no vote,
>  >> people can give a more nuanced "vote" in terms of a number, say from
>  >> -10 to 10 (like the Apache -1.0 to +1.0, but without the decimal
>  >> point, to be less geeky). Some of us have referred to this as
>  >> "voting," but Manuel is right that we should avoid that word and call
>  >> this "scoring," to avoid confusion.
>  >
>  > I agree with the calling it "rating" or "scoring" instead of "voting"
>  > because it is not supposed to be a majority-win vote but a drive to
>  > consensus. I strongly disagree, in fact, with a floating point vote
>  > (or a star system). I support a yes/no vote because  I don't think
>  > there's any meaningfulness to saying "I kinda like his ideas but not
>  > very much" in this context, and because it would complicate any
>  > attempt at calculating consensus to no end. I like the "yes/no" system
>  > because it has a very simple definition of consensus: many said yes,
>  > (almost) nobody said no.
>
>  A floating score may do less to build consensus, but it does more to
>  build synthesis.
>
>  In a global community where there can be thousands of resolutions
>  about the same subject, I should be able to rate one resolution over
>  another. If I can only say yes or no to each resolution, then there is
>  no way I can favor a better one over a mediocre one.
>
>  You could say that I could only vote for the ones I think are best,
>  but in practice that system suppresses new ideas and suppresses
>  synthesis, because existing laws will always have very high scores. An
>  existing law may be preferable to no law. So why should I have to
>  withdraw my vote from the existing law just to promote a new one?
>
>  If we don't have variable resolution scores, then the idea of
>  synthesis scores (as currently stated on the website) becomes much
>  more important. They seem more complex to implement than floating
>  scores.
>
