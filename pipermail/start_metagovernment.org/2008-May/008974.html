First a point of clarification, just to be sure we are all on the same  
page... We are talking about two kinds of scores: resolution scores  
and user scores.

A resolution score is the score a user gives to a resolution,  
indicating how much they approve or disapprove of that resolution  
becoming law. A user score is the score a user has received from other  
users (possibly combined with other factors such as a post count; and  
also possibly differing from one community to another).

I think most of us agree with the concept of resolution scoring  
instead of pure voting. Instead of everyone having a yes/no vote,  
people can give a more nuanced "vote" in terms of a number, say from  
-10 to 10 (like the Apache -1.0 to +1.0, but without the decimal  
point, to be less geeky). Some of us have referred to this as  
"voting," but Manuel is right that we should avoid that word and call  
this "scoring," to avoid confusion.

The question Aur raised was whether user scores should be a weighting  
factor on one's resolution score. My inclination would be that we  
should have the system accommodate both kinds of scores and also  
accommodate weighting, just in case people want to use it. For  
example, we could just have one's final weighted vote be equal to  
something like:

(Res Score / 2) + ((Res Score * User Score * X) / 2)

Where:
Res Score is a user-defined value from -10 to 10
User Score is 1 by default (and ranges from .001 to 1000?)
X is a variable, and can be set to 0 by default.

By making X default to zero, we remove the weighting factor until  
people decide they want to use it.

Concerning Aur's dislike of the concept of communities and Jacki's  
suggestion of making user scores resolution-specific, I am not sure I  
want to abandon the concept of communities.

Jacki, having communities allows us to implement a separate User Score  
for each community. So someone who has distinguished themselves in the  
community of heart surgeons will have a greater say in the heart  
surgeon community... but not anywhere else. In the truck driver's  
community, that person will have a default score. Unless they start  
participating a lot in the truck driver community, in which point they  
may distinguish themselves there as well.

Aur, how else do we associate resolutions with each other? How do  
people even find the resolutions they are interested in? In a global  
society with an infinite amount of available information, how can one  
person know about the issues that are going to affect their  
neighborhood, the river they live on, the road they drive on, and the  
company they work at? There would have to be some sort of tagging  
system that will let a person find things. I think that those tags are  
naturally going to develop into communities, and we should facilitate,  
not hinder, that process.
