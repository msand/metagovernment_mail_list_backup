On May 4, 2008, at 2:25 PM, Aur Saraf wrote:

> On Sun, May 4, 2008 at 8:14 PM, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org 
> > wrote:
>> On May 4, 2008, at 6:26 AM, Aur Saraf wrote:
>>
>>> If we already have non-anon ratings, I thought non-anon discussion
>>> would also be a good idea as a way to fight spam - but that should  
>>> be
>>> thought out, balancing it against the advantages of having a way to
>>> post anonymously (if there really are any, in a world where a
>>> metagovernment rules).
>>
>> This is another reason to consider using user scores as a weighting
>> factor: it solves the issue of anonymity.
>>
>> Imagine a scenario something like this. Anyone can create as many
>> accounts as they wish, but a new account starts with a User Score of
>> 0.001. If that person verifies their identity in some way and claims
>> that account as being representative of their real identity, then
>> their User Score gets multiplied by 1000 (i.e., it jumps to a value  
>> of
>> 1, plus or minus whatever scores they have received so far).
>>
>> So people can make anonymous accounts and post from them when/if they
>> see a need, but they are incentivized to set up and use non-anonymous
>> accounts.
>
> Doesn't solve the problem of spam.

It does some but not all to prevent spam: people have to either set up  
a real identity and spam from that (which will cause their user score  
to plummet from negative community reactions) or they have to spam  
anonymously and their spam will have a very low score and be unlikely  
to have any impact. Combine that with a simple rate limiter (one post  
per minute, for example), and there is very little incentive to spam.

On a different track, who are we to decide what is spam? I would like  
our system to accommodate absolutely every kind of input people want  
to put into it, and allow for community/user filtering of that input.  
One person's spam may be another person's great idea.
