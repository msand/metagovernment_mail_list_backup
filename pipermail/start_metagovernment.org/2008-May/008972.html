>  > I have allot of issues with this discussion. Sorry in advance, for the
>  > wall of text. Here's a heads up, but please bear with me.
>  >
>  > - Voting allows everybody to participate, even those without a thorough
>  > understanding of the subject.
>  >
>  > - Scoring is intended to filter discussions for valuable contributions,
>  > rather than suppress members who have not established themselves.
>  >
>  > - The effort required to participate in a discussion, weeds those out who
>  > shouldn't be participating anyway.
>  >
>  >
>  > Again it seems that voting has become the default means for
>  > making a decision. And again I would like to point out, that voting is a
>  > very bad for a number of reasons. If a member of the community can't
>  > even coherently articulate their opinion, and contribute to a discussion,
>  > hopefully making a better proposal, or rejecting the existing proposal
>  > with a valid argument, there is no reason, for that member to have any
>  > say in a discussion.
>  >
>  > Allowing people to vote encourages mob rule. If a certain opinion is
>  > represented on the news as being urgent, and people are allowed to vote,
>  > we have effectively implemented mob rule.
>  >
>  > The reason I see for a scoring system, is not to reduce what any member
>  > of the community has to say on a certain topic, but to extract the
>  > valuable contributions, from a discussion among 2000 people. I think we
>  > can all agree, that even with just 20 people, all eagerly participating
>  > in a discussion, there is a need for some filtering mechanism, let alone
>  > the amount of people that would participate in a discussion over an
>  > issue relevant to an entire nation.
>  >
>  > What scoring does, is not to reduce the say any member has, based on
>  > their track record, but it allows the messages of any individual user to
>  > be rated, purely based on their merit. If the truck driver actually does
>  > have something relevant to say concerning heart surgery, the few members
>  > of the community that will initially see his message, will recognize
>  > this, rate it positively, thereby publishing it to a larger audience.
>  > Analogous can be said for an old senile heart surgeon, who may have made
>  > valuable contributions in the past, but lately can hardly gain any
>  > support. His messages will never make it to a wider audience.
>  >
>  > Nowhere here is there any need for a vote. Most of the time, a decision
>  > will emerge from the discussion, which all can agree upon. Once that
>  > decision moves on in its life cycle, and is proposed to be merged into
>  > the main repository, as law. More people will see it, they can read the
>  > discussions that occurred surrounding it. Ideally there will be a
>  > summary of the discussion, making the reasons clear, that lead to this
>  > particular choice of legislation.
>  >
>  > If somebody can then articulate a good reason against the legislation,
>  > the community can go back to the drawing board.
>  >
>  > Voting shifts the focus away from making the best decision for the
>  > community, allowing each voter to evaluate a decision, purely based
>  > on the effects it will have on the individual member himself. If he
>  > feels somehow mistreated, by some proposed legislation, he can make
>  > himself heard, in the form of a veto.
>  >
>  > There may be cases, where no decision can recognized as the best decision
>  > for the community (which should be the basis upon which a decision is
>  > evaluated). This shouldn't occur too often, if the discussion is held
>  > properly, the relevant facts and clearly formulated consequences of a
>  > proposal, should make the right decision obvious to everybody. This may
>  > not be the case, if facts are scarce and a decision is mostly a judgment
>  > call. If that is the case, the people can vote for all I care.
>  >
>  > I'll take the position of a tech snob here for a second, just for
>  > arguments sake: If people are willing to vote, it shows a lack
>  > of technical understanding in a subject. For one reason or another, they
>  > are unable to evaluate the facts presented to them and make there
>  > decisions based upon that. When making software in an open source
>  > community, the right decisions can mostly determined, based on their
>  > technical merit. The decisions that do come down to a vote, are the most
>  > banal things you could imagine. Those that everyone feels obligated to
>  > pitch in on, because it is basically a judgment call. As anecdotes of
>  > this, I offer:
>  > - http://www.producingoss.com/en/bikeshed-full.html
>  > And a more concrete example
>  > - http://www.python.org/dev/peps/pep-0308/
>  >
>  > The latter case shows, that on the most banal decision, the syntax for
>  > how to write a conditional expression in one line, no consensus can be
>  > achieved. Simply because it doesn't make that much of a difference, so
>  > there is no technical merit to judge decisions by.
>  >
>  > I could go on, but I've taken enough of your time.
