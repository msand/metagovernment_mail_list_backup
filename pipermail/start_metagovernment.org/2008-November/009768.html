On Mon, Nov 24, 2008 at 12:47 PM, Ed Pastore
<epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:
> On Nov 23, 2008, at 5:42 PM, Jacki Buros wrote:
>
>> I don't think it's realistic to require consensus for a bill to become law
>> -- more important is to facilitate consensus. If the requirement to achieve
>> consensus were present in the current US constitution, we would be caught up
>> in endless supreme court cases debating whether or not consensus was
>> achieved.
>
> It depends on how you view the scope and goal of the project. I think it is
> critical that we require consensus for lawmaking, in order for the other
> basic principles to work. Otherwise you just have mob-rule democracy, which
> is open to many, many criticisms. There are plenty of other open governance
> projects working on this model, and if it works... more power to them, but I
> am skeptical.
>
> Requiring consensus for a law can't possibly work in the United States right
> now. But it certainly can work in a new chess club. As people get more used
> to working to a consensus before enacting a policy, the scope should be able
> to scale upward to larger and larger communities.

I don't think is just a matter of being more used to this model.
Although this of course matters too.

I think there is also something about raw numbers.
As more people get involved the depth of the discussion might grow not
linearly but as n square, or n^n.

Of course while mathematically those discussion might go through a
combinatorial explosion, this will not happen if the topic is bounded.
We are speaking about this and only this. Are we going to put the
table here or there. The number of possibilities are limited, the
number of point of views are limited by the fact that reality itself
in this regard is limited, and then the system will work.

But if the discussion is allowed to go out of the boundaries, it can
easily explode, and never reach any synthesis.

SO by all mean, let us start with consensus, but do not expect that to
always work. Let us keep both the software and our heart prepared for
alternatives.

Think at something as simple as european union. At the beginning all
decisions were based on consensus. This was ok as long as EU was few
states. As soon as it started to expand with all the eastern european
states, the sheer number of states, of specific cases, of details,
made every discussion non conclusive. Until they have tried
(suceeded?) to change it into a majority rule.
