On Nov 23, 2008, at 5:42 PM, Jacki Buros wrote:

> I don't think it's realistic to require consensus for a bill to  
> become law -- more important is to facilitate consensus. If the  
> requirement to achieve consensus were present in the current US  
> constitution, we would be caught up in endless supreme court cases  
> debating whether or not consensus was achieved.

It depends on how you view the scope and goal of the project. I think  
it is critical that we require consensus for lawmaking, in order for  
the other basic principles to work. Otherwise you just have mob-rule  
democracy, which is open to many, many criticisms. There are plenty of  
other open governance projects working on this model, and if it  
works... more power to them, but I am skeptical.

Requiring consensus for a law can't possibly work in the United States  
right now. But it certainly can work in a new chess club. As people  
get more used to working to a consensus before enacting a policy, the  
scope should be able to scale upward to larger and larger communities.
