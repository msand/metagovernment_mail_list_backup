Hello Mike,
and welcome.

I think we all agree that the end experience for the user must be of
great simplicity, and ease of use.

On the other side the internal structure might be much more complicated,
provided it is well thought out, completely understood by the
technicians and totally transparent.

So adding tags to a resolution must be very easy.
But the formula with which the tags are evaluated to find out what
resolutions are similar to a particular one, and how does the weight
from one resolution influence the weight on another resolution does
not need to be easy to use. It would be better if it was, but even if
only technicians and math people understand it. That would not be a
problem.

You mentioned Google. Google is the perfect example. The interface is
easy to use (although you can use quite some tricks if you go and
study the tutorial). But the algorithms used by google to rank and
discover the pages are all but easy.

Also I think there is a number of people here who are resisting any
joint venture between this project and a company. So I think the idea
of collaborating with google at this stage is not really possible.

I am looking forward to your feedbacks, especially from your
experience as a federal government employee.

Pietro

>
> Based on the discussion related to software, I believe that the platform
> should be as simple as possible.  The group tends to be weighted toward
> tech-involved people, a number of whom have coding and development skills.

> technology available in order to make it accessible to the end-user with the
> most basic skills.  The vast majority of internet users do nothing more with
> the internet than check email and the e-gov platform should be just as
> accessible to them as it is to any of us.
>
> I believe, therefore, that one of the most basic discussions should be about
> identifying the platform.  My tendency would be toward "cloud" computing ala
> Google that would lead us toward a decent suite of tools and at the same
> time eliminate the need for a server contract to host the project (and who
> has time to administer those sorts of things in a volunteer organization?).
>
> Taken a step further, since Google is a leader in the open source computing
> movement, perhaps a collaboration with Google on developing the software
> platform would be fruitful.
