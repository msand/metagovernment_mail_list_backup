It is remarkable how many of our concepts start with D, should we play with that in our name?
Something like D-Govenrment, or DDDDDGov or DDDDDemocracy
Which would imediately raise the question of what the D(s) stand for and give us the opportunity to briefly push a lot of our key ideas right there saying it stands for:
- Descentraliced - in the sense that...
- Distributive - in the sense that...
- Dynamic - in the sense that...
- Direct - In the sense that...
- Democracy - In the sense that...

> On Sun, Nov 28, 2010 at 12:28 AM, Richard Franks <spontificus at gmail.com>wrote:
> 
>> Through faults entirely my own, I keep missing the window for "is
>> there a better phrase than open source democracy?" submissions in
>> vilfredo.  So I'm going to write up a few thoughts here, and if they
>> gain any traction, and if there's another proposal round in the
>> vilfredo vote, then I'll try and add it then.
>> 
>> I like "Open Democracy", and I'd be happy to use it more than "Open
>> Source Democracy", which is descriptive only through an implicit
>> understanding of open source software models.  The one issue I have
>> with it is in the form of the rebuttal -- "But our democratic
>> practices are open already, you can run for office and you can vote!"
>> So then you're left with the nuance - well actually, by "Open
>> Democracy" we mean that power is decentralised as much as possible and
>> we're using technology to achieve this.  And you can cite high
>> barriers of participation and so forth.  But as the political phrase
>> goes: "if you're defending, then you're losing".
>> 
>> I liked "E-Democracy" for a while, and certainly it has some use today
>> in discussions, but it's too easily confused with E-Voting.  Also, it
>> doesn't have longevity.  If we envision a future where all forms of
>> democracy are computerised/electronic, then appending the "E-" prefix
>> will become meaningless and redundant.  In addition, my personal
>> feeling is that the one-letter prefix i-this and e-that will quickly
>> be seen as a hallmark fad of the early 21st century.
>> 
>> The problem as I see it is that Democracy should mean all of these
>> things that we're trying to distinguish it with, but the term has
>> become synonymous with expansive representative democracy.
>> Furthermore, representative democracy isn't all that bad.  We want
>> people in place who are able to do what the populace can't - which is
>> to to make certain tough quick decisions in an emergency situation
>> when there isn't time for prolonged deliberation, while being held
>> fully accountable for their actions in those rare emergencies.  I
>> wouldn't expect discussion to ever end on the limits and conditions of
>> this.
>> 
>> But in almost all cases decision making power flows down, when we
>> think it should flow up.  A person in power should be tasked with
>> using that power to enact the will of the all the decision making
>> entities at a lower level of the pyramid.
>> 
>> Most of the time, the Texas Board of Education would be left to its
>> own devices - its membership defined only by those interested
>> stakeholders who choose to participate in its decision-making process.
>> But at the end of the day, you'd need some feet on the ground to
>> perform actions as a result of those decisions.  The US Department of
>> Education would have much cross-over in membership with the
>> state-level boards, open to the same rules of membership.  The point
>> being that many of our hierarchical mechanisms of governance are both
>> functional as they are disfunctional.  The point being that for
>> everyday non-controversial issues, the core stakeholders would form
>> hierarchies naturally, and work with those to get the job done.
>> 
>> 
>> "Hierarchically-Distributed Accountable-Representative Democracy"
>> hardly rolls off the tongue though.
>> 
>> As a term, "Distributed Democracy" strips away the implicit
>> descriptors of what Democracy should be and those which are redundant
>> due to the evolution of the popular conception of it is.  You can
>> describe it in a one-liner as: "Decision making power distributed
>> amongst all those who wish to take part, however and whenever they
>> like."
>> 
>> 
>> There is some precedent with a similar term:
>> http://www.search.com/reference/Dynamically_Distributed_Democracy
>> http://arxiv.org/abs/cs.CY/0412047
>> 
>> But, the dynamic aspect is implicit and their description defines
>> implementation-level criteria that are entirely subject to change.
>>
