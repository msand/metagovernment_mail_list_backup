On Nov 4, 2010, at 8:55 PM, Owen Ambur wrote:

> [[It has been said that democracy is the worst form of  
> government ... except
> for all the others.  http://www.quotationspage.com/quote/364.html  
> However,
> in my view, the very concept of "governance" has been greatly  
> oversold.  Not
> only has it moved fairly far beyond necessity in many cases but it  
> also
> creates a perverse incentive by implicitly relieving individuals of
> responsibility for themselves, their own families, and communities.  
> (As if
> only others could see it their way, we could all live in harmony and
> everything would be wonderful.)  What I am pursuing is not another  
> form of
> government, which implies coercion to a greater or lesser degree,  
> but a
> better way for individuals to work together of their own free  
> will ... to
> achieve goals and objectives on which they *already* agree. If and  
> when that
> becomes very easy to do, anywhere and anytime, the need for  
> "governance" can
> be put into more proper perspective and, hopefully, with the benefit  
> of that
> perspective, we can move on to more difficult problems like those  
> you and
> others on this listserv are aiming to address, e.g., when it is  
> necessary
> and, if so, how best to "enforce" consensus and/or roll roughshod  
> over those
> who disagree.]]

WHat you say above speaks to a core aspect of Metagovernment. Very  
frequently, when I discuss the concept with others and the idea of a  
consensus decision-rule comes up, their reaction is: "But government  
will be gridlocked! How can we pass any laws if we have to have  
widespread agreement on them first?"

And my reply is, basically: Why do we need so many laws?

But even more deeply: How can we pass laws that *don't* have  
widespread support?
