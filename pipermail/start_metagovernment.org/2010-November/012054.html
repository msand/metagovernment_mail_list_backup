Ed and Richard (and a question for all),

Ed Pastore wrote:
> In the re-drafting of the homepage, we have followed this paradigm
> generally, but without any of the external structure of
> voting. There is a lot of borrowing and synthesis in this process,
> but it also has the benefit of being very limber and fast-moving.

It's been called "recombinant text".  It's interesting to see it
growing in the wild.

> I am wondering how far this sort of free-form process can scale. It
> seems to me to be a great way to start out the drafting of
> proposals, before they enter into the more formal mechanisms of
> voting systems.  Or perhaps that it could benefit from its own
> formalization: a structure that actively promotes the blending and
> synthesis of diverse proposals.
> 
> Am I right, or would this process have worked just as well or even
> better if it had been run through, say, Votorola from the outset?

I agree.  I think it's better to start free-form, like you did in that
thread "Home page redesign competition".  Ideally you shouldn't have
to uproot that process in order to tool it up, but rather the tools
should come to you.  I think that's possible with "free-range
drafting".  We could maybe code a prototype of that next month if
there's sufficient need or interest.  You could then hook into the
open voting network (Outcast) without having to transfer your drafts
to a different wiki.  You'd then gain access to the difference bridge,
which would give you the kind of branching/merging support that
Richard mentions below.

Secondly, you'd gain the ability to vote.  You could use any
voteserver that's hooked into the voting network (or any server at all
if we can mirror its votes by guerilla techniques).  However the
purpose of voting is not to pick a winner, but rather to channel the
flow of amendments through a network of discussion cells, each a
staging area for a potential, emerging synthesis.  The entire
population of texts evolves in that process.

Lastly, hooking into the open voting network would give you visibility
in crossforum theatre: http://zelea.com/project/votorola/a/crossforum/
It's going to be a kind of marquee or umbrella UI over the entire
network.  (Our programmer said he can probably support Mailman lists
in the prototype.)

None of this is Votorola per se.  It's just that none of the other
projects has taken an interest in coding the early prototypes, so we
Votorolans do it alone.  (But that's probably my fault for labelling
it an "open architecture", when "open network" is more appealing to
developers, and more accurate.)

(sorry for the long reply)

Richard Franks wrote:
> My only criticism of the process is that mediawiki isn't the best
> platform to fork and merge upon.  If I make a small (but crucial)
> edit to an existing block of text - say the transition section -
> then that's not readily obvious from someone glancing at the section
> that the tiny edit is there.  So it can get lost, or - perhaps worse
> - they'll incorporable my edit into their copy without noticing.
> There's no audit trail with copy+paste.
> 
> Distributed versioning systems (e.g. git/mercurial) handle this case
> really well, but as far as I know there's no user friendly front-end
> like mediawiki that we could use.

They also lack a good cherry-picking facility.  You can't predictably
pull that one "tiny edit" from another drafter's text without getting
a string of other edits attached to it.  That's one of the advantages
of employing a difference bridge.  (If you login under alias 'a' or
'b' here, you can play with the patch controls:

  http://u.zelea.com:8080/v/w/Diff?b=3355&a=3220
  
But the big advantage is to rationalize the discussion by centering it
on concrete differences of position.  The bridge makes it easier to
say things like, "Hey B, What do you think of this amendment?"
http://u.zelea.com:8080/v/w/Diff?b=3355&a=3220#_2

If B likes it, he can press the Patch button.  Otherwise he can state
his reasons against it.  So it goes.  The edit naturally propagates
throughout the population of drafts, except where it is opposed by
reasons.


Question: Is "Outcast" a good name for an open voting network?
