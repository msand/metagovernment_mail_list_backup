>> Thanks, Ned. I get the point, but in a sense I am trying to avoid it.
>>
>> The things that are important to people from day to day are typically not
>> war or pollution or rainforests... people worry about whether or not the
>> tennis court is available, or how they're going to get food on the table,
>> or how bad the traffic is going to be today, or what their kids are being
>> taught in school.
>>
>> I'm saying we help people make those decisions collectively and on a
>> micro scale, and let them get used to making decisions cooperatively and
>> working through differences of opinion within those small communities. Then
>> as people get used to the idea, they will be better equipped to deal with
>> the big, haunting issues like adults; instead of in the childish,
>> confrontational/authoritarian approach we take now.
>>
>> If we want to solve the problem of war this decade, we will fail. If we
>> spend this decade training people to work together on problem-solving, war
>> will eventually solve itself.
>>
>>
>>
>> > On Dec 20, 2014, at 9:33 PM, Ned Conner <npconner at earthlink.net> wrote:

>> > Hi Ed (and Scott),

>> > Ed, using community park management as your example does not permit you
>> to make a very compelling case for local-only government. Maybe try using
>> something like water rights instead as your example, or pollution, or
>> rainforest management, or the phenomenon of war.
>> >
>> > See for example:
>> > http://www.huffingtonpost.com/tag/t-boone-pickens-water/

>> > On 12/20/2014 5:24 PM, Ed Pastore wrote:
>> >> On Dec 20, 2014, at 6:23 PM, Scott Raney <scott at metacard.com> wrote:
>> >>> his is where the engineer in me says that while this sounds nice in
>> >>> theory, there are just some things that don't scale, and mass public
>> >>> input into the proposal generation process is one of them.
>> >> My point is... why do we need large scale governance? I don't see the
>> benefit in the existence (anymore) a nation as huge as the United States or
>> Russia trying to make policy for everyone. Those states are artifacts of
>> our primitive history. The "natural unit" of human society is the city or
>> town.*
>> >>
>> >> And even within a city, decisions should be fractured into smaller
>> units. A city has a park system, and then within that there are individual
>> parks, and within an individual park there may be a set of tennis courts.
>> Each has its own set of rules and most of the important decisions can be
>> made at the lowest level. The city doesn't have to decide the rules for
>> reserving a tennis court... leave that to the people who play tennis in
>> that particular park (or are affected by its play... whomever is
>> interested).
>> >>
>> >> Driving decision-making up to a high level and then having that level
>> impose on the smaller levels is a form of authoritarianism. And often quite
>> counter-productive.
>> >>
>> >> Sure, we'd like to solve all the world's problems. But there are
>> billions of problems. I say we start solving the small ones. As people get
>> used to collaborating on and participating in governance, they will start
>> to build ways to scale that none of us can yet imagine. Because their minds
>> will work differently: they will be used to the idea and have plenty of
>> practical experience under their belts.
>> >>
>> >>
>> >> * Another natural unit of society is the community of interest,
>> regardless of geography. And those are by definition relatively homogeneous
>> and can approach collaborative governance even with large numbers. And if
>> they have a schism in their community... they can always break into two
>> communities.
