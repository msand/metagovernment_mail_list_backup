Thanks, Ned. I get the point, but in a sense I am trying to avoid it.

The things that are important to people from day to day are typically not war or pollution or rainforests... people worry about whether or not the tennis court is available, or how they're going to get food on the table, or how bad the traffic is going to be today, or what their kids are being taught in school.

I'm saying we help people make those decisions collectively and on a micro scale, and let them get used to making decisions cooperatively and working through differences of opinion within those small communities. Then as people get used to the idea, they will be better equipped to deal with the big, haunting issues like adults; instead of in the childish, confrontational/authoritarian approach we take now.

If we want to solve the problem of war this decade, we will fail. If we spend this decade training people to work together on problem-solving, war will eventually solve itself.
