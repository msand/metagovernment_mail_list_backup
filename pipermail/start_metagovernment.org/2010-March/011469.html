> Owen: your second sentence says it all - few people get beyond  
> "talking about" collaboration and actually "doing it".
>
> Therefore, we've been trying to understand what we need to do to get  
> people willing and motivated to actually collaborate.
>
> Having a VERY simple, commonly understood language (PRUB) is a key  
> step in the right direction.  It's not sufficient on it's own (hence  
> our full OpenStrategy system), but it's essential (which is part of  
> the motivation for StratML).
>
> I'm confident that PRUB-strategic-thinking could help clarify  
> Metagov's "objectives", if only by clarifying what is meant by an  
> "objective".  In our experience, the word "objective" can mean one  
> of many different things on the continuum between "a good idea to do  
> something" right through to "a Benefit for end users".
>
> This is precisely why we use the very exact words of Projects,  
> Results, Uses and Benefits when we "translate" people's Objectives  
> into these categories.  Over and over again (perhaps 90% of the  
> time), we find that people's "objectives" are what we would  
> categorise as "Results" (or even "Projects") which are relatively  
> early on the idea-Benefit continuum.
>
> In order to gain clarity and exactness on the idea-Benefit  
> continuum, we therefore encourage people to avoid the use of words  
> like "objectives" and "goals", and instead use the more precise PRUB  
> terminology.  In doing so, it then becomes straightforward to  
> complete the full PRUB sequence.
>
> In particular, it's essential to understand "Uses"  (and in so  
> doing, to become "user/citizen focused").
>
> ONLY Uses create Benefits/outcomes.
>
> Projects don't create Benefits/outcomes.
>
> Results don't create Benefits/outcomes.
>
> So irrespective of how beautiful a Result might be, if it isn't Used  
> then there won't be any Benefits.
>
> Therefore if people are going to define their "objectives" as what  
> we would call Projects, then unless they unequivocally link them  
> through Results and Uses to Benefits, they are probably wasting  
> their time.
>
> Does MetaGov have a list of "objectives"?   If so, and if it's not  
> too long, then I'd be happy to review the list and categorise them  
> as Projects, Results, Uses or Benefits.  Based on our experience,  
> organisations usually find such a categorisation to be enlightening  
> and helpful.
>
> To give you an indication of the stark reality of what we find when  
> we translate organisation's strategies/objectives into the  
> OpenStrategy/PRUB format:
> more than 50% and usually more than 80% of published "strategies"  
> and "Objectives" are in fact Projects or Results
> less than 10% (usually less than 1%) of Uses are defined in any way
> of those few Uses that have been defined, we've practically never  
> found any evidence that the proposed Results will genuinely be Used
> we've similarly found almost no evidence that the defined Uses will  
> actually generate any Benefits
> almost no Users have been involved in the preparation of the  
> strategy/objectives documents
> fewer than 10% of objectives are linked in any way to Benefits  
> ("outcomes" in government jargon)
> so it's hardly surprising that (in our observation) at least 90% of  
> strategies make absolutely no difference
>
> So my offer is there if MetaGov wish to take it up.

> Dear OpenKollab members
>
> I've just joined OpenKollab and am not sure of the protocols for  
> posting so I hope I'm doing the right thing here.
>
> OpenKollab totally resonates with us as we've been doing open  
> collaboration for about 10 years and have built up a detailed, fully  
> functioning multi-stakeholder strategy development system -  
> OpenStrategy (there is some basic information on www.openstrategies.com 
>  - we'll update this site by May)
>
> This email is not a commercial for OpenStrategy.
>
> I'm just sharing some of the enormous amount we've learned about  
> what does and doesn't work with realpeople in collaborative multi- 
> stakeholder groups (we have much more learning than what I've  
> summarised below).
>
> If nothing else, we've learned that what's technologically possible  
> is almost irrelevant - it's what real-world people are actually  
> prepared to do that is the overwhelming constraining factor in large- 
> scale collaboration.  Indeed our OpenStrategy system is perhaps 90%  
> philosophy, psychology and sociology and 10% technology (primarily  
> our web-tool).
>
> Our "collaboration space" relates to large-scale, multi-stakeholder  
> groups, typically in the public sector in the UK (we have an Oxford  
> office) and NZ (head office Christchurch).
>
> For example, we typically work with groups of say 100 organisations  
> who are trying to collaboratively create and implement large scale  
> strategies (eg a county-wide Children and Young Person's strategy, a  
> City Sustainable Community strategy, a multi-city Health Improvement  
> strategy or a regional Climate Change strategy).  We find that:
> most stakeholders say they want to collaborate (it's such a lovely  
> warm concept, mmmmm), perhaps 95% will attend collaborative  
> workshops and talk about collaboration, and perhaps 5% willactually  
> collaborate in small groups of 2-3 organisations (collaborative  
> doing is utterly different from collaborative chatting or  
> collaborative data collecting/archiving/sharing) (and maybe 50% will  
> give advice on how others should collaborate.... there is no  
> shortage of advice, just a shortage of effective working  
> collaboration systems)
> most organisational stakeholders want to continue to work inside  
> their own organisational silos (they usually have to, for financial  
> accountability reasons), so the challenge is to "join up the silos"  
> not"break down the silos"
> there must be a common language when people wish to collaborate.
> In collaborative strategy-space with hundreds of stakeholders, we  
> can't work collaboratively with a plethora of words such as  
> objectives, goals, outputs, outcomes, targets, visions, mission,  
> drivers for change, structures, frameworks, thought-pieces,....  
> specially when they all mean different things to different people  
> (see also StratML - Strategic Markup Language being developed by  
> AIIM and driven by Owen Ambur)
> cognitively, average humans can readily handle 4 +/- 2 pieces of  
> information, so for real-world people there is not much point  
> developing sophisticated collaboration systems based on more complex  
> data sets - they may be meaningful to the tiny percentage of people  
> who are large-scale integrators (often INTJs), but most people in a  
> normal community think and work on a much smaller scale.
> If we want stakeholders to engage and to actually collaborate (as  
> distinct from talk about it), we have to develop collaboration  
> systems that they can get their heads around (if you've read this  
> far, can I plead with you to seriously consider this issue so that  
> your collective brainpower can be brought to bear on creating  
> simple, pragmatic/practical collaboration systems that can genuinely  
> enable large-scale, real-world communities to plan/strategise/ 
> implement effectively to make this world a better place)
> so for effective large-scale strategy/planning collaboration (as  
> distinct from social discussion networks) we need to identify an  
> explicitly defined sub-set of information which meets the following  
> criteria:
> it is the smallest set of information
> which has the highest value
> to the most people
> we are now confident that this subset is (we think, universally)  
> what we call PRUB:
> Organisations run Projects     (organisational action)
> that produce Results            (organisational consequence)
> that communities Use        (community action)
> to create Benefits for themselves        (community consequence)
> all other information is of lower value to the collective of  
> stakeholders, although individual bits of information will have  
> higher value to individuals
> an interlinked set of PRUBs is what we call a SubStrategy
> a collection of SubStrategies is what we call an OpenStrategy
> to create a strategy, start with Benefits and work back to Projects  
> (BURP)
> to implement a strategy, start with Projects and work through to  
> Benefits (PRUB)
> so PRUB/SubStrategies/OpenStrategies are simultaneously strategies  
> (BURP) and implementation plans (PRUB)
> they enable stakeholders to explicitly define exactly what they are  
> doing (as SubStrategies) and readily share them with other  
> stakeholders via our on-line OpenStrategy web-tool
> other stakeholders can search the OpenStrategy to find SubStrategies  
> of interest to them, contact the Project leaders and start  
> collaborating (along the same lines of open source software projects  
> - a programmer kicks off with a good idea and others collaborate by  
> clustering around them)
> so practical collaboration is a "voluntary engaging of the willing  
> with the willing", and it usually happens on a rather small scale
> it's worth considering the mathematics of multi-stakeholder  
> collaboration on multiple topics based on the idea of 'networking'  
> of stakeholders:  if 100 organisations wish to collaborate on 50  
> topics by talking to each other just 6 times per year on each topic,  
> this amounts to about 1,500,000 conversations.... - is that really  
> going to happen effectively - and make any difference in the world?   
> What happens to the content of the communications?
> We put forward the following concept:
> Type 1 collaborative strategy = hierarchy of people - someone at  
> "the top" creates/coordinates it: doesn't work unless top dogs have  
> huge power (in which case they get their way, which seldom means  
> that communities get the Benefits they want)
> Type 2 collaborative strategy = networks of people:  mathematically  
> impossible to network everyone effectively on all topics (as shown  
> above)
> Type 3 collaborative strategy = networks of ideas (eg SubStrategies/ 
> OpenStrategies) so that stakeholders share what they are doing in an  
> on-line OpenStrategy and other stakeholders cluster around them:  
> mathematically very doable - even a complex community will have at  
> most a few thousand SubStrategies and usually only a few hundred  
> (this is important.  Some people want to add more information than  
> just PRUB - but think about it - if a multi-stakeholder group has  
> 500 Projects (and hence 4-500 SubStrategies), that's about 2000  
> PRUBs.  In our system, we also have "Links" between each P, R, U, B  
> and "Evidence" for each Link - so we're already up to 4-5,000 pieces  
> of information, albeit nicely "chunked up" into meaningful  
> SubStrategies.  We find that any more information than that simply  
> overwhelms multi-stakeholder groups - so as noted earlier, we need  
> "the smallest amount of information that has the highest value to  
> the most people" - we're convinced this information SubSet is PRUB  
> which can build into a library of SubStrategies = an OpenStrategy
> Our experience shows universally that stakeholders, at all levels,  
> really "get it" with SubStrategies - they can get their heads around  
> the simplicity of PRUB and the slightly more complex concept of  
> SubStrategies (each SubStrategy may contain several inter-linked  
> PRUBs).  Warning: there are many subtleties in PRUB (eg Orphan  
> Results, end-point Results, unwanted Results, evolving Results, non- 
> discretionary Results etc.....) but the core concept of PRUB is  
> utterly robust and effective.
>
