Matías, since the StratML standard was only recently issued and it is not
yet clear how widely it may be adopted, search services have not yet arisen
to take full advantage of it.  However, since StratML documents are plain
(XML) text posted on the Web, they are indexed by Google and other full-text
search engines.  See, for example, the Google query field at
http://xml.gov/stratml/index.htm#Services  

 

HTML is about the *presentation* of information on the Web, e.g., displaying
text in bold, italics, or paragraphs.  

 

XML is about the *meaning* of the elements, e.g., a mission or goal
statement, the name of a person, a date, a number that is an amount of
currency (money), etc.

 

XHTML can encompass both.  However, using style sheets in combination with
XML is more flexible than hard coding presentation into the files
themselves.  See, for example, the several renditions of former President
Bush’s management agenda at http://xml.gov/stratml/index.htm#PMA (two of
which are HTML automatically rendered from the original XML source).

 

While I will be happy to include in the StratML collection any plan the
subscribers to this listserv may wish to compile, the intent is that StratML
documents can be posted anywhere on the Web and, eventually, indexed by
intermediary services equipped to take full advantage of the intelligence
(meaning) they contain for the benefit of various communities of interest.

 

BTW, since Google has built its competitive advantage on full-text search
algorithms, they may not be among the most likely candidates to leverage the
internal logic of StratML documents (or other XML files based upon schemas).
However, if they don’t, it is only a matter of time before others do so and
current search services become relics of the past.
