On Mon, Oct 12, 2009 at 1:34 PM, Giovani Spagnolo <
giovani at telematicsfreedom.org> wrote:

>  Not sure what's all about Facebook for e-democracy here... maybe we have
> different thoughts.
> I agree that FB is a very nice platform with very well done features.
> I would also agree with the statement that it could be a good place for
> e-democracy movements *IF* started by Civil Society *BUT NOT* if promoted by
> Governments as a way to take seriously into account citizen's input (at
> maximum it could be used for institutional propaganda, and communication of
> results).
>
> But if governments start using FB to create their e-participation tools,
> well then we are far away from real e-democracy. The only and simple fact
> that FB is "closed-source" and governments can't be sure of how FB treats
> their and citizen's data is alone a non-go. Let alone talking about EU
> governments, as FB is a US-based company.
>
> The EUGAGER proposal we submitted was thought to be supported by
> governments as an alternative to facebook (it does not exclude integrations
> for non-secure apps), but it would provide citizen's and govs with "secure"
> apps that can really be trusted (not only in terms of identification, but
> also data integrity and checking). And of course be fully-auditable
> open-source.  (soon news about that... :-)
>
> Maybe you guys were suggesting FB for grassroot apps created by citizens
> and for the citizens... I just wanted to be sure that when we talk about
> real governments supporting open and transparent governance it does not mean
> using facebook to interact 2-way with citizens for serious stuff...
>
> my 2 cents ;-)
> giovani
>
>
> Christopher D. Ritter wrote:
>
> Knowing how complex the definition of e-democracy can get, I would say that
> Facebook has a number of features which I would associate with online
> governance, namely: Profiles, Calendaring, and many, many, different types
> of Messaging. Where it is lacking as far as e-democracy is concerned it
> makes up for in its mark-up language. The point at which sensitive data is
> communicated through Facebook is a point at which personal security becomes
> an issue.
>
> That's something I know nothing about.
>
> What I do know is that big companies are waking up to Facebook and building
> their fanbase on top of it. Many of the applications aren't really relevant
> to the users' needs and primarily focus on driving consumerism.
> Nevertheless, they're using Facebook because of its population boom, its
> ability to spread personally relevant information, as well as the amount of
> customer loyalty it provides.
>
> Given its rising popularity our politicians, I would expect that the
> governments will soon begin communicating with us through Facebook in much
> the same way that the White House communicates through Facbook -- except,
> one would hope, a little more personally. If this is the case, then an
> e-democracy application would be perfectly positioned on Facebook to help
> users administer their local governance.
>
> And if Facebook isn't your thing, make another one on Orkut. :)
