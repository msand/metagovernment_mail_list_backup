This is an interesting dialog, but from my perspective, key points include:

1) government should do only that which is duly authorized by constitution and law and more explicitly documented in strategic and performance plans upon which citizens and other stakeholders should be given the opportunity to provide input and feedback;

2) except as required by law (e.g., social security and voter registration), each of us should be free to maintain our digital personas anywhere that *we* choose on the Web; and 

3) eventually, any intermediary (e.g., Facebook) seeking to "serve" us should be capable of referencing and using our digital personas by virtue of the fact that we maintain them in conformance with an international voluntary consensus standard for such data.  OpenID looks like a good candidate: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenID  It is compared to SAML here: http://identitymeme.org/doc/draft-hodges-saml-openid-compare.html 

While I may occasionally use Facebook, LinkedIn, Google Docs, etc., etc., I am less inclined to do so and certainly have no intention of making them my preferred service provider unless and until they support something like OpenID (and SAML).

  On Mon, Oct 12, 2009 at 1:34 PM, Giovani Spagnolo <giovani at telematicsfreedom.org> wrote:

    Not sure what's all about Facebook for e-democracy here... maybe we have different thoughts. 
    I agree that FB is a very nice platform with very well done features.
    I would also agree with the statement that it could be a good place for e-democracy movements *IF* started by Civil Society *BUT NOT* if promoted by Governments as a way to take seriously into account citizen's input (at maximum it could be used for institutional propaganda, and communication of results).

    But if governments start using FB to create their e-participation tools, well then we are far away from real e-democracy. The only and simple fact that FB is "closed-source" and governments can't be sure of how FB treats their and citizen's data is alone a non-go. Let alone talking about EU governments, as FB is a US-based company.

    The EUGAGER proposal we submitted was thought to be supported by governments as an alternative to facebook (it does not exclude integrations for non-secure apps), but it would provide citizen's and govs with "secure" apps that can really be trusted (not only in terms of identification, but also data integrity and checking). And of course be fully-auditable open-source.  (soon news about that... :-)

    Maybe you guys were suggesting FB for grassroot apps created by citizens and for the citizens... I just wanted to be sure that when we talk about real governments supporting open and transparent governance it does not mean using facebook to interact 2-way with citizens for serious stuff...

    my 2 cents ;-)
