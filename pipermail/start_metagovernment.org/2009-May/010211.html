Let's leave aside DVCS for a moment.
Which is a level of complication that a single online community rarely needs.


I have been wondering if WH2 could be integrated with the pareto
algorithm for a long time. The answer is: not easily. The way the
pareto front works is theough questions, proposals, endorsements, and
generations. It requires an interactive experience from the comunity
that goes through many inputs (sometimes through many days, but it can
easily be done in 1 hour, if everybody is online at the same time).

How you pose the question is fundamental. If you are speaking about
abortion, and you ask:

-what shall we do to the assassins that kill their own babies when
they are still in the womb against the law of the only true Lord,
Jesus Christ.

or you are asking:

-what shall we do to the fanatics that keep on deciding for us about
our body by forcing us to accept unwanted pregnancies, only to satisfy
their sense of guilt to an unexistant god.

you can see how they are both adressing a related issue (although they
are not the same question), but the point of view, is "somehow"
different ;-). So whoever starts the question has an insane amunt of
power. We have seen this already in those days in the prototype. A few
questions had quite a strong spin.

So if you are inserting Al Pareto (Algorithm Pareto, brother of Al
Capone :) ) inside WH2, you are essentially going to first of all
decide who asks what question. Then you have to decide the topic (is
this health? religion? public order?). All those are going to be top
down decisions, that some people will not agree with. Now there are
perfectly fine bottom up ways to go around both of them. FOr the topic
issue, the tags will be the solution (we shall explore this in the
FP7). For the question, the way to go over it is to  ask a
metaquestion.

Example:

-Our community is split on people who want abortion to be legal, and
people who do not want it to be legal. What question would better help
in finding a proposal that heals this fracture?

Then you find a question on which everybody agrees (this is much
easier than solving the problem, I bet the number of generations here
necessary to find an agreement might be only few.

Sometimes finding a good metaquestion is as difficult as finding a
good question. Which is why I have used it only one time. Generally
you use a meta question when a community has already decided they want
to ask a question, but they an't really make their mind on how to
formulate it. Why am I saying all this? Because WH2, with the kind of
people that is attracting, will have some serious problems in how
questions get spinned. To the point that it might make sense for Jim
to make some top down decisions, and ask the question himself. Or ask
the metaquestions.

Then there is the whole issue of the proposals, and the endorsments.
In short you really would have to remake the whole engine of WH2. So I
am not sure if I were to suggest it. I think WH2 is fine as it is for
now.

The other issue is why Vilfredo was done as it is. The idea was not
just to make a website that works, but also to make it with the least
number of assumptions. It is spartane in its very nature. A bit like
delicious in the first years. This because we want to show that it is
really Al PAreto that is making Vilfredo do its magic, and not
something else. We do envision for Al Pareto a future in which it
would be used as an engine in other projects. Like having a plugin for
Drupal, and an application for Facebook. So yes, eventually Al Pareto
could be used inside other systems. But the systems need to permit it.
Either by running external applications, or by being designed in a way
that fits Al Pareto.

You see, part of the program is that being so different it just does
not fit many of the assumption that we usually make. For example that
the number of people endorsing something has any importance. And it is
very easy to break by inserting unneeded limits, like permitting to a
person to endorse only x proposals.

So my proposal is:
while we are in this phase,
with me writing the VgtA paper,
waiting for the FP7 decisions,
why don't you try the website out, maybe asking also there the open
question you would like to have answered. And trying to use it as a
group.

> On May 4, 2009, at 4:19 PM, Jim Gilliam wrote:
>
> Successful open source projects tend to be led by a person or two who does
> all the coding to get the initial version to a place where it is actually
> useful to other people.  Then when other people are looking to solve a
> problem, they see a project that is sort of close to what they want to do,
> so they start tweaking the code a bit and get involved.  That's how the
> project grows.   People coding in their free time for no money don't like
> being told what to do, they do it because they want to make it work they
> want they want it to work!  Sometimes lots of people get involved, and then
> there are disagreements on the future direction, so they fork it and spin
> off a slightly different version, etc..
>
> The metagovernment software (or metascore) doesn't solve a problem for
> anyone, YET.  What metagovernment *is* solving though, is it's getting a
> bunch of people and projects together who are interested in this concept of
> open source governance and getting a dialog going.  That's not a failure at
> all, that's a success!  The nature of this project is it's led by someone
> who brings people together, not someone who codes.
>
> That's my general philosophy on this, and explains a bit of how I'm
> proceeding with White House 2.  I'm trying to get it to a point where it's
> useful to other people, and then I assume people will have their own ideas
> of what to do with it, and can choose to get involved or not.
