Agree - being able to identify voters (potentially anonymously) seems
crucial.

As in my previous message, I think that communities need to be represented
fully in a decision making process, even if only at the end.  I think this
means that proxy voting is inevitable.  The desirability I mentioned of a
sophisticated political landscape is also very attractive.

Some caveats though: one would certainly want to make sure that delegatees
did not abuse their powers.  They should retain their delegated rights for
limited periods.  Those on whose behalf they voted should be informed of the
decisions that had been made, potentially in advance of finality of that
decision so that there was a power to overrule.  Of course, a critical
difference between power through delegated votes and conventional political
power would be the flexibility.  No more automatic 4+ year terms of office.
Instead true representation of people, that can be stripped away at any
point.

If I'm understanding the third paragraph right then the voter's interests
issues are effectively taken care of by the 'community representation' that
I discussed above.  Of course, ideally people might declare interests/step
aside from votes/decide in the best interests of society etc but that, it
looks to me, is taking things to a different level of complexity.

Tom

On 14/05/2009 16:31, "Owen Ambur" <Owen.Ambur at verizon.net> wrote:

> I'm with Ed on this.  My primary concern at this point is that if a "vote"
> is to mean anything, it must be possible to ensure that it is associated
> with an individual and that individual is only allowed to vote once, with
> caveats to enable: a) anonymous voting, as election systems do now, and b)
> *perhaps* proxy voting, if the voters agree to delegate to others they
> trust.  
> 
> As implied in Ed's message, I'm not sure proxy voting should be allowed in
> many, if any instances, because it means that individuals don't care enough
> to take the time to vote and it risks giving too much power to other
> individuals, which inevitably corrupts them.  (The case has been made that
> Members of Congress are too busy and sit on too many committees to be
> required to be present to vote.  However, from my perspective, based upon my
> personal experience, that is merely evidence they have too much power.)
> 
> Another issue that is too complicated and controversial to get into right
> now is the degrees to which each voter's interests may actually be affected
> by the issue to be decided by vote.  For example, if one does not pay income
> tax, it is very easy to vote to raise the taxes of others in order to enable
> greater spending on social causes.  Or if one is fortunate enough to sit on
> a corporate board by virtue of having befriended the CEO, it has been pretty
> easy to vote to raise the compensation of the CEO, whereas common
> stockholders may have no real voice in such decisions. (Under current
> economic circumstances, it is now harder for board members to do that.)
> Colloquially speaking, the issue is how much "skin" one has in the game.
> Another way to consider this issue is the distinction between charity and
> volunteering versus taxation and mandatory "service".
>

> Systems attached to existing governments seem to think that way, but
> for Metagovernment I hope we can hold true to the "anyone" in the
> first principle. As soon as people get artificially disenfranchised
> from a process for any reason, democracy suffers.
> 
> Given that we live in a very global, very connected world, it is
> unseemly that we should disenfranchise people from decisions simply
> because they do not live in a certain geography. If someone cares
> about something enough to spend time acting on it, then it probably
> effects them or their interests.
> 
> A classic example is watershed management. If a locality at the head
> of a river has sole jurisdiction over what pollutants enter the river,
> all people downstream are disenfranchised from an issue which clearly
> affects them. Modern governments have started to develop regional
> authorities for things like watersheds, but now there are so many
> cross-jurisdictional issues that it seems the easiest and purest
> solution would be to invite participation from anyone interested
> enough to participate.
