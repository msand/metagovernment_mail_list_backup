The problem of getting people to care about their own self-governance  
is a chicken-and-egg problem. The way governance is done now makes  
most people so depressed about getting involved; and those who do get  
involved still can't change the way governance works. I think we're on  
to a good solution by offering software to tiny communities and  
letting people see that they can self-govern in non-governmental  
situations. From there, awareness should bloom.

As I understand it, DVCS offers several crucial advantages for a open  
governance system. It not only solves most of the tricky security  
issues, but also mirrors the mesh-of-communities model we have been  
developing: making for a dynamic/organic inter-community structure.  
However, I do agree that the DVCS model can be confusing to the  
uninitiated. From what I understand from Fabe's earlier e-mail, they  
are working directly to overcome that confusion. I'm looking forward  
to seeing it deployed...

>> Successful open source projects tend to be led by a person or two  
>> who does all the coding to get the initial version to a place where  
>> it is actually useful to other people.  Then when other people are  
>> looking to solve a problem, they see a project that is sort of  
>> close to what they want to do, so they start tweaking the code a  
>> bit and get involved.  That's how the project grows.   People  
>> coding in their free time for no money don't like being told what  
>> to do, they do it because they want to make it work they want they  
>> want it to work!  Sometimes lots of people get involved, and then

>> and spin off a slightly different version, etc..
