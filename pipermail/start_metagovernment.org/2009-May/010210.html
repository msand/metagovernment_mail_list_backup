Just to clarify, "consensus" does not necessarily imply unanimity. See  
for example http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Talk:Consensus

In very small groups (ie, <15 people), I think it will equate to  
something like 100% consent, but as we get into larger groups, the  
percentage which defines consensus will have top drop considerably. I  
would not like to see it drop below a substantial majority, such as  
67%, but I cannot think of any issue whatsoever that can achieve 100%  
accord in a very large group.

As for what to call ourselves, I thought the question at hand was what  
to call the software, not the project. "Metagovernment" was chosen in  
large part because the domain name was available, but as the project  
grows, it seems to be rather appropriate. "Metascore," however,  
doesn't really convey much and could probably benefit from an overhaul.


On May 5, 2009, at 12:06 AM, Owen Ambur wrote:

> I have mixed feelings about this.  As a retired employee of the U.S.  
> federal government, I was attracted to this group because  
> “government” is part of its name.  However, I do believe that  
> “governance” is a grossly overused term, usually implying that we  
> want someone else to be forced to do something they might not  
> otherwise be inclined to do.  I do not mean to suggest that  
> governance is not required, because in many cases people do need to  
> be told what they may and may not do.  (As the saying goes, your  
> right to swing your fist ends at the tip of my nose.)  However, one  
> of this group’s avowed values is “consensus through synthesis” and  
> it seems to me that is far from what commonly occurs in governance  
> systems now. http://xml.gov/stratml/crane/MGP.xml   Indeed, it is  
> hard for me to conceive of a system embracing such values as being a  
> form of “governance”. http://www.thefreedictionary.com/govern    
> Ideally, it would become a means of empowerment, by helping to bring  
> together people of like mind in the pursuit of common objectives  
> (which is part of the purpose of StratML).
>
> Besides “synthesis,” other key concepts in the group’s charter  
> include:  “Anyone may contribute …” & “Unless consensus can be  
> reached … there will be no policy …”  It seems to me that means the  
> “policy” cannot apply to anyone who does not agree with it and, to  
> me, that seems contrary to the notion of governance.  As stated  
> previously, I also believe is not the “idea” or “policy” that  
> matters but, rather, the *contributions* that each member of the  
> community willingly makes.  That prompts the thought of naming  
> ourselves Metacommunity.org but, wouldn’t you know it, the name  
> “metacommunity” is already in use. Its meaning may have interesting  
> implications for our joint effort, assuming that in fact we are able  
> to synthesize an objective upon which we actually do agree: http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metacommunity
