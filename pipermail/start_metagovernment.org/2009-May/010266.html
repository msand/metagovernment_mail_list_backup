Jim Gilliam wrote:

> It's kind of hard for me to lay out the goal, mostly because there are goals
> on a lot of different levels, but also because I'm still figuring out how to
> succinctly communicate my vision.  But here goes..

(Forgive me, I'm being too analytical.  I'm always taking things
apart, to see how they work.  Maybe that's not the right approach in
this case.)
 
> I want to provide tools for people to organize themselves democratically
> (democracy, not anarchy) online and make things happen.  I'm less concerned
> with figuring out the proper way to put together a bill, and more concerned
> with connecting people and resources together to actually do things, whether
> the context is their city, their country, or their company, doesn't matter.
>
> To use more business-like language, I believe the successful endeavors of
> the future will be all about bringing all the "stakeholders" together,
> whether it's citizens, employees, management, customers, shareholders,
> whoever, into a network that can achieve things that weren't possible before
> in a top down management system.  A "nation" is the metaphor I'm using to
> pull this concept together into something I can evangelize, hence the name,
> NationBuilder.

You are communicating a synthesis of your project: the whole.
Normally I would question you, to factor out the pieces.  But this is
Thomas's thread, and his approach is not analytical.  Like you, Thomas
is proposing a synthesis.  He suggests that we look beyond our own
projects, and also into the future.  I'm inclined to follow his lead.

I don't think we have a choice.  It's a strange kind of technology we
are working on.  If it works at all - if it can really pull people
together into consensus, and bring down governments - then what chance
does a mere project have of holding it, containing it, and
capitalizing on it?

Jim Gilliam (in reply to Thomas) wrote:

> We should be hoping for MORE projects, not less!   There should not be one
> piece of software that everyone uses, that would be terrible.  I believe we
> are at an early stage of what will eventually be an entire industry/movement
> of such software.

I feel the same way.  But I'm not sure that Thomas was suggesting a
single project.  In any case, I agree with Owen: the ideal is both
competitive and collaborative.  It's like an ecosystem (credit Mark
Murphy).  We don't have an ecosystem at the moment.  We have separate
projects each playing in its own sandbox, dreaming of its own future.
As long as that continues, hopes will be disappointed.

There's a technical reason why it can't continue, and it could serve
as a kind of answer to Thomas's appeal, if nobody has a better one.
(I'm just not sure how to present it, yet.)
