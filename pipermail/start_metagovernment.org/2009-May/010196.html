Interesting that you chimed in here, Jim, as I was about to reiterate  
my feeling that WhiteHouse2.org seems like a good possible codebase  
for Metascore. As not-really-a-programmer, I don't know if this is  
realistic, but I wanted to get the opinions of you, Manuel/Fabe (et  
al), and Pietro on the possibility of using your code with Pietro's  
scoring algorithm and Manuel's DVCS deployment idea.

Each represents a real strength: WH2 is very well developed (not just  
that it is far along, but it is also very well done overall), Pietro's  
scoring system is itself very well thought out and highly innovative,  
and the existing Metascore's DVCS model is both ingenious and  
seemingly vital to a system which wants to be an actual governance  
mechanism.

Of course, as I write this, I now noticed Fabian's update on the  
progress of the existing Metascore prototype. I do not by any means  
wish to simply throw away that development, but I continue to wonder  
(hope) if there might be a way to integrate some of the cool  
programming Jim has done with WH2.

If not... no big. I very much like what I just heard from Fabian, and  
I agree with Jim that this project has been a good meeting point for  
this "tribe" of open source governance folks.

The other thing which continues to distinguish this project is its  
focus on providing actual governance instead of simply informing the  
opinions of our betters... I mean, our representatives. That sets us  
apart as being the third generation in the development of eDemocracy  
systems, so it it is understandable that we would be lagging behind  
the earlier generations.


On May 4, 2009, at 4:19 PM, Jim Gilliam wrote:

> Successful open source projects tend to be led by a person or two  
> who does all the coding to get the initial version to a place where  
> it is actually useful to other people.  Then when other people are  
> looking to solve a problem, they see a project that is sort of close  
> to what they want to do, so they start tweaking the code a bit and  
> get involved.  That's how the project grows.   People coding in  
> their free time for no money don't like being told what to do, they  
> do it because they want to make it work they want they want it to  
> work!  Sometimes lots of people get involved, and then there are  
> disagreements on the future direction, so they fork it and spin off  
> a slightly different version, etc..
>
> The metagovernment software (or metascore) doesn't solve a problem  
> for anyone, YET.  What metagovernment *is* solving though, is it's  
> getting a bunch of people and projects together who are interested  
> in this concept of open source governance and getting a dialog  
> going.  That's not a failure at all, that's a success!  The nature  
> of this project is it's led by someone who brings people together,  
> not someone who codes.
>
> That's my general philosophy on this, and explains a bit of how I'm  
> proceeding with White House 2.  I'm trying to get it to a point  
> where it's useful to other people, and then I assume people will  
> have their own ideas of what to do with it, and can choose to get  
> involved or not.
>
> Jim Gilliam
> http://whitehouse2.org/

> standard

> something
> other than the public interest foremost at heart.  Deep analysis is  
> not
> required to understand that fact.  The only question is how long their
> stakeholders will continue to put up with it until "voting with  
> their feet"
> to go elsewhere.
>
> BTW, good ideas are a dime a dozen.  What is rare and thus valuable  
> are good
> implementations, and such implementations require good strategic and
> performance plans.  It is also very easy to try to tell others what  
> they
> should do, rather than focusing on that which we ourselves can and are
> willing to do.  That's why I am not particularly interested in  
> sharing,
> discussing, or even voting upon "ideas" -- unless they are stated as
> objectives against which resources can be applied and actual  
> performance can
> be measured.  Moreover, it is not necessarily "votes" that truly  
> count but,
> rather, the inputs and efforts each of us is willing and able to  
> contribute
> toward realization of our common objectives.

> have hoped)

> developed:
> http://et.gov/history.htm (Thus far the reality is that .gov
