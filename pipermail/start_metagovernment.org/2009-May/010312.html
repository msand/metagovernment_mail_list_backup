My thinking is that for different areas of policy it will be appropriate to
have different models of inclusion, perhaps with different groups having
different prioritisations in different areas.  I would suggest that these
models should be in themselves susceptible to voting, rather than hardwired
into the Metagovernment framework.

The watershed management example is a very good one.  However, to take a
different example, enabling non-residents to participate in the creation of
criminal law in any country, a culturally very sensitive area to any
society, is likely either to be unpopular, or to prevent the acceptance of
this type of system in the first place.  It could be seen as attempting to
denythe right of political self-determination in political communities.

I'm also concerned by what I understand to be the suggestion (and apologies
if this is a misunderstanding, as I am new to this project) that
participation in the creation of legislation and governance would be based
upon interest.  I certainly think that capturing this interest is critical
to working up proposals for legislative change.  However, I think there
needs to be a final stage where the community as a whole (however that
community has been defined (see above)) makes a decision.  I think this
avoids risks of non-representative changes.  These could obviously be
reversed but you then end up with a very complicated system where the status
of legislation/governance is much less certain than it otherwise might be.

The idea of delegated voting is fundamental to this.  Clearly people cannot
all participate in all areas to which they have entitlement (the ideal
situation).  However, they can build up the areas in which they indirectly
participate, by increasing the scope of their delegated voting.  They could
still retain power to override delegated decisions perhaps.

This would also help with transitions from current national political
systems to Metagovernance: individuals already participating in areas of
Metagovernance could retain those voting rights.  Otherwise, voting rights
on Metagovernment could pass to those people who had been elected
representatives in the old system.  Individuals would then have the power to
divert voting power elsewhere and a considerably more sophisticated
political landscape would surely follow.  There could also be an allowance
given to those that receive considerable voting power through delegation, to
fund their need for engagement and time-investment.

Tom Linton


On 14/05/2009 15:55, "Ed Pastore" <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:

> Systems attached to existing governments seem to think that way, but
> for Metagovernment I hope we can hold true to the "anyone" in the
> first principle. As soon as people get artificially disenfranchised
> from a process for any reason, democracy suffers.
> 
> Given that we live in a very global, very connected world, it is
> unseemly that we should disenfranchise people from decisions simply
> because they do not live in a certain geography. If someone cares
> about something enough to spend time acting on it, then it probably
> effects them or their interests.
> 
> A classic example is watershed management. If a locality at the head
> of a river has sole jurisdiction over what pollutants enter the river,
> all people downstream are disenfranchised from an issue which clearly
> affects them. Modern governments have started to develop regional
> authorities for things like watersheds, but now there are so many
> cross-jurisdictional issues that it seems the easiest and purest
> solution would be to invite participation from anyone interested
> enough to participate.
>
