On May 7, 2009, at 2:32 PM, Manuel Barkhau wrote:

> The abstraction must occur in the discussions surrounding any  
> decision.
>
> If somebody can't decide on a policy, due to lack of knowledge on the
> subject, then I don't think that person should submit a vote. That
> should be left to those concerned enough to to educate themselves.
>
> I don't think we should be supporting ignorance and I don't see that
> delegation does much to help. If a person is unable to make a decision
> themselves, they will hardly be able to evaluate delegates based on
> pertinent arguments. Thus they will delegate based on likability,
> exposure through media, political background etc. Precisely the things
> I think we should be trying to minimize.

I agree with Manuel. To put it another way... in Metagovernment's  
vision, every policy decision at every level of every kind of  
governance is open to anyone's participation. Therefore, people will  
only participate in issues in which they feel engaged and/or strongly  
affected. In other words, people will self-select to the issues most  
pertinent to them. And further, issues will "self-select" into the  
communities in which they belong.


On May 7, 2009, at 2:16 PM, Thomas von der Elbe wrote:

> Now with DemocracyLab the old Lady would just have to choose the  
> general issue of “Climate change” and then give her vote to the  
> value “Sustainability”.
> Other more expert users can then link her value to specific policies

This seems to me to be open to significant abuse. Once you allow  
others to define what a word means, they can twist it... sometimes 180  
degrees.

I have seen this in local referenda, where I was actually asked to  
vote on a ballot measure which said something like "Do you support  
Bill XXX which would increase funding for transportation and help the  
environment." What was I actually voting on? Well, that was left up to  
the policy-makers, who, for all I know could be saying that the  
funding should go to subsidize car-makers who produce gas-guzzling  
cars which get minutely better fuel-economy than current models. No  
idea.

In my opinion, to delegate is to abdicate. I see the same issue with  
your depiction of Votorola, where one must rely on someone else's  
promises about vague generalities.
