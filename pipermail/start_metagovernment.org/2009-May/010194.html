Successful open source projects tend to be led by a person or two who does
all the coding to get the initial version to a place where it is actually
useful to other people.  Then when other people are looking to solve a
problem, they see a project that is sort of close to what they want to do,
so they start tweaking the code a bit and get involved.  That's how the
project grows.   People coding in their free time for no money don't like
being told what to do, they do it because they want to make it work they
want they want it to work!  Sometimes lots of people get involved, and then
there are disagreements on the future direction, so they fork it and spin
off a slightly different version, etc..

The metagovernment software (or metascore) doesn't solve a problem for
anyone, YET.  What metagovernment *is* solving though, is it's getting a
bunch of people and projects together who are interested in this concept of
open source governance and getting a dialog going.  That's not a failure at
all, that's a success!  The nature of this project is it's led by someone
who brings people together, not someone who codes.

That's my general philosophy on this, and explains a bit of how I'm
proceeding with White House 2.  I'm trying to get it to a point where it's
useful to other people, and then I assume people will have their own ideas
of what to do with it, and can choose to get involved or not.

> With respect to the documentation of missions, visions, values, goals,
> objectives, and stakeholders, I hope Strategy Markup Language (StratML)
> will
> become that (international voluntary consensus) standard.
>
> To the degree that service providers fail to come to consensus on a
> standard
> set of data and a common format for sharing such data, they have something
> other than the public interest foremost at heart.  Deep analysis is not
> required to understand that fact.  The only question is how long their
> stakeholders will continue to put up with it until "voting with their feet"
> to go elsewhere.
>
> BTW, good ideas are a dime a dozen.  What is rare and thus valuable are
> good
> implementations, and such implementations require good strategic and
> performance plans.  It is also very easy to try to tell others what they
> should do, rather than focusing on that which we ourselves can and are
> willing to do.  That's why I am not particularly interested in sharing,
> discussing, or even voting upon "ideas" -- unless they are stated as
> objectives against which resources can be applied and actual performance
> can
> be measured.  Moreover, it is not necessarily "votes" that truly count but,
> rather, the inputs and efforts each of us is willing and able to contribute
> toward realization of our common objectives.
>
> Here's how we proposed to address the distinction between interest and
> commitment in the ET.gov process:
> http://xml.gov/draft/ETLifeCycleStage2.htm  Thus far we've been unable to
> marshal the resources to implement those "good ideas".  However, making
> reality speak more clearly for itself (even when it is not as we have
> hoped)
> is among the principles under which the ET.gov site/process was developed:
> http://et.gov/history.htm (Thus far the reality is that .gov agencies have
> not shown much inclination to work together in a well-coordinated manner to
> accelerate the consideration and use of emerging technologies for the
> benefit of We the People.)
>
