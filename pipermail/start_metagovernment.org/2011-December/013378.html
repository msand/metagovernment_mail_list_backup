On Dec 11, 2011, at 7:52 AM, Pietro Speroni di Fenizio wrote:

> Exactly! There are many solutions that would work. The point is that
> it IS possible to have systems where people cannot send each other the
> URL of the proposal.

Instead of (or in addition to) concealing the URL of proposals, why not conceal the URL of people? In other words, eliminate the possibility of people finding their favorite politicians and always voting for that person. People could still have a trust signature so you could know that they are who they say they are, but you wouldn't be able to find them just by looking for them.


>> I imagine this to be extremely frustrating for the voter ... to find his
>> favourite proposal but not be allowed to vote for it. In this case he might
>> refuse to vote at all (or, if forced to vote anyway, he could choose one,
>> which is doomed to fail anyway and so game the system.)
> 
> Ok, so now we have moved from "this is not technically possible" to
> this could be technically possible but it would give emotional
> problems (frustration). I welcome this step. Still I think it is also
> possible to make a system where you minimize this frustration. I think
> the key is in how you present the information to the users. If you say
> that they are voting... and should vote on everything they see, then
> of course they will be frustrated. They have expectations, and the
> expectations will be frustrated. But what if people don't "vote" the
> proposal, but have to "judge" them like a Judge. And like a judge they
> can read the cases that are open but can only judge the ones that the
> system assigns to them. When people realize that this system is in
> place to permit them to have their own proposals fairly evaluated they
> will accept it. Also on slashdot they have a similar system, people ca
> read comments but can vote only when they system decides so. And
> people like it.

But one core purpose of collaborative governance is the empowerment of the individual. Random voting is not nearly as empowering as the ability to vote on anything anytime. If people do not feel that sense of empowerment, they are less likely to take responsibility for their own governance. They would still be prone to feel like government is something that happens to them, not that they control.

Legislation is a push process while adjudication is a pull process. This is because adjudication is (simplistically) about deciding if something in the past was right or wrong. Legislation is (also simplistically) about deciding if something in the future is or is not to be permitted. That difference decides the push/pull nature. It is reasonable that past things should be push because they are a limited set. Future things are pull because they are an open set.

And as a corollary of the above: if someone sees a proposal they like but they are not empowered to vote for it, would they still not be empowered to make a new proposal similar to it? That sounds like a fragmentation of the process instead of a consensus-builder.
