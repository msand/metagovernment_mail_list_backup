Hi Eduardo,

When the vote to implement a proposal is not unanimous, then a decision 
to go ahead and implement the proposal anyway (overriding those opposed) 
would (/prima facie/) not be valid by (D). (It would be awkward to argue 
that those opposed /would have/ accepted it, if there /had been/ a 
reasonable discourse, in the face of the /fact/ that they voted no.)

    (D) A rule of action or choice is justified, and thus valid, only if
    all those affected by the rule or choice could accept it in a
    reasonable discourse. 

When not all stakeholders are participating in the decision making, then 
even a unanimous vote to implement cannot be taken as evidence that the 
absentee stakeholders "could accept it in a reasonable discourse."

The claim is that, except in very rare circumstances (all stakeholders 
participating, unanimous vote outcome), voting will not be as reliable 
as rational discourse as the means through which to decide whether a 
proposal is valid by (D), and whether to implement it. The Discourse 
Principle sets *discourse* as the standard, not voting.
