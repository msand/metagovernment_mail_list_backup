Free speech is indeed a major issue here and why I've never been
comfortable with the proposal of allowing moderators (or "juries") to
make decisions like this. There are some good ones, but I've just run
into too many bad ones to trust that design. My sense is that it's
related to the SDAP problem with representative government: That
position just seems to attract people who are unsuitable to wield that
kind of power. How exactly the individual-based filtering will work
again is an empirical question: We'll just have to try different
things until we figure out how well they work against the disruptive
and those who would try to game the system.

> I think it dangerous to hid away and marginalize an entire group from having
> their opinion heard and leaving it up to past flagging to determine their
> legitimacy. Again at some point one of these trolls might reform or actually
> have something valuable to say. I don't feel it's right to hid what they say
> because a group of people have deemed it irrelevant. I instead argue that
> every post should remain public and viewable but that individuals be given a
> time out for abuse of the posting code. One by one we treat these trolls
> like criminals and give them a trail where the evidence is produced and a
> verdict is reached by an outside jury, everything remains transparent.

Filtering has got to be dynamic: Everything will be in the stream but
people will be able to change their filters on the fly (hide/show
posts based on rating, poster rating, individual filter list, etc.)
Slashdot does something like this, but only with post rating.
Unfortunately none of the big social media sites do what we need,
provide negative feedback to trolls and other disrupters, because they
*benefit* from trolls: Their eyeballs count, perhaps even more than
non-trolls because they spend more time on the sites, and because the
sites actually benefit from disharmony. Getting people riled up brings
them back, and FB and Reddit in particular seem to me to be designed
to encourage this type of behavior. Unlike those sites ours will
actually benefit from shutting trolls down or sending them back to
Reddit/FB/Slashdot/etc. as long as it's clear that they weren't
censored by the system but instead just ignored, or perhaps even
actively shunned, by the community.

> We can draw from the shouting fire in a crowded theater analogy or crying
> wolf. You prefer to automatically block these people from ever being heard
> except by others like them (which might only serve to encourage them
> further), I say we give them the benefit of the doubt and punish them later.

Again, my proposal that it's not the *system* doing this, but the
individuals using it. In your analogy it would be like giving everyone
in the theater noise-canceling headphones that they can adjust the
level of external sound volume that passes in. Sure, some of them will
miss the initial call of "fire", but those with their filters set
lower (or off) will start moving and the rest will see that.

> But my argument is that it's better being an actual public job that people

I've no problem with professionals (indeed paid professionals) doing
most of the work. That's what the Managers are in the Matchism
proposal, and where I expect most of the proposals will come from. I
just have a problem with paying *everyone* to do this kind of work
because unless you have a very sophisticated bidding and management
system (something like what Ned has proposed with Blinap) it just
seems to me to open up too many avenues for gaming the system.

Yeah, but that would be communism ;-) Instead of just trying to remove
the financial gain I recommend we remove the people who would exploit
this design and the jobs they use to do so.

> I think there would be interest in getting paid for activity
> that you had expertise in. Like karma for Reddit, there's always somebody
> stepping in and sharing their knowledge. Here you're helping run the world,
> and getting money for it, it's even more incentive to contribute.
> Competition breeds innovation. If there's money to be made in finding the
> greatest solution to a problem, I believe innovation will occur and we'll
> all benefit from it.

This is reminiscent to me of the government design proposal in
"Looking Backward" (i.e., eventually all companies merge into one
giant conglomerate and politics *becomes* corporate management). IMHO,
however, the two systems are fundamentally incompatible: I'm a strong
proponent of capitalism as an economic system because it's clear to me
that it works better, and is more compatible with human nature, than
any of the alternatives. But capitalism requires regulation and that
can only be done by people who are operating outside of that system.
Matchism requires volunteer work to function, but is also designed to
minimize the amount of effort any one individual has to expend. If we
can get it down low enough (like in the realm of what people now
expend on FB and other social media) we won't have to pay them to do
it.
