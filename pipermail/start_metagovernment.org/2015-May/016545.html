> No, it's way more sophesticated when used in the right way.
>
> 1. Every times  "PULL REQUEST" is made, a PROPOSAL is made
> 2. Every time a "PULL REQUEST" is accepted, a PROPOSAL is accepted by the
> one who accepted the "PULL REQUEST".
> 3. Every time a "REPOSITORY" is "FORKED", the Forker joined that Proposal,
> thus supports the current Version of the Proposal with his Vote.
>
> An Accepted "PULL REQUEST" needs all people who "FORKED" to update their
> FORKS or it needs the guy who made the "PULL REQUEST to also ask FORKERS to

But again, where's the decisionmaking? Seems to me you're equating
"pull" with "vote yes". Where does Github say that a majority has to
pull by a certain time/date to cause a proposal to be adopted? Indeed,
how does it even have any way of reporting that as a percentage of
people who are interested in a particular project, combined across
potentially dozens or hundreds of forks?

Seems to me your obsession with Github has blinded you to how limited
it is as a decisionmaking system, and without decisionmaking you're
not talking about government, you're merely talking about opinion
polling.
