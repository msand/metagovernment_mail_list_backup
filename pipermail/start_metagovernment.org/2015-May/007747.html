Very astute, but maybe a little oversold: Organizing people doesn't
need to be a business nor do we need anyone (or any small group) to do
it. People are self organizing, we just need to provide them with the
technology to facilitate this. The problem is that the tools built
into our brains were "designed" (by evolution) to function best in
small groups in an era of scarce resources and limited technology. The
trick is to create tools that harness these (limited) capabilities and
their associated biases, yet work in a much larger and more complex
environment.

Currently reading Caplan's "Myth of the Rational Voter". He attributes
most of the problems with democracy to the low cost associated with
indulging personal biases ("belief preferences", as he calls them in
his "everything is economics" worldview). The result is political
parties and politicians who are able to encourage and capitalize on
these biases to further their own agendas. Although there are a few
large holes in his thesis, to the extent that he's on to something the
solution seems to me to be exactly what I'm working on: An open voting
system which decreases the cost of voting but significantly
*increases* the costs of indulging these biases (i.e., they have to
make their statements and votes in public where they will be subjected
to scrutiny and fact checking).

> As I hate when somebody compares people to computers, for a hardcore

> update some social software into people. Replace fear by hope, guilt by
> confidence, competition by collaboration.... without that even if
> everybody could program, we will be slaves.

As you'll read on matchism.org I have no problem with this kind of
social and behavioral engineering, but I divide behavioral engineering
in to 5 classes and propose that social engineering should use the
lowest effective class. This "software update" you're proposing looks
like class 4, conditioning, which is the second most invasive of the 5
classes. Far better to provide a decisionmaking system that
facilitates voting rationally without requiring that we change the
internal processing of individuals in the more general sense you're
proposing.
