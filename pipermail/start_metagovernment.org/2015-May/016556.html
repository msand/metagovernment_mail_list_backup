> Would it be prudent to address the idea that the U.S. Constitution was
> meant to be a form of collaborative governance (government of, by, for
> the People) before going about re-inventing the wheel?
>
> In other words, that we should address where things have gone wrong,
> before we try to fix or replace anything.
>
> I have identified two major internal problems with the Constitution:
>
> 1) The election of a federated leader, regardless of the level of
> popular support allowed incompetence to be placed in power and in
> charge of trillions of national assets and atomic bombs.
> 2) The election of paid legislators which created a dominance of
> political activity at the federal level instead of the local level
> where it belonged.  Now, no one participates in local government
> because the federalists have dominated the conversation.  Further,
> requiring election pushes away interested community members from being
> equal voices in their law-making apparati.
>
> These problems were hidden mostly until media and the end of expansion
> to the West exacerbated their weaknesses.
>
> That's a start.  I'm Mark Janssen and have been working on developing
> a world constitution, expanding on the wisdom of the US Constitution
> and built upon the pillars of "liberty and justice for all".  A strong
> foundation which any nation or people should be able to agree upon.
