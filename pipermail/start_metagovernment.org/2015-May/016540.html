Going back to programming,
although it is true that everybody can program, I don't think it is
anything less than utopia to expect everybody to learn it and have the time
to do it. I mean, I know how to program, but already I now I have slipped
behind and I don't understand anymore the languages Vilfredo is written in,
nor the new ones are being used around Github.

For example I am interested in a project called Synthing. Which is a p2p,
secure alternative to dropbox. It is written in Go. Now Synthing at the
moment does a very poor job at something called "selective Sync" which is
absolutely crucial for me. I would love to convince the author to add a
better Sel.Sync or to even fork the system myself to add it, or to pay
someone to do it. Of those 3 options probably I will have to wait for the
first since I am not willing to put the effort to study a new programming
language just for this. Nor (and this is important) is there a system in
github where I can easily put some money on a request, and other people can
add some money too, and eventually some programmer can come, fork, satisfy
the costumers, and get the money.

Apparently on github style democracy we are going to have 3 types of
people: people who know how to program, people who have money to ask
someone to program for them, people who neither know how to program nor
have the money to invest.

And the first group can just go forward and do stuff. Or even do stuff for
others, and get paid.
People in the second group need to be able to explain their needs very
well, and have enough money to convince someone in the first group to make
those changes
And people in the third group just need to be really really (!) good in
explaining what they think should be changed and why it is important.
Different roles.

We had a similar problem in Vilfredo 1 in the wall of text question. People
came out with the greatest idea about how the system should behave. Problem
was, no one was willing to actually implement them. So I would say we need
in Github an integrated way for people to suggest an addition, and put some
money for that. And other people can add some extra money to or fork the
request, and change it a bit.

Forking a request will be easier than forking the actual code.

Cheers,
Pietro
