> My belief is that collaboration isn't necessary or indeed probably

> (though some of those actors may be other special interest groups

>
> I was involved with the Anti Nuke movement in the early 80’s (Diablo
> Canyon). I was a part of an affinity group called the “guides collective".
> We kept watch on the plant for several months prior to the Blockade and
> then during the action we led *affinity groups* through the canyon to the
> back gate of the nuke. We would send a representative to the Spokes Council
> where decisions were made for the *Abalone Alliance* (the group at large).
>  Very similar to #Occupy…..though the group dynamics were a little less
> refined back then. :)
>
> In the 90’s I was with a group from Salt Lake that was protesting nuke
> testing in Nevada. One of the actions I attended began early on a Saturday
> afternoon in a large conference hall in Vegas. Around 250 people gathered
> in the room early in the afternoon to come up with an action plan for the
> next day. It was a fairly typical cross-section of *anywhere* America
> (doctors, lawyers, housewives, hippies, ranchers, children, and so on). The
> conflict at times was a bit intense. One group wanted to burn the flag as a
> symbolic protest. Another wanted to be able to hop the fence and run amok
> on the test site. It was a little crazy for awhile. The facilitation was
> amazing though….and by the end of the afternoon, all 250 people walked out
> of the room all-lined-up for the next days action. It was one of the
> coolest things I’ve experienced.
>
> They used the same model as was mentioned in the first group.…(I want to
> call it *tiered scaling*, though I suppose you and others would probably
> call it *representative democracy*). Either way, it worked really well for
> a group that size. I would like to see a much larger group and another tier
> added to the scaling process sometime.
>
> In my mind, the group in Nevada was deeply *synergized*. And that kind of
> empowerment has created many significant changes in the past. We arrived
> committed to something that held deep meaning….and left in a deep
> *coherence*. It was powerful.
>
>  And that can go either way…..  That's why we need to have a check on the
> influence of the *authoritarian* complex (as you’re good at reminding us
> about).
>
> At least 25% of the registered voters have to vote yes.  This weeds
> out proposals that a large majority just don't see the need to even
> inform themselves on (and so couldn't cast an informed vote on)...
>
>
> I think if it gets that complicated or apathetic we need to scale down in
> some major way. Humans need to have a life as well. If we create a system
> that has that kind of vibe to it, I think it’s probably doomed to fail.
> Actually we don’t have to create that….we have it now.
>
> and also tactics where a small minority try to get something passed by
> deception or other tricks (the example in my book is authoritarians,
> who make up roughly 25% of the general population yet are the source
> of most of the prejudiced and unethical behavior, doing something like
> holding a vote during a holiday or trying to hide something nasty in
> an otherwise harmless-looking proposal).
>
>
> I can imagine having an agreement about not allowing either of those.
>
> I agree with the evolving part, but we need to help that along by
> creating systems that *support* this evolved conception.
>
>
> ….and we are doing just that.

> As for whether consensus is a reasonable goal, you've got your food coop,
