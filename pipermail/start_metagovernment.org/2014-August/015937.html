On Sat, Aug 16, 2014 at 4:50 AM, Jacopo Tolja <jacopo.tolja at gmail.com> wrote:
> To give you a hint regarding participation, basically the different proposal
> are stating the same but in different manner, I abstain from voting because
> I agree with all the version.

I'm thinking this either a missing part of the instructions for
Vilfredo, or indeed a design flaw: How are we to distinguish
"consensus" from just having the majority burn out on the issue and
deciding that it's just not worth the trouble anymore?  In which case
it is not "consensus" that has been achieved, but simply a "last man
standing" decision rule...

Seems to me the only solution to this problem is for everyone to vote
*every time*, even if it's just to not change any of the thumbs from
their default "up" orientation and hit submit.  Other options would be
to allow (require) delegating proxies once you see that all the
options are acceptable, or to simply set an automated vote of "any of
these, but nothing new" for all the proposals in a given round that
would carry forward to every subsequent round.

I've no problem with this part: In any real-world decision there will
be far more simple voters than proposers.  I do feel that if you vote
against something that you provide the reason, which is something
Vilfredo does nicely ensure.

I think it doesn't have a number yet.  That will presumably be
generated automatically at the start of the second (voting) phase.  I
see it in my screen at the bottom of things I have proposed.  Not sure
if the design should be changed so that these new proposals are
visible to other proposers: On the one hand it would make the process
more efficient because it might prevent duplicate entries (which if we
were using the system right and evaluating individual statements would
be a big problem), but on the other hand might suppress the best
options because proposers might just accept what others have written
as "close enough" and not even take the time to propose their own
(which might be better in some small way).
