On Thu, Sep 18, 2008 at 9:52 PM, Mark Frischmuth <mark at democracylab.org> wrote:

> We moved away from this formulation because we believed it was too
> confusing/complicated for users to understand.  Our thinking now is to force
> users to choose a category at Concept/Comment creation, and that users will
> rate poorly things that are miscategorized.
>
> The downside is a lack of subtlety.  The upside is easier user
> comprehension, which hopefully equals higher usage.  I think this is more
> important (for now, at least), because for any of this to matter, it needs
> to be used by as many people as possible.

Interesting demo! I don't see "lack of subtlety" as a major stumbling
block personally, as I assume that the voter votes as a service to the
community, not as an act of personalised self-expression -- the
community has much more use for a yes/no than a maybe.

In traditional paper-based politics where it can take weeks to redraft
legislation, the maybe/present vote can have statistical significance
(perhaps giving incentive for those drafting the legislation to seek
out how to turn the maybe's into yes's) - but that argument drops away
when you take things online, and the cost of redrafting isn't so high.
