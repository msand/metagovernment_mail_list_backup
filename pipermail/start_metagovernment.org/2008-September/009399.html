On Sep 16, 2008, at 3:02 PM, Richard Franks wrote:

> The nice thing, is that you don't have to - all you have to assert is
> that a system has a greater probability of being true, than false.
> Over time, such a system will trend towards "absolute truth" -- even
> if the initial conditions give the system a very slight advantage --
> if such a system uses the "truth" it generates to improve its own
> truth-determining capabilities.

I think the problem Roustam is getting to is that there is no such  
thing as truth; at least in most modern philosophical systems. Even  
saying something as seemingly clear as "there is a computer screen in  
front of you right now" makes all kinds of assumptions about the  
universe (that you exist, that there is a you separate from other  
things, that it is reasonable to divide the universe into things like  
computer screens, that there is a right now, and so on). At best, we  
can say we are striving toward what most people would agree on as true.
