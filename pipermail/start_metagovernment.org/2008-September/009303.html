On Sep 2, 2008, at 1:57 PM, Manuel Barkhau wrote:

> I laughed when I read that. Really if you know anything about Stallman
> that is exactly what you would expect.

Yeah, I guess I have to do more research before I go contacting big  
names like that. As I've noted before, my experience is in I.T., and  
in that realm "open source" has one distinct meaning: "you pay in time  
instead of money." The whole concept of software freedom is pretty  
much lost on corporate operations folks, even in the non-profits I  
have worked for.

> I don't particularly like Electronic-Democracy, because it invokes the
> idea of bringing the current system to a different platform, but we're
> doing more than that. For me at least, E-Democracy is synonymous with
> online voting, so I'm not fond of that term.
>
> Governance should be a part of the term I think.

I think you're on to something there. After all, while a condo  
association or cycling club may use representative democracy to  
constitute their governance structure, most people do not think of  
democracy as anything but about political governments. "Governance"  
however, is generally understood to be about anything that needs  
organization.

(As a side-note, I also was thinking of changing
http://www.metagovernment.org/wiki/Main_Page/2008-08
so that every reference to a "law" is instead a "policy." Again,  
policy is a more generic term which includes laws, but can also  
incorporate less formal governance mechanisms.)

How about something like "participatory governance" or "collaborative  
governance?" My biggest problem with those terms is the number of  
syllables. Fewer is always better when coining a term, but... they do  
seem to encapsulate what we are going for...

I don't much care for the following terms, but this is where I ended  
up when clicking around on Wikipedia:
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Online_deliberation
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deliberative_democracy
