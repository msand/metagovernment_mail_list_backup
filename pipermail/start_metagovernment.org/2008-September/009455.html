On 9/24/08, Nicholas Church <NChurch at syndustryrecords.com> wrote:
>
> as I stated on my user page I have been working on something called
> symetrism for the past 7 (almost 8) years.  I really think some of it
> can be applied to metagovernment and so I will give you the basic ideas
> behind it.
>
> 1- many times "the majority" adamantly support/oppose something, but
> their representatives vote against/for it.  Because of this the people
> should have a direct vote on the issues at hand.
>
> 2- many times the people cannot make up their mind on an issue which
> needs immediate attention.  In order to safeguard against inaction,
> there should be a representative democracy for those issues.
>
> 3- representatives are not necessarily knowledgeable on the subjects
> they are voting on.  This can be changed by determining broad categories
> for legislation and requiring representatives to be "experts" in one
> specific field, for which they vote.
>
> 4- People are different.  They have different ideas about what is good
> and bad.  Attempting to homogenize everyone results in hatred and
> dissatisfaction.  In order to prevent this, allow more efficacy for
> smaller groups, have general "broad strokes" for the larger governing
> body, but leave the details up to the smaller governing bodies.
>
> Since it's inception it has become much more detailed. but those were
> the basic ideas. I built around that by borrowing aspects of different
> forms of governance and came up with this:
>
> A proposed issue is voted on directly by the citizens of the
> region/state etc affected by it.  If they fail to reach a 3/5 majority
> it is then passed on to their localities congress. The issue is
> classified as 1 of 7 different categories (environment, economics,
> education, healthcare, energy. social welfare, child welfare).  If it
> can only be classified as 1 category, the representative who is an
> expert in that field makes a decision.  If it can be classified as an
> even number of categories and the representatives fail to reach a
> majority decision then all 7 representatives will vote on it.
>
> I came across a book a while ago, I can't remember who wrote it or what
> it was called, but the author proposed something like synthesis scoring
> for potential candidates.  You can score candidated between -10 and 10,
> which ensures the candidate with the most support wins instead
> of...say...some people voting for ralph nader and some people voting for
> al gore and Georger W. Bush winning, even though Gore or Nader would
> have been acceptable to the majority.  So I think that may be a better
> way to do that, than my first idea (anonymous candidates because people
> have been proven to vote based on appearance and not on fact).
>
> anyway, why am I sending this to everyone?  Well I do believe that an
> absolute direct democracy is doomed to failure.  It relies too greatly
> on the whims of the people...which are fickle at best.  It was tried by
> the athenians way back when...and they executed socrates.   With every
> fiber of my being I believe a synthesis of direct and representative
> democracy to be the best possible choice for a new system and I think it
> should be incorporated into metagovernment.
>
