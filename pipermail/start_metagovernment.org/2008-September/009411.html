Consider the following statements:
1) "The sky is blue",
2) "The sky is blue at noon",
3) "The sky is blue at noon when there are no clouds",
4) "The sky is blue at noon local time, relative to GMT, when there
are no clouds, when you are on the equator and not during a total
eclipse of the sun",
5) "The sky is blue if we all think it is"

Statement #5 is what you get from consensus, statement #4 is what you
get from successively trying to determine the 'truth'. Is one more
useful than the other? I'd say that it depends entirely on what
question you're asking.

But - just because 100% truth cannot be obtained, or it may be a goal
which does not exist, that is absolutely no reason to not attempt to
reach it and instead sit around stroking our collective beards,
sipping bitter espresso.

Also - there is no reason at all to try to force truth though the
wrangler of metaphysics -- I can imagine the day when its most
compelling paradoxes are defeated by a superior application of logic.

With regards objectivity - how objective can we expect people to be
about things they care about? I think a more achievable goal would be
to allow people to explicitly express their opinion (leading to #5)
and their 'objective assessment' (leading to #4) -- separating and
defining those through the UI. For example - someone who leans
Pro-Life may indicate, on the subject of abortion, that they think
personally that it is wrong, but 'objectively' would not want to
remove the option of final choice.

I envisage a system which leads people to the thought process "This is
what I think is true [check], this other thing is what the available
evidence points to [check]" -- as long as the system accepts and uses
both inputs, then we'll start to train our users (and ourselves) to
separate the two, and not to habitually conflate "truth" with
"opinion", but to value both.
