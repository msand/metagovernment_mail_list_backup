On Wed, Sep 24, 2008 at 8:16 AM, Nicholas Church
<NChurch at syndustryrecords.com> wrote:
>  From youtube: " We will, however, enforce our Terms of Use against,
> say, a website that does nothing more than aggregate a bunch of embedded
> YouTube videos and intentionally tries to generate ad revenue from them."
>
> They're not saying they will sue anyone.  They're basically saying that
> they may deny service to people who do that.  They're a private business
> and if they have a detailed legals page outlining "TOS" they can
> basically do whatever they like in terms of restricting access.

I think they're saying "commercial use" is too legally ambiguous to do
anything about, therefore we're going to use the phrase as a
"best-fit" placeholder to guide your discretion, while reserving our
rights to exercise ours. Particularly telling is this line (emphasis
mine): "We don't want to discourage you from putting the _occasional_
YouTube video in your blog ... even if you have general purpose ads"

In the absence of a legally testable definition of "commercial use", I
believe consideration would be given to how the phrase is commonly
used with regards the redistribution of digital content. Here -
revenue from ads is being used as a partial indicator, by one of the
heavyweights in the industry, without the commercial sale of any goods
containing the copyrighted content.


> RF: "I didn't specify explicitly, but I was looking for a definition as
> it relates to new media"
>
> "new media" is a phrase on par with "web 2.0".

Agreed in the sense that perspective is difficult whilst in
transition, but within the next 10 years we'll likely have a much
tighter and widely-accepted definition for both.  Replace "new media"
with "online digital content" if that works better for now.


> In regards to the "copy"
> (text) and "media" (photos) they'll be treated the same as they have
> always been.

I disagree. The very existence of licenses such as the CC family
suggests that assertion to be false.


> Incidentally it comes down to this.  If we come across
> someone using this stuff in a way we feel to be inappropriate, we'll
> decide if we want to take action.  If so, we contact a lawyer who
> decides whether we have a case or not, the lawyer sends a cease and
> desist letter, if the person doesn't comply, then we go to court.

Why would we want to?

Ever?

It's just words and ideas which we want to spread as far and as freely
as possible.

In fact, the idea that a sub-group of Metagovernment could decide to
take that route at some time in the future is the best argument I've
seen to place the text under public domain - to preclude that very
possibility.


> None of this would dissuade a reporter/blogger from writing about
> metagovernment.

In your opinion. In my opinion there is a small, but not non-existent, chance.

And what possible gain does holding a restrictive and ambiguous
license over the text grant us?
