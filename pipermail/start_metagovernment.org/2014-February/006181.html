Hi,

We do a huge amount of work with large scale, multi-stakeholder strategies,
especially in the public sector (see
http://www.gowerpublishing.com/isbn/9781472427816 for details on my book
'Validating Strategies' which will be published in the UK in March.

Strategies invariable produce many, often competing options - so some sort
of 'prioritisation' or 'Validation' of the strategies is required.

We recommend a 3 step process:

1 - voting - in which people get to vote which option is most attractive to
them.  In reality, the consequence of voting is that the least controversial
ideas (not necessarily the best or most important ideas) win the vote.
2 - clustering - stakeholders cluster around those ideas that are most
important to them, so that if enough stakeholders support a given idea then
maybe they can get on with it even if the rest of the community doesn't want
to support it
3 - clumping - in which those ideas which don't have enough support may be
able to attract support by clumping together with other ideas, editing all
ideas in the process, until such time as a new concept emerges which either
rises to the top of the voting or which attracts a big enough cluster of
people so that it will happen.

Another key principle is to ask people what they are prepared to actually
DO, and DON'T ask them what they WANT.  This may sound like a trivial
distinction but it is utterly profound. In a city redevelopment, if you ask
people what they 'want' in order to minimise congestion on the roads, many
will say they want more cycleways.  If you ask them what they intend to
'do', many will sheepishly admit that they want to drive their cars to work
because there will be less congestion because other people are using the
cycleways.  So in all our strategies, our overwhelming focus is on
understanding what people (end-users) want to DO, not what they WANT.

Hi, Jacopo.

I admit I still don?t fully understand Airesis, but my impression so far is
that it may emphasize voting a bit too much. Please correct me in whatever
way I am misapprehending.

Government and governing bodies outside of government make a vast array of
decisions that affect out life. It can decide questions like:
	* Should we build a swimming pool in this city park?
	* Should we send troops to country X to intervene in a massacre?
	* Should we ban texting while driving?
	* Should we have schools teach a particular new curriculum?
	* Should we raise the tax on tobacco?
Those are just random examples. The array of decisions is seemingly
infinite. And you can easily be a stakeholder in ever single one of those.
But you cannot reasonably vote on all of those decisions with a fully
informed opinion. It is impossible.

And I agree that delegation is not the answer. I think the real problem and
the real answer here is that we are asking the wrong questions. All of the
above are yes/no questions. They assume that there can only be two choices,
and they invite ignorant participation (anyone can say yes or no to those
for any number of reasons).

Instead, those above questions could be stated as:
	* How can we affordably make this park serve the community better?
	* How can we effectively intervene in the massacre in country X?
	* How can we address safety issues with texting while driving?
	* How can we best teach our children?
	* How can we help discourage people from smoking?
For each of those questions, it is not a matter of voting yes or no, but of
building solutions that can get everyone to vote for them. The process may
be much longer, but the result would be orders-of-magnitude better.?

Isn?t that a much stronger, more workable, more persistent form of
democracy? One that builds solutions for everyone?
