On Wed, Dec 14, 2016 at 7:13 PM, Scott Raney <metamerman at gmail.com> wrote:

> On Wed, Dec 14, 2016 at 7:29 PM, Anthony Dunn
> <anthonydunn97202 at gmail.com> wrote:
> > I found myself agreeing with the content but wondering what you're
> actually
> > proposing people do exactly. How do people interface with the system and
> > make laws?
>
> The primary way is "not at all":  If the system has been properly
> designed and implemented after signing up and filling out their
> profiles in most cases they'll just read the email or text message
> they'll get on Friday night that tells them what that week's proposal
> is about along with what vote has been calculated for them based on
> how their matched proxies voted. Then they'll do *nothing* and that
> vote will be recorded for them. If they want to interact, they'll be
> able to do that via an app and/or the web site that will show them the
> details of the proposal, the details of *why* their proxies voted the
> way they did, the early vote counts, etc. They'll then have the option
> of overriding their proxies (or optionally confirming with a direct
> vote, or abstaining).
>
> The "activerts" will be able to use that same app and web site to cast
> an early vote to make themselves available as proxies, though they'll
> actually have to read and understand the proposal themselves because
> there will be relatively few votes and comments from other voters to
> use. The proxy-matching system will still be available and depending
> on how active people are *may* give them good proxy representation,
> but it will have to include a "goodness of fit" estimate to give them
> some idea about how close the matches really were.
>
> Bootstrapping is going to be an issue: For now I've got "bots" that
> will be used as proxies when there aren't enough real voters available
> and have found that it takes relatively few of them to cover the
> domain: Surprising to me was that around 100 gives the required 5
> close-enough matches for all personality types. This result also gives
> a ballpark estimate as to how small of an organization will be able to
> use this system (i.e., it's probably *not* going to be suitable for
> decisionmaking for HOAs or neighborhoods, which is actually how I got
> started on this project). Unfortunately it's kind of an AI problem to
> make those bots *vote* like humans with similar personality
> characteristics. For now I'm just using a relatively coarse 2D scale
> on Openness and Conscientiousness, which at least is a step up from
> just using a liberal/conservative orientation scale like most
> political scientists seem content to use when making voting
> predictions (turnout, party affiliation, etc.)
