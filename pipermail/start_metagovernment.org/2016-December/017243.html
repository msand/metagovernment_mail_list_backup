> My model here is HOA managers (management companies): They can be
> fired like this, but almost never are because it's such a nightmare to
> replace them (I know because I've served on boards and committees
> charged with doing this). Another directly comparable situation is
> city managers, which are also very hard to get rid of (I know this

> opposite of your prediction is true: Managers at both of these levels
> currently don't change their behavior *at all* in fear of being
> second-guessed and replaced (this is classic SDAP behavior, if that
> helps to explain it). If the pendulum swings too far to the other
> direction we can always constrain it a little (golden parachute
> contract clauses, requiring supermajority votes,
> trial/waiting/probation periods, etc.)
>
> > How the firing process would take place isn't covered but presumably a

> even though it should only rarely be used.

> begrudgingly

> I do see it as an iterative process. As explained in that paper (and
> on the MatchSys page on matchism.org) there will be "post votes" so
> that people can change their mind and force a bill to be revoted if
> enough of them do. I've also got versioning built into the data
> structures to enable a kind of alpha/beta test period for bills before
> they come to the general-vote stage. For example the queue might have
> dozens of bills in it, stretching out for months ahead, and might get
> re-sorted depending on how many people vote on them, how big the
> yes/no differential is, whether the Q&A section on each bill indicates
> there are unresolved issues, etc. The big hurdle is the UI: when a new
> version of a proposal comes up the people who have voted or commented
> on it previously shouldn't have to start from scratch, which means a
> lot of programming work hiliting the diffs, allowing them to confirm
> or update their comments, etc. Maybe for version 2.0...
