> There's no direct conflict here: Most bills now come from "special
> interests" (i.e., extremists like the military industrial complex,
> corporations, the religious, gunnnies, environmentalists, etc.), and
> pass through the legislature who essentially just endorse them with
> relatively minor modifications (because they're pretty much required
> to do that if they want to get reelected or employed after they leave
> office). I would expect that original source to remain the same,
> although I'd hope the Executive branch to be a little more selective
> in deciding what gets through (no more renaming bridges and post
> offices, which makes up almost half of what the US congress does
> now!). There's not much risk of SDAP Managers proposing anything like
> "the jew relocation act" because they'll realize a blacklash resulting
> in their removal is likely if they did (Donald Trump, on the other
> hand, would be impossible to remove from office merely for proposing
> relocating muslims). The relatively few bills that originate from the
> Executive branch generally address specific needs that it discovered
> in the course of running the government (mostly spending bills but a
> fair amount of regulatory rules). All those will pass through just as
> they do now, as will most "executive orders" that the legislative
> branch has very little control over now. But again the Executive
> branch will have to learn how to tweak the proposals to balance the
> desires of the people and the needs of government (i.e., giving The
> People direct control allows them to enforce the "benevolent" aspect
> of the benevolent dictator).
>
> But I understand your confusion: It's going to be hard for us to start
> thinking of ourselves as the boss and the mayor/governor/president as
> just another employee in an organization we're in charge of running.
> Calling them "managers" instead of the titles they have now will help,
> but it still will take a little getting used to.
>
> I'd like to eventually have a citizen-route to getting bills on the
> ballot, but I've seen a lot of very poorly written initiatives (not to
> mention the vast majority of petitions on the slacktivism sites) and
> so definitely consider this a distant second as a source of policy.
> IMHO the executive branch generally knows what needs to be done to
> satisfy the needs of the people because they run into these policy
> problems on a daily basis. The main reason they don't do that now is
> because there is so much influence from the "special interests" that
> are jerking around the legislative branch that the executive branch
> ends up serving those squeaky wheels instead of doing what they
> already know needs to be done.
>
> There is a section on Managers in the main manifesto that specifies

> office, they're selected like employees (including the CEO) of
> corporations, i.e., based on their qualifications, not their
