Please accept my apologies if I have caused offense. I  have great respect

I have just stated what I think the public expects, and that the people I
have talked to (experts in researching voting schemes) do not consider

There's also the choice of words. In the literature, a "secure" voting
system usually implies privacy.

Not so fast :-D. What I have said is that it doesnt matter what I think, so

If you want to know, I dont have a qualified opinion. My intuition tells me
that coercion and vote buying are serious problems for public voting, based
on the widespread practice of private voting for elections, and the 20+
years of research into private, coercion resistant remote voting systems. It
would probably take much more than a short email to convince me that these
issues do not exist. But, I repeat, so long as society does not accept
 (imo) public voting, it is a moot point for the case at hand.

For what it's worth, I'd be delighted if you were right and convinced the
general public of it.


> Nor do you think it is difficult to verify the results of a public
> voting system (obviously it is not).  Your only concern is that the
> general population does not expect a public voting system (on this we
> agree) and will reject it (on this we agree to disagree).
>
> Setting all that aside, we could focus on the likelihood of
> success/failure for a private voting system.  Before beginning, I
> would ask you to briefly describe your method of verification.  What
> assurance will you give people that the results are trustworthy?
> (Just roughly, in a sentence or two.)
>

A voting system that satisfies this property is said to be universally
verifiable. You can read up on this in the literature, but basically
a universal verifiable voting scheme allows any third party to verify (in a
mathematically provable way) the results of an election. This usually
involves a public bulletin board, where all the data from an election is
posted for anyone and everyone to see.
