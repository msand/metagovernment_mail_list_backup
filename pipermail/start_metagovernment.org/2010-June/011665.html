David Ruescas wrote:
> I think that we can agree to disagree as to what we think the public
> expects when casting votes, I think that, today, the public expects
> and requires privacy, you do not.

Fair enough.  But just to be clear, we do not disagree that the public
expects privacy in voting.  We only disagree (or agree to disagree)
that they absolutely require it.

> But by all means we can continue the discussion besides that. I am
> interested in different viewpoints, even of the "thou shall fail"
> type!

Me too.  I was attracted by your dismal view of public voting. :-)

> ... Achieving credibility ... requires guarantees of privacy and
> universal verifiability (for starters).  I have already seen that
> there are issues such as vote buying, coercion, that are hard to
> solve even with a secure system, let alone public voting...

For now, I take it you no longer think that public voting is insecure;
in particular, you no longer think it is prone to vote buying,
coercion and other types of voter corruption (I answered you on that).
Nor do you think it is difficult to verify the results of a public
voting system (obviously it is not).  Your only concern is that the
general population does not expect a public voting system (on this we
agree) and will reject it (on this we agree to disagree).

Setting all that aside, we could focus on the likelihood of
success/failure for a private voting system.  Before beginning, I
would ask you to briefly describe your method of verification.  What
assurance will you give people that the results are trustworthy?
(Just roughly, in a sentence or two.)
