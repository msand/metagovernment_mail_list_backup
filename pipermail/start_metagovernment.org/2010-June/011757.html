Ed and Thomas,

Ed Pastore wrote:
> I want to emphasize again that while the term "e-democracy" seems
> ideal for what we are talking about, it is not understood to mean
> what we mean. E-democracy is the use of online tools in existing
> political and governance processes. So for example, the political
> campaign of Barak Obama was a great triumph of e-democracy. Is
> getting a politician elected anything like what we are trying to
> accomplish? ...

We might take it as a windfall.  Ever since the word "democracy" was
popularized (an innovation of Robespierre's they say [1]), politicians
have been waving it around and wrapping themselves in it.  That should
tell us something: people want democracy.  So imagine what happens
when they actually attain it.  All those politicians will fall (neatly
wrapped) into their hands, unable to say a word in protest.[2]

But if we call it something other than "democracy" ("collaborative
governance" or whatever) then people will wonder, "What are they
talking about?"  And those who are opposed will be quick to tell them,
"It is not democracy."

Ed Pastore wrote:
> And note that this applies to the e-democracy.org site as well. They 
> are doing great work, but they are happy with results like getting 
> people more actively engaged in politician's campaigns. It is a 
> natural and beneficial thing to do given the limitations of the status 
> quo, but I thought most of us were interested in something more akin 
> to a revamp of the status quo than a general improvement of it.

I was also under the impression (just like you) that the status quo
included instances of online discussion - ordinary people talking
about political issues, affairs of state, and that kind of thing.  But
when I looking for examples of it, and asked around, we could only
find a few instances - and those few would not have existed without
the deliberate efforts of e-democracy.org.  So I no longer think of
grassroots discussion as part of the status quo, but rather as
something new in an early, seedling stage.
 
Thomas von der Elbe wrote:
> Yes, you are right! E-democracy.org seems to be mainly "just" a
> place for discussions. But if we combine "their" political
> discussion with "our" voting and collaborative drafting, then we
> have the explosive mix, right? All 3 ingredients are necesarry.
 
I agree with Thomas, it's a crucial ingredient.  I also point (again)
to the historical precedence for this kind of mix.  The explosions and
(as you say) revamps of the late 1700's were preceded by a boom in
public discussion.  Back then, the communication technology was
printing presses.  Together with improved transport and postal
services, higher literacy rates, the organization of subscription
libraries, reading societies and so forth, it was all too much for the
status quo.  People who were formerly silent (middle class) suddenly
began speaking out, raising issues that had never been raised before.
The authorities couldn't deflect or suppress all the critique.  As a
consequence, people won the approximation of democracy that we enjoy
(and often take for granted) today.  So it appears that discussion is
a social prerequisite for democracy.[3][4]

It is also a technical prerequisite for *e*-democracy.  The tools of
e-democracy cannot conjure discussion/dialogue out of thin air (we see
no examples of that).  So they can only be effective (it appears) when
they are used by healthy communities, already engaged with political
issues, and so forth.
