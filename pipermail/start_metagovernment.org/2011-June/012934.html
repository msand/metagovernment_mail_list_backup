Pietro Speroni di Fenizio wrote:
> ... But Vilfredo has also its special quirks. It has generations, it
> has proposals that pass the filter (the proposals in the Pareto
> Front). How are you going to do this in a system that generally only
> takes normal voting systems? One thing that I wil not do, is saying
> that Crossforum has captured the gist of Vilfredo, if I feel

We share some of your quirks.  Like you, we allow for multiple
generations of draft answers per drafter.  Like you, we facilitiate
the stepwise optimization of those answers along a path that is
"efficient" in terms of its pursuit of consensus.  I don't know how
this relates to Pareto efficiency in *theory*, but the population
structure on which our evolutionary, recombinant approach is based - a
single generational sequence per user - might be flexible enough to
cover Vilfredo's approach in *practice*.  Can your users draft their
answers in wiki pages?  If so, then this opens up possibilities:

  (a) Free-range drafting.  The users may choose which wiki to draft
      in, and the choice may vary from question to question.

  (b) Difference bridging.  Wherever two draft answers are
      sufficiently similar, the users may easily view the differences.
      They may eliminate them by erasure, if they wish, and bring the
      overall "population" one step closer to consensus.

  (c) Facilitate discussion.  Wherever the difference bridge is
      employed in this manner (b), it serves to formally interconnect
      drafting, voting and discussion media in a way that supports
      focused discussion in a single forum.

  (d) Facilitate growth.  Any such discussion in a single forum is
      automatically exposed in Crossforum Theatre, where it is mapped
      in geospace, votespace, forum space, topic space, and so forth.
      If a "question" can possibly grow in terms of its participation,
      then the physical and logical dimensions of that growth are
      clearly shown.

  (e) Lights, camera, action.  If any of the questions is potentially
      actionable, then the theatre's resource tracks will reveal how
      close it is to any given action, and what amount of any given
      resource a user would have to contribute to move it one step
      closer.

None of this contradicts the logic of Vilfredo's peculiar votespace,
any more than it contradicts Votorola's.  Voting is easy to factor out
as a separate space, a separate medium, one among many.
