> In the appraisal system, the actual decision is made by software, using the
> three independent appraisals as inputs. The appraisals themselves are posted
> publicly at the point the decision has been made, and the code of the
> decision software is open source and publicly available as well. Thus the
> decision making is "radically transparent."
>
> The appraisers are information gatherers, not decision makers. Who they are
> is known (the system selected them in the first place), and their required
> "agenda" is to honestly appraise. If they try to "game the system" by
> answering questions to affect the subsequent software decision, that will be
> detected (the three independent appraisals will have differing answers) and
> investigated, and lead to a nullification of the decision and a lowering of
> their rating.
>
> The functionality of the decision software is proposed (each appraisal
> question has associated decision functionality), and has to pass
> successfully through the appraisal process to be implemented.
>
> The appraisal system provides more decision transparency than any voting
> system can. The decision making in parliaments is so opaque as to be
> cartoonish.
>
>
> *For the collective to be a good cosmopolitan one it must have a means to
> consider all matters of concern to both human and non human actors  and to
> decide which scripts are in and which out.*
>

> do not.

>>>> Steve, Greetings!
>>>>
>>>> I tracked into the links that you provided, including reading the first
>>>> chapter of *Pandora's Hope*. My impression is that Latour's work is
>>>> indeed complementary to the kind of work that is being done in the various
>>>> Metagovernment projects, but (very interestingly!) there is very little
>>>> overlap. The Metagovernment projects (including mine) are focused on
>>>> precisely those decision making situations in which nonhumans *cannot*directly speak (e.g., any voting situation), whereas Latour is interested in
>>>> studying situations in which they can (such as electrical grid power outage
>>>> situations). The world contains both, so attention to both is appropriate.
>>>>
>>>> The studying of "wild" situations in which nonhumans can speak leads to
>>>> policy proposals (e.g. provide more reactive power in the electrical power
>>>> grids). Those policy proposals then compete for attention in the
>>>> rough-and-tumble human decision making systems we have now. The
>>>> Metagovernment projects are aimed at creating better human decision making
>>>> systems, that will more appropriately receive and act upon proposals such as
>>>> those flowing from Latour's studies. To me, that looks pretty complementary,
>>>> and also pretty non-overlapping. (I can empathize that reading my articles
>>>> would have been hard -- not much of interest there from Latour's
>>>> perspective.)
>>>>
>>>> (An example of this divergence appeared in the macospol material. All
>>>> the brilliant new work of controversy mapping culminates in testing the work
>>>> in a "quasi parliament." For those of us working on replacements for
>>>> parliamentary systems, that is rather like building a hot new Lamborghini
>>>> and then testing it by using it as a wheelbarrow in the back yard. In the
>>>> parliaments that we have now, most of the time when a technical vote comes
>>>> up, the parliamentarians ask their lobbyist handlers how to vote and then
>>>> vote that way. To have the controversy mapping really "show its stuff" it
>>>> would need to be tested in decision making systems in which it would
>>>> actually be appreciated and used.)
>>>>
>>>> You write: *Not sure how appraisal will help if it is not universally
>>>> adopted?*
>>>>
>>>> First, indeed, the more widely it were adopted, the more helpful it
>>>> would be. And also, the "adoption" takes place one individual at a time,
>>>> beginning in the realms of appreciation and intent. To me the most
>>>> appropriate way to create and demonstrate the system would be through
>>>> introducing it as an online game. Unlike new voting systems, the appraisal
>>>> system will have to go through a development period before it will be ready
>>>> to make "real" decisions that actually affect people's lives. The
>>>> appropriate impetus for incremental adoption would then be that the system
>>>> demonstrates itself to be better than what we have.
