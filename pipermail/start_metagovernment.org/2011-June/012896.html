Interesting conversation.  My apologies for not having time to follow the
links you point to, but I'll raise a couple of points I hope are relevant.

The title of this thread is voting vs. appraisal, implying an either/or
where one method is superior.  I think it would be more accurate to think of
these as different decision making tools, each of which might be more
desirable in the context of a specific community decision.  There are widely
diverse decision contexts, and I sincerely doubt that any decision mechanism
will be best suited for all contexts.

The elephant in the room that only rarely gets discussed on this list is
authority, as in who has the authority to make this decision?  Neither
voting nor appraisal be relevant if a binding decision cannot be taken.
Looking towards existing power structures, advising decision makers is the
best that can be hoped for in many decision contexts in the short term.  I
don't argue against developing voting and/or appraisal tools, but that a
variety of tool sets will need to be developed that communities can choose
from, and that in the short term tools designed to inform existing decision
makers are likely to be most relevant.

As a side note, nonhuman actors speaking sounds more spiritual than
practical (though I like the notion).  Without taking the time to read the
literature, what's the gist of this argument - that things have an ideal
that they want to manifest, a bit like Plato's forms?  E.g, I'm a river and
I want to flow to the ocean?  I'm a chair and I want to be sat on?  My guess
is that nonhuman actors will always need human advocates in any decision
system, and that designing systems so that the voices of nonhumans are left
to their human advocates probably makes the most sense.

Cheers,
Mark
