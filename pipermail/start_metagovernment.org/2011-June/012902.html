> Steve, Greetings!

> I tracked into the links that you provided, including reading the first
> chapter of *Pandora's Hope*. My impression is that Latour's work is indeed
> complementary to the kind of work that is being done in the various
> Metagovernment projects, but (very interestingly!) there is very little
> overlap. The Metagovernment projects (including mine) are focused on
> precisely those decision making situations in which nonhumans *cannot*directly speak (e.g., any voting situation), whereas Latour is interested in
> studying situations in which they can (such as electrical grid power outage
> situations). The world contains both, so attention to both is appropriate.
>
> The studying of "wild" situations in which nonhumans can speak leads to
> policy proposals (e.g. provide more reactive power in the electrical power
> grids). Those policy proposals then compete for attention in the
> rough-and-tumble human decision making systems we have now. The
> Metagovernment projects are aimed at creating better human decision making
> systems, that will more appropriately receive and act upon proposals such as
> those flowing from Latour's studies. To me, that looks pretty complementary,
> and also pretty non-overlapping. (I can empathize that reading my articles
> would have been hard -- not much of interest there from Latour's
> perspective.)
>
> (An example of this divergence appeared in the macospol material. All the
> brilliant new work of controversy mapping culminates in testing the work in
> a "quasi parliament." For those of us working on replacements for
> parliamentary systems, that is rather like building a hot new Lamborghini
> and then testing it by using it as a wheelbarrow in the back yard. In the
> parliaments that we have now, most of the time when a technical vote comes
> up, the parliamentarians ask their lobbyist handlers how to vote and then
> vote that way. To have the controversy mapping really "show its stuff" it
> would need to be tested in decision making systems in which it would
> actually be appreciated and used.)
>
> You write: *Not sure how appraisal will help if it is not universally
> adopted?*
>
> First, indeed, the more widely it were adopted, the more helpful it would
> be. And also, the "adoption" takes place one individual at a time, beginning
> in the realms of appreciation and intent. To me the most appropriate way to
> create and demonstrate the system would be through introducing it as an
> online game. Unlike new voting systems, the appraisal system will have to go
> through a development period before it will be ready to make "real"
> decisions that actually affect people's lives. The appropriate impetus for
> incremental adoption would then be that the system demonstrates itself to be
> better than what we have.
>

>> Regarding "decisions come from alliances," certainly in public voting
>> systems, legislatures, bureaucracies, and corporations many decisions do
>> involve alliances, and tribes. However, the same history that supports that
>> view also supports the view that non humans and future generations and other
>> entities that cannot directly join the alliances generally suffer when
>> decisions come from alliances of tribes. Alliances of tribes tend to put
>> their own interests ahead of the interests of those outside of the
>> alliances. A "nobody objects" consensus system (such as the appraisal
>> system) can much more reliably support the interests of non humans (through
>> even only a few attuned humans). In such a system, decisions do not come
>> from alliances, and protection of non humans does not depend in each
>> particular decision on "the generosity of strangers."
>>
>> Regarding building tribes who share the value of a more harmonious and
>> just relationship between humans and non-humans, yes! And also, I would hope
>> and expect that such tribes will see the wisdom of having a social decision
>> making system that can be trusted to uphold that value in every decision.
