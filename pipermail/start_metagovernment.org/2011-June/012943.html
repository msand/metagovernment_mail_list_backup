> As promissed, here is my contribution concerning the important issue 
> of argument visualization.
>
> The first scheme http://dl.dropbox.com/u/5910388/proposal_argument.png 
> is composed of a sequence of argumentation points contained in each 
> proposal:
> P11 is point 1 of proposal 1, p12 point 2 of proposal 1 and so on.
> Attached to each point is a field for contra-arguments from other 
> proponents (C2, C3, C5, ...) and the respective responses R1, R2,... 
> from the proponent.
> The arrows indicate connections between arguments (for instance, C2 on 
> point 1 of proposal 1 is connected with point 1 of proposal 2). This 
> connections are more than hiperlinks; they can also be used as a means 
> for comparing arguments.
>
> The second scheme http://dl.dropbox.com/u/5910388/matrix.png is a 
> comparison matrix of the arguments being discussed. It is important to 
> align a predefined grid so that each point in each proposal address 
> the same specific issue. Each point has its own voting poll so that 
> support can be splited and points of consensus or disagreement can be 
> spotted. The color bars refers to the voting on each point, of the 
> proposal, the contra-arguments and the the respective responses. 
> Through this matrix, we can see easily what are the hot points and how 
> is opinion being formed. It is also a way for proponents to receive 
> feedback and correct its own proposal.
>
> These are just schematic ideas, but I think we will need to
> 1) find ways for users to get a fast and easy visualization of 
> different proposals without reading it
> 2) split a document into clearly defined and standard components or 
> argumentation points
> 3) go beyond document editing and incorporate contra-argumentation 
> into the document itself
