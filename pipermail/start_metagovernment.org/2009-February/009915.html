> I agree with a lot of what you say, Robert, and I appreciate the  
> sentiment. But in some senses what you are asking is that we put the  
> cart before the horse.
>
> I now agree with Manuel and Pietro that we need working code  
> *before* we try to take other parts of the project much further.  
> After all, whenever anyone asks one of the many different questions  
> I get frequently asked, my answer always has to be: "well, we need  
> to release the code into the wild and see what people do with it."  
> That after all, is our development strategy: not to replace the  
> government of the United States, but rather to make a beta of  
> governance software for a chess club. That approach has a much, much  
> better chance of replacing the United States (and every other)  
> government than a direct approach. If you investigate how some of  
> the Related Projects are doing, the ones that target national  
> governments, you will find that they aren't making much progress.
>
> Same sort of reasoning goes for working with other organizations or  
> getting funding. If they bother to answer at all (and I can't get  
> people like Tim O'Reilly to even acknowledge me), their first  
> question is about what the code looks like. Without a running copy  
> of Metascore, we are all talk and no action. Even if we had an  
> article published in the New York Times, we still wouldn't have  
> anything to show for it... just more people talking. What we need is  
> something concrete. So if you can code, I beg you to help.
>
> As for leadership, I do not accept the notion that I am the project  
> leader. It would be counter to the idea of open source governance  
> for me to have even the slightest bit of authority within the  
> project. I do however, work quite a bit to promote the project, and  
> again, can't get very far without having something concrete to show  
> people. I also repeatedly ask this group to please contribute more,  
> particularly in coding:
