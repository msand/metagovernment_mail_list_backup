On Feb 18, 2009, at 5:12 PM, Michael Allan wrote:

> We can argue that a de-facto technology is not even in the cards.  It
> will not be possible to corner people into a given set of tools and
> standards - whether of Google's, or of Metagovernment's, or of any
> other project's.  We can forsee that the continued success of any
> given technology can only be by the choice of the users.  There will
> *always* be other viable choices on the table - other paths of
> development, other standards, other tools and toolsets.  The users
> will expect this, and it will be impossible to deny them this - an
> entrenched monopoly is impossible in this domain.

...and then:

> If we want an independent, verifiable, public mechanism for people to
> vote and comment on the topics of the day and get those compiled  
> results
> to decision-makers...

If we leave it up to entrenched decision-makers to decide what they  
will listen to, then it is possible for there to be an entrenched  
monopoly. They can choose MicrosoftVotes because it gives them the  
most flexibility to do what they want to get away with in our  
government. And they can choose to ignore any input from other  
mechanisms.

In this regard, Metagovernment is certainly distinguished by its  
intent to supersede decision-makers by making them obsolete.  
Theoretically, other tools could start out as advisory and then grow  
into full governance mechanisms, but MG is working from the very  
outset on the idea that individuals should not have the power of  
decision-making for the community. This means our initial approached  
all of our architecture is built around bringing consensus out of  
conflict in such a manner that end decisions can be reached by the  
crowd.

> I think we need to look at the structure of that "table", and make
> sure it can do its job, right from the start.  I'm going to suggest
> that the crucial thing is transparency - laying out the cards so that
> everyone (tech, non-tech and user) knows it's a fair deal and can see
> the choices.

Again, that fairly-well describes this organization. Everything this  
project does is publicly archived; anyone may join; most everyone uses  
their real name. There is nothing hidden and no one excluded. Sounds  
pretty transparent.

But I do agree that it would be great to have a larger API where other  
views can interoperate. This project certainly has the right name (ie,  
"meta") to enable that. If you want some server space or something to  
work on it, I'm sure we can help.

I am busy today and honestly haven't had time to read through your  
entire explanation. But FWIW, from this group session at Transparency  
camp, I hope to get a meta-organization of electronic democracy groups  
going, at least as a separate list server if not more.
