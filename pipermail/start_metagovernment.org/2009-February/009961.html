On Feb 11, 2009, at 12:55 PM, Mark Murphy wrote:

> There is a positive incentive to provide residency (your positions are
> more likely to be examined), but it is not required, nor does not  
> being
> a resident of a community prevent one from raising an issue or

> position.

That is very much in line with our principle of "geographic  
distinctness within a global community."
http://www.metagovernment.org/wiki/Basic_principles

The principle fell into dispute not on its own merits but because it  
is based on the idea of user-scoring, which itself is in dispute.

> True. However, bear in mind that decision-makers may or may not wish  
> to
> take that into account, and it is their right to examine the data and
> make decisions as they see fit...at least in my vision for cooperative
> democracy.
>
> That represents a difference in philosophy between the Metascore
> ("intended to act as the *replacement* for the governance system of  
> any
> community which currently relies on representative democracy for
> governance") and cooperative democracy (intended to serve as a
> complement to representative democracy, not necessarily a flat-out
> replacement).

And that's why we grab for the brass ring and try to dispense with the  
decision makers. I don't see any reason why it should be up to a  
handful of "special" individuals to determine... well, anything.

Although there are notable individual exceptions from time to time,  
the very reliable rule is that "power corrupts." I think that the best  
way to remove the possibility of corruption is not to make many minor- 
repairs and safeguards to try to counter-balance the natural tendency  
to corruption, but rather to simply, cleanly eliminate any possibility  
of corruption.
