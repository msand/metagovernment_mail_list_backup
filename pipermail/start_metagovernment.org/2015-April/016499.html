On Thu, Apr 9, 2015 at 7:07 PM, Scott Raney <scott at metacard.com> wrote:

> Sorry, I'm lost. Exactly what problem is this multiple voting proposal
> supposed to solve? I see no advantages and at least two big
> disadvantages:
> 1) Single issue voters will hoard their votes and expend them all on
> the issue they care about, skewing the results.
>

Yes, but why would that be bad?
If they care so much about them, that they do not decide on all the
"proposals" that were made before, just to be able to vote on the current
one, that's legit, because they will only be able to do it once, because
afterwards, their votes are gone - and I mentioned a "rate r" with which
people would lose their votes if they don't vote and just hoard :-)
So given that, do you still see this issue?




> 2) People don't worry about losing votes: They routinely fail to cast
> the ones they already get! Providing more votes just decreases their
> value even further, increasing the number that just won't be cast.
>

It's not about giving people "more votes", because if you give everyone
more votes, it changes nothing, but you can spend more diverse if the
options you can vote on increase too.
Of course, it needs time for people to get used to it, but because a major
part in my proposal is extremely short voting periods, it means you can
form habits and you have something to discuss with your neighbors and ask
them how they spend their votes :-)

I think people do get little votes and there are a lot of constraints about
how and when to spend those votes.
It's a certain time slot where you can vote, if you miss that one, you are
out. Upfront you even have to register and make sure you are allowed to
receive votes to spend in the first place.
And at last, the choices you can make are extremely limited and often so,
that its virtually impossible to decide what kind of impact they would have.

My proposal is about extremely short intervals, and voting on many
proposals (i can make up some proposals in private and have others vote on
them, so they pass votes to me).
Because there are many small proposals that could "intergrate" into bigger
ones, you as a voter get more direct feedback and because voting periods
are short, you can vote differently the next time taking advantage of what
you learned from the feedback.
