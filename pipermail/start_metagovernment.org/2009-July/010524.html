On Mon, Jul 6, 2009 at 9:02 PM, Ed Pastore<epastore at metagovernment.org>
wrote:
> On Jul 5, 2009, at 1:36 PM, Pietro Speroni di Fenizio wrote:
>
>> I was thinking, why am I so skeptical about adding a discussion on the
>> side of Vilfredo? After a lot of thinking I came back to your talk.
>> When you express how you feel that in representative democracies our
>> only political action are the 2 seconds in which we vote, usually once
>> every year (between regional election and national election). But is
>> this correct? Is this really the only form of political action we
>> have? What about discussions? What about writing articles, or blog
>> entries. What about what we are doing right now, right here. After all
>> I can say something that will inspire you to say something that will
>> inspire Tim O'Reilly to say something that will inspire someone at the
>> White House to say something. Let's call this in-direct democracy!
>
> I wanted to address this issue separately, as it was something I did not
> make very clear in my presentation.
>
> All of that extraneous process around our decision-making is indeed part
of
> the political process, but it is not (in my opinion) democracy. You can
have
> the same conversations and even the same upward-trickle of ideas in a
> monarchy or almost any other form of government.

Well, actually not. You often get tortured if you become too unpleasent.
But I will agree that while you are allowed to speak, this have little or no
effect.
Like a wheel that can turn on itself, but is not linked with the engine.

>
> i think that what defines democracy is the participation in the actual
> decisions. In representative democracy, the act of voting for a candidate
is
> the only actual participation in the actual decision-making process that
we
> get. Everything else is not democratic because the participation is not
> guaranteed. I can debate and protest and petition for years on one issue
and
> potentially be completely ignored by my representative. In that case, all
of
> my actions are irrelevant to the actual decision-making process.In the
> status quo, I can only *hope* that my representative will listen to me.
And
> I have to pick one or a very few issues to be heard on, or it is almost
> certain they will ignore me as a crackpot if I bug them about every issue
on
> my mind.


But if your critics to a law where to appear next to the law, in the moment
in which everybody is voting the law, would you not feel that your right of
criticism has suddenly become politics?


As Beppe Grillo (italian comedian, among the top 10 bloggers in the world),
said:
we have so many delinquents in the parliament,
when we vote the criminal record of each should be attached under each name.
Then at least each would be able to chose the crook he prefers.


>
> The critical difference in open governance is that all decision-making is
> open to participation by everyone. Whether or not the debate is integrated
> into the system may be relevant and helpful, but the most important part
is
> opening up the decisions to everyone. That's the real promise of
democracy,
> and any system in which any citizen is not guaranteed input into any
> decision is less than democratic.


I disagree on this. I fear that if people discuss things in room A, and then
vote in room B, too many people will never enter into room A, will just stay
in room B, and not realise what is really going on.

There have been many situations in History where we all believed we were on
the verge to reach a really good and fair society.

Universal Male Voting was surely one. Voting for Women was another.

You can understand how those moments gave rise to those ambitions. After all
if the poor people are allowed to vote, what would stop them from voting
someone who it really a friend of the poor?

People vote with their guts, and the guts can be easily manipulated with
some precise advertisment. And this is what is stopping them. Even a direct
democracy like this will not really change the status quo. The discussion
must be interweaven inside the voting.

The results of the discussion must be available WHEN you are voting.
