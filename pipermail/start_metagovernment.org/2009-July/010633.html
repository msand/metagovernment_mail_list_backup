Ed wrote:

> As for the Basic Principles... it just seems reasonable that our
> fundamental principles should not be particularly malleable, otherwise we
> lose our integrity.
>

This is an interesting argument since the metagovernment project seems to,
in general, support 'open source democracy' -- a common criticism of which
is the potential for moment-to-moment changes in fundamental policies and/or
legislation.

Mark's concern that restricting edits leads to "a sort of mediocre,
apathetic complacency" ... is perhaps justified and (i believe) happening
now.

I propose that we try to find a compromise -- synthesis ;) -- to allow for
ungettered growth of the wiki without putting our principles or our
integrity at risk ;)

Also, this will only become a bigger problem as the wiki (hopefully) grows
in size.

I have created a page on the wiki for this question:
http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Edit_policy

Thoughts from the group?

> Hi, Mark.

> I welcome your participation and also your dissent over the edit policy.
>
> The justification for the edit policy on the Main Page is that it is
> extremely public-facing. People who visit our project often only view the
> main page, and the impression they get from there may be all they get. If it
> is off-kilter for half a day, that's a bunch of people that see something
> which does not represent the project. I know it doesn't seem like the wiki
> way to have something be somewhat restricted, but consider that Wikipedia's
> Main Page is not even editable at all. The use of an alternate page as
> Matías linked it a viable workaround that allows people to work on an
> improvement without losing the current version which is known to have
> consensus.
>
> As for the Basic Principles... it just seems reasonable that our
> fundamental principles should not be particularly malleable, otherwise we
> lose our integrity.
>
> That said... note that the pages in question are not protected by other
> than policy. Only the Copyright page is protected against editing, because
> it wouldn't be nice for someone to suddenly change the legal statement about
> the availability of the content. If you really think a page needs to be
> changed, you are still free to do so. For example, people have fixed typos
> and made other small edits from time to time. The main objection I have is
> about changing the tone or general idea of the Main Page without using the
> established suggested-page method.
>
