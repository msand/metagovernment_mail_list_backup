Greetings Harry,

> "... a large part of the personal ethical decision making process - after
> all the evidence has been digested - is still subjective ...

I guess the subjective component is essential to any agreement because
the subject is the sole authority.  Nobody can say whether I agree or
disagree, only I the subject.

> ... and the only way we can "objectify" subjective opinion is to
> measure it. The only fair way to treat the results of such
> measurement is to give them equal weight, regardless of how each
> opinion was arrived at."

One danger in objectifying assent (or dissent) is the illusion that we
can hold the resulting object in hand.  It might tempt the technician
to physically remove the *form* of assent from the subject's hands in
order to get a better grasp of it.  At that point, however, the
reality of the content dissolves.  (I believe this is our current
predicament in society.)  Conversely, if the objectification or
formalization of assent has the effect of putting it more firmly in
the subject's hands, then its reality is strengthened.

> What I think I'd like you to focus on is my proposed algorithm
> because the point of that is to prioritise Dissent. i.e.to make it a
> "constitutional requirement" a) to allow (and to hear dissent) and
> b) to answer it and establish agreement that it has been answered
> satisfactorily before moving on to any decisions about the base
> proposition.

  http://www.fullmoon.nu/rtpforum/phpBB2/viewtopic.php?p=868#868

I agree that expressions of dissent ought not to be suppressed.  By
the same token, expressions of dissent and assent ought positively to
be enabled in the first place.  Currently they are not enabled and I
think the best way forward is to put the means of their expression in
the hands of individual subjects.  Do you agree?

I want to recommend this essay by Adam Curtis, if you haven't seen it.
It's about looking at others and ourselves objectively:
http://thoughtmaybe.com/video/all-watched-over-by-machines-of-loving-grace
