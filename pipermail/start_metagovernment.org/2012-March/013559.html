> Maybe that's a good example in itself: we simply ask the participants
> to declare a preference from time to time.  This might require a
> formalization of the process (voting) and this is where the danger
> would come in, that we formalize the process in such a manner that we
> restrict its timing, say.  So we choose when to ask the question and
> when to receive the answer, and the subject does not.  By removing the
> form of expression from the subject's hands, the content (his or her
> expressed preference) is drained of meaning.  It is no longer
> legitimate as a basis of decision.  And yet we take it for that very
> purpose.  This is a mild example of what I mean.
> 
> The loss of legitimacy mirrors the subject's loss of control over the
> content.  Speaking more generally, he or she becomes less like a
> subject of the process and more like an object to be manipulated by
> it.  These manipulations actually occur, of course, and where the
> fruits of power are at stake, as in elections, they occur to the
> extreme.  Their force rests on an equally extreme technical flaw in
> the formalization of the vote, namely its physical removal from the
> voter's hands.  Do you see what I mean?
> 
> > More importantly, I'm focussed (in this context) on the principle of
> > appropriate use of the "scientific method" and it's role in the
> > decision making process. The point is to ensure that the debating
> > process (which is vastly more important than the voting process) is
> > "forced" to include all intelligent aspects of the debate, including
> > all shades and sources of dissent, (even the irrational) before
> > decisions are made, optimally by consensus. (Voting is "only"
> > necessary where we fail to achieve consensus and thus represents a
> > "failure" of the debating process)
> 
> I fear that we have no hope of equal access to such debates without
> the structural guarantees of the vote, in particular its guarantees of
> ubiquity and equality.  I would even argue that the proper purpose of
> the vote (held by each of us like a key) is to unlock access to all
> those areas and aspects of society in which equal opportunity is
> paramount, most especially to political communications.  It is not a
> fallback, but rather a first step.
> 
> For witness, consider that the political decisions in today's society
> are *already* made on the basis of elaborate discussion and debate.
> The problem is not the internal conduct of those communications but
> the lack of access to them.  The doors to the "debate hall" are well
> guarded and even the vast majority of elected politicians are barred,
> to say nothing of electors.  Isn't that a fact?
