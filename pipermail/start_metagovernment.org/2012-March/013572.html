I mean the form of the vote - time, place, method, etc. - is critical.
More precisely, the imposition of any particular form will invalidate
the content for purposes of decision making.  I think formal voting is
necessary (see below), but it is also necessary that no particular
form be imposed on anyone.

> You also, however, appear to be alluding to some means of
> structuring the debate where voting becomes an integral part of that
> structure. You've probably explained that in some detail in previous
> posts which I haven't read but I suspect we're not that far apart.

We both emphasize the centrality of communication among the decision
makers, and so our thoughts seem to run in parallel here.  But I think
we are still some distance apart.  I see no likelihood of such
communication happening without the structural support of the formal
vote.  By this I do not mean support for any particular process of
decision making (you spoke of "process votes") but rather personal
support for the individual in society at large.  Without a vote firmly
in hand, the individual will find little opportunity to communicate
with others on matters of politics.  It may appear to us that the
citizen abstains from politics out of apathy (as in contemporary
democracies) or is forcefully excluded (as in tyrannies), but the
technical cause in both cases is the same and the political result is
the same; the individual is dispossessed of the vote and is thereby
dispossessed of political freedom.

> [edit]Kinship-orientation
> Anthropologists identify egalitarian cultures as "kinship-oriented," because they appear to
> value social harmony more than wealth or status. These cultures are contrasted
> with economically oriented cultures (including states) in which status
> and material wealth are prized, and stratification, competition, and conflict
> are common. Kinship-oriented cultures actively work to prevent social
> hierarchies from developing because they believe that such stratification could

> A good example is given by Richard Borshay
> Lee in his account of

> of teasing is proportional to the size of the kill. Lee found this out when he
