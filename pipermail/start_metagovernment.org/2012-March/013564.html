Greetings Michael

I read your "Structural Fault" page. Now I understand the confusion
between us over the importance of "the vote".

I agree with your sentiments but not your analysis. The chief flaw is
historical:

"It was only ever a technical design flaw that precluded this   
development in the first place, and brought us instead to the present
situation where the organized parties make the decisions and exercise the
rightful power and political freedom that were intended for the citizens."

This suggests you believe that the designers intended the citizens to have
their "rightful power and political freedom". This impression is
strengthened by your frequent implied conflation of "decision" with
"election result".

This belief is not just misplaced in the United States, but in every
country on the planet with the notable and sole exception of Switzerland
which, though not perfect by any stretch, does allow its citizens to make
REAL decisions - not just play bit parts in elections.

ALL other existing national political systems are designed to prevent
democracy. (The US Constitution explicitly so) And part of the means of
prevention is to label those existing systems democratic. Whether that is
done in genuine ignorance of what democracy is, or, more cynically, as a
routine Platonist mechanism to try to fool citizens into believing they
already live in democracies we can leave as an exercise for the class.

In any case what we're up against is NOT a "technical design flaw" but a
political strategy and we will get nowhere trying to modify their design.

I trod a similar route nearly 40 years ago when I too believed the problem
was with the electoral system and all we needed to do was tweak that. As a
result, I spent several years campaigning for electoral reform.

Then I began to learn about democracy and realised that the electoral
system was primarily designed to prevent it, or, in historical terms, to
resist any pressure to implement it.

What we have seen over the past couple of thousand years is the VERY slow
spread of power from absolute monarchs, to elite bodies which retain ALL
the powers of the absolute monarchs. The membership of those elite bodies
(parliaments, congresses, knessets etc) has expanded from dozens to a few
hundreds and the means of elevation to those bodies has varied from
nomination to elections, but when you compare that tiny progress in the
equalisation of power to the progress we've made in other social areas,
you begin to understand how well the ruling classes have maintained their
grip on society.

Democracy has absolutely NOTHING to do with elections. Elections only
decide membership of the elite bodies who retain absolute power. Democracy
is ONLY about "We The People" being the decision makers - NOT electing
people to make decisions on our behalf. Which is not to say that you can't
have elections within a democracy, but those elections are for people to
IMPLEMENT the policy not to DECIDE it.

I rather thought that was the whole point of the meta government project
and why I became interested in it in the first place.

Now, you can make very strong arguments against the implementation of
Democracy as a social decision making process, but this is not an issue
for us as, presumably, we're all signed up to this project because we've
accepted the case for democracy rather than against.

I am, therefore, no longer remotely interested in the electoral voting
procedure. Society's continual focus on that is merely a distraction from
the Social Decision Making process we should be talking about - where
voting is actually a sign of failure, though occasionally necessary
because we have failed to reach any consensus other than "a decision is
required."

That, I thought, was the chief aim of the meta-government project. If I'm
wrong about that, I apologise for wasting yours or anyone else's time!

Regards

Harry
