> I like their motto: "economics as if the people and the planet

>
> In the section on "democracy and participation", the organisation  
> describes a collaborative decision-making tool it has developed  
> called Crowd Wise.
>
> This bears many similarities to the tools being discussed and  
> developed here, except perhaps in the counting method, and it is  
> already being tested (this autumn the 1500 members of a London  
> community football club will be invited to use the tool). It seems  
> that this is largely happening through physical meetings. Although a  
> Q&A says that the foundation has "already developed a free tool for  
> online consensus voting", I can't find this on the website.
>
> Here's a section of the description of Crowd Wise:
> In stage one, beginning with an open question, which could be  
> anything from ‘what should we spend our money on next year’ to  
> ‘what’s the best way to tackle urban poverty?’, Crowd Wise  
> invites participants to work together to create and refine possible  
> answers. This is a collaborative process, where answers can be merge 
> d, split or refined by anyone, in order to create the most interesti 
> ng, wide ranging and appealing range of possibilities.
>
> Then, instead of voting for their favourite, each participant is  
> asked to rate each idea from best to worst. This reflects that in  
> complex issues, people’s views are rarely black and white. Rather, t 
> hey may love some options, be able to live with others and find a fe 
> w completely unacceptable. Votes are then counted (using a system ca 
> lled the de Borda count) in a way which identifies which option has  
> the broadest support. Looking at the results, it’s easy to tell how  
> much support each option has.
