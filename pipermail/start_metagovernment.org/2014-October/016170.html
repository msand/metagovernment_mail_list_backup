> > In my understanding/experience, generally it works best to work out the
> > vision first, to the point that one would be able to recognize a coded
> > instantiation of the vision. Otherwise, one generally winds up throwing
> away
> > a whole lot of code. (Even more than "with a vision".)  (There are others
> > who think that visions are a waste of time.)
>
> That would be me ;-)
>
> > At this point, it does not appear to me that there is a common vision
> within
> > the community. Which means, at this point there is no shared guidance or
> > motivation for coding.
>
> Agreed.  And as for me I think that's fine: I'm not here to join a
> collaborative development team.  Been there, done that, didn't work
> out all that well.  IMHO it's just not how new ideas get established.
> My experience is that the way it actually happens is that a very small
> development team, perhaps even just one person, codes up a working
> prototype that once it has been shown to be useful *then* gains
> additional developers *very slowly*.  It's very much a case of too
> many cooks spoiling the broth.
>
> Until then, my recommendation for the best use of Metagovernment is to
> discuss the underlying philosophy, ideas for how to build these
> systems, and a source of guinea pigs for running the prototypes on.
> The recent runs of Vilfredo I think have been beneficial all around,
> for example, because they not only improve that system but provide all
> of us with information about what we should include (or not include)
> in our own systems.  I.e., even if the output of these runs leaves
> something to be desired (as is my opinion of the new MG home page)
> it's still worth doing because of these secondary benefits.
>
> > The particular project that we have been discussing on this thread (an
> > on-line open-source Virtual Worlds Social Systems Laboratory) is huge,
> as in
> > "land a human on the moon" huge. But, if we had the $160 billion that
> Apple
> > is sitting on, we could probably get it up and running in a couple of
> years
> > (and thereby maybe Save The World). (If anybody knows somebody at Apple
> ...)
>
> You may be right, but then again, there have been a great many
> examples where such projects have been complete disasters.  I was just
> at DIA a few days ago picking up my wife and I asked one of the
> information people about the automated baggage system that they spent
> half a billion dollars on but end up scrapping after a couple of years
> because it just wasn't reliable enough and was costing more to
> maintain than it would cost to do it manually.  And here it is 20
> years later and they're still handling baggage manually.
>
> I think our best bet will be to adapt some existing (or soon to be
> released) system, which is something a small team could do.  When the
> time comes...
