On Tue, Oct 7, 2014 at 9:21 PM, Ned Conner <npconner at earthlink.net> wrote:

> My (here proposed) answer to this question is that it might be good to start
> by proposing fundamental "filter principles", that any proposed vision must
> be in accord with (in order to be considered as a candidate). Coming up with
> a list of such filter-principles to use in vetting candidate visions would
> be very metagovernmenty work, and if done well would produce a list that
> would potentially have lasting and general usefulness. (Scott will want to
> have "effective" and "efficient" and maybe "Paleolithic" on that list.)

Minor quibble: Paleolithic ("stone age") is probably not the best way
describe my proposal.  A better term is EEA-matching (Era of
Evolutionary Adaptation), that interval that humans evolved to best
fit their environment.  That said, it is correct that this is mostly
the Paleolithic and/or Pleistocene eras we're talking about, but I
recommend Wade's "A Troublesome Inheritance" for evidence that
significant evolution has occurred even in the last few thousand
years.

> In the interest of grounding this discussion with an example: were we to
> decide to undertake "The Metagovernment Vision Project", and to execute the
> plan here proposed, and to use Vilfredo as our synthesis/decision tool, my
> first filter-principle proposal (to Vilfredo) would be THE PRIME DIRECTIVE:
>
> To be acceptable, a candidate vision (social system or sub-system) must be
> explicitly, intentionally designed to eliminate Power Over Other People in
> every way possible. By design, there must be no concentrations of power
> entailed, caused, or permitted within or by the envisioned social
> (sub-)system.
>
> The reason for this is simple. It does not matter through what means power
> is initially concentrated (through political, criminal, police, military,
> economic, financial, religious, or whatever other means) -- the end result
> will always be the same because power is fungible: any form of concentrated
> power can be used to acquire more power of any kind. As soon as there is any
> concentration of power of any kind produced by any social sub-system, the
> overall social system will commence to morph toward further concentration of
> power: the existence of concentrations of power sets up positive feedback
> loops that lead to further concentration of power.

I like the way this is totally outside the box (doesn't even contain
the word government!)  And I'd even sign on because I agree with it in
principle.  But, like Blinap, it may be a bit of a stretch to go
directly from where we are currently to there without any intermediate
steps.  In particular, eliminating financial and military power are
just things I don't think we're ready for yet.  Maybe we should
restrict the metagovernment vision to dealing with political power
(leaving eliminating religious power to the individual projects,
because I think we *are* also ready for that one).

> (Given that Vilfredo always wants to start with a question, I guess we would
> have to play Jeopardy to come up with a question to which this proposal
> would appropriately respond ...)

I'd still recommend we try out a rank-order system instead.  Everyone
generate a list of what they want, we use that tool to rank order
them, then pick the top 5 to generate a proposal.  Which we can then
work on in Vilfredo if it still needs tweaking.
