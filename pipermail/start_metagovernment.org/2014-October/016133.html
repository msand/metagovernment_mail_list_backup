We have been speaking of Sociocracy several times now. And there are some
people that like it, some people that do not like it. But sociocracy is
made off several pieces, and there is one of them that maybe we should
apply now. The piece is that each decision should be reviewed regularly.
When people can give back feedback for that decision, and in case change it.

I don't remember that we never really worked on the vision as a
collaborative group effort. And if we did I was (my fault) distracted.
Maybe it would be a good time, bow that we are raising so many elements to
go back and question this very basic building block.

Has anyone any objection to running a "what should be the Vision for the
metagovernment community?" question?

A community Vision quest :-).

Cheers,
Pietro

On 6 October 2014 20:42, Ned Conner <npconner at earthlink.net> wrote:

>  Speaking for myself (and too briefly, because these listserv email
> exchanges have not delivered enough "bang for the buck" over the years to
> keep me playing ...), the vision statement that you linked to does not at
> all express the vision (of destination, of strategy to travel there) that I
> am working from.
> ( http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Vision#Vision )
>
> The first sentence expresses one particular design specification that I
> heartily embrace (for the new global social system that is my
> destination-vision).
>
> The second sentence does not at all express my vision of how to get there.
>
> First, "spreading out" is not the important part; "spreading up" is the
> important part. For example, the CIA has spent the last hundred years in
> South and Central America systematically crushing every community that
> appeared to be on the way to implementing a better/successful social
> system. The Powers That Be cannot afford to have better examples existing,
> so they crush such examples as they appear. (Read "Confessions of an
> Economic Hitman" and "War is a Racket" for evidence.) The "how to get
> there" strategy that the sentence suggests fails to take this reality into
> account. (Just because the Hobbits in the Shire choose to ignore Mordor
> does not mean that Mordor will ignore the Hobbits in the Shire ...)
>
> Second, the notion of "collaborative governance" is very much in dispute
> within the community, and has been for a long time. That term cannot be
> used in the vision statement unless/until its meaning has been clarified
> and agreed upon.  (See for example:
> http://www.metagovernment.org/wiki/Collaborative_Governance_v2.0 )
>
>
> The third/last sentence of the vision statement again expresses several
> laudable functional specifications, but too vaguely to be of much use. (It
> is the sort of might-mean-anything sentence that politicians use in
> campaign speeches.)
>
