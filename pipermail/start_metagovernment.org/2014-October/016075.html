> Now that I have explained how the process of simple decision making works
> let me explain why I consider this method superior to any other method I
> have seen so far (I exclude Vilfredo which is pretty similar). Objections
> are considered. No one can have a proposal been chucked up his throat. On
> the other side this veto power can only be used for reasonable reasons. It
> is important that any personal mythology, belief system, unreasonable
> assumptions, personal biases, are either suspended. Or somehow made
> explicit, and a person accepts not to act based on it. Apparently to do this
> people need to be at a certain level of maturity

And here it is, the second major flaw in sociocracy: An expectation
that people will behave reasonably and that there is a moderator
available to help them "see" the flaws in their reasoning.  This is
exactly the same problem that I mentioned before is causing such
problems on that German Pirate Party list: There are certain members
of that development team who are apparently *obsessed* with the idea
that a moderator will step in and "correct" behavior that doesn't
comply with some standard.  And the list of possible violations is
quite extensive.  One of the favorite targets is the "killer phrase"
or "killer argument" (Totschlagargumente, which Google comically
translates as "manslaughter argument").  Unfortunately Wikipedia
inexplicably doesn't have an English description for this concept (the
link to "thought terminating cliche" isn't really right), but you can
get a good idea by running Google translate on the German page:
 http://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totschlagargument

My main problem with the German team's obsession with correcting this
behavior (through censure) is that it gives extraordinary power to the
moderators, but also imposes a huge burden on them (a system based on
this would make the East German Stasi look like a model of efficiency
by comparison ;-)  But it's also a major burden on everyone else who
has to just sit and watch this process because it's just a waste of
their time.

> Also it is VERY important to note that there is no time where people vote if
> they “like” a proposal. No counting of the votes. If you like a proposal or
> not is irrelevant. The only thing relevant is: “do you have reasonable
> objections to it” that shows how it would hinder us or not help us in
> reaching our aim. As such all the neutral people, and also people that might
> not like a proposal still will not stop it. This is VERY powerful, as in one
> step sidesteps all the majority versus minority elements of voting theory.

And also removes the incredibly powerful force of *competition* as a
tool to shape a solution.  In which case you end up with something
like the new MG home page, weak and probably completely ineffective
for its main purpose of recruiting new members.

> Vilfredo versus Sociocracy
>
>...Thus Vilfredo is inherently parallel, while
> Sociocratic discussion is linear (or circular).

And as a result inherently inefficient because everyone has to sit and
listen to everyone else's debates rather than just being able to read
a digest (e.g., the top rated 2 or 3 comments: As you can probably
tell, I hate meetings and consider them an archaic tradition).

Question about sociocracy implementations: All the examples I've seen
assume people sitting in a room.  Is there an electronic
implementation?  How does moderation work in that context?
