On Fri, Oct 3, 2014 at 9:40 PM, Scott Raney <scott at metacard.com> wrote:

> On Fri, Oct 3, 2014 at 9:56 AM, Steve Coffman <steve at stevecoffman.com>
> wrote:
> > My guess is that whatever system humans puts together for the next
> iteration of *Democracy*, it’s going to look more and more like what’s
> happening at *Valve*.
> > In my mind, it will be self-organizing, very creative, employ the
> assistance of AI, ….and will most likely be way more complex than we can
> possibly imagine from our current vantage point. Scaling won’t be an issue.
> We will be so connected and empowered technologically that coming to
> decisions in large groups will be relatively simple and routine.
> >
>
> I hope you're wrong, because I think we can't wait that long ;-)
>
> > In a decade or two, I don’t think there will be much *government*, per
> say. That job will be (is being) taken over by the Corporate model of
> managing our affairs. The Corporate model will be taken over by the
> Cooperative model. The Cooperative model will run on a continually evolving
> platform/network/program/intelligence for managing the mission of our
> self-organization. The mission is *All for All*.  This will be (is being)
> driven by the same shift in consciousness that bring all of us to this
> list….and will be built on the bits and pieces now developing among us.
>
> No offense intended, but this strikes me as being naive to the point
> of being delusional.  You seem to have completely ignored the fact
> that Valve or any corporation simply doesn't have to deal with the
> issues that we face when trying to enable the general population to
> make better decisions.  Resource redistribution I consider to be the
> most significant of these: There *are* no poor, undereducated,
> disabled, old, very young, mentally ill, or otherwise disadvantaged
> individuals at Valve so they are simply spared the difficult decisions
> about how they're going to provide for those people.
>
> You've *also* made two other fundamental mistakes.  The first is the
> assumption that cooperation is a viable alternative to competition as
> a governing principle except in extremely homogeneous domains.  This
> is just wrong, as been shown countless times in countless areas.  It's
> against human (and indeed animal) nature, something we ignore at our
> peril.  Maybe think of your proposal as a type of prohibition, which
> is another example of an "enlightened" principle that just never works
> out in the real world.  People simply don't follow laws that they
> consider immoral, and while we can count on a certain amount of help
> from human's instinctive conceptions of fairness (which other animals
> share, telling us that it's a deeply embedded one), you can't
> legislate morality which is what your proposal, and any sort of
> cooperative design, would require.
>
> The second is the classic "humans as a blank slate" assumption that
> sociologists are particularly prone to.  Unfortunately they've all
> been proven wrong at every turn and this is in large part why
> sociology in general is ranked at the very bottom of the social
> sciences (which in turn are ranked at the bottom of all sciences) in
> terms of prestige and perceived rigor and effectiveness.  The fact is
> that sociocracy is just another feeble expression of this philosophy,
> a watered-down Marxism that has no more chance of being successful
> (and of course note that sociocracy *is* a product of sociologists).
> Like morality, you can't legislate altruism, or educate it into
> people, which is something your Cooperative model requires.
> Particularly when it not only fails to meet the morality requirements
> but also lacks simple engineering efficiency (e.g., the pace of
> development and improvement in a domain is highly correlated with the
> degree of competition).
>
> > As together we choose, together it will be.  :)
>
> Problem is, we need a better system *now* even if we can't *all* agree
> on it.  If 25% of the population has to get dragged into a new thing
> that they're just instinctively not comfortable with, well, we just
> need to ease their pain as much as possible without allowing them a
> blocking vote.  Insisting on "consensus" or "consent" from them is
> just another way of guaranteeing preservation of the status quo.
>   Regards,
>     Scott
