> You maybe have too high a standard for what is science: Building
> bridges that fall down *is* science by my definition, at least if you

> the phase when you know enough to build bridges that don't fall down.
> And while I'd agree that the science of decisionmaking (and perhaps
> indeed of all of psychology) is very primitive compared with "hard"
> sciences, there is still much to be gained by taking the most robust
> findings into consideration in our designs (e.g., Altemeyer, Milgram,
> Aronson, Arrow, etc.).  Some of our new bridges will still fall down,
> but hopefully what we know about social psychology and political
> economics will cut a few years off the time it takes to build our

> > On the end point, which text I did suppress, a good goal might be better

> Being an engineer, efficiency and effectiveness would have to top my
> list.  I.e., there has to *be* a decision, it has to be achievable
> with a reasonable expenditure of effort, and the resulting policy
> would have to be implementable and achieve the desired result.  Note
> that including consent/consensus as part of the design works strongly
> against the first two, and is although it helps with the third, it
> certainly isn't a necessary component.  As I've stated before (and
> studies have shown), people will obey a law they don't like and
> wouldn't vote for as long as they consider it to be moral.  Our job as
> social engineers isn't to make everyone happy or give everyone warm
> fuzzy feelings that their input is valued.  We just need to build
> systems that are better than those we're currently using which are not
> only corrupt and inefficient, but are so unreliable that they put our
> very existence as a species at risk.
