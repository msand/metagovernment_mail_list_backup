Very interesting, Ned.

yes, of course Vilfredo cannot handle lengthy proposals. Nor can it handle
lists, unless you break them up in singular items. At which point it might
be very inefficient. A different system should be designed to handle lists,
where each person can add an element to the list. Each person can raise
objections to an element in the list. And raise dependencies between
elements.
This can only be there if that is there.
This one instead is mutually exclusive respect to that other.
This system would be an alternative to whatever we have right now and has
not yet been developed (or even thought properly).

I am not sure if you really need to do the work you suggest to produce a
Vision. Especially if we accept the principle that decisions should be
reviewed regularly. I would go for a "good enough for now, safe enough to
try" kind of temporary statement. And then proceed from there. In Italy we
say that the perfect is an enemy of the good.

Said that if you think doing this directly would be damaging for some
reasons (not good enough, not safe enough) then I will support you in
however you want to develop the vision.

Cheers,
Pietro

> I see a community Vision quest as potentially a very worthwhile project.
> The question then becomes, how to proceed?
>
> We could start by hammering out a single vision statement through
> Vilfredo, as we did the Home Page text. But, in order for a brief vision
> statement to actually mean anything useful, it must be backed by an actual
> vision of something. If the something is a social system or social
> sub-system, it is going to be a very complex thingy, with myriad moving
> parts all interconnected and interacting. (Just a brief overview
> description of Scott's "ThePlan" is 85 pages long. The brief overview
> description of Isonomea that was on wiser.org had 160 pages. The Votorola
> material is similarly lengthy, as are some of the Sociocracy texts.)
> Vilfredo cannot handle such lengthy and complex proposals.(Yes?)
>
> My thought is that we need to have already reached a common vision in
> order for it to be worthwhile for us to try to produce a brief vision
> statement backed by that common vision.
>
> So then the question becomes, how might we best begin the project of
> reaching a common vision?
>
> My (here proposed) answer to this question is that it might be good to
> start by proposing fundamental "filter principles", that any proposed
> vision must be in accord with (in order to be considered as a candidate).
> Coming up with a list of such filter-principles to use in vetting candidate
> visions would be very metagovernmenty work, and if done well would produce
> a list that would potentially have lasting and general usefulness. (Scott
> will want to have "effective" and "efficient" and maybe "Paleolithic" on
> that list.)
>
> Once we have the list of filter-principles in hand, we would then have a
> very powerful tool to apply in trying to select/synthesize a common vision.
> Once we had a settled vision, we would then be ready to hammer out a brief
> vision statement backed by that vision.
>
> The foregoing is (now) my proposed plan for executing "The Metagovernment
> Vision Project".
>
> The right way to use Vilfredo at the beginning of this plan (if Vilfredo
> is to be our tool) would be to propose each individual filter-principle
> separately to Vilfredo. Then the list of filter-principles would be
> populated by the winning result of each Vilfredo thread.
>
> In the interest of grounding this discussion with an example: were we to
> decide to undertake "The Metagovernment Vision Project", and to execute the
> plan here proposed, and to use Vilfredo as our synthesis/decision tool, my
> first filter-principle proposal (to Vilfredo) would be THE PRIME DIRECTIVE:
>
>  To be acceptable, a candidate vision (social system or sub-system) must
> be explicitly, intentionally designed to eliminate Power Over Other People
> in every way possible. By design, there must be no concentrations of power
> entailed, caused, or permitted within or by the envisioned social
> (sub-)system.
>
> The reason for this is simple. It does not matter through what means power
> is initially concentrated (through political, criminal, police, military,
> economic, financial, religious, or whatever other means) -- the end result
> will always be the same because power is fungible: any form of concentrated
> power can be used to acquire more power of any kind. As soon as there is
> any concentration of power of any kind produced by any social sub-system,
> the overall social system will commence to morph toward further
> concentration of power: the existence of concentrations of power sets up
> positive feedback loops that lead to further concentration of power.
>
>  (Given that Vilfredo always wants to start with a question, I guess we
> would have to play Jeopardy to come up with a question to which this
> proposal would appropriately respond ...)

>>>
>>> The first sentence expresses one particular design specification that I
>>> heartily embrace (for the new global social system that is my
>>> destination-vision).
>>>
>>> The second sentence does not at all express my vision of how to get
>>> there.
>>>
>>> First, "spreading out" is not the important part; "spreading up" is the
>>> important part. For example, the CIA has spent the last hundred years in
>>> South and Central America systematically crushing every community that
>>> appeared to be on the way to implementing a better/successful social
>>> system. The Powers That Be cannot afford to have better examples existing,
>>> so they crush such examples as they appear. (Read "Confessions of an
>>> Economic Hitman" and "War is a Racket" for evidence.) The "how to get
>>> there" strategy that the sentence suggests fails to take this reality into
>>> account. (Just because the Hobbits in the Shire choose to ignore Mordor
>>> does not mean that Mordor will ignore the Hobbits in the Shire ...)
>>>
>>> Second, the notion of "collaborative governance" is very much in dispute
>>> within the community, and has been for a long time. That term cannot be
>>> used in the vision statement unless/until its meaning has been clarified
>>> and agreed upon.  (See for example:
>>> http://www.metagovernment.org/wiki/Collaborative_Governance_v2.0 )
>>>
>>>
>>> The third/last sentence of the vision statement again expresses several
