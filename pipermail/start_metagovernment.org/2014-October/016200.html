On 23.10.2014 18:13, Bruce Nappi wrote:
> Sorry I couldn't jump into this discussion sooner. After years of 
> grappling with the democracy problem, I've proposed a very radical new 
> approach. The KEY was solving the freedom problem. No matter how 
> people juggle the elements of current democracy, after a vote is taken 
> to select a SINGLE approach, all of those who don't agree with it get 
> their freedom of choice denied. I solved that riddle. You can read 
> about it on my website A3society.org/democracy 
> <http://A3society.org/democracy> . Briefly, this new approach uses 
> voting in a very different way. Rather than creating one choice that 
> can be voted yes or no, or two polarized approaches to choose from, it 
> creates a SMORGASBORD bill with many options. Each option is tagged 
> with a cost and related restrictions and limitations. When people 
> "vote", they vote separately on each option. The votes are "private" 
> from public view, but recorded. There is no longer anything like a 
> bill. After the vote is taken, EVERYONE GETS WHAT THEY CHOSE! Everyone 
> pays the cost that was stated in the "bill" and must comply with its 
> limits and restrictions.
>
> Please don't react to this with yes and no thinking. Try to follow 
> where it goes. There would be no need for legislatures like we have. 
> This is direct democracy in action. There is no longer any structure 
> for political parties. There is no longer any structure for 
> legislature corruption. There is no long any structure for 49% of the 
> voters to be denied their choice. The need to give decision makers an 
> exam, as noted in a comment below, is no longer even in the picture.
>
> The first challenge that comes up is, of course, how does this address 
> what appear to be single option decisions? For example: should we 
> build a bridge or not? Should we go to war? Should a shopping mall be 
> allowed a permit? But in each of these "apparent" single option cases, 
> they are not so single optioned. Take the U.S. and the current ISIS 
> disaster. This does not have to be treated like a single issue. Sure, 
> there's the "bomb them" crowd. Let them vote to do that. Remember, 
> there's a price tag. So, each "bomb" vote might have a $50,000 price 
> tag that the voter must commit to pay. The vote, however, would also 
> allow the "negotiate" voters to chose their option, which may have a 
> different cost, like $2,000. HOWEVER, this process would also allow 
> voters who want to approach the problem through "humanitarian aid" to 
> choose that option, and select from a range of support costs: $100, 
> $500, $1000, "pick your contribution". This last option makes it clear 
> that we have to approach this new world with totally different 
> thinking. What is the "value" for a voter in choosing a $100 support 
> package vs. $1000? Do they get future travel vouchers to the war area 
> after it recovers? Maybe business vouchers? This will surely force us 
> to be smarter about what we are doing.
