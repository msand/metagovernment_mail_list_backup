>    - THE DESIGN PROCESS: AN INTROSPECTION

> ------------------------------
> THE DESIGN PROCESS: AN INTROSPECTION
>
> What follows is my "watching myself" report of the systems design process
> that I have been living for the past many years. In brief, here is the
> process flowchart:
>
>    1. Formulate The Question
>    2. Live The Question
>    3. The Popcorn Process
>    4. Translate The "Understandings"
>    5. Break The New Design
>    6. LOOP: Formulate The Next Question
>
> ------------------------------
> Formulate The Question
>
> We need an example, and since this is an introspective report, we'll use
> my first question.
>
> My purposeful social systems design work began on February 22, 1996. I was
> in the middle of reading a book by Paul Hawken (The Ecology of Commerce),
> and experienced one of them thar epiphanies:
>
>    1. In the book, Mr. Hawken was suggesting tweaks to our existing
>    social systems that he expected would make them better. I understood that
>    the tweaks he was suggesting were some of the best tweaks that I had
>    encountered up to that point.
>    2. I understood that what he was suggesting would not work (that the
>    tweaks would not make our existing social systems significantly better).
>    3. I understood that my seeing that his ideas would not work "put the
>    ball in my court": If this won't work, what will? That was the original
>    question for all of the work that I have done since.
>
>  Since that original [meta]question, there have been thousands of
> questions formulated, right down to the question presently at hand: "How am
> I going to help Max understand the difference between a bureaucrat and a
> systems designer?"
>
> The design process is intensely recursive. "Formulate the question" is the
> first step of the process, and is at the same a project in its own right,
> to which the entire process may apply: "How shall I formulate this
> question?"
> ------------------------------
> Live The Question
>
> Once formulated, the question must then be "activated", which includes
> both "grokking the question in fullness" and energizing it (caring about
> finding answers). For me, this is not an active process. It has more to do
> with becoming quiet and balanced and open. Things like meditation can help.
> The step has to do with clarifying intention, not with trying to figure out.
> ------------------------------
> The Popcorn Process
>
> To use an analogy, living the question heats the oil that the popcorn is
> sitting in. As the heat becomes sufficient, at first one-by-one, and then
> perhaps in a flood, answers to the question will start "popping in". Their
> popping in does not involve any direct effort on my part, and in fact is
> more likely when I am not making mental effort, while showering or cutting
> vegetables or whatever.
>
> I have no idea where the answers pop in "from", nor do I have any
> introspective insight into the processes that produce the answers. Maybe
> all of the processing occurs in my physical brain, or maybe the answers are
> telepathically communicated from the Galactic Logos -- I don't know. (Those
> are not necessarily the only two possibilities ...)
> ------------------------------
> Translate The "Understandings"
>
> Sometimes answers arrive in "ready to use" form: sentences, pictures,
> design drawings, etc. But, for me, much more often they arrive as "wordless
> understandings", that must then be translated into words and design
> elements and such. The translating can be hard work, and can introduce
> distortions, so that the answer-as-translated may wind up being
> significantly different that the answer-as-received. This is particularly
> true when the original is very abstract and then must be applied concretely.
>
> From the perspective of the adage: "creativity is 10% inspiration and 90%
> perspiration", the Popcorn Process is the 10% inspiration, and the
> translation process is the 90% perspiration.
> ------------------------------
> Break The New Design
>
> Applying the answers received results in a new or modified design. If one
> is a real designer (and not just a bureaucrat or a politician pretending),
> the work (and play!) then becomes to find as many ways as possible to break
> the new design. Thought experiments, simulations, bench-testing, laboratory
> experiments, field tests -- the sincere whole-hearted aim is to find ways
> to break the design.
>
> In the beginning, breaking the design is easy -- just looking at it hard
> may break it. As the design progresses through iterations of the process,
> finding ways to break it becomes harder - in fact, that is one of the main
> ways that progress can be measured.
>
> Again the design process is intensely recursive: answering "How can I
> break this design?" is a project in its own right, to which the entire
> process may apply.
> ------------------------------
> LOOP: Formulate The Next Question
>
> Whenever a way is found to break the design, it leads to the next
> iteration of the design process: "How can the design be modified to survive
> that way of breaking?".
> ------------------------------
> ------------------------------
> FACILITATING THE DESIGN PROCESS
>
> So now that we have a sketch of the design process in hand, we can ask
> what might be done to help the process along.
>
> The first answer to that question that jumps right out is that we can
> collaborate and share, with respect to each step of the process (with the
> possible exception of the "Live The Question" step).
>
> One example of the sort of thing that we could share, and collaborate in
> compiling, would be a list of criteria that could be used to evaluate and
> compare different decision system designs. This list might potentially and
> particularly be useful (to us systems designers) with respect to Step 1
> ("Formulate the Question"), Step 3 (The Popcorn Process), Step 4
> ("Translate"), and Step 5 ("Break the New Design").
>
> We systems designers "don't do RFPs", so it would not be useful to us for
> that. We CREATE systems; RFPs are for SELECTING among systems that others
> have created. (Bureaucrats don't create; they select.)
>
> Bentley recently undertook a project of this sort: he examined all of the
> discussion tools he could find, and compiled a spreadsheet of design
> elements and patterns that they manifested. He then went through and asked,
> with respect to each element and pattern: "Is there a way to make AllAngles
> better by incorporating this feature into its design?" (At least that is
> what my ouija board is telling me he did -- we would have to ask Bentley to
> know for sure ...)
