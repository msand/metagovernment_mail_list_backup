Pietro Speroni di Fenizio said:
> Hi Michael,
> thanks for looking into Vilfredo. Thanks for taking the time.
> 
> Vilfredo is progressing a lot recently. We had some funding from an italian
> government foundation and we are re-writing the code. The new one will is
> being written in Python with a framework and a complete division front end
> backend. This was one of the main problems that kept it from developing in
> the past.
> 
> We also awakened the google
> group<https://groups.google.com/forum/?fromgroups#!forum/vilfredo-develops-in-athens>,
> and invited any person who was interested in it to come and join us. You
> are of course welcome to join in, as anyone else who is in the
> metagovernment group.
> 
> Regarding the documentation, it is unfortunately correct that the
> documentation around is incomplete. Especially the English one (the Italian
> one is a bit better with a series of 4 ten minutes video that explain
> pretty much all). I have started gathering all the information at the github
> wiki page <https://github.com/pietrosperoni/Vilfredo/wiki>. For now there
> are three videos in English. And yes, we should add a page to it on the
> front page.
> 
> Having now worked on Vilfredo for 4 years I learned many things. One of
> them is that it works well, in fact very well as a tool for small groups
> (5-25 people) to build consensus. And it only works for some kind of
> questions. The one that I generally call the "how" questions. So I do not
> present it anymore as an eDemocracy tool, but as a consensus building tool.
> This might seem like a big limit, but actually it is VERY common that when
> you go to the nitty gritty work on trying to write a text, or taking a
> decision, you always have less than 15 people. There are even people who
> claim that you cannot be productive if you have more than 12 people
> discussing on a topic. If Vilfredo could double this limit from 12 to 24 it
> would have made a huge improvement. And not just for the government.
> 
> As part of our involvement with the foundation we got in touch with several
> people working for the parliament, or for various parties. Right now some
> people of the Italian Pirate Party have started using Vilfredo as a side
> tool to their liquid feedback to develop proposal that they then insert in
> liquid feedback. On the other hand two italian MP have suggested that
> eventually we might use Vilfredo for the Italian Parliamentary committee.
> Those are the committee that decides the actual laws, before they are voted
> by the parliament. This is of course a long term dream, but at least you
> can see what is that we are aiming for.
> 
> Vilfredo itself has changed a lot. From the simple system we use to have,
> it now have much more feedback loops, and integrations. When you vote
> against a proposal you are expected to explain what's wrong with it. After
> you have voted you can see how others have voted, and change your vote, to
> get the best Pareto Front you can get.
> 
> For me I keep on presenting it here and there. I develop the theory.
> 
> What really Vilfredo would need are people working in the user interface.
> But the foundation I am working with makes some really good user interface.
> You can see here <http://partecipa.civici.civiclinks.it/> a tool we just
> developed together which is now being used by the government to ask for
> ideas on how to change the constitution. The triangles of votes are my idea
> (with a Russian colleague) from a paper we presented at ePart of a couple
> of years ago.
> 
> So, yes, Vilfredo is progressing nice and steadily. I haven't checked in
> with Metagovernment lately. Mostly because I felt the group has moved on so
> much I would have to explain everything about it, and I just did not have
> the time. If I think Metagovernment was the first group to actively use and
> test Vilfredo :-).
> 
> Oh, yes, I also started making videos. Sometimes from my talks, but not
> always. Here <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8cQuz9kAybI> is for example a
> very nice (IMNSHO) video in which I explain the graphs. Something which is
> slowly becoming one of the main features in Vilfredo.
>
