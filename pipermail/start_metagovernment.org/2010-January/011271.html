Thanks for the link, Suresh.  From my perspective, here are the most
important points:

 

Innovative ideas should not
<http://blog.spigit.com/permalink/2009/04/15/corporate_innovation_is_not_a_p
opularity>  always be subject to a popularity contest, and this is often
where crowdsourcing contests fall short. Votes don't always provide enough
input on an idea's value
<http://commetrics.com/articles/crowds-do-not-innovate/>  . 

 

. the issue with ideas in government is not in their vision or objectives,
but in their execution.

 

The truth of the latter statement is not limited to .gov agencies.  Good
ideas are a dime a dozen.  What is rare and far more expensive than
necessary are good implementations.  StratML Part 2
<http://xml.gov/stratml/index.htm#Part2>  is about turning strategic plans
into performance plans.  What matters is the inputs (resources) and
processes that can be brought to bear to accomplish objectives.  To the
degree that the focus is on forcing others to do anything rather than on
that which is within our power to do individually and collectively for
ourselves, voting is a distraction in the near-term and counterproductive in
the long run.  It merely serves to enable us to project the problem onto
someone else and absolve ourselves from acting.

 

BTW, in the U.S. federal government, the (former) leaders of the CIO
Council's (CIOC) Architecture and Infrastructure Committee (AIC)
commissioned the ET.gov site <http://et.gov/> /process to facilitate the
identification and formation of communities of practice (CoPs) around
emerging (innovative) technology components and specifications, i.e., to
crowdsource consideration of emerging technologies to groups of the experts.
However, based upon the relatively light usage of the site and process, one
might conclude that: a) .gov folks don't place much priority on
collaborating around emerging technologies, and/or b) the ET.gov site and
process have been poorly implemented.
