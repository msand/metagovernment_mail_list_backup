> On Thu, Jan 21, 2010 at 2:54 PM, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org 
> > wrote:
> I'll just shoehorn in here that I think there need to be some real  
> attempts at definition. There are now many words such as open  
> government, government 2.0, and eDemocracy to name some popular  
> ones, with very little consensus on what any of them mean.
>
> I'll reiterate what I said on the originating thread, which is that  
> there appear to be (at least) four distinct areas of focus:
> 1. Government data, transparency & accessibility of services
> 2. Participation in government processes (bureaucratic)
> 3. Political processes, campaigns & elections
> 4. Participation in government decision-making (legislative &  
> executive)
>
> The term "government 2.0" usually seems to apply to only 1 & 2  
> (though for some people only 1).
>
> "eDemocracy" tends to refer most to 3, with 4 (sometimes) and 2  
> (occasionally) included.
>
> And fwiw, Metagovernment (our latest favored term is "collaborative  
> governance") is focused almost exclusively on 4, which tends to get  
> overlooked most... particularly by politicians, who have a vested  
> interest in shifting focus away from it.
>
> And on a separate note, I'd like to emphasize that a US focus, while  
> inevitable for US-based meetings, should be at least somewhat de- 
> emphasized for two reasons. First, the most desperate need for  
> openness is not in the federal government, where we see quite a lot  
> already; but in local government, which is often completely opaque,  
> inaccessible, and corrupt. Second, because the internet knows no  
> boundaries. Instead of fighting the inevitable by trying to force  
> web-based processes to obey geographic limits, we should be  
> embracing the connectedness allowed by a global internet.
>
> Note that these two points mesh nicely: instead of focusing on a  
> single national government, if focus is placed on localities, the  
> ideas are much more readily ported around the world.
>

>> I think piggybacking on Politics Online is a smart move, and the  
>> difference in focus should be seen as a strength rather than a  
>> weakness.  Those of us who organize the OpenNY forum (the  
>> OpenGovNYC meetup re-branded) were planning to repeat our PdF- 
>> piggybacking this year as well.  We recently heard from Micah and  
>> Andrew that PdF was already planning its own unconference, and from  
>> what I understand from a distance, we're figuring out right now how  
>> it all fits together.
>>
>> I'm laying this all out because I think the overall lesson is that  
>> we ought to coordinate our efforts across all of these groups to  
>> provide an ongoing open gov narrative.  Think of it as a giant open  
>> space grid with the all of the the cities in the U.S. on the X  
>> axis, and the entire 2010 calendar on the Y axis (I've been pushing  
>> a similar vision for NY).
>>
>> This was, in part, the rationale behind Participation Camp.  It was  
>> meant to build on Transparency Camp, and a Collaboration Camp was  
>> discussed for December but never happened.
>>
>> To wrap, from what I can see in the threads, the highly-motivated  
>> band of open gov organizers on these lists is particularly focused  
>> on how to make *authentic citizen participation* a reality.   
>> Politics Online and Transparency Camp are focused, as they should  
>> be, on specific domains, but there's a need to focus on  
>> participation specifically.  Participation, I'd argue, is the "hard  
>> problem" of open government, and needs the most TLC to help it grow  
>> to maturity.
>>
>> We need to find some way to build momentum through these camps and  
>> coordinate existing (fragmented) efforts.
>>
