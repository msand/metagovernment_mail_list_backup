Thanks for continuing to think of us, Suresh.

I think the core problem we're facing in regard to your fund is mostly  
one of timing: Our member projects are all relatively undeveloped, and  
looking toward any kind of financial sustainability has not seemed  
practical. Most of the projects are fairly ideals-based (contrast to  
distributed manufacturing, which seems to have a fundamental profit  
motive), and have been more interested in getting software out the  
door than working out "details" like money.

That said, there certainly might exist models for collaborative  
governance software to be used commercially, such as in private  
organizations. But I think all of our member projects release their  
code on a very liberal open license, so I'm not sure if that is  
practical. I have to leave it to the individual projects to determine  
if they see potential here.

P.S. For those who haven't followed, these are the current active  
projects of the Metagovernment project:
http://metagovernment.org/active

> The difference in the two approaches was that we focused on making  
> it clear that the rationale behind the Pooled Fund is that it is  
> focusing on Building Capacity and Project Scalability.
>
> After discussions with several groups in the space we identified the  
> following items (below) as the information that we need in order to  
> try to drive collaboration within the space and to then finance the  
> group that comes together.
>
> Maybe the drivers for collaboration are different in the OpenGov  
> space (fine distinctions/definitions notwithstanding) and therefore   
> the engagement models will be different. This would mean that we  
> would have to gather different information and approach the problem  
> differently.
>
> Let me know your thoughts on whether a similar approach might bear  
> fruit with those that you are working with.
>
>
> Structural Variables
> Project Mission Statement
> Project Goals and associated tasks: what do you want to accomplish  
> in the next 12 months?
> Project Needs: what are your resource requirements?
> Business Model: can your project be self sustaining in the next 12  
> months
> Relationships: what projects do you have a relationship with? What  
> projects could you have a relationship with where, collectively, you  
> can develop a collective value proposition.
> Scalability Variables
> Is your output part of value chain? It is easy to use output of  
> project as a building block towards building other things.
> is your design modular? - designed for interoperability
> is your product replicable? - released under an open license,  
> shareable online via a clone-able repository.
> Project consistently applies a local standard. (designed for  
> interoperability)
> Sharing parts and suppliers
> Project is sufficiently visible in the network.
>

>> On Thu, Jan 21, 2010 at 2:54 PM, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org 
>> > wrote:
>> I'll just shoehorn in here that I think there need to be some real  
>> attempts at definition. There are now many words such as open  
>> government, government 2.0, and eDemocracy to name some popular  
>> ones, with very little consensus on what any of them mean.
>>
>> I'll reiterate what I said on the originating thread, which is that  
>> there appear to be (at least) four distinct areas of focus:
>> 1. Government data, transparency & accessibility of services
>> 2. Participation in government processes (bureaucratic)
>> 3. Political processes, campaigns & elections
>> 4. Participation in government decision-making (legislative &  
>> executive)
>>
>> The term "government 2.0" usually seems to apply to only 1 & 2  
>> (though for some people only 1).
>>
>> "eDemocracy" tends to refer most to 3, with 4 (sometimes) and 2  
>> (occasionally) included.
>>
>> And fwiw, Metagovernment (our latest favored term is "collaborative  
>> governance") is focused almost exclusively on 4, which tends to get  
>> overlooked most... particularly by politicians, who have a vested  
>> interest in shifting focus away from it.
>>
>> And on a separate note, I'd like to emphasize that a US focus,  
>> while inevitable for US-based meetings, should be at least somewhat  
>> de-emphasized for two reasons. First, the most desperate need for  
>> openness is not in the federal government, where we see quite a lot  
>> already; but in local government, which is often completely opaque,  
>> inaccessible, and corrupt. Second, because the internet knows no  
>> boundaries. Instead of fighting the inevitable by trying to force  
>> web-based processes to obey geographic limits, we should be  
>> embracing the connectedness allowed by a global internet.
>>
>> Note that these two points mesh nicely: instead of focusing on a  
>> single national government, if focus is placed on localities, the  
>> ideas are much more readily ported around the world.
>>
