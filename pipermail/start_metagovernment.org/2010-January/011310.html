Replying to Ed (verification) and Pietro (forking);
also mooting the idea of register mirroring (bottom):

Ed Pastore wrote
> I can think of so many that I don't know where to start. There are
> obvious ones, like an admin deleting a post they don't like, or
> changing a user's trust rating...  But then there are subtle things
> like an admin moving an issue they like to greater prominence, or
> authenticating a single sock puppet past the sock puppet
> controls. These latter sorts of things may not be caught for a long
> time, which is why relying on the "crowd" to police the admins isn't
> reliable. Sure, people could fork to a new engine... but only if
> they notice a reason to do so.

You mention trust levels and sock puppets.  Both come together in the
registration lists.  Registration lists are what the voting engines
see, so the crucial thing is to ensure they're correct.  I added a use
case for this, with example data from the live trustserver:
http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Streetwiki#Verification_of_registration_lists

There should be no way for an administrator to tamper with that data.
Can anyone see a loophole?  We should be able to prototype most of the
verification stuff, within a week or two.  It's part of the prototype
design.

> If however the admin's actions had to be duplicated by three other
> geographically disparate admins, then the chance of any kind of tampering is
> extremely low.

This is essentially what the watchdog/verifiers do.  They duplicate -
both data and procedures.  It should be possible for any technically
inclined user to verify an entire voter register on her own, in this
way.

> Again, I understand that distributed redundant systems are no easy task,
> but it is something to at least keep in mind. I am not sure if these operate
> on the same principle, but I *think* the root nameservers are set up in a
> similar way:
> http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_nameserver

They designed the Internet to be highly robust.  (You probably know,
ARPA was a military outfit.)  I guess e-democracy has to be robust,
too.  Redundancy is a big part of that (I agree).

Pietro Speroni di Fenizio wrote:
> ... The old forum broke down, and we, the old crowd, decided we
> wanted now to make a new forum, where we could decide how it would
> work. We discussed all the details, and also the name. Among us was
> a new person, we did not know very well. Before we finished deciding
> he bought the domain name, installed a standard forum, and invited
> everybody in. Calling it "our website". But he kept all
> control. When we discussed this further, the community has already
> settled. It was starting to grow. We tried to set up a concurrent
> community, but the community would only visit it in the
> week-ends. And eventually it died... It was a great lesson.
>
>  After some years this guy lost interest in the topic, so he defined
> a few low level administrators to policy the system, went to live in
> Africa, and we pretty much lost all tracks of him. The board
> remained there, with its google adsense, request for donations when
> you sign in, and is now the top forum in its topic... Forking only
> makes sense if you have some really good novelty.

Hopefully it won't be possible for anything like that to happen within
the e-dem infrastructure.  All essential services must be fork-able.
Even if a fork isn't popular at first, it must be viable.  For the
most part, I think the design of the prototype ensures this.

An idea: we might improve fork-ability by enabling a form of register
mirroring.  It would have use cases similar to vote mirroring:
http://t.zelea.com/wiki/User:ThomasvonderElbe_GmxDe/Vote_mirroring#Use_cases
But instead of users free-ranging across voting engines, they would
free-range across competing streetwikis and voter registers.  (If this
is a good idea, I think we can probably prototype it in a few weeks.)
