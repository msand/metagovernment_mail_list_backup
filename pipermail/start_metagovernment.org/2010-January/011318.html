>> I'll just shoehorn in here that I think there need to be some real
>> attempts at definition. There are now many words such as open government,
>> government 2.0, and eDemocracy to name some popular ones, with very little
>> consensus on what any of them mean.
>>
>> I'll reiterate what I said on the originating thread, which is that there
>> appear to be (at least) four distinct areas of focus:
>> 1. Government data, transparency & accessibility of services
>> 2. Participation in government processes (bureaucratic)
>> 3. Political processes, campaigns & elections
>> 4. Participation in government decision-making (legislative & executive)
>>
>> The term "government 2.0" usually seems to apply to only 1 & 2 (though for
>> some people only 1).
>>
>> "eDemocracy" tends to refer most to 3, with 4 (sometimes) and 2
>> (occasionally) included.
>>
>> And fwiw, Metagovernment (our latest favored term is "collaborative
>> governance") is focused almost exclusively on 4, which tends to get
>> overlooked most... particularly by politicians, who have a vested interest
>> in shifting focus away from it.
>>
>> And on a separate note, I'd like to emphasize that a US focus, while
>> inevitable for US-based meetings, should be at least somewhat de-emphasized
>> for two reasons. First, the most desperate need for openness is not in the
>> federal government, where we see quite a lot already; but in local
>> government, which is often completely opaque, inaccessible, and corrupt.
>> Second, because the internet knows no boundaries. Instead of fighting the
>> inevitable by trying to force web-based processes to obey geographic limits,
>> we should be embracing the connectedness allowed by a global internet.
>>
>> Note that these two points mesh nicely: instead of focusing on a single
>> national government, if focus is placed on localities, the ideas are much
>> more readily ported around the world.
>>

>>>> Hello All,
>>>>
>>>> I woke up this morning to three different threads regarding this year's
>>>> open gov unconferences.  I'm actually in Melbourne from now until the end of
>>>> May, working with Collabforge <http://collabforge.com> and diving into
>>>> the Australian Open Gov scene (which is quite ripe! check out the Aus Fed's Gov
>>>> 2.0 task force <http://gov2.net.au/> blog).  I'll be back just in time
>>>> for PdF and the accompanying unconference.
>>>>
>>>> (Apologies to those of you on multiple lists who will receive duplicates
>>>> of this... And thanks Steven and Wayne for asking for my opinion :-) You got
>>>> it.)
>>>>
>>>> Participation Camp did indeed piggyback on PdF, and this worked out
>>>> quite well, even though I started things far too late to really integrate
>>>> with PdF's plans.  One great moment was having Steven, Craig Newmark, and a
>>>> bunch of other open gov heavies who were in town for PdF in on an excellent
>>>> session by local open gov leader City Councilmember Gale Brewer and attended
>>>> by many of NYC's most active on-the-ground open gov organizers.
>>>>
>>>> I think piggybacking on Politics Online is a smart move, and the
>>>> difference in focus should be seen as a strength rather than a weakness.
>>>> Those of us who organize the OpenNY forum <http://opennyforum.org> (the
>>>> OpenGovNYC meetup re-branded) were planning to repeat our PdF-piggybacking
>>>> this year as well.  We recently heard from Micah and Andrew that PdF was
>>>> already planning its own unconference, and from what I understand from a
>>>> distance, we're figuring out right now how it all fits together.
>>>>
>>>> I'm laying this all out because I think the overall lesson is that we
>>>> ought to coordinate our efforts across all of these groups to provide an
>>>> ongoing open gov narrative.  Think of it as a giant open space grid with the
>>>> all of the the cities in the U.S. on the X axis, and the entire 2010
>>>> calendar on the Y axis (I've been pushing a similar vision for NY<http://opennyforum.org/open-space/>
>>>> ).
>>>>
>>>> This was, in part, the rationale behind Participation Camp.  It was
>>>> meant to build on Transparency Camp, and a Collaboration Camp was discussed
>>>> for December but never happened.
>>>>
>>>> To wrap, from what I can see in the threads, the highly-motivated band
>>>> of open gov organizers on these lists is particularly focused on how to make
>>>> *authentic citizen participation* a reality.  Politics Online and
>>>> Transparency Camp are focused, as they should be, on specific domains, but
>>>> there's a need to focus on participation specifically.  Participation, I'd
>>>> argue, is the "hard problem" of open government, and needs the most TLC to
>>>> help it grow to maturity.
>>>>
>>>> We need to find some way to build momentum through these camps and
>>>> coordinate existing (fragmented) efforts.
>>>>
>>>> That's my ten cents.  I have some ideas on what this might look like,
>>>> but I've written enough :)
>>>>
>>>> G'day!
