I'll just shoehorn in here that I think there need to be some real  
attempts at definition. There are now many words such as open  
government, government 2.0, and eDemocracy to name some popular ones,  
with very little consensus on what any of them mean.

I'll reiterate what I said on the originating thread, which is that  
there appear to be (at least) four distinct areas of focus:
1. Government data, transparency & accessibility of services
2. Participation in government processes (bureaucratic)
3. Political processes, campaigns & elections
4. Participation in government decision-making (legislative & executive)

The term "government 2.0" usually seems to apply to only 1 & 2 (though  
for some people only 1).

"eDemocracy" tends to refer most to 3, with 4 (sometimes) and 2  
(occasionally) included.

And fwiw, Metagovernment (our latest favored term is "collaborative  
governance") is focused almost exclusively on 4, which tends to get  
overlooked most... particularly by politicians, who have a vested  
interest in shifting focus away from it.

And on a separate note, I'd like to emphasize that a US focus, while  
inevitable for US-based meetings, should be at least somewhat de- 
emphasized for two reasons. First, the most desperate need for  
openness is not in the federal government, where we see quite a lot  
already; but in local government, which is often completely opaque,  
inaccessible, and corrupt. Second, because the internet knows no  
boundaries. Instead of fighting the inevitable by trying to force web- 
based processes to obey geographic limits, we should be embracing the  
connectedness allowed by a global internet.

Note that these two points mesh nicely: instead of focusing on a  
single national government, if focus is placed on localities, the  
ideas are much more readily ported around the world.
