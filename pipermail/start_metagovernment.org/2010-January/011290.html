Ed Pastore wrote:
> I'm still caught up in a business trip and can't dig through all of this,

> leery of is the above, where precedence is ultimately given to admins.

> (ie corruption) in admins.

I answered generally to your concern on the 10th:
http://metagovernment.org/pipermail/start_metagovernment.org/2010-January/002480.html
Do you have a specific example of corruption?  We can try to document
it under the use cases.

> Additionally, in terms of trust, I'd like to remind you of Manuel & 
> Pietro's idea of an internal trust mechanism (sorry; can't dig up the 
> reference from my phone). It basically uses spheres of influence to 
> determine user authenticity.
>
> As an example, if I set up a hundred sock puppets and have them all rate 
> each other as trustworthy, the fact that all of their relationships are 
> internal to their group (with no/few external relationships) would allow 
> the software to essentially collapse them all into one person.

Detecting sock puppets by statistics or pattern matching, and raising
alarms - sounds OK (maybe tricky, like Pietro says).  In any case, the
machine probably shouldn't take corrective action on its own.  It'll
make mistakes.  We'd rather allow a sock puppet to live another day,
than offend a bonafide user.

I documented the principle use case for sock puppets.  It illustrates
how the trust network is supposed to function:
http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Streetwiki#Policing_for_local_sock_puppets

The crucial problems I see at this point - and where documentation
should help - are:

  a) Visualization in the wiki, especially the layout of
     neighbourhoods, streets and other common areas.  We need to
     understand what we're trying to build in there (or I do anyway,
     because it's not always clear to me).

  b) Explaining it to people - concerns were expressed off list that
     streetwikis might be controversial
