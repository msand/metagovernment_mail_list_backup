> Dear Alexander
> Is hard to learn to use tools, imagine to learn coding the tool.

It's not about coding the tool, it's just about starting to learn the
basics of coding.
It can start with "markdown" to co-create a wiki on github. Afterwards
comes some html/css to make a static page on github.
Then later can come some javascript basics to add interactivity - and i'm
sure the more high level concepts of what is being programmed can be more
easily discussed in english if there is slowly a basic understanding of
programming itself.

Some people might have an understanding of how a car engine or a bicycle
works - this doesnt mean they can build bicycle parts or a car engine, but
at least it enables them to follow a discussion about bicycle mechanics or
cars and they can contribute or ask questions - while people who have no
clue what a bicycle even is - cannot participate.



> I recently introduce Airesis to a referendarian committee that had 5 ICT
> involved in the management of this participatory group, they all consider
> Airesis too rich in features, the best they had so far was a forum!
> Now that they start to play with the toy we start to receive compliments
> for the features they did not imagine where available.
>

Which happens, because Airesis was build for a customer group without them
even knowing about it. It's only afterwards that they need to learn about
features and build an opinion.
On top of that - non-programmers cannot really imagine what is possible and
what isnt or at least what is easy and what is hard without knowing
programming.

I'm suggesting that we build our tools bottom up. Our current standard is a
mailing list and was/is a wiki. That's what the our community is using
right now.
By discussing and building automation around the processes we already use
we can move forward step by step - but I can't imagine this being done by a
few people and the rest just "smart assing" :-)



Participatory budgeting is a decisional process over left over money that
> the current administrations put on the table to show that they are willing
> to open the participation. The current processes involve organizing live
> meeting with facilitator, nothing more limited to the participation, the
> time the place the coordination take large part of the effort over the real
> decisional and deliberative  moment. Surely the face2face is making good
> impression but is worth the energy invested?
>
So your potential customer group needs live face2face meetings. I don't
have potential customer groups that insist on that. In metagovernment we
also don't have that afaik.


> I believe that the live and the virtual should go hand on hand but if we
> have to wait that every one learn to code will never happen.
>
This is just for us - our community in metagovernment should start learning
to code and we should craft our own automation around our processes.
We grow from there. If something good comes out of that over time - more
people might join - maybe young people who already know how to code.
If we document everything well and keep entrance barriers low by even
making an educational program that teaches aspects of programming necessary
to understand the tools we build it becomes even more sustainable.


> Coding is not for everyone!
>
I think here is where we differ in opinion. I think in the past people said
reading/writing is not for everyone, but in fact - without being able to
read and/or write - you cannot really participate in society. The same will
be true for code in the future and it already started - self driving cars,
drones, home automation, 3d printing, artificial body parts - all jobs of
today will be automated and the new jobs will involve telling machines what
to do - may it be "drone delivery services" or whatever. Who cannot program
cannot participate in business.
Since fall 2014 the UK teaches every kid in the nation to learn programming
so in 10 years, when this more automated world will be a reality - they
have a nation of young programmers with 10 years experience - and some say
10 years is what it takes to become an expert in something.

PERL is a cruel cryptic language.
I'm talking about the ENGLISH of programming, which means: HTML, CSS and
JAVASCRIPT - everyone who is using a browser is basically one step away
from creating interactive webpages. A Programming language is like a real
language - you can talk about the weather or you can talk about quantum
physics - if you dont understand quantum physics doesnt mean you cannot
learn the language - maybe that was the problem with PERL back then - the
topic that you had to "program" about was hard, not the language itself.
...even though i admit, perl is a very cryptic language.
