On 7/13/2011 1:38 AM, Pietro Speroni di Fenizio wrote: 

Hi Ed, 

I am not really sure how to insert this.

 

One of the things we noticed in Vilfredo is a difference between "what" and "how" questions. Or more in general between questions that are looking for a single answer, and questions where multiple concurring answers can be accepted. For example if we are speaking about, what shall we do about global warming, we have several strategies that needs to be followed in parallel. While if we have decided that we are going to build a solar panel, then we ask: "how" shall we build this solar panel? we are essentially asking a question where we want a single, complex answer.

 

The division is not strict and there are how question that needs several answers and what questions that have a single answer. And often you can rephrase a how question as a what question, and viceversa. But generally there is a difference.

 

This is important in Vilfredo because we have found how Vilfredo is a great tool for a limited group of people to tackle a how question. But it does not seem to work so well with what questions or if the number of people is too big.  And it is for this that we are developing the bubble system as part of Vilfredo.

 

Now I think this division should be expressed in the page, but I am unsure on how to add it, after all here we are speaking about type of decisions, and my discourse is about type of questions.

 

Cheers,

Pietro

 

On 12 July 2011 13:24, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:

At the recent Skype meeting, we touched on a topic which I believe needs more attention in most governance projects: clearly defining different kinds of decisions.

Some of our mechanisms may make sense for one kind of decision, but not for another. For example, coordinating the construction of a community service such as a homeless shelter may be a matter of just getting the material and labor necessary to make it happen. But making gambling illegal or legal is an societal ethical decision of a completely different context.
