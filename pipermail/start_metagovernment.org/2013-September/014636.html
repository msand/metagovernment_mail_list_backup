Max and Ned,

Thanks for replying.  Both of you point to an omission that I want to
correct before answering further.

Max said:
> ...  History of philosophy suggests item 2 [we're ideologically and
> technically non-partisan] is a sort of technocrat social engineering
> item.  Item 1 [Metagov is where it's starting] illustrates that in
> that it is a neuro linguistic programming statement (NLP). ...  So I
> am rather in that tradition but I am not interested in doing this in
> that way.

Neither am I; sorry to give the wrong impression.  My lever image (SL)
is partly to blame.  It implies force, but I intended none.  The role
of technicians is the usual one of developing and building facilities,
not ruling a technocracy.  Item (II) is intended rather as a counter
measure to technocracy, nipping in the bud any technical solution that
would dominate the field, and thence society.  How it might do that
must wait till I can answer in regard to (III).  (more below)


Ned Conner said:
> ... after studying your proposal I have the uneasy feeling that,
> through the very abstract and obscurely-defined term "reflector", a
> particular ideological stance is (perhaps) being snuck in through
> the side door, with which stance I may or may not agree. Are you
> still trying to set our community aim as that of reaching consensus
> without [or, as Mike puts it, before] making decisions?

Originally I thought of setting Metagov's sights exclusively on a
reflective start (III), but I agree that would be inconsistent with
the principle of openness (II).  Let me correct myself by saying that
a reflective start is just one start technique; I think Metagov should
be open to all start techniques and do its best to support each.  This
what I now suggest for principle (II).

> I do not quite understand what you mean when you say [II] that
> "we're ideologically and technically non-partisan". Does that mean
> that we never do and never will reach consensus or make decisions
> with respect to any ideological or technical matter? ...

No, that wouldn't be possible.  We still must make decisions and
sometimes they will bear on technical matters.  When they do, however,
I suggest we bracket them in a way that doesn't violate the principle
of openness (II, as revised).  So:

> ... (Example: Ed proposes that, in our decision systems, there be no
> distinctions between governors and governed. I heartily agree with
> that proposal. Does that make Ed and me partisans?)

No, it shouldn't.  If you believe that a particular start effort needs
to enforce a policy of "no distinctions", then you could enforce it
within the context of that start effort.  But you shouldn't enforce it
universally within Metagov if doing so would violate (II).

Otherwise message (I) rings hollow.  Only through openness to all
start possibilities can Metagov convincingly back the claim of being
the place where it starts.

Metagov already honours (I) and (II), at least in practice.  There's
no other place we can go to speak together on such an over-arching
topic and retain any hope of putting our words into action.  Metagov
already has an advantage here and I think we need to press it.


Max said:

I hope you have a better impression after reading my correction above.
Despite my forceful metaphors, I don't propose to push anyone around,

I'll stop here and give you and Ned a chance to reply.  I haven't yet
answered concerning the specifics of reflective start (III), but only
items (I) and (II), which I think are the general conditions to obtain
sufficient resources for any kind of start.
