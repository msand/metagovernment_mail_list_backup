> I agree, this an important problem.

>> The same applies to the question about the numbers of supporters and
>> rejecters of a proposed solution: how do you find out where the
>> majority is moving to?
> As you recognize, the problem is not created by our proposal and is
> not specific to it.  It already exists out there.  And we don't offer
> any specific solution to it.  But we know there are two broad
> categories or approaches to a solution.  One approach (A) is to count
> on the elimination of other tools.  Ultimately, only a single tool is
> left, or a single platform of standard, interchangeable tools.  So the
> problem itself is eliminated.  This approach is illustrated here:
> http://metagovernment.org/pipermail/start_metagovernment.org/2012-October/005111.html
>
> (B) The other approach stands on a principle of technical equality and
> freedom of choice for users.  Any tool is allowed.  It follows that no
> solution to the problem of integration can be forced in advance.  Tool
> providers are expected to negotiate solutions bilaterally, or opt into
> existing technical agreements.  So a mix of solutions is possible and
> anything may emerge.  This approach is illustrated here:
> http://metagovernment.org/pipermail/start_metagovernment.org/2012-October/005114.html
>
> Several groups have already taken approach A and we can join them.  Or
> we can take approach B and be the first.  We know that Metagov has
> always been inclined to B in principle.  All we propose here (Conseo,
> Thomas and I) is a mechanism to move forward on it.  We rig Metagov as
> a plug-in framework with a neutral tool switch.
>
