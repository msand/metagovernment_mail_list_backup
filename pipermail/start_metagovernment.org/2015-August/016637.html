On Mon, Aug 24, 2015 at 2:04 PM, Jacopo Tolja <jacopo.tolja at gmail.com> wrote:

> Some of the volunteer on the core group put some money in advance to finance
> development hoping the association will return them in better days.
> The better days are not coming and donation are still just sufficient to
> keep the sysadmin and the hosting and the domains. We need to develop the
> mobile app, finalize few bugs, make the UI more friendly and usable.

Ah. In this case, I would agree that a crowdfunding option is indeed
your best option. In my experience there are two ways these can
succeed:

1) You propose to create (or maybe just finish) a product that has
great "sex appeal" and that people will be clamoring to get their
hands on even if they've never actually used even a prototype. Not to
poo-poo your project, or the field of electronic direct democracy in
general, but I just don't see this happening for any of us.

2) You've got a loyal and dedicated customer base that is willing to
support the project because they are *already* relying on it. That is,
your existing users will be the ones who fund you. I don't know your
community will do this for you, but if it were me, this is where I'd
concentrate my efforts. If it fails, at least you know that you need
to go back to the drawing board and create the product that at least
*some* people find indispensable.

> One Public Administration in a specific town that show strong interest in

Right, *this* is what I was thinking was the major problem. Seems it's
only a minor problem, but my guess is that this will almost always
happen if you try to work *with* an existing government rather than
against them (i.e., as an alternative decisionmaking system).
