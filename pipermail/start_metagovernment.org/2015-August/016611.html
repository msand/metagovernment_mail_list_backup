There are a lot of these in the US too. They're not doing well:
Graduation rates are very low and there are many horror stories of
kids having to return to regular public schools and retake entire
grades to make up what they didn't learn in their on-line school. But
IMHO the problem is not the schools, it's the quality of the students
they're attracting (i.e., it's apparently not the best and brightest
switching, it's the kids who were struggling with public school to
begin with). But this shouldn't matter, at least if there weren't
SDAPs and others with vested interests in charge: There should be a
standard (core) curriculum and a student should be able to test out of
*anything*. Establishing that would quickly separate out the good from
the bad. And if the kids in a particular school don't make progress
(again, as measured by their test scores), their accreditation goes
away to make room for a competitor that will deliver.

We need to be able to ask the question of whether Kahn Academy is
better than your local public school. It's unanswerable now because
not only do we lack the metrics to do that assessment, but it'd be
impossible to even get the public school system to participate in this
sort of comparison because of their monopolistic and anti-competitive
mindset. Which is, again, something a true direct democracy could
solve, but IMHO won't ever be solved otherwise.

Again, my response to this is that it indicates that there is
something missing in the philosophy. Sure, there's always going to be
grunt work, but if you can't inspire *passion* in the volunteers, your
goals are either insufficiently bold, or perhaps in the wrong place
entirely. For example, a system that is founded on working with
existing government IMHO is one of the things that will drive *away*
volunteers, not encourage them. The goal at the start has to be to
*replace* the existing system, not supplement it.

Just for me, I'd rather see more quality than quantity. There are lots
of flaws even on the web site, and my recollection is that the
software was the same way (buttons that didn't work, a tutorial that
got stuck, etc.). Much better to find a niche and fill it well and
completely than try to solve every problem (and in every country) all
at once. And not to appear American (English) chauvinistic, but if you
believe that the problem is that there is insufficient critical mass
in any one country, better to put 100% of the wood behind the one
arrow that is the English language version because that by far is the
biggest potential market (indeed, as the most widely spoken second
language in the world, you could have more success with a solid
English version than if you supported every other language *combined*
if each of those others is even a little bit flaky or incomplete).
