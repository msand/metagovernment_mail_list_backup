> I am definetly against using the term open. Open is even weaker than 
> "free" for people knowing the FOSS discussions and is a buzz word 
> since the web 2.0 and social networking. If we really want to stick 
> with open, we could use "libre" instead to give it at least a more 
> international and distinct meaning.
> But "open" is almost pointless and as it has been explained before it 
> is actually redundant. Democracy implies some degree of 
> openness/transparency. We should definitely brand the term democracy 
> in a concrete way where we want to go. Having a look at the Metagov 
> wiki I think we seem to have a consensus that consensus (synthesis) 
> building is the way. So instead of simply using some technical term we 
> should put this focus on socially reflected synthesis (as in hegelian) 
> in it. We don't want to write simply another management tool, but what 
> we want to do is to map social reflection and consensus building 
> inside society as closely as possible, to get the most efficient (read 
> reflected) decision and work processes.
> My proposal therefore is to put that in front. "Consensual democracy" 
> for example. Democracy as implemented today is a very weak and 
> crippled form of it actually and does not imply the principle of 
> consensus building, although this is in reality the core strength of 
> democracy (as it is also being pointed out on the Wiki) and not its 
> weakness (as often wrongly argued). Still even the current form has 
> always been advertised with its openness, so this would be nothing new 
> then.
>
> Another possibility would be to use the term "universal" to imply that 
> we hack a system that is not bound to the principle of states any 
> more, but rather recognizes each subject as a member of a universal 
> democratic process. I don't know if this is really a consensus here, 
> though. One could of course always combine several attributes.
