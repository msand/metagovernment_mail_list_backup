The debate in question was over the term used to describe the concept, not
the group. So for the moment, Metagovernment (or Metagov) is still the name
of this group. In fact, I think there was a separate Vilfredo proposal about
that.

 

This was a discussion of whether or not we could use a better term for the
overarching idea, which this project first called Open Source Governance and
then Collaborative Governance:

http://www.metagovernment.org/wiki/Collaborative_governance

 

If you disagree, then we haven't reached consensus. Same with Michael. And
so presumably we stick with the status quo. Collaborative Governance isn't a
bad term; it was just felt by some that it is not easily understood by
others and that it doesn't convey much sense of democracy.

 

The idea behind using the term Open Democracy is that it does pretty well
bring up an image of what we are talking about. And while all democracy is
supposedly "open," the fact that we would describe this brand as "open"
hopefully makes the listener/reader question what makes it different from
all other brands of democracy.

 

Here in the U.S., I certainly don't feel that democracy is open when
Congress has secret sessions, the executive branch has entire secret
agencies and vast troves of secret information, and much of the legislative
process is done without any *chance* for me to have any meaningful input.
Much of our legislative process at every level of government is carried out
in "informal" meetings between lobbyists and politicians: not something that
is open or democratic in any way.

 

So given all of the above, can you get on board with the term, or would you
like to propose another (or stick with what we have)?

 

 

On Dec 31, 2010, at 10:36 AM, Owen Ambur wrote:





Since I apparently have not used Vilfredo to vote on this issue, I won't
feel that I've been treated unjustly if others decide to use Vilfredo (or
Votorola or any other means) to agree to use the term "Open Democracy" --
even though it doesn't make a lot of sense to me and I may not choose to use
that term myself.  (I'll probably to continue to use the term "Metagov" in
one way or another, at least until I'm banished from the group.)

 

To me, democracy is not democracy unless it is open in the sense that
everyone has an equal opportunity to influence the outcome.  So, to that
degree, it seems to me calling democracy "open" is redundant.

 

It seems to me that the primary objective of the Metagov group is to apply
electronic means to enable individuals who choose to do so to participate
more efficiently and effectively in decision-making in which they have an
interest.

 

It also seems to me that it cannot accurately be called "open" unless
individuals are free to use any means *they* choose to participate.

 

Thus, while it doesn't make a lot of sense to me yet, I am encouraged by my
understanding of the intent of Michael's so-called "cross-forum theatre". 

 

(It seems to me that what is required to support the concept efficiently and
effectively is an open data standard for posting the necessary information
on the Web.  To me, it seems that XML is the logical choice for the format
of the data and that the challenge is to agree upon the core elements and
structure of the data to be shared.)

 

"Metagov: Open, Electronically Facilitated Group Decision-Making on the Web"
may not be a concise or catchy phrase but it seems to me to be what we're
talking about, isn't it?

 

Meta ~ about http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meta

Government
<http://www.bing.com/Dictionary/search?q=define+government&FORM=DTPDIA&qpvt=
define+government>  ~ the management or control of something

"Gov" (as in .gov) implies *electronic* government (eGov)

 

Metagov is about the use of electronic means to manage or control something.


 

Personally, I'd prefer that such means be "open" (in the sense of open
data). 

 

I am adamantly opposed to trying to use such means to impose management or
control over anyone who does not explicitly agree to the use of such means
for an explicitly identified objective.

 

I would prefer that such objectives focus on results that can be observed
and measured in the real world -- and achieved by those who are
participating -- rather than merely opinions about what somebody else might
be expected, much less forced to do against their will.  

 

Owen

B) Criteria For The Ideal Phrase

 

The ideal phrase would find a good balance between the following attributes:

- accuracy

- clarity

- brevity

- descriptiveness

- uniqueness of meaning

 

It would also take into account the connotations of the words (common

definitions).
