I haven't bothered to get an exact understanding of it, myself. (It's
not my own idea.) But it does solve a lot of problems.
 * Proposals with no interest can be safely ignored
 * Good proposals will have time to gain interest
 * The voting period will be longer for controversial proposals
 * The voting period will be shorter for proposals with rough consensus.
 * No risk of a small group getting through proposals without the
knowledge of the larger group of people.

My understanding may not be entierly correct. But in general, this is
how it's done. Lets say you have 1.000.000 registred voters and a
"democracy constant" of 7.

If all of the 1.000.000 voters will vote yes on the proposal, it will be
resolved in 7 days.
If 80% of the voters votes "yes" and none votes "no", it will be
resolved in about 9 days.
If 60% of the voters votes "yes" and 40% votes "no", it will be resolved
in 35 days.
If 2% votes "yes" and 1% votes "no", it will be resolved in 700 days.
If a single person proposes and votes for a suggesten, and it doesn't
attract any mote attention, it will be resolved in about 20.000 years.
(Plenty of time for at least 1 person to find it and vote against it.)

All of the above values suppose that everyone votes the first day and
that nobody changes their votes. But this system is designed for the
votes to change during the discussion of the proposals.

This will include all delegated votes. We are expecting that a lot of
people will delegate to one of the current traditional parties. That
means that the suggestions within the "normal" politics will involve a
large percentage of the voters.
