[StratML Parts 1 & 2 do not address version control per se.  However, yes,
applications and services implementing the StratML standard can and,
hopefully, will leverage the <Identifier> element for version control not
only at the plan level but also at the element level within each plan.
Hopefully, services will emerge that maintain a complete archival history of
all versions of all elements of all plans (or at least those of interest to
their own stakeholders).]

[In Parts 1 & 2 the only contact information is for the "submitter" (i.e.,
the person who submitted the plan/report in StratML format) who may or may
not be the appropriate POC for comments on any particular goal or objective.
However, in Part 3 it may be appropriate to reuse the <FirstName>,
<LastName>, <EmailAddress> and <PhoneNumber> elements as children of a
<PointOfContact> child of each goal and objective.  Other means of directing
comments to the appropriate person/place could also be considered, depending
upon the interest of those participating in specification of Part 3.]

[It seems to me that routing of comments depends more upon the focus of the
comment (e.g., the goal or objective it addresses) rather that the
attributes of the individuals submitting them.  In those cases, the
<Identifier>s associated with each goal and objective can be leveraged by
applications and services to route comments appropriately.  However, I
recognize there are special cases (e.g., comments to Members of Congress)
that may warrant special treatment.  In those cases, it seems to me that it
might be more appropriate to rely upon applications and services rather than
to try to build the necessary logic into the StratML schema itself.  Again,
though, that is just my thought at this point.  I can be persuaded
otherwise, and what the StratML Committee actual decides to do in Part 3
will depend upon: a) the interest and willingness of individuals to do the
work, and b) the consensus of the "members" (participants) of the Committee.
(Participation is open.)]

[We mean literal linkages, e.g., hypertext links.  However, conceptual
linkage is also an important point.  A lot of lip service has been paid to
the concept of "strategic alignment" that doesn't really amount to much in
reality.  StratML enables literal "alignment" (linkage) of records to the
strategic goals and objectives they support, a vice versa.]

Citation of relevant legal or other authorities

[StratML Parts 1 & 2 do not include any element(s) explicitly referencing
"authorities," e.g., laws, regulations, and policies.  That concept is on
our radar for consideration in Part 3:
http://xml.gov/stratml/index.htm#Part3  However, what actually makes its way
into Part 3 will depend upon the consensus of participants in AIIM's StratML
Committee.  Indeed, whether there will be a Part 3 depends upon whether Part
2, like Part 1, is approved as an ANSI/AIIM standard.  Part 2 is about to
enter the ANSI/AIIM review process.  Meanwhile, as indicated above, AIIM is
preparing to advance Part 1 for ISO approval.  Such approval will depend
upon the interest and support of a certain number of other countries besides
the U.S.]

Categorization of goals and objectives based upon various taxonomies,
This is interesting.  These are all linked on the page at
http://xml.gov/stratml/index.htm#Part3 .  I recommend folks take a
look at these.  Structured taxonomies are important when we have
hugely generalized facilities like stratml.  All of our Metagov
projects are generalized.  We could have a process for introducing
these structured schemas and linking them to our projects.

[I'm not sure what you mean by "they are all linked on the page ..."  A link
is provided to a summary of the U.S. Federal Enterprise Architecture (FEA)
Performance Reference Model (PRM), and the other FEA "models" are also
summarized on that page.  However, the PRM is just one of many taxonomies by
which goals and objectives might be categorized, and if we do enable
categorization in Part 3, I'd like to do so in a generic way that enables
users to identify the taxonomy they are using along with documenting the
category(ies) within that taxonomy under which each goal and objective is
being classified.] 

Note: StratML Part 2 includes the Relationship element as a child of
PerformanceIndicator to enable users to link inputs, processes,
outputs, and outcomes to each other in the ValueChain, if they wish
Point of contact elements as children of the Stakeholder element to
enable automated transmission of input and feedback to designated
points of contact for each Goal and Objective
Personal attributes of individuals and groups of Stakeholders
Version control at the element level, perhaps by applying a time/date
element or attribute

This is quite powerful.  This is one way that the product of
government funding can end up back in the hands of the people who paid
for the work in the first place.

[I agree the <Relationship> element is conceptually quite helpful.  However,
the gap between potential results and actual results is commonly quite
large.  Just because the capability is supported in the standard doesn't
necessarily mean that anyone will use it or that they will do so
sufficiently to achieve large-scale network effects.  Indeed, it remains to
be seen whether even the much simpler Part 1 will be used, much less Part 2
or even more complex additional parts.  For example, I have not yet seen any
evidence that subscribers to this listserv plan to use any part of it.] 

Referencing of <Project>s and <Program>s.
See, for example, Project Management XML Schema (PMXML)
Also interesting at http://xml.gov/stratml/draft/StratMLGlossary.xml#Program

Well, I know for myself, at least, that StraML is more comprehensive
than I can fully encompass at the moment as far as structured data is
concerned.  My research into applying StratML at the local level
showed me that most organizations are still struggling with thee
basics of what must have been version 1 of the specification, and that
includes myself!

[Absolutely.  That's why it is important not to get ahead of ourselves and
overload others with too much complexity to soon.]

That said, I think that we have 2 sides of a coin here.  Most of the
metagov projects are concerned with methods for deliberation online,
and a strategy document is the product of such a deliberation.  I go a
step further and say also that the product needs go back and inform
the method to the extent to which that is possible, and hence the
progress in identifying ways in which StratML can be integrated into
the way we write positions and strategies and format, from the very
beginning, a proposal that is to be deliberated.

[I agree that the potential for synergy between the StratML standard and
Metgov applications is great.  The question is whether we can muster the
will and wisdom to capitalize on that potential in ways that will be
meaningful and manageable by others.]
