Owen Ambur wrote:
> BTW, w/re the issue of open versus closed source, I believe what matters is
> whether the *records* are open and managed in systems trusted by the
> stakeholders.

Source is sometimes crucial, at least for some applications.
Democracy is inherently a public application, for example.  Things
would get complicated if the core, underlying software were privately
owned.  Free/open source is maybe the simplest solution: the public
codes the software, and the public owns it.  (Maybe too, that kind of
self-determination is more in the spirit of democracy.)

> Both the content of the record and its authoritative *presentation*
> should be maintained in assured inviolate form, apart from the
> databases in which the "votes" are tallied and under the control of
> at least one other "authority" trusted by the voter...
