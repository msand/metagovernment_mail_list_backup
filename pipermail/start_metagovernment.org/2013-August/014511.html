Ned> (I really appreciate your ability to analyze issues!)

I see your suggestion as appropriate within the context of the 
"popularity paradigm" of public decision making. This is a kind of 
problem that arises when voting (popularity) is used to make decisions, 
and to frame the discussion of decision making. But when "no unresolved 
objections" is used as the event that pulls the decision trigger, this 
kind of problem does not exist in the way that it does in voting 
systems. The framing of the problem changes, which radically changes the 
domain of available best solutions.

But, that discussion (of your paragraph) needs its own separate thread 
(or other venue). It is one sub-topic within the more general "defining 
consensus" topic, which is in turn one sub-topic within the "Society 
Bylaws" (for the Metagovernment society) topic, which is in turn one 
sub-topic within the "Global Democracy" topic -- except that those 
various topics are not really hierarchically related; they are 
inter-related in all sorts of different ways. The new software needs to 
be able to handle that gracefully.

> Example: a woman wants an abortion. Who is involved in that decision? 
> My short answer is that there is a hierarchy of decisions. (for my 
> long answer see http://www.fullmoon.nu/book/chap.php?id=c08)
>
> First there has to be an overarching social decision which sets the 
> parameters for the acceptability (or otherwise) of abortion for 
> anyone. That will be a "community" decision where some communities (eg 
> catholics) will block it for all purposes with the possible exception 
> of threat to the mother's life. Some will grant it for virtually any 
> reason but only up to the point at which a foetus would be viable if 
> born etc.
>
> That community decision will also, usually, determine who needs to be 
> involved in a specific case, doctors, social support, adoption 
> agencies etc. So that sets the scene for the specific individual 
> decision. But, from the "scoping" point of view, the crucial thing is 
> that when determining the Principle, anyone who has even just an 
> academic or religious interest can legitimately participate in the 
> debate and vote because we're determining Society's ethical views on 
> the permissibility or otherwise of a specific behaviour.
>
> But when it comes down to a single specific case, the "constituency" 
> is reduced to a handful of "voters" and one of them, the pregnant 
> woman, has both a super-vote AND a super-veto. Other participants have 
> a minor vote and minor veto.
>
> The woman can clearly refuse to have an abortion and no one can 
> gain-say that; hence her super-veto. And she can insist on having an 
> abortion regardless of anyone elses wishes - the super-vote. However, 
> unless she intends to operate on herself, she has to attempt to get 
> the co-operation (minor vote) of, at least, the medics in the case or 
> else they can exercise their minor veto and refuse to carry out the 
> abortion.
>
> More importantly, assuming we're in a community in which abortion is 
> permitted in principle, then none of the opponents of that policy have 
> ANY right to be involved in the specific case.
>
> So the scope is determined by whether we're talking principles or 
> specifics. Principle (is a behaviour permitted/prohibited) is a matter 
> for the community - which is, presumably, already self selected 
> geographically or constitutionally. Specifics are decided by those 
> directly affected by or required to implement the decision.
>

> grounds that do not form part of the "constituency"; they have no
