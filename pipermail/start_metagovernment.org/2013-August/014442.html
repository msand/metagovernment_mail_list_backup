Yes Ned, i think you captured it well.
It's actually not that different from what we currently have when it comes
to "the rules".
It's more, that getting shares is somehow "mandatory" and current owners
have to offer "buy back options" which every shareholder could accept or
not, but by default, the people who put in the work get the shares.

I think, because people are not used to this, it's not easy to sell the
idea and people will be very sceptical in the beginning.

Also when it comes to shares "wasting away", the current system has such a
mechanism by giving out new shares, watering down existing ones (kind of
like inflation) and the "wasting away mechanism" will just be more
transparent and adhere to predictable rules.

But there's also the problems of how people will decide, because if there
is many shareholders, the problem of how to establish good democratic
pratices supported by the proper tools, follows immediately.

>    - In such a system, the richest (initial investors) would no longer
>    have a free ride forever after the initial investment, which would be
>    fairer, and would over time spread wealth away from the richest toward
>    those who actually do the work.

>
> *Whats entrepreneurship all about, from an equity point of view?*
> Leave customers and product aside, its all about creating equity for
> yourself and investors. Once you've it up and running, you might be able to
> lean back and let the structure work for you. Thats why 98 of 100 persons
> of the FORBES MAGAZINES "Richest Persons Charts" are entrepreneurs and
> probably many of them within the financial industrie, because they leverage
> the rules of money and equity.
>
> *STATUS QUO*
> Whats a common practice or topic you have to get into when you become an
> entrepreneur and want to found a company? Its contract law and corporate
> law.
> You pay wages, you get shares, and often there are "founder teams", so
> they craft a company or partner agreement in which, amongst other things,
> document:
> - who gets how many shares?
> - how many shares do external investors get?
> - how might be able to vote to which degree?
> - if an investor/partner leaves, will he lose his shares? can they be
> bought back?
> (think of facebook and the programer who helped zuckerberg in the early
> stages)
> - do partners have to earn there shares? (so called VESTING)
>
> ...so essentially, there is a lot of custom rules or best practices, like
> (tag along, drag along http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tag-along_right) ...
> which are just set up to make sure what happens to EQUITY at any point in
> time.
>
>
> *NOW WHAT?*
> Something that i believe to be essential in order for an alternative
> approach to work, is a "social process" of constantly evaluating results to
> measure progress from day one and have a lot of transpareceny (for example:
> in a startup)
>
> *3 Techniques which are already best practices in their domain*
> *TECHNIQUE 1*
> There is a method called "Business Model Canvas", which is a top-down-view
> of a startup which you draw on a canvas with your team (could also be done
> digitally).
> Then you attach hypotheses and their tests (todo's for the team) to every
> area of the company (partners, (activities+ressources), product, (channels,
> relations) customers) and work through them during the next week.
> So you ITERATE through this process generating business model canvas after
> business model canvas, making progress visible, + it helps to not lose
> focus.
>
> *TECHNIQUE 2*
> There is a methodology called "agile management technique" and one of them
> is called scrum. Most software developers should be familiar with it.
> Within scum, there is a technique called "PLANNING POKER" which is some
> kind of card game you run through before each 2-week-sprint-iteration
> (which means 2 weeks of doing work) to upfront try to estimate how many
> tasks you could accomplish from an priorityqueue of features/tasks.
>
> *TECHNIQUE 3*
> Financial Bookkeeping and Depreciation. Where many "accounts" exists and
> you update them for each business transaction and you also calculate the
> depreciation over time, so you know your "true costs" because you have to
> bear "replacement investments" in mind.
>
>
> *PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER*
> Now if you use all those 3 techniques, you will eventually end up with a
> process, where a digital business model canvas can give transparencey to
> all participants about the current state of the company. Combined with the
> Planning Poker Approach, its possible to measure progress or do some kind
> of bookkeeping or point system, where you measure how much all your points
> are worth and after each iteration you could reevaluate in retrospective to
> what degree your assumptions became true and this practice will sharpen
> everyones ability to make such guesses (learning process).
>
> Now if you also account for depreciation (some todo's, like results of "a
> customer interview" or "cleaning a room" will depreciate faster than a
> bought "office table"), you could calculate an average rate of depreciation
> with which earned points waste away.
> Then there is also those points who have to be "written off" after you did
> some kind of work which turned out to be a waste of time.... ...but the
> process of this is weaved into the CORE TECHNIQUE (with business model
> canvas) around which you build everything else and which helps you to keep
> focus.
>
>
> *What does it mean? And how is this related to the "STATUS QUO"?*
> 1. Now, what if i buy an office chair and give it "points" during a weekly
> planning poker session? The "office chair" also has a value in "USD" or
> "EUR". If many companies will do this approach, you essentially get an
> "exchange rate" between your companies internal point system and "USD" or
> "EUR" and the like.
> 2. What if this process works well, why not make these points REAL SHARES?
> And if you do so, why not make everyones SHARES waste away with the average
> rate of depreciation, so that people have to re-invest, in order to not
> lose all their shares over time? (=> This will essentially make all this
> equity agreements or "VESTING" rules kind of superfluous, because if
> someone decides to quit and doesnt want to sell his shares, they will waste
> away over time)
> Why should I invest work in earning shares if they waste away???
> Because as long as you have shares, they decide on a potentially "real
> time basis" how much of the companies revenue (in USD or EUR or the
> like...) goes into your pocket. So you dont work for a wage, but for those
> points which eventually are shares and which will decide your monetary
> income flow (potentially minutely dividends)
>
> IF this techniques, which in practice should make the average person
> better off, becomes mainstream practice (which is totally possible, because
> if there are role models who work this way successfully, it will spread
> virally, because it will mean more money and more power with less work for
> everyone beside the top 1%, but it has to start bottom-up and success of
> practicioners will automatically spread it and make it a mainstream
> practice.
> ... THEN: shareholders will lose equity over time to those who are
> actively doing the work, which makes the rules fair again. And because
> everyone or every company will create their "point system" which will end
> up having an exchange rate in EUR/USD, they all get used to this and it
> will or might lay a foundation to tackle the monetary system as such, but
> for now, this is totally of reach.
>
