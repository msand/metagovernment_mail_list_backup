Hi Paul and Alex,

Paul, thanks for the link to the CESJ material -- good stuff!

Alex, I very much agree with you that social arrangements that maintain 
and increase concentration of wealth/power are problems to be solved.

To the extent that a particular corporation "succeeds" (from the 
perspective of its owners), wealth/power are concentrated (in the 
corporation, and in the owners of the corporation). This is not a 
problem from the perspective of the corporation, but is a problem from 
the perspective of the rest of humanity, and nature, and future generations.

I don't quite understand what leads you think that tinkering with the 
rules of equity (within corporations) will solve the problems that 
corporations keep causing for the world as a whole (poverty, war, 
environmental destruction, etc.). Each corporation will still be 
narrowly pursuing its own interests, with the rest of us having no 
choice but to continue trusting "The Invisible Hand" to protect the rest 
of us from corporate predations. (We have been trusting The Invisible 
Hand for the last few hundred years, and things have not been going so 
well ...)

------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Alex> I think, because people are not used to this, it's not easy to
    sell the idea and people will be very sceptical in the beginning.

It is pretty easy to predict that the people who will most skeptical 
will be the richest people in the world. Any particular startup can 
indeed choose to "spread the wealth" internally, but then the richest 
people will back competitors to the startup that give them better equity 
deals, and will instruct their captured politicians and regulators to 
make trouble for any startup that presumes to try to "spread the wealth".

As Paul suggested, "binary economics" presents arrangements that are 
very close to what you are suggesting. The CESJ organization (that has 
been promulgating binary economics) has been in business since 1984, as 
yet without much success (you and I had never even heard of them). If 
you want "real effects NOW", starting with trying to change the rules of 
equity within corporations may not be the best choice. The attempt would 
be unlikely to succeed against the opposition of the richest people in 
the world, and even if it did succeed, the world would still be at the 
mercy of successful corporations.

------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Alex> But there's also the problems of how people will decide,
    because if there is many shareholders, the problem of how to
    establish good democratic pratices supported by the proper tools,
    follows immediately.

YES! My view is that in order to really solve "the problems of 
concentrated power", we need to create, demonstrate, and popularize a 
full direct democracy, that is capable of rendering corporations 
obsolete, globally. (Because people are not used to this, it's not easy 
to sell the idea and people will be very skeptical in the beginning ...) :^)

In order to decide to change the rules of equity, or the rules of money, 
or any other rules, we need a new decision system through which to 
decide such things. That has to come first. We need to decide, together, 
how we are going to decide.

("We" means you and me and Paul and Stephen and Conseo and Ed and Pietro 
and Mike and the other members of the Metagovernment community and then 
others in the broader e-dem population, and so on. We talk about the 
importance of starting at the community level, but then we don't do it.)
