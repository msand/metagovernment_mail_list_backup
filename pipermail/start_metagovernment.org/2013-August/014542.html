I read what you wrote, but i'm not sure if i understand everything.
But a thought came to my mind while reading it.

Probably, there are many ways in which things could be done and many tools
that could be used. So i already think that blinap or self-governing public
mirrors are over-analyzed. ...lots of theory crafting. If we had a working
prototype that wouldnt need people to OPT IN, but would come to people
automatically and they have to OPT OUT (ex. using email as a technology),
then some questions could be easily answered by just observing what people
will do :-)

...no need to think stuff through and to make assumptions, just let them
use it and observe.






On Mon, Aug 26, 2013 at 10:26 PM, Michael Allan <mike at zelea.com> wrote:

> Suppose you agree with me that we shouldn't crowd-vote the arguments.
>
> http://metagovernment.org/pipermail/start_metagovernment.org/2013-August/005749.html
> How then can we formalize the "unforced force of the better argument"
> and systematically discriminate it from the weaker arguments?
>
> The human mediation in Ned's Blinap hints at a possible solution:
> http://home.earthlink.net/~npconner/images/yEd/Decide.html
>
> It wasn't designed for this purpose, ofc.  The issue is a decision not
> a continuous, tentative, fallibilist judgement, which is the reality
> we want to support.  And it serializes the outcome through a temporal
> sequence of objections, rather than standing the objections
> simultaneously in space, as an argument map would.  But the idea of
> semi-professional human mediation is there.
>
> There's also my design for self-governing public mirrors.
> http://zelea.com/w/User:Mike-ZeleaCom/Public_mirror
> The micro-decisions of a public mirror have nothing to do with the
> weighing of arguments; so again, the design doesn't apply as is.  But
> maybe it could be adapted?  Instead of harvesting threads from all the
> world's discussion media and sites, imagine that it instead vacuums up
> bits of argument (premises, facts, objections, etc); weighs them; and
> fits them into a formal argument map.
>
>
>            Tributary
>              intake
>
>    Tributary   |   Tributary   |
>       intake   |   intake      | submitted argu-bits
>                |               V
>            \   |   /
>             \  |  /
>              \ | /
>
>           Trunk intake
>
>                |
>                |      |
>                |      | weighted argu-bits
>                |      V
>                |
>                V
>
>           Argument map
>
>
> Now I can now participate in the formalized argument from whatever
> discussion medium I prefer.  And I can participate informally without
> any complicated software.  The "argu-bit submitters" and other intake
> operators will take care of all the formalities.  I can concentrate on
> the more important work of listening, thinking and talking.
>
> Voting is still required by the weighers, ofc.  But the weighers will
> be disinterested experts held accountable to the informal arguers
> (like me) who elect them to office.  My electoral votes will now have
> the desired effect of improving the overall quality and fidelity of
> the argument map (of all argument maps) produced by the weighers.
>
> And maybe I'll occaisionally submit argu-bits of my own to the intakes
> too, because that'll be easy.
>
> What do you think?  Or has this been attempted before?
