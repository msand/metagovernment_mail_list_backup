> I'm not going to do a point by point rebuttal of your response to Bentley,
> but you are way off on just about every point. Email is an insecure and
> dated platform, and I would not be surprised if it were completely
> superceded in the next five to ten years. It is extremely relevant to look
> to how young people interact with digital networks and the current thinking
> is based in the rise of "social media". Now, to some extent this is the
> latest buzzword, but you also cannot deny the power of emerging platforms
> of Facebook and Twitter.
>
> The problem that I see is that these platforms are branded products of
> corporations competing to own these spaces. The new civic spaces need to be
> free of this kind of domination, and that is the bigger issue that must be
> faced in re-inventing government and finance.
>
> You are right that any limited prototype is not likely to reach the levels
> of participation to become self-sustaining spaces of collective work, but
> there already are counter-examples to your claim. Wiki in general as well
> as blogging platforms as a category are much more significant than email
> and are the kind of non-branded social media that is essential. Take a look
> at Wikipedia, many people use it daily and a smaller group collaboratively
> produce it. Now, I would recommend Wagn over Wiki-media (the platform for
> Wikipedia and many other community sites) because of the merit of the
> design, though I can understand if you think I might be biased.
>
> Bringing critical mass to your project is a problem in social
> architecture, not technical architecture. How is this meta-government
> effort ever going to go beyond the small network who post here? Email lists
> have much higher barriers to participation than interactive web-sites.
> Email has its place (for now), but in the end it is just one of many
> transport protocols. HTTP is much more powerful and flexible, and RESTful
> APIs even more so.
