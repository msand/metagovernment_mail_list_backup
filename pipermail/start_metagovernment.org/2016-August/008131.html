I would have thought that a consensus-building model would have been
the most appropriate for running Metagovernment: Small group,
homogeneous group, common goals, etc.. But our runs with Vilfredo and
Loomio along with a lot of reading about soviet communism and communes
have disabused me of that, and not because I think there's anything
special about our little group: Getting humans to agree on pretty much
*anything* is like herding cats. It's just the way we are, I'm afraid.
This conclusion fortunately *does* reinforce two of the main concepts
in the Matchism manifesto:

1) People are genetically programmed to defer to others who are
willing to take responsibility (aka "leaders"). Therefore the most
natural form of government for us humans is a system where the vast
majority of decisions are made by designated
experts/leaders/executives.

2) The major thing missing from our current systems is accountability:
Our instinctive deferential behavior evolved in an environment where,
e.g., those who voted to go to war with a neighboring band or to kill
and eat that mammoth spotted nearby were the same people who were
going to risk their lives in the effort, providing an automatic and
built-in check for reckless or self-serving behavior. Since it is no
longer possible to do that (though I'd love to send Obama in to be a
spotter and possible collateral damage before every drone strike), we
have to create a system where The People supply that accountability
correction. A simple open-voting majority-rules voting system I
believe will supply this missing ingredient (delegation not being a
necessary component of that, but merely an expedient).

Probably a coup: Ed seems to have lost interest in this project and
IMHO should turn responsibility over to someone else (maybe you? ;-)
That person would just take full responsibility for fixing the site,
including writing an appropriate Home page. The rest of us will
kibitz, but ultimately defer to our chosen (indeed even self-chosen)
leader.

I've got nothing against allourideas.org or other collaborative
environments, but I believe it is a mistake to consider them tools for
managing government. They're tools that an executive might want to use
to try to figure out what The People want when there is doubt, but
IMHO the vast majority of the time there should be little or no doubt
as to what the right thing to do is and so having a vote (or just
doing it and expecting a correction later on) is the proper way to
govern.
