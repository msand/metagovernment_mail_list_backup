On Nov 27, 2013, at 10:20 AM, Paul Nollen wrote:

> maybe the board can be appointed by sortition out of the members who want to participate in a board, such as in Athens some 2000 years ago ;-).  The board then can "elect" the chair, or it can be by "rotation" . Anyway, representation by sortition is not an (elitist) elected representative system.
> http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sortition

Non-elitist is good, but I am also looking for effective and responsible. I think we could do better than sortition from an undefined pool. Since we're small and the potential pool is enormous, there is a very real possibility that any random group of people could insert themselves into our pool and effectively take over the organization for their own ends.


On Nov 27, 2013, at 10:32 AM, Stephen Coffman wrote:

> Will there be someone in charge of keeping minutes, and someone responsible for dispersing funds that the group has decided to pay out?
> If so, then they could be the secretary and the treasurer. Then we would just need a 'puppet' president to run any meetings the group decides are necessary.
> 
> It seems to me the real issue is deciding how management decisions will be made outside of having elected representatives.  

My idea is that the company itself would have employees who act as regular staff: making management decisions, doing bureaucratic tasks, etc. (e.g., filing papers, applying for grants, managing accounts). I would be the first employee (with no or deferred salary until such time as the company had a grant to pay one).

In nonprofits I have worked with in the past, the employees were relatively "powerful" compared to the board. But the board was still ultimately responsible for governance, and could remove any employee.

Since one of the major functions of this company would be to take in funds and disburse them; the governance becomes critically relevant. We need to be able to ensure that those funds are disbursed sensibly and responsibly.

Also, I am hopeful that if we can come up with an alternative corporate governance structure; we would provide a model that other nonprofits could use for their governance. After all, representative democracy is a poor form of governance for a charity.
