It's not a rhetorical question, since I don't know the answer.
Perhaps - as you say - widening participation would increase a sense
of issue-ownership, and decrease cooperation.  Perhaps if people
didn't feel like their input was being completely thrown out, because
the system was able to recognise different levels of input, then there
would be a corresponding increase in compromise.  My feeling is that
we would see more of the latter.

But my feeling is useless since I have nothing solid to back it up.


>> There is a problem of recursion here - if you can't find consensus
>> with the tenets, then you'd need to first work out the meta-tenets,
>> and perhaps the meta-meta-tenets, etc.  But I wonder if Michael is an
>> exception here, or if simply the knowledge that your higher-level
>> input is embedded into the details of a final decision you wouldn't
>> otherwise support, would enable more people to act the same way and
>> join the consensus?
>
> I cannot even parse this.

Well say one of your tenets is that the name shouldn't include the
"e-" prefix.  As it stands, with a handful of participants, it's
realistic to have a list-serv or site-hosted discussion and gain
consensus.  But at a certain scale, individuals form into factions
around issues, and become less responsive to appeals to compromise.  I
think the only way to combat that is to increase the number of issues,
and thus dilute the power of factions.

For example the pro "e-" faction may be part of a consensus on a
mini-issue like "the name should be clearly understandable to a wide
audience today".  So you link in a few more issues and build an
argument - "the name should be as future-proof as possible", and "the
e-prefix is already becoming old-fashioned - e.g. you've got mail!".

It's intuitive to me to say that this would invite competing
arguments.  Then you've no-longer got a big pro/anti issue but a
larger set of smaller ones - if an individual is part of the consensus
on 3 out of 4 mini-issues, then they wouldn't they find it easier to
compromise on the last one?

As much as I agree with you about the necessity for a modus operandi,
I think a system which doesn't take account of the petty side of human
nature, will fall victim to it.  I think it's safer to assume that
none of us will ever follow any modus operandi 100%.


>> Synthesis isn't all-or-nothing, but with a single

>
> Here is something I can answer. It is possible to consider multiple
> voting, like rating the answers, or ordering them. But I have a
> _strong_ intuition (funded on some partial math results) that the
> result of this would be that the Pareto Front would grow a lot, even
> to become the whole list of all the proposals. At the end an all or
> nothing seem to be the most acceptable solution. You decide where the

If there is no hierarchy to the issues, then I agree.  If some issues
are structured to cancel and supersede others, then you mitigate that
problem, since only the top branches of the tree are used to calculate
the front.  How to fairly construct that tree is a different problem,
and one I've been hacking away at for a few years now with only
moderate success.
