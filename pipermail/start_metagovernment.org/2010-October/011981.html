This is a really interesting topic, because so far we have not seen
the appearance of parties on vilfredo.
It might be because it is too early, or because the questions did not
ask for them. But I have my own theory.

You see, an important element is Vilfredo is that proposals are
anonymous when they are being voted. As such
people vote more respect to what they truly believe than respect to
agreeing to some party affiliation. In fact if the system was to grow
to the point to include thousands of participants, and it should scale
in a form of distributed network, this should be written right in the
code. SO that no one could know which proposal he will evaluate, and
no one can invite another to vote for their proposal. People can
convince each other of a particular point, but not point to a
particular proposal that embodies that point.

Also parties seem to come together with the process of summing up
votes, after all the bigger your party is, the more power you have.
But in Vilfredo each user has already plenty of power, the power to
write one proposal directly in the pareto front and to veto all other
proposals to reach consensus. Maybe that's why we don't form parties.

Still at times we have observed the appearance of a polarity in the
proposal. This is different from the parties in the sense that the
people agreeing on one topic in one question seldom agree on another
topic in another question. It's like if me and you share on think (say
we are both libra), but then on the topic of soccer, I share my
affiliation with the soccer club with Ed.

When this type of polarity appears my suggestion is that eventually
the question is interrupted or frozen (we still need to implement a
frozen system), and the pareto front of the proposals in the last
round be used as the alternative answers to be voted in a Condorcet
voting round. We admit that we are unable to reach an agreement, but
instead of having someone from above decide what are the alternatives,
those are defined by the process, and then the voting happens.

I think this is becoming to abstract for now. I am considering to
introduce relation between proposals. But the fact that everybody need
to also agree on what the relation is is making this quite difficult.
For example:
proposal A: let's go to Paris
proposal B: let's go to France

Of course A implies B. So if someone votes for A we can assume (or
invite) that he votes also for B. But there are other situation where
not everybody agrees on the implications. It will be eventually
implemented. But not until I have fully worked out the maths around it
:-).
