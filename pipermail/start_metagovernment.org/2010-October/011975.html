On Mon, Oct 25, 2010 at 1:02 PM, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:
> I think this points toward a deeper issue in all our open-drafting systems
> (and in closed systems too). The way a question is presented greatly affects
> peoples answers. Further, in open drafting, there is the liklihood of many
> very similar proposals.
>
> Given that, a question-framing process seems helpful if not downright
> necessary if we ever hope to scale to larger communities.

The question will be re-framed by the answers, explanations and
discussions it generates.  This is to be expected when exploration of
the problem space discovers information not initially available.  This
will happen with or without a formal/automated process for re-framing
the question.  But I agree - a system designed with this in mind will
be more scalable.

I would also argue that such a system would provide better results.
If a question is re-framed only in the minds of the participants - and
not in some tangible form - then the participants will be providing
answers for different questions, and trying to fit all those into the
same slot.


> Of course, there's also the risk that locking-in to one question might
> hinder synthesis...

Agreed.  This is my motivation for breaking big questions into
smaller, more easily handled, chunks.
