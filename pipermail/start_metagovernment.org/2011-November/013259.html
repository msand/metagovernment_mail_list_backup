On Mon, Nov 7, 2011 at 12:34 AM, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:
> On Nov 6, 2011, at 4:24 AM, John Vandenberg wrote:
>>
>> Over at wikimedia we usually disenfrancise community members who
>> havent reached a certain edit-count, and we also require a limited
>> number of edits in the preceding twelve months.
>
> Is this encoded in software, or do you manually have to check the edit count
> of each participant? Clearly, if we want to scale, we need something which
> has at least some automation to it.

We have a web page for individuals to check their own eligibility, and
automations that verify each voter is eligible and scratch their vote
if they are not eligible.

e.g.
http://toolserver.org/~pathoschild/accounteligibility/

For the Wikimedia Board of Trustees election, a list of all eligible
accounts is compiled and given to SPI, who run the election.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Software_in_the_Public_Interest

> Also, counting edits for a wiki makes a lot of sense, but what counts for
> us?

wiki edits, mailing list posts, etc.  MG would probably want to
include activity on sister-projects.
Wikimedia has counters for all our mailing lists; there are quite a
few email stats packages like this - the most important feature is
collating posts by one person with two email addresses.

http://www.infodisiac.com/Wikipedia/ScanMail/

> So far, the rule we have used in small-scale decisions has been
> participation on this list in the previous month.

Whenever we have a threshold for Wikimedia decision making processes,
there is someone who 'should' be included but is disenfranchised by
'the rules'.  Wikimedia often have a discussion before the 'vote'
about which threshold is suitable for the specific item to be voted
on.

I think 'activity in the previous month' would disenfranchise too many
people who have been very active but are busy for a month or two with
other parts of their life.
