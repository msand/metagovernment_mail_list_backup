In a previous post you mentioned the danger of an outside group taking 
over Metagov and its money just with the force of their masses of votes. 
If everybody was allowed to vote and a majority can decide ... there is 
the problem.

Conseo is working on a solution for that: To each vote the voter can 
connect the amount of resources he is willing to contribute. This could 
be Dollar, working hours, ... . So everybody can decide for himself 
where his money goes.

our prize-poll: http://zelea.com/y/vw/xf/#s=G!p!prz*b12&c=DV

(the dollars seem to be just made up examples)

On Sat, 05 Nov 2011 15:53, Ed Pastore wrote:
> On Nov 3, 2011, at 7:39 AM, David Bovill wrote:
>
>> In order to embed the principles discussed on this list (and being 
>> applied to the development of softwares), into a legal constitution, 
>> such that there is no or as little conflict as possible between the 
>> running of the administrative system (ie the voting software), and 
>> the legal governance, we need to choose a legal structure that allows:
>>
>>    1. Full legal membership from individuals anywhere in the world.
>>    2. Zero administrative cost for adding and deleting members
>>       (including zero requirement for legal advice / costs). This
>>       would hinder extending membership to large numbers of people,
>>       and or members unwilling or unable to pay for full legal
>>       participation.
>>    3. No a priori or adhoc structure to the membership (such as fixed
>>       number and classification of a board of directors). This would
>>       impose a top down power structure on the decision making
>>       process, and cause artificial separation between the general
>>       membership and the decisions of the network.
>>    4. Full legal recognition of online voting, digital signatures,
>>       delegation and other means of voting where physical presence at
>>       meetings is not possible.
>>    5. Flexibility - the ability to change the structure of the legal
>>       structure through a vote of them members, suing the system of
>>       their preference (ie the software) at a time and in a manner of
>>       their choosing, without artificial restriction based on
>>       regional law, or based on prohibitive cost (by requiring for
>>       instance legal advice to make such changes).
>>    6. The ability to create a wide range of charitable, ethical,
>>       not-for-profit organisational structures - without restricting
>>       these to preconceived ideas of what constitutes "charitable"
>>       activity.
>>    7. The ability to create nested and / or p2p organisational
>>       (legal) structures. This allows the membership to structure
>>       legally binding decision making in a distributed, local, or
>>       domain specific fashion, and not be forced into central and
>>       hierarchical structures.
>>
>> Robust legal structures are possible, which allow the above. None of 
>> them are perfect yet, but some of them are sufficiently simple and 
>> flexible to allow us to create "legal shells" which embody our 
>> principles. It does however rule out structures like Vereins, or 
>> classic charity structures, as the legal hub so to speak, all though 
>> these can be incorporated as member organisations with specific 
>> national or anational roles. The overall legal architecture does not 
>> need to be complex, but should mirror / identify with the software 
>> architecture.
>
