On Nov 3, 2011, at 7:39 AM, David Bovill wrote:

> In order to embed the principles discussed on this list (and being  
> applied to the development of softwares), into a legal constitution,  
> such that there is no or as little conflict as possible between the  
> running of the administrative system (ie the voting software), and  
> the legal governance, we need to choose a legal structure that allows:
> Full legal membership from individuals anywhere in the world.
> Zero administrative cost for adding and deleting members (including  
> zero requirement for legal advice / costs). This would hinder  
> extending membership to large numbers of people, and or members  
> unwilling or unable to pay for full legal participation.
> No a priori or adhoc structure to the membership (such as fixed  
> number and classification of a board of directors). This would  
> impose a top down power structure on the decision making process,  
> and cause artificial separation between the general membership and  
> the decisions of the network.
> Full legal recognition of online voting, digital signatures,  
> delegation and other means of voting where physical presence at  
> meetings is not possible.
> Flexibility - the ability to change the structure of the legal  
> structure through a vote of them members, suing the system of their  
> preference (ie the software) at a time and in a manner of their  
> choosing, without artificial restriction based on regional law, or  
> based on prohibitive cost (by requiring for instance legal advice to  
> make such changes).
> The ability to create a wide range of charitable, ethical, not-for- 
> profit organisational structures - without restricting these to  
> preconceived ideas of what constitutes "charitable" activity.
> The ability to create nested and / or p2p organisational (legal)  
> structures. This allows the membership to structure legally binding  
> decision making in a distributed, local, or domain specific fashion,  
> and not be forced into central and hierarchical structures.
> Robust legal structures are possible, which allow the above. None of  
> them are perfect yet, but some of them are sufficiently simple and  
> flexible to allow us to create "legal shells" which embody our  
> principles. It does however rule out structures like Vereins, or  
> classic charity structures, as the legal hub so to speak, all though  
> these can be incorporated as member organisations with specific  
> national or anational roles. The overall legal architecture does not  
> need to be complex, but should mirror / identify with the software  
> architecture.

Yes, I think I am laying the groundwork for all of the above with the  
draft charter
http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Charter/Draft1

I am keeping it deliberately extremely simple at first so we can build  
consensus around the basics. We can flesh out the details once we have  
passed that first step. Please feel free to edit as appropriate.

What I still don't know is what existing legal structures we can look  
to to help us create a formally-recognized organization. You indicate  
that you are aware of some of these; can you please list some so I can  
research them? I will have limited availability for the next week, but  
I am very eager to explore this further to see if we can get this  
moving forward.

>  Clearly it is important to embody the mission charter in the  
> constitutional structure. However what is important is how it is  
> agreed / created and how it is changed. The "change rules" need to  
> be according to the principles of the LD software and not some  
> rigid / historically inherited / regional legal structure.

The rules that my draft links to were developed by using Votorola to  
create a decision-making algorithm for editing our own wiki with  
consensus. I think it is a good starting point for the basis of the  
organization as a whole, no?
