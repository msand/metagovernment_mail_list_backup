On Mar 25, 2009, at 11:26 AM, Owen Ambur wrote:

> With respect to the thought that “communities last only as long as  
> there is interest,” the communities in which I am most interested in  
> participating are those which:
>
> a)      have clearly documented in open, readily sharable format  
> (e.g. StratML) what they aim to accomplish (longer-term goals and  
> near-term objectives), and
> b)      measure and report progress against their common goals and  
> objectives.
>
> To the degrees that goals may be achieved, there may be no need for  
> the community to continue to exist.  However, in many, if not most  
> instances there will be needs/opportunities for ongoing maintenance,  
> if not also enhancement of the results achieved

For the purposes of this project, communities are any collection of  
people who share a common interest. So it could refer to the group of  
people working for global nuclear disarmament, or the group of people  
who worship Britney Spears.

This broad definition inherently includes the possibility of  
communities morphing over time, as people's interests and attitudes  
change, and as the environment in which the community operates  
changes. This thus solves for the inertia of institutionalism because  
communities will necessarily be changing/merging/splitting as these  
conditions change. There is nothing stopping a community from formally  
stating goals, but they may have to adapt over time as conditions  
change. Or they may not... if they have defined goals well enough to  
maintain a strong community.
