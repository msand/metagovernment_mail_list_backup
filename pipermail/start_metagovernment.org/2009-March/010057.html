Hi Allan,

before I give you an answer in detail I shall define the area that I am talking 
about. 

As I said before did I get the idea because of the discussions about the 
European constitution but it applies also to the US constitution or any other 
big law. These are so big that not a single person can handle the whole 
document. They consist of a few chapters and many articles and even more small 
paragraphs. The process I am describing here refers to articles and 
paragraphs. 

The concept is that they are moderated by one person who can be seen as a 
benevolent dictator (as leaders from open source call them self). These 
moderators have the power of integrating suggestions or not. And here comes 
the necessity of a separation. The moderators can do a good moderation by 
being good leaders and using their "power" correctly. But having the 
possibility of a separation "bad leaders" can easily be exchanged. This 
results in the situation that the moderator has only got the right staying in 
this position as long as people are accepting him/her as the person doing the 
job in the best way.

Addidtion or exchange of the issues to the chapters or the whole constitution 
can only be achieved by a poll. So there is no moderator or someone in power.

Am Mittwoch 04 März 2009 schrieb Michael Allan:
> Hi Gerold,
>
> > I think the best is to separate the task into small and big dissents. For
> > small dissents I would prefer if the group stays together in which case
> > maybe a tool like the one they are using for developing the GPL could
> > help: http://gplv3.fsf.org/comments/gplv3-draft-4.html
> >
> > This has got the advantage that people can see which part of the article
> > got edited and discussed most. They can also vote for a suggestion.
> > I believe that in this case a separation because of little dissent will
> > not happen so fast since bringing in ideas is very easy.
> >
> > Big issues need to split apart in my point of view and a poll can show
> > which way is better later on.
>
> You afford a dissensus (splitting apart) but not a consensus (coming
> together).  So dissent may multiply into a false dissensus - one that
> has no substance in reality - being held apart merely by inertia, and
> the lack of affordances to come together.

The "coming together" is because of group building. I think it is a social 
behavior that people want be part of a group where they feel being accepted. 
So only a moderator that can give this kind of feeling will be accepted.

>
> Or, dissent may express itself in a petition to an authority - the
> moderator of the dominant position - with the power to accept or
> reject.  If rejected, dissent is suppressed.  So the result is a
> consensus maintained, not so much because it's the best of the
> alternatives, but because it's the only alternative.  So it is a
> consensus *de facto*, rather than *de jure*.

... *de jure* consensus depends on the size of the discussed issue, eg a 
chapter
... *social* consensus applied for small issues, eg paragraphs

>
> > > In other words, how can a newly introduced idea - a better variant -
> > > climb to the top of the heap?
> >
> > Getting an issue from the bottom to the top is indeed very difficult,
> > therefore I think it has to go from "top to bottom".
>
> Generally speaking, this could be a problem.  A top-to-bottom process
> is necessarily mediated by power - force and threat of force - that
> ensconses itself in an organization (power structure).

With the top-to-bottom process I mean that there was first only one aggregation 
around a person with power and that power can very easily be split, separated 
or exchanged. So I do not see any power with force here.

>
> Power has a price.  So the process will be mediated by money, too.

How can money be relevant if everything is transparent and the person in 
*power* has actually no right since she/he can easily be *exchanged*? Maybe I 
just don't see it.

>
> > I can could imagine the following process to be implemented:
> > (1) someone starts an issue (tags need to be set for filtering so that it
> > is easy to find similar topics and avoid redundancy)
> > (2) this person is moderating the issue by accepting suggestions or
> > ignoring them
>
> Some dissent will be lost here - suppressed by the moderator's power
> structure (she will have many willing helpers).

The moderator will only have power if she/he is doing a good job otherwise 
a group of people can copy the issue and start new. Again, the moderator has 
to prove herself and has no real power.

>
> > (3a) if a group of people is not happy with the moderation, they start a
> > separated issue by copying it with the whole history (this is what I mean
> > with top to bottom)
>
> Most of them will leave the process, at that point - they will walk
> away.
>
> Whatever dissent remains (insuppressable, and strongly held) will go
> to fragmenting the process into a dissensus.  OK.

I believe people prefer to stay in a group as long as they are respected in 
the group and happy with the process even if there is dissent.

>
> > (3b) if the problem gets to big the issue should be split in two or more
> > parts to make it easier for the participants to follow the discussions
> > (again top to bottom)
> > (4) finally the issues have to take a vote
>
> There will be too many minor fragments to consider.  So the voter will
> either vote for one of the dominant positions (usually two or three),
> or she'll walk away from the process.

Two or three is a good number which people can handle. I count on the chance 
that at least one of the moderators is doing a good job and integrating good 
ideas from the group. Most people will follow that person.

>
> Effectively, you have centralized and serialized a process that is
> better when decentered and parallel.  (I shouldn't say *you*, as your
> method - thus far - is basically a model of the status quo.)

Yes, I do not see centralization generally as bad and that is why I want 
centralization for small issues. Big issues should be a collection of small 
issues where the small issues can only be integrated by a positive poll. There 
is no moderation.

>
> > Some additional ideas:
> > - issues can build a crowd (similar to having friends)
> > - people can get a "reputation" when other people vote for them or if
> > they manage to pass an issue through the whole process
>
> (This is potentially dangerous, as yet another centralizing tendency.)
>
> > - issues can merge to one issue
>
> Yes, I think this is closer to the mark.  The crucial thing is to
> afford a consensus that neither suppresses dissent, nor is
> unnecessarily fragmented by it.  It's hard to imagine how, exactly,
> except by facilitating the flow of innovations from the periphery (in
> dissensus) to the center (in consensus).

It is a socializing thing. People do not like to be alone :)
