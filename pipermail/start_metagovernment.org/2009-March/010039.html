Hi Allen,

I was trying to catch up some of the issues you were discussing lately and the 
case studies on your pages which are very intersting. I like the P2P approach 
which reflects the function of the internet so far.

So finally here are my answers:

Am Freitag 27 Februar 2009 schrieb Michael Allan:
> Welcome Gerold, mrskutcher
>
>
> You allow competing drafts - variants of a single proposal.  So there
> will initially be dissensus.  Are there any mechanisms to afford
> (enable and promote) a consensus, on a single draft?

I think the best is to separate the task into small and big dissents. For 
small dissents I would prefer if the group stays together in which case maybe 
a tool like the one they are using for developing the GPL could help: 
http://gplv3.fsf.org/comments/gplv3-draft-4.html 

This has got the advantage that people can see which part of the article got 
edited and discussed most. They can also vote for a suggestion. 
I believe that in this case a separation because of little dissent will not 
happen so fast since bringing in ideas is very easy. 

Big issues need to split apart in my point of view and a poll can show which 
way is better later on.

>
> How do you guard against the "bias", as Tim here decribes it, "towards
> those relatively few entries [variants] that manage to gain an early
> lead in net votes"?
>
>  
> http://groups.dowire.org/groups/consult/messages/topic/2IbPilDgy4CLnyMHbSjP
>LB
>
> In other words, how can a newly introduced idea - a better variant -
> climb to the top of the heap?

Getting an issue from the bottom to the top is indeed very difficult, therefore 
I think it has to go from "top to bottom". 

I can could imagine the following process to be implemented:
(1) someone starts an issue (tags need to be set for filtering so that it is 
easy to find similar topics and avoid redundancy)
(2) this person is moderating the issue by accepting suggestions or ignoring 
them
(3a) if a group of people is not happy with the moderation, they start a 
separated issue by copying it with the whole history (this is what I mean with 
top to bottom)
(3b) if the problem gets to big the issue should be split in two or more parts 
to make it easier for the participants to follow the discussions (again top to 
bottom)
(4) finally the issues have to take a vote

Some additional ideas:
- issues can build a crowd (similar to having friends)
- people can get a "reputation" when other people vote for them or if they 
manage to pass an issue through the whole process
- issues can merge to one issue

of good quality. Though I think that this would be too complicated for a 
normal user to understand. This rules can be found here:
http://wiki.opentution.org/doku.php?id=rules
