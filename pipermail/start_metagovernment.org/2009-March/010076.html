Good point, Robert.  It is important to distinguish between private
individuals exercising their individual liberties versus: a) public
officials, and b) corporate officers.  Indeed, what distinguishes "business"
(including We the People's business of government) are the types of records
required.  To the degree that the intents and actions of public and
corporate officials affect others, they have little or no right to privacy -
certainly not from those affected by their actions (unless they are
criminals).

 

Of course, that does not mean public or corporate officials should be
required to expose themselves to harassment (e.g., spam) or worse (physical
harm).  However, it does mean that it should be very easy to hold them
accountable for their intentions and actions.  (Their intentions should be
documented in StratML format and all of their actions should be documented
in reliable records that are readily available to stakeholders and to which
their digital signatures are attached.)

 

BTW, if public officials were held to such a standard, the threat to
individual liberties could be greatly reduced.  The greatest risk to
individual liberty is to allow public officials to use a false right of
privacy to do secretly that which they could not do openly.  A good, first
step would be to begin applying and reporting metrics on the performance of
public officials in documenting their goals and objectives in StratML format
on the Web.

While the elimination of corruption is a worthy goal, your solution sounds
worse than the disease.  It seems to erase every last vestige of privacy
from your citizens.

When you uniquely identify every citizen, where will you store this
information?  Who will have access to it?  Who will audit it?  Who will
determine what I am allowed to buy?  What will happen if a hacker gains
access to the database (which they most assuredly will at some point)?  How
can I be assured that my legal purchase now won't become illegal later, and
how can I be assured that I won't be punished for it?

Privacy has proven a time-tested deterrent to oppression.  Would you really
throw it out to potentially eliminate corruption?  I think you need to come
up with a different solution, one that doesn't drive a stake into the heart
of personal privacy.

I guess it's about helping government to do a better job.  But we're
aimed at replacing government (Metagovernment), or at removing
decision-making from its hands (DemocracyLab, Votorola, et al).  Our
focus is therefore on helping the public sphere to do a better job.

With regard to government, consider three types of transparency, of:

 a) governance (substansive)  -  impossible

 b) governance (non-substansive)

 c) services

(a) Substansive transparency of governance is impossible.  It would be
unacceptable to government, as it would undermine the authority of its
decisions.  Government is all about authority.

(b) Non-substansive transparency might be useful as an instrument of
propaganda.  Public opinion might be manipulated, for instance, by
selective transparency.  (But this sounds like a difficult knife
trick.  Other, safer channels of propaganda are available.)

(c) Transparency of services is perhaps all we can expect.  The
government may choose to expose parts of its bureaucracy to public
scrutiny.  This makes sense, because politicians are always at odds
with the bureaucracy.  Selective exposure might give them an added
lever of control over it.  (Not to mention, the level of service might
improve.)

So transparency might have no bearing on the public (as such), but
private individuals could benefit from improved services.  That's my
guess,
