While the concepts of ¡°transparency¡± and ¡°accountability¡± are great,
much lip service has been paid to them as ¡°business¡± (including waste,
fraud, and abuse) has continued as usual.  To be truly meaningful and
effective, those concepts must be made more specific so that *performance
metrics* can be applied to them and reported to stakeholders.  http://ambur.
net/RMmetrics.htm 

 

Transparency means *records* having the four attributes outlined in ISO
15489 ¨C integrity, reliability, usability, and authenticity -- and
accountability means that such records must clearly document who knew what
and when they knew it, including exactly what they saw when they view the
information upon which they acted.
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Records_management 

 

ISO 15489 itself must be further specified as requirements against which
performance can be measured, e.g., DoD STD 5015.2.
http://jitc.fhu.disa.mil/recmgt/ 

 

Transparency also means that the records themselves must be created and
maintained throughout their life-cycles in open (nonproprietary),
machine-processable format (e.g., XML) so that the information they contain
is readily subject to indexing, aggregation, reporting, and analysis.

 

It would be good to stop paying lip service to these concepts and get on
with implementing and improving the relevant technical standards, including
standards for measuring and reporting the performance of each of us in
creating, managing, and sharing records.  The fact that it seems to be so
hard to do so implies deep-seated psychological reasons that we may actually
prefer to allow our leaders to continue to avoid accountability in order to
afford ourselves the same opportunity.  Those reasons are the subject of my
ongoing series of papers at http://ambur.net/index.html#recordkeeping  The
next in the series will address the psychology of rumors.
