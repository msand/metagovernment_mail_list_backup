May I suggest on this regard to everybody to study a bit of Voting
theory. I too need it.
Because all those problems are very well known in that field. And
there are classes of voting types, depending on what results do they
give.

For example A Condorcet method is ANY system that returns the
Condorcet winner as its result. And the wikipedia lists 6 different
such systems:
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condorcet_method

Maybe we could require that the result MUST follow some particular requirements.
Then we can have several systems, but the result would be the same
provided the winner they select follows those requirements.

http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voting_system

On 8 February 2010 19:57, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:
> This question is primarily targeted at Michael, but others are very welcome
> to chime in. It has to do with his proposal for free-range voting, where a
> single issue can be opened to voting on multiple different software
> platforms (ie, active projects) and the results get normalized in some way
> such that all those votes can be understood as one whole.
>
> Manuel raised a particularly interesting objection, which concerned
> decision-making. If people are voting on a single issue on multiple
> different platforms, then how would the community (the community relevant to
> the issue) decide how to act on those votes?
>
> Say a proposal is rejected in Candiwi, is accepted in Votorola, and is
> ranked #17 in its topic area in WhiteHouse2. Then what meaningful
> information can be taken from that result? Or more concisely, even if the
> voting is more consistently positive (or negative) over different platforms:
> at what point does a community say that a proposal has been accepted or
> rejected?
