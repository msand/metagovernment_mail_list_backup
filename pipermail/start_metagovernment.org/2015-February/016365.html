The problem here isn't with precision, it's with underlying and
unspecified assumptions.  You seem to be coming from a "independent
city-states" model of civilization, whereas my position, I think shown
clearly in many places in the Matchism manifesto, is that that
position is fundamentally flawed and actually an existential threat to
our species.  SDAP control, which is our primary governmental need as
a species, can't happen unless we *all* live under the same system.

May be, I would just argue that the second step is the far more
important one and so the one that needs to be handled first.  It
doesn't really matter who crafts the proposals or how they do it.
What matters is how they are adopted.  In representative democracy we
mostly have proposals written by corporations or other special
interests.  Making their job easier doesn't seem to me to be a great
leap forward.  The real problem is the bought-and-paid-for politicians
who approve the proposals.

> Only actions will inspire people and INSPIRATION is needed in order to

Maybe true, but "inspiring the masses" is pretty hard, especially in a
non-crisis environment (and in a crisis environment about the only
thing that's easy to do is inspire them to violence).  So IMHO the key
to success is to make the new (voting) system as quick and easy as
possible.  Design goals for improving discussion and debate being
secondary to that primary goal.

becoming standard.  This is not how great software, or great
government, will be developed.  Not to mention their negligible
political clout: An endorsement by the pirate party would seem to me
to have only negative consequences...

The analogy would be that some people in my town decide to legalize
cocaine and they "fork" the law, remove the part that prohibits
cocaine purchase or use, and then starts following the new law instead
of the old one.  "No need to merge the changes back in!"  That is not
government, it's anarchy.

I'm counting on that, as I'm sure we all are, to help people get used
to the idea that a transition is possible.  It's not going well: Here
in Boulder, which has one of the most educated and politically aware
populations in the world, we still regularly find out about decisions
(and sometimes even whole decision-making bodies) that operate in the
shadows outside public awareness or oversight.  For this reason I've
kind of backed off on my previous plan to have a sort of "mirror"
government that votes on exactly the same bills as the real
government.  If we don't have access to what we need here in Boulder,
it's going to be even more unworkable to try it in most other places.
We should all push for as much openness in our existing governments as
we can get, but IMHO we can't count on their help at all to ease the
transition because they don't even feel obligated to share this
information when they're *not* under threat of organized
second-guessing or replacement.

I know Lisp, I've programmed extensively in Lisp.  JS is not Lisp.  JS

development of Fortran, Pascal, and the C++ template package, all of
which were (fortunately) irrelevant even before a committee went and
mucked them all up.

I've looked at it enough to develop a strong dislike for it.  Take the
star rating widgets you see on Amazon and Netflix.  Each of them
require over 200 lines of Javascript code (and that's condensed,
probably 2 or 3 times that with comments and whitespace), and probably
took weeks to write, and continue to require constant tweaking because
of browser updates and incompatibilities.  I wrote a smoother working
version in Livecode in 10 lines in a couple of hours (and that was
picking up the tool cold after almost 10 years).  Programming in JS is
strictly for amateurs and strictly for those who are blind to the fact
that browsers are doomed as a technology (ever used an android or
ipad?  Would you read gmail or do Facebook or query Yelp in a browser
window instead of the app?  Only if you're clueless).
