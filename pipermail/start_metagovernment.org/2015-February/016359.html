On 02/08/2015 09:48 PM, Scott Raney wrote:
> On Sat, Jan 31, 2015 at 7:43 AM, Michal Štěpánek
> <michael.stakor at gmail.com> wrote:
>
>> IMHO anarchist believe that social redistribution by "elites" e.g.
>> government happens mostly under the threat of revolution, that social
>> redistribution is natural to society of people and that "elites" are just
>> preventing it.
> In other words "charity".  This position (shared by most libertarians)
> is clearly wrong as it's been tried and doesn't work (there's a page
> on the Matchism site that specifically addresses the issue:
> http://www.matchism.org/charity/)
>
>> I is about faith, but I thing it is better to believe that people are good
>> and be a little bit wrong, than to believe that we are all rotten and force
>> people to act according to it.
> Adding a moral categorization phase to the process is IMHO not
> helpful.  We can design the system to work well regardless of how many
> "good" or "bad" people there are and under what particular
> circumstances they behave those ways.  Indeed we can design the system
> so that it functions without the requirement of making this kind of
> moral classification at all...
>
>> There were a few insightful comments about communism in there, particularly
>> the need to go *through* an anarchist phase before true communism can be
>> achieved.  But even this was oddly reminiscent, albeit completely
>> contradictory, to what Trotsky had to say about the need to go through a
>> bourgeois phase to get from peasant farmers to communism.  Which is it?
>>
>> IMHO it is meant
>> 1st  - peasant -> bourgeois  -> anarchist   -> communist
>> 2nd - peasant -> bourgeois  -> ?                -> communist
>> but it could be absolutely wrong
> Sounds reasonable to me, but you're extrapolating.  None of the
> (other) theorists I'm aware of include all 4 steps.  And I think the
> whole premise is probably wrong too: Much as I like Trotsky in general
> (though he does tend to whine and hold grudges) his claim about the
> need to pass through a bourgeoisie phase neglects what I find to be
> the biggest problem with the bourgeois: Their sense of entitlement.
> Once that is established it is very difficult to get them to give up
> anything for the "common good".  I think you'd have a better chance of
> success by selectively educating peasants to turn them directly into
> communists than trying to push an entire population *through* two
> other phases, both of which are pretty sticky because of that acquired
> sense of entitlement (anarchists, or at least libertarians, also
> positively reek of that in my experience).
>
>> So I'm still stuck with the basic question: How does any form of anarchism
>> really deal with any sort of wealth inequality other than handwaving about
>> how it won't ever develop if the participants are truly motivated to
>> prioritize the enterprise over their individual preferences?
>>
>> If we look back at Spanish revolution, there was initial forced
>> redistribution in Robin Hood style. I believe they were ready to repeat it,
>> if they would see that there is some unjustified inequity again.
> Agreed.  You see the same thing in the Trotsky's "permanent
> revolution" where anytime the system starts to revert a good top-down
> whack will bring it back into shape.  Not exactly great social
> engineering IMHO: Better to design the system so that regulation is a
> little more continuous and fine tuned...
>
> I'm also not really convinced by the Spanish civil war examples that
> the anarchists always point to: Sure, they had great numbers of people
> *claiming* to be members of this movement, but it didn't last long
> enough (a few years) to see if it was really a stable system.  People
> will put up with a lot for short periods of time, but until the system
> stabilizes to the degree necessary for them to say "yeah, I could live
> this way for the rest of my life", it's hard to call it a success.
> And the Spanish revolution didn't last long enough to make that kind
> of claim.  My prediction would be that it would fare far worse than
> the kind of top-down communism the Soviets imposed (which at least did
> last a very long time, albeit requiring fairly brutal "adjustments" on
> a regular basis).
