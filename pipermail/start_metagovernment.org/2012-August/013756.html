Hello Alex,

"... because things do not improve as fast as i'd like them to improve". 
Well, then let us find out, why this is the case and what we can do 
about it.

"Normally the hardest thing is to finde common ground on which we could 
build." For me, that is easy. In my eyes our government sucks so bad, 
that I would go for almost any alternative, that offers true 
improvement. To fight over which of all these proposals is the better or 
the "right" one is useless anyway: it's the audience, that decides which 
one will be applied.
And there's one thing, that can be learned from the CDU (German 
conservative party): the citizens shy away from parties, that shows 
dissension. That's, in my eyes, the only thing the CDU does well.

So, my offer for common ground would be: we are a community of people 
who try make this world a better place. There is a friendly, sportive 
competition among the proposals and, yes, sometimes we get carried away 
by fervour, frustration etc., which we forgive, because we all are only 
human.
We will help each other with friendly criticism, swapping of ideas, 
information etc.. And, once the audience has decided on which solution 
to apply, those, whose solution did not make (for then), will wipe away 
their tears and swallow their pride and help pushing forward, for its a 
long way to go.
Should we disagree on something, we simply state that and move on to 
find out on what we agree and focus on that. And we constantly remind 
ourselves that those, who are ignorant, deprive themselves from the 
chance to learn something and therefore are intellectually stagnant.

To keep myself from getting entangled in useless intellectual fights I 
use the technique of stating open questions instead of placing 
statements. (As an example, see my response to Michael on Votorola). 
Also, sticking to facts and indicators can help avoiding getting stuck 
in arguments on who is right. And, last but not least, let's remind us, 
that we are fighting against the "others" and not ourselves.

To put this discussion "on its feet" as we say in Germany: which topics 
should we elaborate on?

I would like to find out where the problem of getting people on board 
lies. And the first question would be: what facts, or at least 
indicators, do we have, that can tell us something about the reluctance 
of our comrade citizens to join the bandwagon?

There's one indicator I can offer: I found Metagovernment only by 
coincidence. It does not seem to be known in the press. If this 
perception is right, then the question is: Which leads to the question: 
What can we do about it?
