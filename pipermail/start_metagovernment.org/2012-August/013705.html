On Aug 1, 2012, at 7:28 PM, Michael Allan wrote:

> If our aim were to ruin Facebook's business, then true, we would not
> have the law on our side.  But that is not our aim.  Thomas states it:
> 
>  I would like to use an open source alternative to Facebook.  Mainly
>  because I don't want a huge company to mess with my data.  But since
>  I can't convince my friends it doesn't work.  I guess, there is a
>  "network-effect" where people go where the others already are and
>  then they all stay there.  If this is true, open source alternatives
>  will never have a chance.
> 
> Thomas is asking for choice.  Social publicity/promotion networks are
> a "species of business" that Facebook dominates and because of that
> dominance there are no viable alternatives.  Thomas wants to fix that.
> Am I reading you correctly, Thomas?

The aim is not to ruin their business, but the effect is. In order to make Facebook a free (as in freedom) place, it needs to have the corporate interest removed. But you do make an important nuanced distinction: we're not out to destroy Facebook, just to fundamentally change its nature.

However, I am still leery of relying on them to facilitate the process. Make a physical-world analogy. Imagine if Facebook also provided free access to physical meeting rooms (with billboards on the walls, I guess) in their headquarters building. Wouldn't they have the right to throw us out of a room for whatever reason they please? We're not paying them anything: they're just offering a free service to us.
