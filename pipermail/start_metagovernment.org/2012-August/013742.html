I believe that both of you have a good point. Ronald is correct in saying
that many people are not interested in the political process. Even if we
could minimize the cause of this, there will always be people unwilling or
unable to fully participate. This is the reality of the matter. However
Alex is also correct in that there are masses of people who are completely
disenfranchised, but desperately want to feel as if they have made a
difference.

I know people who fit into both categories as well as those that have
participated through political parties and other independent forms of
activism. Ronald is also correct to say that these are not the people we
need to be worried about necessarily. When the tool or ecosystem of
internet democracy is matured, they will be ready.

People like grandmother and the working class are the demographics that
will be required if we want to really foment the change necessary to evolve
our democratic structures.

For that reason I suggest a dynamic distributed democracy as a compromise.

http://firstmonday.org/htbin/cgiwrap/bin/ojs/index.php/fm/article/view/2584/2250

This allows people to both take part in the process as a direct contributor
to the online forum, as well as people to select representatives to
contribute for them.

Of course this has its own downsides and the implementation is
significantly more difficult and open to malicious behavior, it satisfies
the problems brought forward in this thread. On the other hand there is
also a very important and distinct advantage. DDD transitions our political
discussion and activity from mass-media and political parties into our
communities of friends and family.

Rather than fight the existing political structures and mass media we
simply cut it away and render it useless. When you can elect people you
know and trust as representatives whenever you want, the election cycle
disappears. So it has all of the advantages of both direct and direct
democratic processes.
