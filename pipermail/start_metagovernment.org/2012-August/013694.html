On Aug 1, 2012, at 3:05 AM, Steven Devijver wrote:

> İ think the problem with this tactic is that as soon as the takeover bid is taken serious by the wallstreet market facebook shares in the market will actually increase in price. There will be a huge premium to pay if "we" are the ones that what to buy shares.

If our aim is to completely gut the profitability of the company, then why would our interest in buying it increase its value? I think there is no way we can really predict what the market will do, and that we would just have to try it and see what happens.


On Aug 1, 2012, at 2:59 AM, Michael Allan wrote:

> We might have anti-trust laws on our side.  Your initial post is clear
> that the aim is to get around Facebook's monopoly.  As long as that
> remains true, Facebook cannot arbitrarily shut down the group without
> breaking the law.  They're allowed to have a monopoly, but not to
> abuse it.  It would be a clear abuse to deprive the group of the use
> of Facebook for the purpose of promotion and publicity, because it is
> precisely here that Facebook has its monopoly.

They don't have a pure monopoly, as there are certainly other social networks out there. Also, it seems like they would have a good case for saying they don't have to support a group whose purpose is to ruin their business (American courts take profits very seriously).

Even if we did have a good case against them, they could still shut down the group. Our recourse would be to engage in a protracted legal battle against a horde of lawyers, which isn't really the point of the exercise.

>>> [...] So there should be an external petition such as on
>>> http://www.change.org/ 

As I think about this, a petition wouldn't be quite right. We're not asking Facebook to do anything. The real tool we need is something where people can pledge funds. It would probably make sense to have a low cap on how much people can pledge, like $20 or maybe $100, so we don't get spammed with billion dollar pledges.

> In any case, we should be careful to follow the rules.  That group Ed
> pointed to was shut down for breaking a rule.

I am pretty sure they can remove a group for no reason; as I understand it, groups are their property. They can also change their terms of use at any time.
