>
> People
>

>

>

>
>
> in principle... because it empowers people!
> in practice it depends on the user interface and the "gamification" of 
> the consensus process that happens through it.
>
>
>     How does Votorola provide transparency on how the results came about?
>
>
> everything is transparent and everyone could could analyze the whole 
> consensus tree.
> it also has a decentralized structure and everyone could create its 
> own votorola community which connects to others through the mechanism 
> of vote mirroring, so everyone might join a community he or she trusts.
>
>
>     If it's for the common man: is that report simple enough for the
>     common man to understand?
>
>
> yes, but in practice it again depends on the user interface!
>
>
>     If it's for the common man: how do you deal with those, who can't
>     handle a computer?
>
>
> you dont!
> how do you deal with those who cant write in a normal election?
> how do you deal with those who dont know about the date the election 
> takes place?
> they have relatives that could help those people.
>
> the argument you are putting up here is: YOU MAY NOT USE THE INTERNET, 
> BECAUSE THERE ARE PEOPLE OUT THERE WHO WILL NEVER USE IT!
> 1. These people will die out sooner or later
> 2. We are far from a situation where such systems might become the 
> status quo
> 3. these systems might be used in very different ways, even through 
> pencil and paper (where the table is a multitouch table...)
>
> ...so in the end it again is only a question of "user interface 
> design" and how you enable older people or so... to use the system.
>
>     If it's for the common man: how do you deal with those, who refuse
>     to put their trust in a computerized solution?
>
>
> your argument is not legit!
> people already trust computerized solutions for online banking, for 
> telephone calls, for hospitals and the machines that help sick people 
> to survive, and they also trust in a lot of other mechanisms.
>
> how do you deal with people how dont trust the ballots in normal 
> elections? Did you ever watch a magician like DAVID COPPERFIELD make 
> stuff disappear in front of your eyes? So what in principal exactly 
> makes the "ballots in normal elections" more trustworthy than 
> computerized elections?
>
> RIGHT! ... NOTHING!
>
> It all depends on transparency and maybe on decentralization, so that 
> you dont have a single point of failiure or so...
> People accept it, because they've used to it and they've grown up with 
> it. They dont accept it because they know how it works exactly.
>
>
>     How does Votorola fit into the existing political process?
>
>
> That only time will tell....
>
>     What changes to the existing political process are required to
>     introduce Votorola?
>
>
> no changes at all, or many changes. ...one cannot know how people will 
> use systems like votorola and how it will affect the current status 
> quo, one can only guess.
>
>     Does Votorola require legal changes (changing the constitution)?
>
>
> no.
>
>
>     And the most important questions:
>     What does the roadmap for introducing Votorola look like?
>
>
> i think such a roadmap doesnt make sense.

> using it and eventually it goes viral and spreads.
>
>     How do you want to attract citizens and politicians towards Votorola?
>
>
> we'll see. first find users to test, try to incorporate data that
