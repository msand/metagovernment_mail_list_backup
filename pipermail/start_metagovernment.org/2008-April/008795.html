When I first started working on a social network for my local community, I
learned two things:

1. My definition of social network wasn't any different than MySpace, and
2. My community had no idea why they should use the neighborhood website
instead of MySpace.

No amount of customization or additional features (calendars, security, et.
al) managed to change their perspective. The "pie in the sky" idea to
someday connect every voter to the mayor, every citizen to every law, every
tax to every payer -- didn't look any different than MySpace.

I decided that the problem started with No. 1. In order to build the
software, I needed to understand what I was building -- not from a "software
perspective," but from the perspective of an "Open Source Society." For this
reason, I believe that starting on the "Good Book" is essential to our own
beginnings.

If we're successful, we'll end up with something that is simple enough for
outsiders to understand, and should serve to keep us on track when we're
developing the actual application.

> This is very much my thinking as well. If you look at almost any other
> species of social animals, you always see a leader for each group.
> This is the basic way an animal group can maintain order.
>
> If you give those animals language, self-awareness, commerce, a vast
> global society, and technology to create enormously complex social
> groups... do they still need to rely on the basic animal leadership
> structure?
>
> It seems to me that, with the advent of internet-based social
> networking, we have finally come up with the mechanism to implement
> something better. I really have no doubt that human governance will
> move entirely to the internet sometime in the next hundred years. My
> motivation in getting together a group of open-source-proponents to
> create it is to beat the traditional institutions to the punch.
>
> If open source doesn't succeed first, humanity could remain under the
> rule of traditional institutions which simply have moved to the
> internet. Or perhaps something even worse; where Microsoft or NewsCorp
> end up in control (via FaceBook and MySpace).
>
> Right now is a potential turning point in history, where humanity will
> decide what sort of mechanism will be in control of online governance.
> Will it be traditional leadership-based governments, or will it be
> commercial corporations, or will it be all people brought together in
> the most open, inclusive, and transparent system we can possibly create?
