Question: If we're not 'voting' -- ie, if there is no tally of whether
a person agrees or dissents, how do we know when consensus is reached?

Does one have to propose an alternative in order to dissent?

And, if only 1 person of 2 million proposes an alternative, and all
others agree with the first proposal, is consensus reached?

I can see and agree with our not wanting a vote by majority, but
because these bills have real consequence and since most cannot be
undone easily, there needs to be formal criteria for "consensus". This
is the main benefit of a vote -- it is unequivocal in its fairness
(hence its being the fallback for difficult disputes). I would be
interested to know how we determine consensus without some sort of
count (ie, vote) of something related to user activity (be it page
views, or counter-opinions).

Jacki



On Sun, Apr 13, 2008 at 2:27 PM,  <harrystottle at fullmoon.nu> wrote:
> Manuel Said:
>  "the main one being that voting is actually not a good way to do
>  democracy."
>
>  spot on. He added:
>
>  "Resolutions shouldn't be produced, in which the majority can inflict its
>  will on a minority. You have to build consensus with the whole community.
>  This is an important distinction and one of the main reasons open source
>  works. The Idea is not to find out what the majority wants, but what
>  everybody can agree upon. Only in very rare cases should there need to be
>  any formal voting procedures."
>
>  This is one of the primary focusses of my own work. See for example
>
>  http://www.fullmoon.nu/book/chap.php?id=c07_1#athens or
>  http://www.kuro5hin.org/story/2007/3/29/52250/4540
>
>  Democracy means People Power, it does not mean "Majority Rule", it means
>  AT LEAST "Majority Rule" but that is very much a last resort to be adopted
>  under two conditions only:
>
>  1 There is a consensus that a decision MUST be reached on the substantive
>  issue but
>  2 No consensus can be reached on that issue.
>
>  The example I give in my "Democratic Cannibals" story (second link above)
>  is the decision on "which side of the road should we drive". We cannot
>  afford NOT to make a decision because that would produce carnage. But we
>  might have a split within the community based on which side of the road
>  our existing vehicles are designed to travel on.
>
>  In such a case the "fair" solution is also the cheapest. i.e. to favour
>  the majority but compensate the minority (who, presumably, will have to
>  buy new cars or pay to have their cars adapted). i.e. the whole community
>  bears the cost of ensuring that the minority don't suffer as a result of a
>  decision by the majority.
>
>  Essentially there is no ethical case for arguing that a minority should be
>  forced or obliged to tolerate a decision by a majority which causes harm
>  or cost to that minority. This imposes a form of ethical hierarchy or
>  priority on the decision making process.
>
>  For example:
>  The first aim - and ideal result - is to achieve true consensus - the
>  complete absence of dissent.
>
>  If that fails, the next decision has to be whether we still need a
>  decision at all.
>
>  If no agreement can be reached on that, then the next question is
>  restricted to those who are convinced a decision must be taken. Can they
>  arrive at one which affects only themselves and excludes those who do not
>  wish to be party to it.
>
>  If no such restricted decision is possible, do they agree between
>  themselves that they must, nevertheless, seek to impose their decision on
>  the dissenters (and do they have the means to do so?)
>
>  Meanwhile the dissenters will be holding their own discussions about how
>  much their dissent matters and whether imposition would force them to
>  leave (or fight) the rest of the "democratic" group.
>
>  In other words, voting is always a sign of failure to meet the initial
>  ideal. And once we have dissent, the real issue - for the majority - is
>  whether the issue is so important that it justifies overriding the dissent
>  with measures ranging from compensation through to military force.
>
>  The Athenians recognized those basic rules and remain the only significant
>  historical attempt to implement them. They failed partly because while
>  they understood the need for consensus either on the issue or at least the
>  need to reach a decision (voting never took place, under the Athenian
>  system, if there wasn't a prior consensus that a decision was necessary)
>  they didn't account for the sheeplike tendency of people to follow those
>  who spoke forcefully or charismatically rather than those who spoke
>  wisely.
>
>  It is that weakness in the original democratic model which I am most
>  concerned to tackle. The web, fortunately, holds the solution, in that it
>  provides a level playing field in which all contributions can be stripped
>  of extraneous influences. Here, it need not matter what we look like or
>  sound like, our ideas can stand or fall purely on the basis of their
>  intrinsic merit. And we can present our arguments using whichever
>  techniques best suit our abilities.
>

>  instance, in the IRC channel that Manuel is suggesting - or any other real
>  time chat. At least not on a serious issue. (Actually that was a strange
>  suggestion from Manuel giving that he's also commented on the need to
>  ensure accessibility to those with various disabilities - with which I
>  agree)
>
>  I can - and will - take hours (sometimes days) to formulate my thoughts or
>  responses and have no intention of being "rushed"!
