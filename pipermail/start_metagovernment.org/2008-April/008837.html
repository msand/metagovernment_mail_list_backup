On Apr 13, 2008, at 3:24 PM, Jacki Buros wrote:
> It would seem that, initially, a governing body would need to be
> established with a some sort of scope or mandate .. but, that scope is
> then realized according to how effectively the governing body is able
> to govern vis-a-vis the other governing bodies with similar scope. If
> it doesn't govern well in a particular area, another governing body
> may take over the responsibility.. if none does and an egregious gap
> exists, then a new governing body would have to be proposed in order
> to govern as needed.

This is very similar to the concept of the Consensus Score for any  
particular resolution, just on a larger scale.

The idea of the consensus score is that once a proposition hits a  
certain critical mass of support, it is granted some (mathematical)  
inertia so that a small dip in support won't readily deactivate it.  
Then the only ways to change a law would be to either have a dramatic  
drop in support or for someone to come up with something better:  
something which synthesizes it.

I am wondering if we could develop both concepts at the same time. If  
the lifespan of a resolution and the lifespan of a governing body are  
both based on the same sort of math, then can we just have one formula?
