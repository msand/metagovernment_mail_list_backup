I think branches are important, since legislation is often lengthy and
people may agree with most of what's there but disagree with one part.
If we have branches, there ought to be a way to merge the result of
branch development (once consensus on a branching element is reached)
back into the 'trunk' document.

One way wikipedia solves this problem is by keeping their article
topics relatively short and focused, and allowing one to 'include' a
subpage into a main page. We can implement something similar, but the
size of our articles cannot be limited only for the sake of
convenience. Thus, we may need to allow branching.

> It might be useful if we could outline the main benefits and disadvantages
> of having development branches as well as the main trunk. Wikipedia doesn't
> have branches and seems to manage quite well without them. But probably 90
> percent of OSS projects do tend to use development branches.
>
> As I see it there might only be 1 law in effect at the time but the
> development of the law may go off in different directions only to merge back
> in later, or perhaps a development may be abandoned or closed, or
> synthesised with another development also not yet part of the agreed statute
> books. So its theoretically more adaptable as a result of more possible
> variations being explored, and it provides a useful framework for major
> developments to be collaboratively trialed and managed without contaminating
> the latest "stable release".
>
> Now that I say that, I can't think how wikipedia get away without branches
> as they have inter-related articles and topic micro-sites that require
> considerable collaborative development?
>
> I think the answer is that they keep their branches very small. ie. the
> discussion pages with reference to the edit history is where branches are
> explored and debated. If branches can be kept small then more effort goes
> into trunk development and the project can advance at a faster pace.
>
> It makes a lot of sense for us to have some facility for branches, but for
> there to be constant pressure (perhaps as in wikipedia's case exerted by the
> system itself as there is little facility for large branches) to keep them
> as small as possible. In manufacturing terms this keeps us a low inventory,
> lean outfit with a high return on our investments and the ability to quickly
> shift output capacity from one area to another.
>
