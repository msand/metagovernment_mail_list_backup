I couldn't agree more -- the benefits of a metagovernment would be
complete transparency and the participation of all in the legislative
process. Since the advent of television, public debates are more about
personalities than they are about issues and ideas -- I feel that the
internet can have an opposite effect of similar magnitude, and has to
some extent with the rise of political blogs, but most of its
potential has yet to be realized.

I would argue that a successful partnership with legislative bodies
and with educational institutions (once the site is built) would help
to stem this problem of the ebb and flow of content -- irrespective of
whether the application is meant to supplement or replace the existing
government. To successfully supplement the government, it would have
to be capable of replacing it. Otherwise, all you have is a fancy
political blog. The capacity to replace the government empowers the
users in a way that would otherwise not be felt -- it becomes a "what
if we were in charge" kind of experiment rather than a mere forum of
opinion. Hence, my excitement over the metagovernment project.

I say this in order to communicate that I am personally committed to
the development of the metagovernment, and, to introduce the idea that
voter apathy might be a concern.  Wikipedia has only a small number of
users who contribute content though very many enjoy its benefits. In
designing the software, we should ensure that participation is as easy
and straightforward as possible.

Jacki
