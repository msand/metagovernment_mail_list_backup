I do see your point.

I was looking for ways for people to become more involved with the political
processes without requiring them to sign into their account on an hourly
basis. From my perspective, many will rely on mobile phones, public
computers, or possibly ATM machines to register their votes. Those who own
their own computers and internet connections would possibly have an
advantage when it comes to the process.

If we can find a way to ensure that those without 24hr internet access
aren't disenfranchised, I think my concerns would be answered. However, as a
frequent user of Digg.com and theSixtyOne.com, I know that my daily activity
provides me with better connections and higher points than those who only
check in once a month. Maybe this is a strength of the system: where those
more dedicated are naturally rewarded for their commitment.

I'm on the fence with this one.

> to be notified

>
> Stages would be something like initial proposal, discussion,
> revisions/amendments
> and eventually it will be queued for merging into the main repository,
> signifying
> consensus. At any of these points you can choose to be notified and
> receive a certain
> number of messages. During the discussion phase, you may choose to receive
> only
> the most important messages, or the positions of participating groups.
> Some may
> wish to be informed of all legislation up for merge, and only participate
> in the
> discussion if a bill ever gets that far.
>
> I would hope that holding a vote would be something so special, that
> everybody would
> want to be notified. It isn't just an indicator of a controversy, but
> widespread voting
> shows that people are unable to build consensus. Voting is a sign of a
> problem within
> the community itself.
>
> I've already let go of not having tools for voting in the software,
> since there will
> inevitably be cases where it is necessary, but lets please not think
> of that feature
> being the default.
