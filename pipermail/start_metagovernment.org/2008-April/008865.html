There should be, in the start-up phase where the constitution of the 
community's government is written, a part where the people grant the 
government particular rights -- levying taxes, setting laws that control 
commerce, etc. Also, the definition of the community it is meant to 
govern. Otherwise, the people will have to implicitly authorize the new 
community's government via the existing government (as in a law passed 
to create an organization to govern X). If we are going to promote the 
creation of governing bodies, this process needs to be supported in the 
software, no? There needs to be a 'proposal' phase of the potential new 
governing body while its supporters attempt to work out its constitution 
and the people decide whether to authorize it.

But, I'm thinking about it some more, and this is really an implicit 
question -- is there a need for a 'constitution' if the people represent 
themselves and there are no 'legislators' other than the people being 
legislated? Example, if some governing body were not authorized to levy 
taxes but then the people vote to do so, should this not be OK? And, if 
OK, why have the limit placed in the first place? Maybe the constitution 
should only outline the criteria for consensus, the process for 
approval, etc. Thoughts? In the absence of defined roles per governing 
body, could we not run into conflict where several governing bodies are 
all legislating on the same issue (all, simultaneously attempting to 
solve the same problem)? Yes, it will work out in the long run, but 
until then it could create some chaos.

Thoughts?


Ed Pastore wrote:
> It looks like several of our conversations are leaning toward the same  
> conclusion: that open source governments need to be self defining.
>
> Earlier, I had suggested that we define "government" as "the  
> organizing principle of a community." Of ANY community, from a handful  
> of people who get together to play poker on Saturday nights, to the  
> people who work in a particular hospital, to all the people living in  
> one urban area, to everyone in the world. Each of those is a  
> community, and each community needs some sort of governance.
>
> If we can allow any community to form its own government, then the  
> issue of branching and merging is handled by the communities. If there  
> are two distinct communities on either side of a river, and if these  
> two communities see a commonality between them, then they can form a  
> third community which contains both. The two smaller communities can  
> continue to operate, but will only make laws relevant to issues only  
> affecting their side of the river. The larger community would handle  
> issues that affect both sides of the river. If the smaller communities  
> find that their issues are all better handled by the larger community,  
> then they will just fade away over time as people stop participating  
> in them.
>
>
