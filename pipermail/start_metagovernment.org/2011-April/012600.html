Thomas, on yours, I'm not sure I see the value of a decision-rule for  
articles. Would the be in the wiki? We don't want every change to the  
wiki to require a vote, do we? Or am I misunderstanding? I stated mine  
as one overarching decision rule, since it is a definition of  
consensus. For anything which needs consensus, including a change to  
the meta-rules, the definition should be pretty much the same.

Let me also be clear that I am still pretty shaky on a lot of what I  
have proposed. In other words, I am not sure I 100% advocate what I  
said as it is stated.

As a tangent of that, I really like Owen's idea of being able to vote  
for things based on how much other people support them (which in a  
sense is a reflection of how much one supports them onesself), but I  
can't figure out how to begin approaching encoding that into a  
ruleset. I think that would require returning to my now-long-overruled  
idea of scoring proposals instead of simply voting for or against  
them. In that case, I am about 80% in favor of the proposal I made  
myself, and about 60% in favor of yours. That sort of scoring adds a  
huge amount of complexity to the overall process, but... it's true.  
That's how I feel, and I think most decisions are not absolute but  
rather degrees of preference.

In other words, if everyone likes your idea, I'd be happy to go along  
with it. But I'd prefer my idea. And I would even more prefer  
something else which I have not been able to state yet. No idea on the  
table has my 100% support at this time.

>>> @ Ed:
>>>> I don't want to get overly complex, but perhaps it would be  
>>>> reasonable
>>>> to use some more complex math than simple counting or percentages.
>>>>
>>>> For example, how defining consensus (C) as:
>>>> ( X * (P+2) ) - 1, rounded up.
>>>>
>>>> where P = the number of people voting,
>>>> and X = a variable set by the software, defaulting to 0.8.
>>>>
>>>> Therefore, for X = 0.8...
>>>> For 1 person, C is equal to 2. (i.e., impossible)
>>>> For 2 people, C is equal to 3. (i.e., impossible)
>>>> For 3 through 7 people, C is equal to P. (i.e. 100%).
>>>> For 8 people, C is equal to 7.
>>>> For 20 people, C is equal to 17
>>>> For 100 people, C is equal to 81.
>>>> For very large numbers of people, C is basically equal to 80% of P.
>>>>
>>>> In other words, the standard is, roughly:
>>>> Impossible for one or two people. Complete consensus for small  
>>>> numbers
>>>> of people. Near-consensus for intermediate groups. And 80% (or X)  
>>>> for
>>>> large groups.
