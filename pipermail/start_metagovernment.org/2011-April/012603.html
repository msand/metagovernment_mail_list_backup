On Sun, 10 Apr 2011 16:34, Ed Pastore wrote:
> Thomas, on yours, I'm not sure I see the value of a decision-rule for 
> articles. [...] We don't want every change to the wiki to require a 
> vote, do we?

Actually I tried to draft my position in a way, that this would 
naturally solve itself. Over every wiki-article (including the homepage) 
there can break out an edit-war, i.e. the need for voting. If not, then 
a single person should be enough to change it: if he is the only one, he 
holds 100% of the votes for his change. But you are right, he shouldnt 
have to wait for the waiting-period.

So I added in my draft (as Conseo suggested) the (very simple) rule for 
starting a new poll: An edit-war must be declared.

http://metagovernment.org/wiki/User:ThomasvonderElbe/draft_rules

Btw, I dont see the need to open a Votorola-poll for every little case, 
where 2 people disagree about one word. This could be done on the list 
and as soon as a third takes sides and one week goes by, its done.

> Or am I misunderstanding? I stated mine as one overarching decision 
> rule, since it is a definition of consensus. For anything which needs 
> consensus, including a change to the meta-rules, the definition should 
> be pretty much the same.

I feel, changing the meta-rule should be harder, than changing the 
home-page. And like in my above example, a little change on some 
wiki-page should be really easy and fast, shouldnt it? Also because 
nothing is fixed forever. If it becomes important to more people, it can 
always be changed later. If a Votorola-poll has been started, it is 
ongoing forever anyway. It never stops.

I like the idea of having the waiting period be a variable of the number 
of participants and the degree of consensus. Thats what you are 
proposing, Ed, right? But doing it with a math-formula (which has to be 
that complex I guess), it feels pretty confusing to me (even with me 
having a little math-background). I can imagine it repells "normal" 
participants. Is the shortage of the waiting-period worth this price? Or 
how, if we manually set this data for now? We can always change it later.

>>> Hey Thomas and Ed,
>>>
>>>> @ Ed:
>>>>> I don't want to get overly complex, but perhaps it would be reasonable
>>>>> to use some more complex math than simple counting or percentages.
>>>>>
>>>>> For example, how defining consensus (C) as:
>>>>> ( X * (P+2) ) - 1, rounded up.
>>>>>
>>>>> where P = the number of people voting,
>>>>> and X = a variable set by the software, defaulting to 0.8.
>>>>>
>>>>> Therefore, for X = 0.8...
>>>>> For 1 person, C is equal to 2. (i.e., impossible)
>>>>> For 2 people, C is equal to 3. (i.e., impossible)
>>>>> For 3 through 7 people, C is equal to P. (i.e. 100%).
>>>>> For 8 people, C is equal to 7.
>>>>> For 20 people, C is equal to 17
>>>>> For 100 people, C is equal to 81.
>>>>> For very large numbers of people, C is basically equal to 80% of P.
>>>>>
>>>>> In other words, the standard is, roughly:
>>>>> Impossible for one or two people. Complete consensus for small numbers
>>>>> of people. Near-consensus for intermediate groups. And 80% (or X) for
>>>>> large groups.
>>>> Wow! I like this! Its flexibility perfectly fits with my feeling of how
>>>> it should be in large vs. small groups.
