On Apr 3, 2011, at 9:30 PM, Owen Ambur wrote:

> Interesting proposal, Ed.   I wish it applied to Congress.
>
> It seems to me no time limits should be applied to voting, at least  
> not initially, but that the pool of eligible voters should be able  
> easily to determine when their votes (positive or negative) may  
> begin to “count” (make a difference), such as:
>
> a)      When 3 to 7 people have voted for a proposal, and
> b)      Whenever the percentage of votes in support of a proposal is  
> near (just below or just above) 80%.
>
> Under those circumstances, I doubt any time limit would be necessary  
> to motivate individuals to vote on issues about which they care –  
> unless the number of proposals is large enough to create an burden  
> on voters.  However, if that proves to be the case, I suspect that  
> sufficient numbers might vote negatively on most issues, in protest  
> of being so burdened.
>
> Alternatively, they may wish to impose a new rule raising the degree  
> of support required to more than 80%.   Perhaps the percentage  
> should automatically increase as the number of proposals rises, to  
> account for the burden that is implicit.
>
> Also, perhaps time limits should be applied when the required degree  
> of agreement has been reached, before proposals are considered to be  
> approved, and perhaps those limits should be inverse to the degree  
> of agreement on each proposal.
>
> Those thoughts prompt this further thought:   How about enabling  
> voters to condition their votes upon the actions of others?
>
> For example, for some proposals that I personally oppose, I might be  
> willing to accept a level of 80% support among other voters, whereas  
> I might insist upon a higher or accept a lower percentage for other  
> proposals.  And I might be willing to accept a proposal that has 90%  
> approval sooner (in less time) than one that has 80%.
>
> Of course, too, there is the case of greatest interest to me, in  
> which those who favor an objective are willing and able to  
> accomplish it by applying their own resources and efforts, in which  
> case I may vote for it even though I don’t plan to contribute to  
> it.   At least I would not vote against it unless I believe that it  
> might be harmful to me or others.
>
> That would be similar to the concept of “challenge” grants, whereby  
> one or more parties pledge to match resources provided by others.   
> For example, I might be willing to match the average monetary  
> contribution of others to develop and support an application  
> enabling users to do likewise with respect to other objectives  
> openly documented on the Web in StratML format.
>
> What does not interest me much, if any, is voting just for the sake  
> of voting, i.e., without any understanding, much less commitment to  
> see that the resources necessary to achieve the objective might  
> reasonably be applied.
>
> BTW, if your proposal is implemented in a working system, I’d be  
> supportive of establishing an objective to try to recruit 100 people  
> to participate in testing it.
>

> I don't want to get overly complex, but perhaps it would be  
> reasonable to use some more complex math than simple counting or  
> percentages.
>
> For example, how defining consensus (C) as:
> ( X * (P+2) ) - 1, rounded up.
>
> where P = the number of people voting,
> and X = a variable set by the software, defaulting to 0.8.
>
> Therefore, for X = 0.8...
> For 1 person, C is equal to 2. (i.e., impossible)
> For 2 people, C is equal to 3. (i.e., impossible)
> For 3 through 7 people, C is equal to P. (i.e. 100%).
> For 8 people, C is equal to 7.
> For 20 people, C is equal to 17
> For 100 people, C is equal to 81.
> For very large numbers of people, C is basically equal to 80% of P.
>
> In other words, the standard is, roughly:
> Impossible for one or two people. Complete consensus for small  
> numbers of people. Near-consensus for intermediate groups. And 80%  
> (or X) for large groups.
>
