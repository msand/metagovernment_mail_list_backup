I'll readily confess that I glanced not even at the Wikipedia page
before writing my response - however please disabuse me of the
contextual deductions I incorrectly made before dismissing it.

I was making a general point, and one which I did not present to be
disagreeable, that any forum to collect and distill public opinion and
group wisdom could be better able to serve that public through the
democratisation that technology provides - as opposed to physical
meetups which would seem to be inclined to produce leaders and
discriminate against those unable (through disability, locality, etc)
to attend.

So perhaps you would have found the point less disagreeable if I had
framed it as "making the public sphere better than it originally was",
rather than the contrast I was going for by suggesting that the
improvements that the correct application of technology can
potentially bring to the process are significant enough to cast any
and all previous implementations as "broken"?

However, I get the impression that your appreciation of the public
sphere lies in its ability to inform a governance, rather than its
ability to stimulate and solicit as much of the public will as
possible?
