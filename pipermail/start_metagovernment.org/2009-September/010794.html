Ed Pastore wrote:
> On Sep 21, 2009, at 6:49 AM, Giovani Spagnolo wrote:
>> In our proposed application "draft2gether"
>> (http://www.telematicsfreedom.org/en/projects/draft2gether-0) we
>> propose that the user - after a "x" number of comments, ratings and
>> amends done to one draft text, is mandated to rate "n" random other
>> amends before getting the right to continue participating.
> This is a clever approach. It is reminiscent of what I just saw on a
> beer-ratings site http://ratebeer.com which does not count any
> place-ratings from a particular person until they have reviewed ten
> places. What you're proposing for draft2gether makes a lot of sense in
> an ongoing community, where new proposals will constantly emerge.
>
> My only concern would be that it might encourage people to spew out
> random ratings just to get back to rating the item they are interested
> in. That would then degrade everything at once.

maybe not, (it is yet-to-be-experimented), but we believe that this
feature, in addition to the feature that calculates the number of
seconds before the user is allowed to rate the comment/amend based on
the lenght of it will slowly "teach" users that since they have to wait
a few seconds, and their eyes will inevitably be focused on that block
of text, they might as well read or skim through it and just don't give
a "random" rate, but a more approximate rate from what they saw.


>> Also, we thought about an algorithm that gets the length of a given
>> amend/comment and calculates a number of seconds needed before the
>> user is really allowed to rate it (ie.: based on configurable average
>> reading speeds - ie. 400wpm for
>> skimminghttp://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reading_%28process%29#Reading_rate).
>> This could be a better approach in terms of usability. What do you think?
>
> This sounds arbitrary and unlikely to be very effective. All a person
> has to do is wait until the button allows them to rate.
we are talking about a few seconds, there will be a configurable cap in
the admin page (i.e.: not more than 10secs), to avoid breaking the user
experience. the intention is to teach users to collaborate by rating
before reading/skimming the text.


> They could be playing video games or talking on the phone for all you
> know. It makes me think of some websites where they don't let you
> click the "Agree" button on their terms-of-use until you scroll all
> the way to the bottom.Most people just immediately scroll all the way
> down (still without reading) and then click the Accept button.
That's much different from the scenario of draft2gether, where the user
is rating comments/amends to the current version, not long "walls of
text" neither the whole document...

> Alternately, what if a person reads most of something but then has to
> log out for the day. They come back the next day, read the last bit
> they hadn't read yet, but then still can't rate because of a time limit?
no, the time limit is very short, the user mustn't have his ux broken by
the feature...
