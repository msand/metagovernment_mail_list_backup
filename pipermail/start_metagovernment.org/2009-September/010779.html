Hi Ed, Pietro, All


I think some really interesting points are being made here about the
scalability and performance of e-democracy systems.


In the article a week ago in the nyt "democracy 2.0 needs an upgrade"
the author reflected on how the results of direct participation
efforts are somewhat weaker than than they need to be in terms of
getting qualitatively superior plans (than current legislative systems
produce).

I think everyone on this list agrees that direct and "emergent"
democracy is the way of the future. In the next decade i think we hope
to see these forms compete for legitimacy with the traditional
institutions. E-democracy tools can also wildly improve the results of
the existing parliamentary/congressional systems, so producing "better
plans" is a moving target,  but kinda like the internal combustion
engine, we will hit a wall where squeezing more milage out of this old
technology (representative d) is physically impossible.


But why do so many direct democracy attempts fail? Lack of
participation. My main reflection on that is that most humans are not
wired for direct democracy, and don't care or have time for all the
details. Sure some people swing that way but most people most of the
time tend to pick somebody they like and follow.

This is where the work that michael allan and thomas vonderElbe i
think provides an important missing component. I was fortunate to have
a sit down with michael last week and his concept about a voting
cascade really opened my eyes that this fills a huge hole in the
emergent democracy universe. what do we do about the 96% of people who
are only mildly interested in politics?

 - I first came to know about the voterola idea a couple years ago and
to be honest i thought it was a "neat trick" but kinda out there.  now
that i understand the project better and the way that they are
developing the project as a "pollserver" which can (or will)
read/collect votes and polls from almost any kind of website - now it
is more of a "wow" response. I am far from fluent or what they are
doing or where they want to take it but i am certainly enthusiastic
about the concept.

Any one not familiar with the project - what they are working on is a
"voting cascade" that can handle pretty much any democratic decision,
policy or person. The cascade is an "always on" proxy system where
each individual places their trust in someone else, who is either
advancing a proposal or just being an advocate. In the cascade you can
"vote" for your friend, your neighbor or the president, big or small,
low or high, it doesn't matter. Wherever you put your vote, that
person will wield your proxy and your democratic interest is
represented by the person you deem best. Those people you vote for
cast their vote in turn, and those people vote for others. Eventually
a majority of those votes cascade into one leading candidate/solution.
People get a vote for each decision, so you don't need to put all your
proxy votes with one person, but you always have defaults established
so that your vote is always pointing somewhere. Maybe you have three
public figures you respect so you default your social policy, economic
policy, foreign policy votes to each of these people in turn, who then
may play "powerbroker" to compose or effect the actual language of
what becomes law.


The operating question for all voters/paticipants is always "who do
you trust". This is unique to this voting method. - but the more you
think about it the more it makes sense to me that trust, not votes,
not proposals, is the fundamental element in democracy.  Where you
place your trust is the fundamental decision that all the rest follows
from.

for example we all have news sources that we trust. these news sources
frame every thought we have about politics. If i read the globe and
mail everyday, well like it or not i am more or less a meat puppet for
the G&M, but that is fine because of all the options i trust them most
to have a balanced point of view. I am quite aware that what i read
five years ago is the foundation of what i believe today.


Who i trust defines more than anything where i exist in democracy
space. Who i trust is measurable and trackable, and non-specific to
issues or decisions, so the information is always valid. Through
social networking sites, i am continuously adding information about
who i trust or follow, so linking these preferences to democracyspace
seems all but inevitable.

If democracy is a marketplace, who i trust is like the portfolio i
hold. In this analogy, the vote cascade can and hopefully will
function like a stock market - each day we can learn about who the big
movers are, locally and globally. Think tanks, NGO's and celebrities
will be the democracyspace's equivalent to global corporations, except
in democracyspace there is solid and vigorous representation from the
local and micro-local levels. Because i can change who i trust at any
time - it keeps everyone honest.

Overall in such a system i think you would see (you would want to see)
a fairly smooth gradient of power and accountability. If you do the
math, you know that the exponential nature of the cascade will mean
that for any given vote/poll you will observe 1-10 degrees of
separation (usually less than five) between individual voters and
winning candidates/proposals. The reality of politics is that in all
cases, you need a significant voting block maintain real
accountability. If one of your supporters brings 10% of your total
votes - you will always take their calls and accept their amendments
whenever possible. If your supporters are at odds with each other, you
have to work out a sensible compromise to keep one or both from
jumping ship. As individual voters, we are always looking for "hard
working reps (big or small) who know and understand us, and who get
results week after week, keeping us informed as necessary. All of this
produces, i think, a healthy and stable democracy.


The voting cascade is not a magic bullet of course, we need a whole
range of tools for transparency, evaluating and ranking proposals, but
it provides a good idea for a baseline system that i can imagine a
majority of the population participating in - getting the most
democracy for the least effort.


MLP
