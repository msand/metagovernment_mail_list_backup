>> *Executive summary*
>>
>> What your suggesting as impossible has already been done. I Googled
>> "google" and it gave me exactly the information I wanted out of
>> 2,820,000,000 possible results in .44 seconds. We just need an
>> implementation specific to "governing". I am not saying Google is perfect
>> at it, but I am saying your idea that it is impossible is flawed.
>>
>> I don't like filtering because it segregates groups that have the same
>> opinion filtering the same thing. This happens on both sides of an issue
>> until neither side even sees each other, or one is completely muzzled. We
>> want understanding and some measure of consensus. Not a system that tells
>> you exactly what you want while allowing you to ignore what you don't
>> personally believe.
>>
>> Also, it could be really cool what you could do already by farming public
>> twitter posts.
>>
>> I then go on to try to preempt his next post, "But Google does filter" by
>> explaining a few cases where filtering does make sense. The filter should
>> strive to have no bias not represent the bias of a group of people.
>>
>> -----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
>>
>> What your suggesting is not possible actually already exists in a
>> different form.
>> Google seems to be able to do it. When I Googled "Google" it gave me.
>> 2,820,000,000 results (0.44 seconds)
>> It took them a lot of time and money but they have figured out a way to
>> help you wade through the internet and find what you are really looking
>> for. We just need a "search engine" and UI that bring you the relevant
>> information.
>>
>> Here are just a few ideas for what it could do.
>>
>>    - Combine comments that are essentially the same and have the reader
>>    read it once. Instead of reading the same thing 10,000 times your can read
>>    it one time with the appendage that 10,000 support this.
>>    - Give users a variable rank like Google page rank that assigns
>>    weight to peoples comments based on how many people source that person.
>>    Essentially, the more people that back you the more weight your comments
>>    have.
>>    - I am sure there are a lot more and invite anyone else to add these
>>    are just two I thought up off of the top of my head.
>>
>>
>> It isn't a perfect system. You can still game Google with shady SEO but
>> Google is getting better at giving you relevant information and is working
>> on their system all the time. We would need to do the same.
>>
>> I just don't like the idea of filtering because it promotes segregation
>> instead of consensus. Groups of people will consistently filter the same
>> thing and then only get information that they want to see fueling their
>> current opinions which then filter more things and so on. It will happen on
>> both sides of an argument further separating them from each other. The goal
>> should be to educate and bring consensus about a proposal. Not just to tell
>> you want and ignore what you don't.
>>
>> You could actually do an experiment by farming twitter posts and creating
>> a report on an issue. If only I had the money to build it. It would be a
>> cool experiment.
>>
>> I can see your argument brewing already and will try to answer it. "But,
>> Google does filter!". They do their best to be an impartial deliverer of
>> information without judgement. Your idea of filtering has the judgement of
>> those who created the filter deciding what information to deliver. I think
>> that is the difference. I never said there couldn't be auto--aggregation
>> and weighting of arguments based on people influence. But, I don't think we
>> should auto ignore based on judgement. The technology should be as unbiased
>> as possible. Giving you the in information to to make your own decision.
>>
>> That being said I do believe there is a place for filtering. Just not in
>> the way you would implement it. Non-sensical posts for example (should be
>> throughly tested to keep from judgement). But if you are talking about
>> expanding a new road in your city and someone writes, "But the sky is
>> Blue!" is an example of non-sensical. Or, spam filtering people who are
>> just advertising something.
>>
>> Also, in creating systems that keep you from being able to game the
>> system could use filtering. Like if 1000 people said the same thing, but
>> all their accounts are new within a short time and that is their only post.
>> You can probably assume it is someone trying to game the system. That being
>> said, there should be a system set up where you can plead your case if you
>> are filtered. That way if you really were a person that happened to signup
>> during that 1000 post time and said what you did as your first post you
>> could refute it and then be recognized by the system. Notice all of my
>> examples keep from the filter having judgement on any issue.
>>
