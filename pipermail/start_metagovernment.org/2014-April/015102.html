+1
I like that this is a more participatory system. I think Ed nails it on the
head that if you allowed a person more say, as in they actually
participated in crafting the decision, they would value it more. If they
valued it more they would participate more and you get a positive cycle
instead of the negative one we have today.

Enforcing would then be a lot easier because people who took part would
value the policy more.

Cheers,
James

On Tuesday, April 29, 2014, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:

> Scott, we seem to fundamentally differ here. I've explained my rationale,
> but I don't see yours. So I'll restate mine:
>
> I hold that voting and proposal generation/tuning must go hand-in-hand and
> absolutely should not be separate.
>
> A root problem with the status quo is that people are presented with a
> proposal and asked to vote it up or down. In the U.S., we call this a
> referendum, and it is horrible. The crafting of the proposal is where all
> the actual decision-making happens (the devil is in the details), and
> almost everyone is disenfranchised from that. And then when people are
> asked to vote, they don't care, since they have so little influence on the
> important parts of the matter: the details. A million vote for, a million
> vote against: what does my input matter?
>
> Voting *guides* proposal-tuning. It tells you what is getting buy-in and
> what is not consensus-building. While proposals are being crafted, we might
> come to a point where half are for and half are against. You suggest a
> majority-rule forced vote. I say that's a horrible solution. What if
> instead one person said: "Wait a minute, I have a completely different
> idea; hey everyone, see if this solves your issues better." A lot of those
> alternates might fail, but some genius might come up with something that
> gets almost everyone onboard. I say that is infinitely better than forcing
> a majority rule. And it cannot be done unless the voting and
> decision-making are mixed. You know you have an answer when the votes pile
> up on one proposal.
>
> And to repeat: I see no value in forcing decisions when there is not a
> consensus. Or high majority. You say in another post something along the
> lines of: a bad decision is better than no decision. Why? Why do we need to
> force decisions before their time? Maybe that is true for business, but not
> for social governance. What is the value of government if it is not of the
> people? Again, sure, maybe in case on imminent disaster. But otherwise, I
> cannot see the value of forcing society into bad decisions.
>
>
>
