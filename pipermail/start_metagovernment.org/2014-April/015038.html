I think I can dispense with threaded conversation in this instance and just reply to a few key points.

I'd like to reiterate my request for an elevator pitch. Your approach in your first message was, effectively, to list a bunch of criticisms of this group, and then direct us to all read your book. That's not fair, as it places a heavy burden on the audience. You should be able to summarize your book in a minute. It won't get across many of the deeper explanations or even major parts, but you can certainly get across the main message. And I am under no illusion that that is easy. It took me a long time to write the short little elevator pitch for Metagov. The shorter something is, the harder.

(There's a deeper lesson there, too... there is something to be said for text limits, as anything that can be said with a ton of text can often be said with very little text and very much thought.)

Concerning the failure of this group to adequately facilitate participation... I and many others agree. It is a criticism that has been raised here many times. And many have proposed various solutions. But nobody has implemented one. In the spirit of adhocracy... you are welcome to do so. Anyone is! Please do. Or try to get others to. I will help as I can, but my time is unbearably limited right now.

Concerning the definition of consensus; you're right that that should likely be changed. The wiki is a wiki. It can be changed by whomever is willing to do so. The only hard rules we have are that the home page and the Basic Principles pages need buy-in from the group before they are changed. There is also debate here about consensus, which is why I chose the subject line here. Blinap is a pure consensus model, as I understand it. Votorola promotes consensus by not having a set decision-rule. All projects are aimed at building consensus... even if some don't expect to reach unanimity. There's nothing wrong with striving toward perfection. So consensus is the most accurate word to describe what most of us are striving for. "Supermajority" does not have nearly as much buy-in among the projects. So if you want to replace "consensus," I would ask that you propose another word. And perhaps propose a system to use for deciding the word. Vilfredo works well for this sort of process of... building consensus. ;) Perhaps post it here?
http://vilfredo.org/viewquestions.php?room=Metagovernment

As for confounding proposal crafting with voting, I think they can and should be mixed. Look deeper at Votorola... it takes a long time to understand it fully, but once you do, you may see how effective it is at blending these two. Actually, Vilfredo does as well though by entirely different means. Both use voting as the means of proposal-tuning. It is really quite effective. You know when voting is done when everyone has voted for the same thing. If they haven't, then you can compare disparate proposals, see who voted for what, discuss, and propose something new to try to capture all the votes.
