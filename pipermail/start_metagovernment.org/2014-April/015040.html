On 22 April 2014 05:01, Scott Raney <scott at metacard.com> wrote:

> On Mon, Apr 21, 2014 at 2:16 PM, Bentley Davis <Bentley at bentleydavis.com>
> wrote:
> >
> >> "The best we can do is to design a system that supports filtering that
> >> stuff out of the feed"
> >
> > I wouldn't limit us to that one solution. Your statements read like you
> are
> > not open to other options.
>
> Open, sure, but I haven't seen or thought of any reasonable
> alternative.  Unless the system has a way to filter the information
> presented to each user, I claim that it is unworkable because it will
> simply require too much time and effort to use on any non-trivial
> problem.  Anyone disagree?
>
> >> "my second issue, which is the use of the term "facts" and "truth" in
> >> settleit.org and other debate systems."
> >
> > To my knowledge you won't find the words 'fact' or 'truth' on the
> > SettleIt.org website. Everything is a statement which can be debated.
>
> Truth is on there as is "knowledge" which I equate to "facts".
>
> >> "In the abortion debate, one of the "facts" is that the Bible says it's
> >> wrong."
> >
> > In your example of the abortion debate, one of the statements is that the
> > Bible says it's wrong. In SettleIt.org under that statement people would
> be
> > debating that the Bible does not say that abortion is wrong and others
> will
> > debate the weight of the bible on the abortion debate which includes if
> > someone should believe in the Bible.
>
> OK, so every SettleIt debate has the option of including a branch
> which will settle the issue of whether the Bible is true ;-)  Maybe
> now you can see why filtering is a good strategy?
>
> > I believe one of the major reasons for constant disagreement and
> inaction is
> > the tendency for people to disregard others concerns instead of
> addressing
> > and resolving those differences. If you just filter out religious
> statements
> > then you are not engaging with a large section of the group.
>
> But that's my choice.  I'm not denying any of them their right to free
> speech, I'm denying them the right to freely speak *to me*.  I don't
> want to hear it as it is annoying, time-wasting, and of no value to
> me.  When enough other people express the same opinion, instinctive
> social behaviors will kick in and they'll simply stop being annoying
> like that (again, this is covered more completely in my book and
> Altemeyer's).
>
> > You can't make
> > a group decision by ignoring the concerns of others in the group. You can
> > use the religious filter to realize and express the reasons for your
> current
> > opinion but it does not serve the group.
>
> This sounds a little like that "consensus" thing again.  "Serving the
> group" does not require serving every member of that group.  Some
> people are just going to have to go along with what the majority (or
> supermajority) prefer.  In a lot of cases, at least if we do things
> right, it'll be the authoritarians who end up with the short end of
> the stick, and this is as it should be because it has been clearly
> shown that when they get the big end they turn around and beat people
> with it.
>
> > Obvious nonsensical and destructive
> > statements should probably be filtered out but it will take time to
> > determine those standards.
>
> Right, and in my proposal there is an option even above flagging, and
> that's moderation (which should be very rare, but is a necessary
> component for dealing with the dangerously antisocial).
>
> >> "editing other people's stated opinions just feels 'icky'."
> >
> > I will work on making this more clear and answering it here is helping me
> > work through the text.  SettleIt.org is not a place for personal
> opinions.
> > Entering a statement is stating that this is a reason to believe or not
> > believe in another statement. It is assume to be reasonably shared by
> > multiple people and could be expressed in several ways with some ways
> being
> > clearer than others. All statements are an attempt to be a fact but the
> > extent to which they are true can always be debated.
>
> It is certainly more elegant to use the same system for this part of
> the evaluation, but again I'd be concerned about the length issue.
> Seems to me it's just more efficient to flag something as "unsupported
> opinion" and take a few percent off the weight for every person who
> flags it that way.
