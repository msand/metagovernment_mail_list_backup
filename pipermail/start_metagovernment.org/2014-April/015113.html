> > Scott, we seem to fundamentally differ here. I've explained my
> rationale, but I don't see yours. So I'll restate mine:
>
> Efficiency is my primary concern: You can't expect people who are not
> interested in an issue to participate in the back-and-forth debate
> that the proposal generation/refining process requires.
>
> > I hold that voting and proposal generation/tuning must go hand-in-hand
> and absolutely should not be separate.
>
> And I totally disagree! ;-)
>
> > A root problem with the status quo is that people are presented with a
> proposal and asked to vote it up or down. In the U.S., we call this a
> referendum, and it is horrible. The crafting of the proposal is where all
> the actual decision-making happens (the devil is in the details), and
> almost everyone is disenfranchised from that. And then when people are
> asked to vote, they don't care, since they have so little influence on the
> important parts of the matter: the details. A million vote for, a million
> vote against: what does my input matter?
> >
>
> Right, and I'm not making the argument that the proposals should be
> handed down from on high, but only that they are developed by the
> people who are interested in them and that we don't try to force all
> of those who are not into this "sausage making" process.  Once they've
> refined the proposal to a high degree, achieving the best possible
> version of it, they can take it to the general population.  Who will
> likely vote no on it (even in the best cases less than half of bills
> pass in legislatures, most commonly because of resource allocation
> issues).
>
> I'm flexible on (and still researching) the proposal refinement
> process, but tend to think that repeated voting is not an efficient
> way to do it.  My current thinking is that people read a proposal then
> post a pro or con statement about it, or maybe ask a question about.
> Those statements themselves, and the answers to the questions, can
> then be rated by people who don't know enough to make their own
> statements.  *This* is where my sorting/filtering process comes in:
> The raters can't be forced to wade through the entire list of
> statements, the useless and offensive ones should either be filtered
> out or sorted down toward the bottom.
>
> After reviewing these comments the proposer could then modify the
> proposal to take this information into account and everyone who
> commented on the previous version will get an invitation to review the
> updated proposal.  If a war breaks out, forking the proposal should
> also be an option.
>
> > Voting *guides* proposal-tuning. It tells you what is getting buy-in and
> what is not consensus-building. While proposals are being crafted, we might
> come to a point where half are for and half are against. You suggest a
> majority-rule forced vote. I say that's a horrible solution. What if
> instead one person said: "Wait a minute, I have a completely different
> idea; hey everyone, see if this solves your issues better." A lot of those
> alternates might fail, but some genius might come up with something that
> gets almost everyone onboard. I say that is infinitely better than forcing
> a majority rule. And it cannot be done unless the voting and
> decision-making are mixed. You know you have an answer when the votes pile
> up on one proposal.
> >
>
> I say that person will have the opportunity to suggest a change a
> proposal, or make their own, and the people who are interested in
> knowledgeable in that area will help them refine it (or unfortunately
> blow it out of the water as being unworkable in some cases).  But
> again, we can't allow this small group who is interested in the
> subject area to make law unsupervised, nor can we expect the general
> population to participate in every timeconsuming refinement process as
> the alternative.
>
> I'm not exactly clear on how to determine that a proposal is ready for
> a general vote, and tend to think that this is an empirical question.
> Whether it's that a certain time has elapsed, the debate has slowed,
> the proposer thinks it's ready, or a sufficient percentage of the
> affected individuals have weighed in an given it a sufficiently
> positive review, or maybe some combination of all of those things, I
> just don't know yet.
