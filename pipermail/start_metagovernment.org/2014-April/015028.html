I propose the opposite conclusion: a well-crafted consensus system corrects for the ignorance of the majority by empowering the minority to provide sane input into decisions that could otherwise be overwhelmed by the ignorance of "the masses."

>> First procedurally, the word "consensus" doesn't contradict with "vote" in many systems, because voting can be iterative. People keep voting until consensus is reached. In that sense, voting is more of a barometer of whether or not a proposal needs more work in order to garner consensus.
> 
> And in the mean time?  No government at all?

No hasty decisions, yes! What good is a decision that sucks for half the people? Why should we rush to the wrong decision? Unless there is an army about to invade or a giant meteor about to strike, do we really need to rush governance? We are no where near the level of invading armies or world-saving governance, so I don't see any need for hasty decision-making here. Especially if it leads to awful results.

We do have a decision rule, which was developed quickly using Votorola as the mechanism to reach consensus on it. It is at:
http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Rules

Recently, I got into a debate with one other person who disputed that decision rule. He hasn't defended his position recently, so it looks like the above stands for now. That decision rule was made about two years ago. See
http://metagovernment.org/pipermail/start_metagovernment.org/2011-May/003898.html
for part of the development.

>> As for the practicality of consensus governance, I don't think it is worth compromising over. Sure, a majority-rule system can get results faster. You are either for or against abortion, death penalty, etc., and there can be no building toward synthesis. And those issues themselves are products of the 2-sided system. Trying to boil the world down into being for or against abortion is silly. The issue is much more nuanced than that, and a majority-rule system kills that nuance.
