On Thu, May 1, 2014 at 12:16 AM, Scott Raney <scott at metacard.com> wrote:

> On Wed, Apr 30, 2014 at 7:12 AM, James Hancock <jlhancock at gmail.com>
> wrote:
> > So, now everyone takes part in the proposal/refinement phase? But then
> they
> > send it to the voters for approval which is also everybody? I thought you
> > said small groups define the proposals which then get sent out for mass
> > voting? Now you are just confusing me. And, you still haven't defined how
> > those small groups get chosen.
>
> Self chosen (volunteer).  This is just the way things naturally work,
> and work in our current systems: People interested in particular
> issues (those pesky "special interests") are the ones who will be
> willing to get their hands dirty generating and refining proposals.
> It is simply not reasonable to expect *everyone* to participate in
> this part of the process because they don't have the time or the
> knowledge or even the interest in doing so.  This is the fatal flaw in
> most of the other systems I see on Metagovernment which seem to assume
> unlimited participation rates.  The notable exception being Blinap
> which solves this problem via sampling (which, although it would work,
> has other shortcomings that I've addressed previously).
>
> > Also,
> > "
> > Well, if they provide that perq to *every* voter, that's not a broken
> > system, that's a benevolent dictator ;-)
> > "
> > Doesn't actually address the issue. It is just redirection. How will you
> > keep those small groups from being corrupted?
>
> It doesn't matter: If they get corrupted, at least to the extent that
> the resulting proposal conflicts with the needs of the many, the
> majority-rule vote at the end will simply reject that proposal.  This
> matter is discussed further in my book, but I think we have to assume
> and adapt to the fact that the better funded/more motivated special
> interests *will* have a much higher generation and approval rate for
> their proposals.  But this is not necessarily a bad thing because it
> is a counterbalance to the "tyranny of the majority" problem.  It's
> mainly a problem in our current representative governments because of
> practices like logrolling (vote swapping) where the corruption bubbles
> up from the committee level to the floor vote level.  That can't
> happen in a direct democracy because organizing it would be akin to
> herding cats.
