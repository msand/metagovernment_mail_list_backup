Yeah, the filing is the easy part. The hard part is paying lawyers to
contact people who try to use the name for something they will claim
is unrelated to your use. Unlike the social-enforcement route, if you
don't aggressively defend your actual trademark the legal system here
will consider it null and void if the time comes to use it when a
completely unambiguous infringement occurs.

> On the other hand, if you do not patent and it can be patented then someone
> else can and then you almost can not use it yourself in your own company.

Actually that's not true either: Patents can be prevented or even
voided after they're issued by "prior art". The trick, though, is to
completely disclose what you're doing (open source I would think would
be sufficient) and with an accurate timestamp so that if someone tries
to come along and patent your idea they can't. I used to do this all
the time when I was in the software business: Whenever I came up with
something potentially patentable I'd post a detailed design document
about it on the MetaCard mailing list, the archives of which would
serve as a prior-art database should someone else come along and try
to use my idea against me or my company (my allergy to software
patents having developed some 30 years ago...)
