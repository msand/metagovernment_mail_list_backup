*Past* voting! This is an example of the well known fact: points distributed randomly in a high-dimentional cube are roughly equidistant. In other words, if people are indifferent to some questions, they will act as noise drowning out the signal. Instead one should measure correlation *after* the delegation. That is one should look at how often the votes are overwritten. I bet this correlation will be close to 1.

Hei Scott,

thank you for your mail, I'll answer myself ;)

I just had a look at the database again, so:

1) There were 499.009 direct votes and 428.952 of them were made by
voters without delegations (i.e. voting weight = 1).

2) Yes, we were surprised that there is such a low correlation between
the past voting decision by delegates and the voting decisions of the
delegee. We discussed this and came to the conclusion that there are
other factors than representativeness which play a role when delegating
a vote, i.e. sympathy and popularity. This also would have implications
for the behaviour in a representative democracy -- I think it would be
interesting to check how people agree with the program of the party they
vote for. I expect a similar correlation.

3) The Pirate Party advocates direct democracy, so many members liked to
vote themselves. Additionally, some were not in favour of the delegation
system. And then there is inactivity: Members typically discovered the
delegation system over time and if a member gets inactive after voting
on a few initiatives, we have no delegation in the records. Activity is
power-law-distributed.

4) That is a very interesting question -- my gut feeling tells me that
this never happened, but I might be wrong! In order to check this, I
would have to process all database dumps, i.e. read a dump, query some
table, read the next dump... If you are really really interested, I can
put that on my todo list for end of November.

5) uff, so many questions :D Yes, if you want to introduce a flexible
quorum, you could e.g. lower the quorum over time based on the average
agreement rate. The delegation system just gives some voters a higher
voting weight so that they can push selected initiatives they like.
Lowering the quorum is like pushing ALL initiatives a bit. The rate of
accepted initiatives would be the same for both solutions, though. But
it would influence, WHICH initiatives are successful -- lowering the
quorum is like taking the top-X initiatives of a time period, the
delegation system lets some delegates pick the initiatives which should
pass.

6) The open voting system indeed is an issue. I would guess that there
were some discussions, but unfortunately I am not aware of any.

7) Yes, we do have the text but we still are working on a data privacy
concept to store the dataset at our secure datacenter -- the text of
proposals is very sensitive, as it often reveals the identity of the author.

Thank you for the offer -- yes, as soon as we got permission from the
Pirate Party to release some (anonymised?) part of the dataset, we will
work on further analyses and then we are happy to share the research
ideas from the very beginning with your mailing list :) If you give me
the name of that mailing list, I can also advertise it in my group, as
we do have multiple political scientists who are interested in the study
of democratic systems.

Cheers
Christoph
