> Tacit approval is a dysfunctional model for democracy AFAICT.

Oooh, don't let Ed hear you talking like that ;-) In fact, "tacit
approval" is by far the largest factor in our current
misrepresentative democracies and I would admit is even a fundamental
characteristic of my proposal. The major difference being the group
and position you're "approving" of when you don't take direct action:
In misrepresentative democracy, and indeed IMHO in LQFB, you're
approving of the SDAPs/Activerts whereas in AutoMatch through inaction
you're only approving of the position of the proxies who have been
psychologically matched to you...

Also agreed: LQFB would be a great improvement over our current
system. But since it's still deeply flawed and if we're changing

> No, because the individuals aren't intelligent enough. Collective
> intelligence can only manifest itself if the right method is employed
> and its apparent injustice is understood as actually being better.
> It means putting ideological prejudices aside and understanding why
> liquid feedback works, for example.

Well, "works" is a fairly large exaggeration of what has actually been
shown. Even in the homogeneous groups tested to date the low turnout
and poor voter/delegate voting correlation tells me that it would have
the same problems as misrepresentative government if anyone actually
tried to use it on the general population (that is, the SDAPs would
end up making all the decisions). It's probably even worse than that:
Because the samples tried so far are all high-openness personality
types, it's quite possible that LQFB would fail to work even for
homogeneous authoritarian groups because the ability to game the
system (via demogoguery, in particular) would be so much more commonly
exploited by people from that kind of population. Please reread
Altemeyer's Global Change Game experiment and try to imagine how
individuals would be able to find and exploit these weaknesses in
LQFB, particularly if you mixed in some double-highs as he did (who do
you think would end up with all the delegations?):
http://matchism.org/refs/Altemeyer_2003_RWAInheritEarth.pdf

Agreed: I certainly plan to design for the eventuality that 10 billion
people could cast direct votes, or that only a few thousand do and the
rest use those people as proxies. Direct sampling would save a lot of
CPU cycles (you might only need to sample a few hundred individuals
to, e.g., make a policy on abortion), but with the downside being that
few humans would trust the results because most people simply don't
grok statistics.
