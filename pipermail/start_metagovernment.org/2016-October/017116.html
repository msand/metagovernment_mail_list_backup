On 10/7/16, Scott Raney <metamerman at gmail.com> wrote:
> On Fri, Oct 7, 2016 at 4:30 PM, Ned Conner <npconner at earthlink.net> wrote:
>> Here are some of my counter-principles to Scott's "Axioms of Government":
>
> It's like shooting fish in a barrel: The only thing more pitiful than
> an elitist is a "consensusist" ;-)
>
>> (D) A rule of action or choice is justified, and thus valid, only if all
>> those affected by the rule or choice could accept it in a reasonable
>> discourse. (This is the Habermas Discourse Principle.)
>
> OK, so who defines "reasonable"? If someone says "Abortion is Murder",
> can I conclude that they're not being reasonable and just ignore their
> input?
>
> But this isn't really the big problem with the consensusist
> philosophy, which is that it assumes that no decision is preferable to
> one that is made over the objections of some minority in the
> population. This is not only just an irrational position to take, but
> it also displays a really profound ignorance of what the human species
> is really like. People are biologically *programmed* to accept that
> things don't always go their way and to defer to majority opinion.
> It's called "socialization", and it actually has a significant effect
> on behavior in most primate species and even many social animals even
> less closely related to humans (wolves and elephants are just the two
> most common examples). To cast this feature of humans aside as being
> "unfair" is not only incompetent social engineering, it's also highly
> authoritarian (as in "Hey, you don't know what's good for you, we're
> going to protect from having some policy you don't like from being
> inflicted on you even when you're absolutely sure that it's really no
> big deal. Hope you like the status quo!").
>
>> By extension, a population-scale public-matter decision system is
>> legitimate
>> to the extent that it produces decisions that are in accord with (D).
>
> Meaning nothing important will ever be decided. Don't we already have
> that with the US congress? In the past year they've only passed a
> total of 24 substantive bills.
>
>> Anyone can propose anything, on equal footing. (Some must not be more
>> equal
>> than others, either with respect to opportunity to propose or with
>> respect
>> to treatment of proposals proposed.)
>
> So I've got to wade through all the proposals made by high-school
> students as part of their homework for their civics class and give
> them the same priority as proposals from the Global Manager (the
> equivalent in my system of something like "president of the world")?
>
>> A proposal succeeds at the point that it reaches the status of facing no
>> unresolved objections.
>> Vote-counts do not decide. ("Might makes right" is not a principle that I
>> buy into. It ignores/overrides (D).)
>
> Exactly. Again, no progress, profound ignorance of human nature.
> Fortunately Ned is in such a small minority here that there's no
> chance his opinion will count for anything ;-)
>
>> Guesses about what will or will not work are just guesses. Show me your
>> test
>> results, and I will show you mine.
>
> So, your theory is that if I design an airplane wing with a computer
> (or indeed with a slide rule) and some knowledge of aerodynamics  I'll
> have no better chance of successfully flying than someone who straps
> boards on their arms and starts flapping? Good thing most scientists
> would consider this to be a ridiculous statement or there'd be no
> progress in any of those fields either...
>   Regards,
>     Scott
