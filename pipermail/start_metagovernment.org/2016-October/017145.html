This is the problem: They'd have to either go through a lot of work to

> b) If (a) is not possible, could you provide some details on the dynamics of
> delegation? Specifically, how often did voters overwrite the votes of their
> delegates? How often did they switch delegates? What was the fraction of the
> sector delegations among total delegations?

These are good questions, and I'll ask them in my reply to him.

> His answer actually explains a lot:
>
>> Yes, we were surprised that there is such a low correlation between
>> the past voting decision by delegates and the voting decisions of the
>> delegee.
>
> *Past* voting! This is an example of the well known fact: points distributed
> randomly in a high-dimentional cube are roughly equidistant. In other words,
> if people are indifferent to some questions, they will act as noise drowning
> out the signal. Instead one should measure correlation *after* the
> delegation. That is one should look at how often the votes are overwritten.
> I bet this correlation will be close to 1.

Again, I'll ask, but I'm guessing you're going to be disappointed:
Past votes should be a reliable indicator of tendencies
(political/moral/technical or whatever other criteria a voter
typically uses) and IMHO the fact that they don't correlate already
tells us everything we need to know. My prediction is that the number
of overrides is going to be so small as to be insignificant, but it
still would be interesting to know whether the participants do
something like remove or change a delegation after it happens.
