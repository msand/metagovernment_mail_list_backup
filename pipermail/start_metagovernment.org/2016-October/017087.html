> I do (hypothetically) moderate already, in that if someone starts
sending outright commercial spam to the list, I will boot them. But if I
take action against someone based on much fuzzier judgements of the
content and motivation of their posts, it is very difficult to determine

Glad your thoughts are heading in the right direction. Yes, it
is normal that these are the next things to worry about once you
realize that no moderation is no democracy either. How can one
individual possibly do the right thing?

Luckily some people have seen this movie several times already
and can give some hints. The generally accepted real-life solution
to this challenge was conceived in the early 18th century and is
generally known as the Separation of Powers. It suggests that in
order to have a *democratically* moderated mailing list we should
have the following:

	- A legislational body that will formulate the rules of
	  code of conduct.
	- An executive body that will try to implement those
	  rules to the best of their intellectual and ethical
	  understanding.
	- A judicial body that will exercise oversight over the
	  executive and defend the rights of individuals should
	  any abuse take place.

So the ingredients needed to do this are two-fold: A method for
this community to agree upon a code of conduct and a method for
this community to elect individuals among itself to fulfil the
executive and judicial roles.

Any attempts to somehow simplify this architecture (like letting
the assembly also be the judges or letting the assembly also do
the moderation) will typically fail painfully for the reasons I
think I have elaborated and underpinned with research docs in the
convivenza document.

So you may find it overkill to think even a mailing list needs a
full blown democratic architecture to become democratic, but
I'm afraid no-one has figured out a decent shortcut.

There is an indecent one, which is however generally very accepted:
It's the shortcut by which a company is entrusted to run the
platforms and is expected to somehow respect democratic needs.
Think Reddit or Facebook. Since we are here to discuss eDemocracy
and know that handing out the power to a company is not a solution,
I don't see any alternative to creating the formal preconditions
of inner justice and democracy, then discuss the tools to go with it.

LQFB scales well, but it may be overkill for a small group like

> One temporary compromise might be to use qualified majorities.

Since suspending voting rights of individuals is a spiny issue
I would rather use a 90% majority than introducing any statutory
procedure by which that one person would have to be excluded.

And since this is pretty much the normal case in most situations
I don't see how qualified majories can not be a permanent
"compromise" rather than temporary.
