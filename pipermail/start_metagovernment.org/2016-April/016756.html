On Fri, Apr 1, 2016 at 5:47 PM, Scott Raney <scott at metacard.com> wrote:

> I see that Ned has put up a bunch of new proposals on Airesis in response
> to my post. You can get to them from the Metagovernmant home page there:
> https://airesis.eu/groups/metagovernment
>
> I'm going to post another message here with my 2 cents on them, but in
> this post I'm going to do a "meta" analysis of why I'm not highly motivated
> to actually use Airesis, or indeed any of the other  systems that people on
> this list seem to be drawn toward.
>
> The problem is that IMHO you all seem to have misjudged the actual need
> here. What you're designing are debating systems, not decision-making
> systems. The problems with our current government systems aren't that the
> proposals are poorly written or that they don't take into account
> everyone's perspectives and needs, it's simply that the wrong ones are
> passed. The ones that get passed reflect the needs and desires of our
> psychologically "specialized" misrepresentatives rather than that of the
> neurotypicals that make up the vast majority of the population.
>
> If this is the case, we don't need to be intimately (I might even say
> "gruelingly", based on my experience with Vilfredo) involved in the
> generation of the proposals, but merely need a way to efficiently weed out
> large numbers of inappropriate ones. Hence my proposal to use
> psychologically matched proxies to do the winnowing, on an
> individual-proposal basis, rather than a "lesser of two evils" blanket
> abdication of responsibility that is characteristic of representative
> government.
>
> Sure, I recognize that the proposal generators need some sort of sand box
> where they can play around with ideas and get feedback, but IMHO pretty
> much any sort of forum can be used for that (email, FaceBook, Google Docs,
> or even something more sophisticated and specialized like Airesis or
> Vilfredo), and so I maintain that designing debating systems is not the
> most efficient use of our time. We need to concentrate on the actual
> decision-making part of the process, not the proposal generation part. The
> biggest problem in our specialized domain is "How do we get *everyone* to
> participate in making the decision, not just those with an ax to grind?".
> Concentrating on the proposal generation part of the process just makes
> that problem much worse because of the time burden it imposes. And IMHO
> making this kind of imposition even results in worse proposals because they
> end up being so watered down as to be ineffective (as I believe is clearly
> shown in the new Metagovernment home page statement that resulted from the
> most recent Vilfredo run). Better to have a few strongly worded and
> effective proposals approved than have a large number of weak
> "consensus-based" proposals pass that don't actually end up solving the
> problems they were meant to address.
>   Regards,
>     Scott
