You can print questions on a piece of paper as well – and asking people to mark more than just one box should not really be a problem if you really care to be part of the decision system. 

However, voting should make use of 21st century technology; there is no reason not to move voting online. We have the technology and knowledge today to make this possible with a risk of fraud 1000 times less than the traditional methods and will be cheaper and faster and can provide you to with various options to eliminate noise or bias from the system.

>The vote value is always a one and we shall rely on better education

Absolutely agree, better education is the real answer, in the long term. But even to get there, as to provide better education along these lines, will require a major change in the socio-political scene, which consequentially requires a vote to change – so we end up are catching our tail. We need an interim solution to get us there and something like this might be good enough to motivate them learn more about issues at hand if they really want to be part of the decision system.

>You receive factual informations prepared by the supporter of each positions

This is prone bias/ misleading, as such any proposal has both pros and cons, and naturally a supporter of that position will see/present things more along the line of pros and less on the cons. If anything these information leaflets should be put together by neutral body – political/economical/social analyst. Even in that case, a lot of people hear/read between lines what they want to hear. 

I think getting them to mark an answer to a question will make them more aware of what they actually vote,. Say  you vote to get a fancy car, and one of the cons is that you will have to starve for a year to get that car, than one of the question should be bringing that issue/risk forward as such – do you agree to starve for a year to get that car?

Or on the line of short vs long term – do you agree to get your car knowing that there is a high that this will forfeit (in the long term) your child chance for better education?

On Fri, Apr 1, 2016 at 6:16 PM, Scott Raney <scott at metacard.com> wrote:

Here are Ned's most recent Airesis proposals, with my 2 cents after each:

 

1)  "We" (initially those of us interested, then The People of Earth) wish/need to "find" the "best" global social system that we can. (The one that we have now is not doing so well.) To do that together, "we" must first come up with and agree on how we will measure "best". So, we need a list of metrics (perhaps prioritized) applicable to global social systems that can be objectivelymeasured. (For a definition of the term "social system" as it is being used here, see http://home.earthlink.net/~npconner/htm/BAD/BAD02.html )

 

Much as I like an engineered solution to all problems, my considered opinion is that we lack the information and technology we need to to make the kinds of determinations this proposal requires. So, instead, I propose an incremental prototype-based approach. Under the tenets of Matchism, there's no problem operating in a low-information environment like this, we just have to trust that we'll recognize a good system when we see one (i.e., the system we end up *will* match our (current) needs if only our assessment system can actually collect *all* of our opinions, rather than just those who traditionally speak up when decisions are being made).

 

2) Particular designs are created, then proposed, then tested and compared, then selected or not. That is the way that designs are "found" intentionally. (The other way that designs are "found" is unintentionally -- the design just happens by accident.) So, to "find" new global social system designs intentionally, we need a decision system that can receive and rigorously, independently, realistically test and compare design proposals. Proposals are invited -- what decision system design shall we use?

 

Majority rules. Sometimes derided as "tyranny of the majority". Rejection of that in favor of collaborative or consensus-based models is actually counter to the tenets of Matchism: If the majority of humans are comfortable with a solution, even if it means imposing some sort of discrimination against some minority or the environment or whatever, then *that*, by definition, is the proper decision. This of course opens up the possibility that we will bring back slavery, deny women the right to participate, etc. if that's what the majority decides. If you believe that this will happen and are opposed to Matchism or a majority-rules base decisionmaking system on that basis, you are actually a chauvinist and an authoritarian: You are attempting to impose your world view on the majority of people who would rather do it differently. Not that there's anything wrong with that, but best evidence to date is that you are in the minority and that your views will therefore be discounted. Ironic, isn't it?

 

3) Basically we have two choices: build a laboratory in which we can realistically test and compare competing global social systems designs harmlessly before inflicting one of them on the world, or not. (Armchair ideologue amateurs will choose to skip the laboratory testing -- amateurs always do. Something to do with a variation of the Dunning–Kruger effect.)

 

As with #1, this IMHO is a recipe for inaction and so not a viable proposal. We simply lack the knowledge and technology to do this. People's natural conservatism will prevent USSR/Red China type disasters from occurring if SDAP-based leadership can be prevented from seizing control (again, if The People, as a whole, are the ones actually making the decisions). If they make a hash of it by our current thinking, it's our current thinking that's wrong.
