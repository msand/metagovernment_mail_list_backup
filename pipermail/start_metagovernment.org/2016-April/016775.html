I've played with a few of these government-sponsored budgeting systems, and always come away discouraged: There's just no way for an ordinary citizen to make any sense of the budgeting process the way it's done now. Not even counting a fundamental misconception in your description above: Unlike all of us, the government doesn't operate on a fixed budget. Not only do they have the power to create money out of thin air (fiat currency), but they (we) have the ability to set the tax rate to provide however much, or however little, The People want.

 

The trick I think is to change the whole way budgeting is done to a "per capita" basis. For example, "How much do you want to pay the police force this year, per person in the community they serve?" This allows the presentation of all sorts of relevant information, like how much communities of similar size pay, per capita, for equivalent service (comparing communities of similar size, socio-economic class, etc.) Same with schools: "How much do you think we should pay *per student* in this community?" "How much, per person, should we spend on national defense?". Then the hard one, the "Standard Income" (the matchism equivalent to the minimum wage, pensions, disability, and most other forms of welfare): "What is the minimum income required to live a comfortable and dignified existence in your community?" The taxes would then be *automatically* adjusted to provide this level of support, and shown in comparison with GDP in that community (studies show that tax rates are economically optimal at about 30% of GDP, as compared with the 40% they are in the US and the 50%+ they are in most European countries). This is totally different from the way it's done now, where setting taxes and spending are done at completely different times by completely different groups with completely different agendas.
