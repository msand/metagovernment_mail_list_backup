> "Does this answer your larger question?" Unfortunately, not at all. What I
> see (and I have seen a lot) is that there a lot of smart people that spend
> a lot of time and energy on working brilliant concepts. At the same time
> there a lot of people anticipating a change to happen in our political
> world.
>
> But progress is painstakingly slow, if there is any at all. It seems to
> me, that we think our concepts are so brilliant that the world is just
> waiting for us to deliver so they can use it, but that is, at least
> according to my experience, not the case.
>
> Just as Per Norbäck has described in his Blog
> http://pernor.wordpress.com/2011/01/18/the-fight-for-new-democratic-forms/things are not so easy. For Per things are so bad that he titles his blog
> "A failure or success?".
>
> James Watt, the inventor of the steam machine and his partner spent more
> than ten years being on the knocker until his invention was finally
> accepted and turned into a success. Finally, his invention kick off on of
> the greatest revolutions in history: the industrial revolution.
>
> If we leave our inventions to themselves without promoting and lobbying I
> am afraid they will go down without ever being known to the world.
>
> That is why I am concerned about how all these concepts fit into big
> picture, because, if they don't, I do not see a chance for them to ever
> come to life.
