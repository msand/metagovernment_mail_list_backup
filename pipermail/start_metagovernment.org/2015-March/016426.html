Partially, although a large part of the design is to minimize the need

> In case of stability I guess there will be not much interest in any
> alternative (including Matchism) and
> in case of instability you scenario is not suitable.
> Is it so ? What do you say ?

Agreed, and in that case I'd say that a well designed syndicalism
would have a much better chance of succeeding, just as it started to
during the Spanish revolution. I still don't see it as a long-term
solution, but it could be a great a short-term patch for the kind of
chaos that comes when an authoritarian regime is overthrown. For
example the Iraqis, the Libyans, and the Egyptians would have been way
better off if a quick-and-dirty syndicalism could have been
implemented when their national governments bit the dust. Much easier
than training people up on a Matchist system, or any other nation-wide
direct democracy. And of course it's much easier to bootstrap because
it doesn't rely on a functioning infrastructure the way an electronic
direct democracy does. The disadvantage of course is that it'll be
hard to encourage people to train up (even minimally) on a system
they'll only need if their national government fails...
