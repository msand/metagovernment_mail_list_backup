> On Nov 20, 2009, at 12:17 PM, Joshua N Pritikin wrote:
> >There is polling where you call a bunch of people on the phone (or
> >mail
> >out surveys) and people respond off the top of their head without
> >thinking about the issue deeply. Then there is deliberative polling
> >where you select a random sample of more than 100 people, bring them
> >together, deliberative on an issue with expert testimony, and come to
> >some kind of conclusion. The two processes have some commonality
> >in name
> >("poll"), but produce vastly different kinds of results.
> 
> What most projects here are considering is polling of absolutely
> everyone who wants to participate,

Wouldn't that be a self-selected sample?

How are you going to claim anything special about what these groups 
produce if they are self-selected?

> and developing consensus out of an ensuing deliberative process, 
> managed by software engineered for the task.

I don't see why it makes a difference how you manage the deliberations. 

If the sample is self-selected then your sample is not representative of 
the target population, and it will be hard to claim that any results are 
the conclusions that most people would arrive at if they had a chance to 
think about it carefully.
