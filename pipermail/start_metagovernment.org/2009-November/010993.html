Ed Pastore wrote:
> When I went to a series of conferences in Washington earlier this year, I 
> saw a lot of people starting to understand a glimmer of the promise of 
> collaborative governance, but most of them didn't have any belief that it 
> could actually be made a reality. If people see a group of people 
> presenting at a major conference all working on different systems toward 
> that generally common end, I think they might begin to see that this is a 
> real possibility.

It's a political aim.  You're looking for solidarity, where people
come together in a common cause.  It's the dream of every politician.
I can't fault you for expecting that others - entrepreneurs, academics
and engineers - ought to share that dream and pull together in
acheiving it.  I can't fault you because I make the same mistake in
regard to my own ambitions.  I expect that politicians like you, and
entrepreneurs (like Jim and Craig) and academics (like Pietro and
Giovanni) ought to lend a hand in developing an open technology.
Still, I'm even more naive, because I see all this and here I am,
writing it down - that the politician dreams of building consensus,
the entrepreneur of building enterprise, the academic of career and
the engineer of machines - yet *still* I cannot see why these are
incompatible.
