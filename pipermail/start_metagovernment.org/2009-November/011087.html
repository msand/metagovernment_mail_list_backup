On Fri, Nov 20, 2009 at 09:19:49PM -0500, Craig Simon wrote:
> Joshua N Pritikin wrote:
> >The opinion of the people who don't care is valuable because these
> >people are potentially best able to determine with least bias what
> >is the best solution for society as a whole.
> 
> You've just hit on a major conundrum of political science:

OK

> reconciling the simultaneous needs for the "best solution" and
> popular legitimacy. Ideally, they're congruent. In practice, until
> the chosen action has unfolded, we can never know whether the
> choices of dispassionate experts (or people presumably made expert
> by their dispassion) will overlap with what the citizenry might have
> expressly demanded if given the opportunity.

Well, I don't really agree with how you are framing the problem. Let's 
start with some definitions:

raw public opinion == What a person thinks about an issue before 
thinking about it deeply.

refined public opinion == What a person would think about an issue 
after thinking about it deeply.

The idea of a deliberative poll is that you take a random sample of the 
population and give them a good space to deliberate on an issue. 
Participants come with a raw public opinion and develop a refined public 
opinion. After the deliberations, the panel of citizens is in a position 
to report on the issue in question with refined public opinion. Since 
the random sample is statistically equivalent to the population, the 
position(s) they adopt _are_ the positions that the population at large 
would arrive at if they had a good space to think about the issues.

(For a longer discussion of the same, see Fishkin 2009)

> My own approach has been to develop a method that can:
> 
> 1) Scale to help as many people participate as possible, recognizing
> that the passionately self-selected ones will of course be among the
> first to show up;
> 
> 2) Channel participants into deliberative subgroups to ensure that
> everyone who has something to say will get vetted by a minimum
> number of peers in exchange for the responsibility of vetting
> others, and;
> 
> 3) Advance the most popular and "best" ideas through a process of
> Coalescent Bubbling (So far I've only been able to build a primitive
> demo piece of the 3rd element, but I haven't given up).

Fine.

I respect your efforts. I am only arguing that you ought to acknowledge 
the chief shortcoming in your approach---that random sampling is not 
employed. Furthermore, I hope you will explicitly support other efforts 
towards deliberative democracy which incorporate a random sampling 
approach.
