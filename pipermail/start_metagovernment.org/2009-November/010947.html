Re: [Start] Under what form do you think that this kind of consensus building technique, can be applied for a whole nation governance with maximum efficiency?W/re legitimacy, perhaps this short paper referencing French and Raven's work may be of interest:  http://ambur.net/French&Raven.htm

Jean Lipman-Blumen's conceptualization of "control myths" is also relevant: http://ambur.net/toxicleaders.pdf

With respect to morality, the thesis of my ongoing series of papers at http://ambur.net/index.html#recordkeeping is that there is no substitute for good and complete records that are readily accessible to stakeholders.

One of the things that was disappointing as we converted the strategic plans of .gov agencies to StratML format was how few of them explicitly value honesty.  See http://xml.gov/stratml/index.htm#Carmel and particularly the frequency listing of values in the pane on the right side of the screen at http://www.xmldatasets.net/data/fedgovt.xml

With respect to the role of eGov, consider the implcations of the Theory of the Firm in the context of political institutions and practices:  http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_the_firm  

Information technology holds the potential to revolutionize "governance":  http://ambur.net/Cult&Tech.htm  

Hopefully, AIIM's emerging StatML standard will make a contribution toward that end, by enabling those who have common objectives to more easily identify each other and work directly together in pursuit of those objectives.

  Hi Michael/Ed/Craig,

  [a] Transition to e-democracy

  I still think that some element of transition might end up being highly desirable and quite possibly inevitable.  I suppose that the key question is: what legitimacy would current political representatives (and structures) have when functioning alongside healthy, viable e-democracy?  The only hypothetical source of legitimacy that I can think of is that they might be supposed to have some greater level of knowledge or suitability through continuity of office.  But I do not think that this would end up corresponding to reality.  I would have thought that their role would be quickly superseded, in itself, although many current political figures would presumably remain in the public eye and gather large numbers of default delegated votes.  On a related question, there's the issue of the extent to which existing institutions could compete with any fully-functioning system of e-democracy.  E-democracy would have such strengths - flexibility, participation, ability to tie in to other institions, etc etc - that there's the possibility that the e-democracy/conventional democracy relationship could look a little like that between computers and abacuses, for example.  

  Anyway, that's the way I see things.  However, I accept that having e-democracy only as an auxiliary mechanism for decision-making would add much.  Similarly, having it working in combination (as an additional 'chamber' for Congress or 'house' of Parliament) with the existing systems would be good.  I don't think there's anything particularly wrong in thinking about the different options, though at the same time I do very much agree with Ed that what matters most  is the gradual progress in demonstrating the ability to provide benefits for communities, starting from the bottom up.

  Michael, I'm not sure I have totally understood your description for how e-democracy would work in combination with current institutions.  I think that you are saying that e-democracy would 'stand behind' elected representatives in the way that political parties do, so that they will be aware of the voting preferences of their constituents before voting.  Thus they will have pressure to vote along the lines that their constituents would like, in the same way that elected representatives currently have pressure from their political party to stick to the party line.  That's pretty different to my version above (the different systems act essentially independently, and either the output of one is fed to the other, or the outputs of both are combined somehow).  I think that my version has advantages in terms of enabling flexibility and allowing, essentially, a different set of representatives (with correspondingly more focussed expertise) to take on each issue; indeed, one can even imagine one person being delegated all votes on an issue.  But I can see it's not an open and shut case.

  In terms of respect for existing institutions, I think this is more from an awareness that change brings real possibilities for harm as well as improvement.  As for constitutional change, I think that one should not be too pessimistic about possibilities for this.  If one imagines a situation where e-democracy had proven its ability to function well on a nation-wide scale (on a steadily growing 'shadow' basis), and was functioning well as a gatherer of opinion on that scale, then I think it's possible to see that attempts by political parties/political elites to hold on to power by maintaining existing political structures could generate significant tensions.  The most obvious parallel in terms of political elites trying to resist greater political participation is possibly 1989: a situation involving resistance by those with political power to the wider dissemination of that power, as here.  Of course, the analogy should only be taken so far.

  One final point - my work on ethics in the economy has convinced me that e-democracy is the only way that ethics can be introduced to economic decision-making on a widespread and meaningful basis, in the way that is needed to address urgent environmental and social problems.  Thus there are big incentives to achieving comprehensive, inclusive success for e-democracy, in the gradual, benefits-based way described.


  [b]  Moral platform

  Much shorter comments, you'll be glad to hear:

    a.. Opening up the moral platform to a much wider set of themes is a great idea. 
    b.. I'm a little concerned about the use of the word 'utopia', because it's so often used in a pejorative sense because there's insufficient regard for achievability.  Maybe, though, that would only require a relatively little re-branding, and maybe the usage of such a well-known term would be good.  As long as the website was clearly for the mapping of current positions and the realisation of achievable goals (giving that a totally open definition) then I think would certainly be on board. 
    c.. I think your final point here about fitting in with existing practice ties in here: it might be helpful to make the site more about plotting a course from where we are than thinking abstractly about inventing a new world (or possibly structuring the site so that that type of input doesn't dominate (though I'm not sure if one can do this in any way objectively to avoid top-down influence)).  Linking the site to the legal one will draw in users.

Thanks for the ideas for the project to develop that, but I don't imagine that my skills (or probably resources) are likely to be sufficient.  I imagine that social science-type contributions are all I'm likely to be good for.
