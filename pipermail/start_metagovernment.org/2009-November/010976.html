>
>  (P)  (Q)  (R)
>    \   |   /

>      \ | /     v
>       \|/
>       (A)
>
> In this picture, we're editing a bunch of proposals (P, Q, R).  We're
> creating changesets in the process.  These changesets either flow
> downward into the act (A), or they do not.  Is the above an accurate
> picture?
Yes but remember a proposal can affect more then one article.

> Suppose A is "America's Healthy Future Act", which is currently before
> the U.S. congress.  Suppose it's not being decided by the senate, but
> by the public via Candiwi.  How many proposals (P,Q,..*) would you
> reasonably expect?
This depends highly on the community so a can only guess ...
I think not many because the voting will cause proposals to be
rejected if their is no consensus. Overtime they should naturally
converge into one that everyone agrees on and accepts.

> What is the maximum number of proposals (on average) that a new
> participant could handle, and still make a useful contribution to the
> process?
I think people will specialise in their area of interest and rely on
"experts" in others. Adding tagging of proposals and articles as well as
rating of comments should improve this.

> Another thing: Your home page says "Candiwi is like a wiki, but you
> can't edit the articles directly."  So A cannot be edited.  In fact,
> there's nothing we can do with A, in itself.  So it really isn't a
> part of the decision process.  It might as well be a statute on the
> books, right?  It needn't actually be stored in Candiwi.
Yes the articles in the main repository represent what the community
has achieved consensus on. This can be thought of as statute and since
we are building candiwi on a distributed version control system their is
nothing stopping you from cloning the main repository.

> If that's true, the only problem that remains is how to decide among
> these proposals, each of which is an alternative *replacement* for A:
>
>  (P)  (Q)  (R)  ...  etc.
As I stated above I think conflicting proposals will converge or be rejected.
The voting process can be thought of as a kind of natural selection that
favours consensus. We are thinking about a way to enable a choice between
several proposals but I have some concerns about it.
*  It will keep people on their side of the fence because they will
form opposing
   groups instead of collaborating.
*  I think editing and getting things done is more important then
voting or endless
   discussions because voting is meaningless if you cannot create that which is
   being voted on.
Also when I look at the Proposals that have been rejected so far most of them
get accepted later on after minor edits. I think this is because people will not
invest much time in work that they know conflicts with what most people want.
I see the voting process more as a way of preventing submission of premature
content.

> Why must these proposals be stored in Candiwi?  Why not in various
> other wikis, scattered over the Web?
As soon as we add importing and exporting the content can be distributed
freely on the web and a candiwi instance is nothing more or less then a place
where a community is collaborating and trying to achieved consensus.
