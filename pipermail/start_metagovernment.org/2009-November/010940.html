Hi Michael/Ed/Craig,

[a] Transition to e-democracy

I still think that some element of transition might end up being highly
desirable and quite possibly inevitable.  I suppose that the key question
is: what legitimacy would current political representatives (and structures)
have when functioning alongside healthy, viable e-democracy?  The only
hypothetical source of legitimacy that I can think of is that they might be
supposed to have some greater level of knowledge or suitability through
continuity of office.  But I do not think that this would end up
corresponding to reality.  I would have thought that their role would be
quickly superseded, in itself, although many current political figures would
presumably remain in the public eye and gather large numbers of default
delegated votes.  On a related question, there¹s the issue of the extent to
which existing institutions could compete with any fully-functioning system
of e-democracy.  E-democracy would have such strengths - flexibility,
participation, ability to tie in to other institions, etc etc ­ that there¹s
the possibility that the e-democracy/conventional democracy relationship
could look a little like that between computers and abacuses, for example.

Anyway, that¹s the way I see things.  However, I accept that having
e-democracy only as an auxiliary mechanism for decision-making would add
much.  Similarly, having it working in combination (as an additional
Œchamber¹ for Congress or ¹house¹ of Parliament) with the existing systems
would be good.  I don¹t think there¹s anything particularly wrong in
thinking about the different options, though at the same time I do very much
agree with Ed that what matters most  is the gradual progress in
demonstrating the ability to provide benefits for communities, starting from
the bottom up.

Michael, I¹m not sure I have totally understood your description for how
e-democracy would work in combination with current institutions.  I think
that you are saying that e-democracy would Œstand behind¹ elected
representatives in the way that political parties do, so that they will be
aware of the voting preferences of their constituents before voting.  Thus
they will have pressure to vote along the lines that their constituents
would like, in the same way that elected representatives currently have
pressure from their political party to stick to the party line.  That¹s
pretty different to my version above (the different systems act essentially
independently, and either the output of one is fed to the other, or the
outputs of both are combined somehow).  I think that my version has
advantages in terms of enabling flexibility and allowing, essentially, a
different set of representatives (with correspondingly more focussed
expertise) to take on each issue; indeed, one can even imagine one person
being delegated all votes on an issue.  But I can see it¹s not an open and
shut case.

In terms of respect for existing institutions, I think this is more from an
awareness that change brings real possibilities for harm as well as
improvement.  As for constitutional change, I think that one should not be
too pessimistic about possibilities for this.  If one imagines a situation
where e-democracy had proven its ability to function well on a nation-wide
scale (on a steadily growing Œshadow¹ basis), and was functioning well as a
gatherer of opinion on that scale, then I think it¹s possible to see that
attempts by political parties/political elites to hold on to power by
maintaining existing political structures could generate significant
tensions.  The most obvious parallel in terms of political elites trying to
resist greater political participation is possibly 1989: a situation
involving resistance by those with political power to the wider
dissemination of that power, as here.  Of course, the analogy should only be
taken so far.

One final point ­ my work on ethics in the economy has convinced me that
e-democracy is the only way that ethics can be introduced to economic
decision-making on a widespread and meaningful basis, in the way that is
needed to address urgent environmental and social problems.  Thus there are
big incentives to achieving comprehensive, inclusive success for
e-democracy, in the gradual, benefits-based way described.


[b]  Moral platform

Much shorter comments, you¹ll be glad to hear:
* Opening up the moral platform to a much wider set of themes is a great
idea.
* I¹m a little concerned about the use of the word Œutopia¹, because it¹s so
often used in a pejorative sense because there¹s insufficient regard for
achievability.  Maybe, though, that would only require a relatively little
re-branding, and maybe the usage of such a well-known term would be good.
As long as the website was clearly for the mapping of current positions and
the realisation of achievable goals (giving that a totally open definition)
then I think would certainly be on board.
* I think your final point here about fitting in with existing practice ties
in here: it might be helpful to make the site more about plotting a course
from where we are than thinking abstractly about inventing a new world (or
possibly structuring the site so that that type of input doesn¹t dominate
(though I¹m not sure if one can do this in any way objectively to avoid
top-down influence)).  Linking the site to the legal one will draw in users.

> I agree with that.  I admire your respect for existing institutions.
> But constitutional change is historically unlikely.  What about this
> combination?
> 
> 1. Existing institutions (as they are)
> 2. Open participative system (something new)
> 
> Both 1 and 2 are mutually independent and non-interfering.
> Therefore no constitutional change is required to add 2.  Compare it
> with the party system, which also arose extra-constitutionally.  It
> dramatically changed the function of government without touching the
> structure.
> 
> Consider also that the parties are nothing but primary electoral
> systems.  So we make 2 a *better* primary electoral system, and we
> enter by the same back door the parties did, 150 years ago.
> 
> It's more than that.  There are technical and social-theoretic reasons
> why the combination (1 and 2) is stronger than 1 or 2 alone.  It's as
> though society needed two legs to stand on.

ED ADDED

> This is similar what I envision for the most part, and why I advocate
> starting governance systems in very small communities and then scaling
> upward. If, for example, a new nonprofit organization is in the
> process of starting up, they could choose to use one of the Active
> Projects as their sole governance mechanism. If they are successful,
> then members of that group might start using the same system to
> replace individual committees in other organizations they work with.
> From there, maybe eventually someone will think to use it to run a
> small branch of their township government. And upward/outward from
> there.
> 
> So I see it not exactly as a shadow as much as a creeping replacement,
> bit-by-bit and only as it proves itself worthy in smaller cases.
