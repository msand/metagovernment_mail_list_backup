Welcome back Jacki, you haven't posted in a while.  (I'm about to
retire from email myself, and get back to coding.)

My definitions of "e-dem" and "e-gov" are probably too extreme for
most.  I define e-dem as technology in support of everyday people, and
e-gov as technology in support of administrative systems.  I use these
extreme definitions because I learned from social theory that people
need to be shielded (as such) from the effects of systems, and
strengthened (as such) from within.  So I take integrity as the whole
challenge of e-dem.  If it enables people to collectively stand up in
public without being knocked down, and to speak for themselves without
being shouted down, then I say they *are* already a democracy.
Nothing more is needed.  There is no central role for government and
politics.

But this is only a stance.  It's more a way of dealing with reality
than a completely true statement of it.  It also makes it hard to
communicate with others who don't share the same stance.  Nothing I
say makes much sense to them.  (Some people are more open minded
though, and can see both sides. ;^)
