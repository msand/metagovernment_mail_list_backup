I think what some of us are after, is an
"internet enabled, collaborative democracy"
and you can substitute enabled by enhanced,
and democracy with government.

Maybe it could be shortened to:
collaborative e-democracy
or
collaborative e-government.
And the word collaborative is meaningful because we need to stress
that we are not just after a democracy, like we already have. Just
done through the internet. But we are after something new.

And then we could express that we do not want representatives, thus we
are looking for:
non representative, collaborative, e-government
or
non representative, internet enabled, collaborative democracy.

Which is quite a mouth full. But just explaining what you mean will
probably take up all your time.

On 12/11/2009, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:
> I'm trying to use the common definitions of the words (since common
> definitions are useful to common understanding), and as I see it:
>
> E-government is about making government data and processes more
> accessible to citizens. And to a lesser extent, allowing for more two-
> way communication in government bureaucracy decision-making.
> http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-Government
>
> E-democracy is about using internet technologies in various democratic
> processes.
> http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-democracy
>
> While our efforts can fit under the umbrella of the term "e-
> democracy," I think it is an unsuitable word simply because it means
> so many other things as well. A politician sending e-mails to get
> people to vote for her counts as e-democracy, and certainly has very
> little to do with our aims.
>
> In any event, I don't see any conflict in a group presentation to the
> Gov 2.0 Expo. It just seems like a way to get our idea out to a much
> broader audience.
>
> When I went to a series of conferences in Washington earlier this
> year, I saw a lot of people starting to understand a glimmer of the
> promise of collaborative governance, but most of them didn't have any
> belief that it could actually be made a reality. If people see a group
> of people presenting at a major conference all working on different
> systems toward that generally common end, I think they might begin to
> see that this is a real possibility.
>
>
