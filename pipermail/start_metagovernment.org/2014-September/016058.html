On Tue, Sep 30, 2014 at 4:47 PM, Gustavo Menezes <menezesgc at gmail.com>
wrote:

> >The problems come when *public* decisions are made in a
> >hierarchical structure because those at the bottom end up having too
> >little control over the process.
>
> You would be right in most cases, but I it does not apply in a truly
> sociocratic govrenance system. And I tell you this from personal
> experience. Sociocratic organizations are not imposed; they are founded
> upon the principle that no one objects it. Therefore, those at the top
> agree to the fact that everyone's opinion, at any level, should be taken
> into account at any moment. If that is not the case for a certain
> °sociocratic" organization, then I would say they are not following a
> "truly" sociocratic model.
>
> >Again, I think you're inventing a new form of government here.  Which
> >is fine, but you can't use the term sociocracy to identify it because
> >that term is already being used to describe a different kind of
> >system.
>
> I am really not. It seems to me you are not aware of how sociocratic
> governance systems really work. It is also possible that my experience is a
> different one than for others. One way or another, it only adds to the
> point that maybe it would be better to discuss it more thoroughly.
>
> > I don’t imagine that a sociocratic system is the only way to accomplish
> the
> > complex task of separating the functional from the structural aspects of
> > hierarchy. But hierarchies may serve a purpose, as leaders emerge
> everywhere
> > without formal hierarchies anyway, as long as there are built-in
> mechanisms
> > for subverting the possibility of a small number of people having
> power-over
> > everyone else.
>
> >Such as direct democracy ;-)
>
> I disagree. Direct democracy does not really tackle most problems of
> governance structure (problems related to the activities of Executive
> offices of current political systems of developed countries, for instance),
> as much as it tackles the problems related to decision-making structures
> related to laws (problems related to the activities of Legislative offices
> of current political systems of developed countries, for instance). You
> probably would not want a national referendum approach to decide how to fix
> the toilet in a government building! And thats fine! Direct democracy does
> not fix all the problems we have in governance systems, neither does
> sociocracy. But sociocracy does tackle more of the structural problems than
> direct democracy, as far as I know (or think I know).
>
> >How many is too many? 2?  Maybe 3?  By the time you get enough
> >leverage that sociocracy has an organizational advantage over direct
> >democracy, you've institutionalized the problem of authoritarians
> >rising to power (just like our current systems do).
>
> You may have a point, but it is up for discussion. I think you may be
> right that in most cases sociocracy has a decision-making disadvantage over
> direct democracy. However, the sociocratic organizational structure is
> WAAAAAAAAY better than most other governance systems, while direct
> democracy does not really deal with the governance structure problems at
> all (this should really be discussed in more depth...).
>
> I have argued before that I do not believe there is a single best
> governing or decision-making model which fits for every circumstance.
> Organizing a community with 20 people is much different than organizing a
> country with millions; or billions! I believe the model that works for the
> first will not work for the second. Metagovernment is somewhat interested
> in new governance systems for more complex communities, as far as the main
> page statement goes. Therefore two really difficult problems are
> intertwined, which are the structure of governance and the decision-making
> process of governance. I think they should be discussed separately to come
> to fruitful conclusions.
>
> As far as governance structures goes, I would say hierarchic structures
> with more than five levels would be too many... It is not written in stone,
> is just a personal feeling (based on limited experience). A general rule of
> mine would be that the less levels, the better. Therefore, no hierarchy is
> better, insofar as the community is somewhat small enough so that everyone
> can be heard in a practical way. But most organizations have a lot of
> people, which makes it very unpractical to hear everyone in a way that they
> will feel heard. Therefore in some cases hierarchical governance systems
> may prove to be a better solution. Heck, the people should be able to
> choose it or not, and I know that in some cases people will willingly let
> someone else decide for them in good will. That is the point of being
> politically free!
>
> However, as good as hierarchical systems could become, I would argue that
> most organizations (of any kind) with more than 4 hierarchy levels will
> have problems, regardless of size, which are usually both structural
> problems of management and decision making problems per se. I believe this
> is why some people are so attracted to the enlightened dictator fantasy,
> because it is so simple; management (dictatorship) is very straightforward,
> and decision-making (centralized in the enlightened) would be exerted in a
> supposedly "good" way for everyone. Of course this is not true, and the
> problem is both in structure and decision-making. This is also why direct
> democracy is so appealing to many other people; like in enlightened
> dictatorships decision-making is exerted in a supposedly "good" way for
> everyone (albeit through direct voting by everyone in the community).
> However, direct democracy systems usually does not really deal with
> governance structure. And since direct democracy does not really deal with
> governance structure issues, this is also why direct democracy can easily
> co-exist with hierarchical structures.
>
> Another important aspect mentioned earlier is that the decision-making
> problems of Executive offices of political systems is very different from
> the problems of Legislative offices to enact, amend and repeal laws. Direct
> democracy usually focuses on the Legislative issues related to laws (and
> rightfully so!) and, therefore, are more or less unrelated to the
> structural problems of hierarchy inside governance systems (which is more
> of a problem in the Executive branch).
>
> In conclusion, I think the discussion on sociocracy may help with the
> issues related to structure more so than direct democracy.
>
> Besides, I would think that discussing sociocracy is not out of the realm
> of Metagovernment goals anyway ("...We explore, challenge, and collaborate
> with each other in the development of various ideas and technologies for
> these new governance systems....").
>
