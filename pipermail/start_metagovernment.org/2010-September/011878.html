I guess nothing could stop them.  As long as all namegivers agree to
do something with the domain name, and no *other* laws/contracts are
broken along the way, then they can do as they please.  I'm not a
lawyer, but I think *any* contract can *always* be amended or
deprecated by mutual assent of the concerned parties.  (Otherwise, it

If what I said above is correct, then the amendment clause goes
without saying.  It's redundant.  Should we remove it?

I think I finally understand this thing (it makes my head spin): The
public (the voters) are not party to the contract.  They should not
place trust in the contract itself (which is always amendable) but in
the namegivers.  All it takes is a single namegiver to side with them,
and they can use his veto to prevent back-pedalling on the donation.
They can prevent the name (and the time, effort and money they
contributed to it) from being stolen for other purposes.

They can only do that at the cost of destroying the name.  But (you
were right all along) the namegivers can together ressurect the domain
name.  Nothing can prevent them from doing so.

Strange conclusion: it doesn't really matter *what* the contract says.
It's only a statement of intent and some pre-canned procedures for
resolving differences along the way.  All that really matters is *who*
the namegivers are.

It's looking good, I think.  All we have to do is polish up the
phrasing, then we can prepare a copy for signing.
