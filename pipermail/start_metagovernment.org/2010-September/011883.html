Ed Pastore wrote:
> Imagine a vote on, say, the legalization of marijuana. Someone may 
> wish to vote for it, but realize that a future employer may see them 
> voting for it and make assumptions about them as a result. So they 
> vote against it even though they want it to pass ...

I agree with you, Ed. I made my peace with public voting though, because 
we can allow each voter to have his own unique position. So the voter in 
your example must not choose between yes or no, instead he can have the 
position "I want further scientific research into the potential benefits 
of marijuana." This is neither yes nor no, more something in between. 
This way in such delicate questions a whole society can move slowly and 
gently over time towards its consensus. Nobody needs to be radical in 
opposition to the rest of society but step by step the whole society can 
reach a position, which seemed radical before.
