On Mon, Aug 9, 2010 at 10:16 PM, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:
> On Aug 9, 2010, at 3:58 PM, Richard Franks wrote:
>
>> Are there any designs for an anonymous voting system which could not
>> be corrupted?
>
> Nice to hear from you again, Richard!

I'm surprised you remember me, but thanks :-)


> The first is like the SlashDot approach: simply allowing completely
> anonymous input, but giving it significantly less weight. This demotes those
> users from the level of trusted participant, but allows good anonymous
> contributions to be elevated to as much prominence as the community thinks
> they deserve. Of course, this applies better to discussion than to voting.

Could it apply to voting, at all?


> The second is the trust metric. There are numerous ways trust can be
> calculated, but it all comes out to the same basic principle... allow users
> to create non-identifying personas, and then have those personas prove
> themselves within the community. Any persona which has gained the trust of
> others in the community gets proportionally greater power within the
> community. Yes, one person could develop several trusted personas, but
> that's not necessarily corruption: each of those personas has proven itself
> within the community (and can be downgraded by the community at any time).
> Someone trying to set up a botnet of numerous, inter-trusting personas would
> not be able to prove themselves outside of their own sphere, and thus all of
> their input would collapse to that of one participant.

I would attack it like this -- I'd analyse existing groups of a
similar size to the one I wished to spoof, work out a plausible ratio
of internal and external validations and instruct my group of
co-conspirators to have their alternate personas follow that pattern.

I guess it's not all-or-nothing though.  You could just as easily
integrate that solution into one where some individuals opt-out of
anonymity and provide a real-life validation point for those who trust
them and wish to remain anonymous themselves.


> The third is my system of distributed administration. In terms of anonymous
> voting, it would mean that users would have to authenticate to the back-end
> system, but then could present an anonymous persona to the front-end. This
> seems open to administrator corruption, which is where distributed
> administration comes in. Any software would have any number instances, each
> of which would have independent administrators. Each instance would checksum
> all the others, such that any deviation from the neutral standard (the
> community of administrators) would be removed from the administration trunk.

Couldn't a rogue or otherwise compromised administrator reveal
everyones identity at any point?
