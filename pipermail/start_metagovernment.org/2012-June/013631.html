On Jun 10, 2012, at 7:18 AM, Michael Allan wrote:

>> There is no shortage of jurisdictions to provide for a diversity of
>> tabulation systems. As far as the many alternative ways of
>> structuring voting, whether in elections proper or in some
>> qualifying preceding process, while i agree that these should be
>> explored and experimented with, it seems unnecessarily confusing and
>> costly to have multiple such systems in use for each decision,
>> despite the choice and supposed security advantages. In any case, at
>> some point some sort of common standard would have to be developed,
>> and they would have to be certified by government or some trusted
>> entity, both in order for the votes to count, to avoid multiple
>> voting, and for the systems to mirror each other, no?
> 
> Why the stamp of authority?  Suppose all we have is freedom of choice.
> Each mirror sets its own standards for choosing which sources to image
> from and which of their votes to image.  Each individual chooses which
> mirror (or combination of mirrors) to trust.  Elected officials make
> their own choices concerning which results to trust.  Those who make
> the wrong choices and make unpopular decisions as a result will see an
> immediate decline in their prospects for re-election as expressed by
> the voters.
> 
> In this way, all of us together have the means and incentive to keep
> the results accurate and intelligible.  But if we instead delegate the
> trustworthiness of the system to an authority *apart from us*, then
> that authority would have the means and incentive to corrupt the
> system.

I think this part of the exchange, above, gets at the core of Michael's argument, and expresses is very well. If we want something that the people will trust, why not let the people decide what it is that they trust?

But Michael, I have to ask if the argument ends there. If people are given the freedom to choose their form of participation, there still needs to be a meta-entity to aggregate that participation: the cross-forum (or does it have a new name now?). Does that system not suffer from some of the same weaknesses of centralization? Sorry if you have already explained how you intend to deal with that, but even if so, it seems important to re-state it here.
