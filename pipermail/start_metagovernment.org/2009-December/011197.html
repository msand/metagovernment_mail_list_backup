The Pareto Front and the Pareto Frontier are the same thing. Some
people call it in one way, some in the other.
So if you know what a Pareto Frontier is, you are set. Except you
still need to understand how to apply this to voting.
Essentially we are considering each citizen as a different dimension.
We define a solution A as dominating a solution B if everybody likes A
as well as B. And at least one person prefers A to B.

If people are only allowed to express their preferences in a 0/1 way
(yes/no). Also called Approval voting. Then the domination solution is
even easier.
A solution A dominates a solution B, if the set of the people that
like B also like A, and at least one person likes A but not B.

In other words the set of people who like B is a subset of the set of
people that like A,
In other words the set of people who like A is a supraset of the set
of people that like B.
In other words, one is contained in the other, strictly. The container
one is dominating the contained one.

Once we have found what solutions dominate what others, we eliminate
all the solutions that are dominated by something. What remains is the
Pareto Front(ier)

and minute 25.

Although you can apply the Pareto Front to a system where you rank all
the solutions. The Condorcet result and the pareto front would not be

But it would be interesting to present them side by side. But, as I
