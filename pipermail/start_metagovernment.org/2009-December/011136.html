As I was reading the papers a few things came to me.
The first is that we really should do our best to answer their questions,
and the questions are

   - What will attendees learn?
   - What curtains have been lifted?
   - How can attendees use this?

Also in presenting the projects we need not so much to speak about them, but
show how each project is really bringing something new. And what is new. Why
is it needed. And what have we learned in this. How can other follow our
footsteps. We  are not going there to sell metagovernment.

Let me answer those questions according to the Vilfredo Project.

The aim of the Vilfredo Project was not to make a coll website, but to give
an example of a different form of decision making;
traditional decision making in democratic societies is rooted in Voting
theory (add 5 minutes of crash course on voting theory).
All of voting theory is based on the idea of answering a closed question by
counting how many people support the various options.
And then there are various ways of voting and of counting.
Vilfredo rejects the assumtpion that we already know what are the options
and instead proposes a search algorithm that uses human beings to look for
the possible alternatives.
The two things we are doing that are new are:
a) using a -human- genetic algorithm to find the possible alternatives
(instead of simply having the people in charge decide them)
b) Comparing the sets of people voting for the various solutions, and taking
the pareto front of the solutions thus generating a set that represents all
the users
(instead of simply summing up the votes)

We can do (b) because we don't have a fixed number of solutions that must
pass from one generation to the other.

The results that we found where:
 a)   people agree more on important things: we tend to agree on values
while we tend to disagree on the  weight we give to the values
 b)   there ARE solutions out there; most question reach an answer fairly
soon (in less than 20 generations, often 5 to 7)
 c) the solutions are usually more complex

problems we did find was that people get bored quickly

while problems we did not find: most people would have predicted that
ignorance of the participants was going to be a problem, but in fact we
never found that to be a big problem. AT the worse some solutions had to
spell out why they were presented, and why other solutions were not really
viable. But the fact is that the solutions from the experts will never be
destroyed by the solution from the non expert because the expert will not
vote for them, and then both will make it to the pareto front.


This is in essence what I should say for Vilfredo.

And in all this the URL of the website or the examples are not even needed.
This is what I mean when I say that we should not go there to sell our
project.

Now, can we have those questions:
-what is that they are doing that is new
-how was it done before
-lessons learned
-problems that they have found
-expected problems that they did not find.
answered for the other projects as well?
