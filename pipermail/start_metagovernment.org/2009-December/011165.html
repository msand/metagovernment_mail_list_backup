I think Jacki is spot on.
The real thing that needs to be done is improve communication in
candiwi so questionable proposals are spotted fast, as opposed to
complicating the way to contribute.
Also 'watching' pages would could tie in very well with tagging and such.

As for the 'stalemate' problem, I fully expect the participation rate
not to be very high and seldom higher then ~50% for voting (if we can
get a part of the lurkers to at least vote ...) and ~10% for
article/proposal contributes.
http://www.wikipatterns.com/display/wikipatterns/90-9-1+Theory
This is why our default voting settings are 5% participation so things
can get in easily but 90% approval so questionable edits can be
blocked quickly.
But a community can easily kill itself if they do not trust each other
enough and make contributing hard by high participation requirements
or different rule sets for different articles.

PS: I wouldn't recommend a participation setting > 100% candiwi will allow it :)

> Perhaps it would be sufficient to allow people to 'watch' pages that
> are particularly important to them, so that they are notified if any
> change is proposed (and thus important pages will have higher rates of

> I am not sure if there is a good way to ask this as an open question,
> something like : 'How to balance rates of participation in Candiwi
> with the desire for rate of growth / rate of change?'
>
> or, 'How to avoid potential stalemate scenarios with poor
> participation in Candiwi?'
>
> This last question comes across the wrong way; I don't mean to suggest
> that this is a likely outcome, or even a certainty. But it is a
> theoretical possibility that one could build a candiwi in a community
> with an average participation < 10%. What then, could the wiki never
> move forward? If these users disappeared (fake email, etc) could it be
> frozen in time?
>
> Both are questions and scenarios I was considering when the idea for
> 'article-level settings' came to mind. It would allow for a community
> to lower the threshold of acceptance (to, say, 5%) for most articles,
> but retain control over those with major impact on the organization.
