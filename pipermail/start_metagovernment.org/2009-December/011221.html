On Sun, Dec 27, 2009 at 10:52 AM, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org>wrote:

> On Dec 27, 2009, at 1:33 PM, Suresh Fernando wrote:
>
> Suresh, I think you have the totality of reasons wrong as to why they
>> would reject debt. It is not only because they would have to pay it
>> back. It is also because of the political consequences of taking on
>> debt, and who socially controls outcomes when you access resources by
>> way of debt. Sure, there are probably some that will reject debt
>> because they'll have to pay it back. but, this is not always the case.
>>
>
> This also might well be true and is not something that I have really
> thought much about. If `principles` make it such that they can`t accept debt
> (or equity) from other investors then there`s not much we can do. This
> stance will substantially inhibit their ability to scale their operations
> and actually bring about change in the world, however.Whether we like it
> or not, money does make certain things possible...
>
>
> This is one of the issues Metagovernment is working through in considering
> this proposition. Our principles are clearly stated
> http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Mission_and_vision
> and basically are diametrically opposed to anyone having privileged
> interest in governance (including our own governance). So most if not all of
> our member projects would be fundamentally opposed to allowing investors to
> have a special say in how they do things.
>
> That said, I think your pooled structure may sidestep this issue...? If an
> investor's money is going through the filter of first the pooled fund and
> then the coordinating group before getting to the end-project, then would
> investors really have any expectation of influence?
>

Good point. In setting up the structure, the governance of the actual
relationship with the projects would be delegated to the OK Pooled Fund
Management Group. Hence, the investors, specifically, would not have direct
influence. That said, they would want to know that the management group is
engaged in a way that their investments are `protected`, in some sense.

>
> I would think that their thinking would have to be that they can invest in
> something they agree with and hope to get a return based on the model of
> that project/s... or they can not invest. And that that would be the extent
> of the control they could expect to have.
>
> P.S. As for your conclusion that rejecting money would inhibit a project's
> influence, I don't think that's necessarily true. Projects such as Apache,
> Firefox, Wikipedia, and Debian are predominantly volunteer efforts, yet they
> are certainly world-changing.
>

This is very true. The exception to the rule that money is a driver for
projects is the open source model that you are citing. I still maintain that
this is the exception to the rule and that, on balance, it`s easier to drive
a project if you have some money!
