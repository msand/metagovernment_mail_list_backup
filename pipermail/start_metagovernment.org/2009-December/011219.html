> I think that active projects could be profitable, just like other

That is an intriguing approach. I guess all it requires is creative  
terminology in the license. Can you think of a specific company/OSS  
project that does this well?

> It is easy to view a company conformed by two distinguishable  
> groups: investors and employees. Investors conform the directory,  
> that throws guidelines to be accomplished by the employees. How  
> investors organize their workers to realize those general orders in  
> the most effective way is a matter of another nature.
>
> Today's accepted way is a vertical structure. But is this the best?  
> Maybe we can prove that collaborative governance is a far better  
> structure. A drawback is that decision-making last longer than a  
> boss' decision. In the other hand employees could resolved their  
> everyday problems organically, take decisions that effect him/her,  
> understand the organization behaviour and see orders as fair ones,  
> fell integrated by other means than playing a football game with co- 
> workers. Unbounded motivation.

There are two model organizations we could use together for this  
approach. I have not researched either one extensively, but they each  
handle one side of the equation with an open approach:

E Text Editor, the open company:
http://metagovernment.org/wiki/Open_company
is developing a trust metric for paying "employees." This allows them  
to forego standard employment and instead allow anyone to contribute  
to the project (as with OSS) and *get paid for it.*

MyFootballClub, the web-community-owned sports team:
http://metagovernment.org/wiki/MyFootballClub
handles the other side of the equation: ownership by a community of  
members. In a sense, MyFootballClub resembles any publicly traded  
corporation; but it differs in that all members have equal say (one  
vote each); voting is more detailed than the basic votes given to  
corporate investors; it is primarily organized through the web; and  
instead of investing, members pay quarterly dues. It is a nice  
starting point for the idea, but such concepts really could benefit  
from a more sophisticated governance structure. That is, they have a  
board of directors instead of a collaborative governance mechanism.

If these two approaches are combined, one can imagine a company where  
anyone can be an "owner" and anyone can be an "employee." Wow. The  
more I think about this, the more my head spins. A company completely  
defined by the amorphous community of people who feel like  
contributing to it.
