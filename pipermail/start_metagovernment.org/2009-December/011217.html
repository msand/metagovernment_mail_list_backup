Response to Michael`s comments:

For my 2 cents, I have another proposal.  I don't know if this is
counter or complementary to OpenKollab's.  It comes out of private
discussions held earlier this month:

 1.  Demonstrate an open architecture with:
       a) Input of two or more cooperating projects; and
       b) Output of interoperable tools.
     This is a few weeks work.

 2.  On the basis of that demonstration, apply for funding.

This would work and is actually the way that we would prefer to engage with
you since the relationship between the projects would be defined.

Note that what is unique about the approach that we are taking is that we
are looking for ecosystems of projects to fund, not individual projects.
This means that in order to satisfy the criteria that we are looking for,
projects need to be working together in some way.

 3.  . . . continue working together on the common parts.

I think this is a better approach than front-loading the process with
money, then hoping for good work to come of it.  In my own experience,
it usually doesn't happen that way.  (Based on their own comments, I
think Ed and Owen may agree on this point.)

Don`t worry no one will be throwing money at projects. There will need to be
a business case that demonstrates a clearly defined plan for the group of
projects to move forward together. It won`t be easy to satisfy those
criteria. Furthermore, the scale needs to be sufficiently large to attract
investors.

We are looking for the leaders, and associated projects, in this group that
have ambitions to roll out their infrastructure globally, can see how the
can work with other projects in this system and have the plan and the team
to execute on the vision.

> Ed,
>
> Here is my response to Owens comments - see inline.
>
> Slide 10 -- "Visions" are not "measurable"; goals and objectives should be.
>
> This is true but the first priority for social investors is that the
> business have a vision that can be articulated. Clearly as a part of the
> operational plan specific milestones that are measurable will have to be
> defined.
>
> Slide 13 -- "Projects that are collaborating" ... on what?  Presumably, the
> pursuit of measurable objectives.
>
> Our thinking around this idea has actually evolved recently and the slide
> deck should really be updated. Initially the idea was to have a very
> defined
> focus on collaboration infrastructure projects. We have now expanded the
> scope and are looking at systems of projects that are connected. For
> example, we are currently mapping out relationships in the Open
> Manufacturing space as well as starting to explore relationships within
> specific regional local foods ecosystems.
>
> The local foods ecosystems have clearly understandable customer, supplier,
> partner relationships and, obviously, they operate within clearly defined
> geographic boundaries and therefore people within the ecosystems have
> personal relationships.
>
> Slide 14 -- Synergy should be based upon common values, goals, objectives,
> and stakeholders (as well as visions and missions.)
>
> Agreed
>
> Slide 15 -- $50K seems like a large amount for the minimum.  I suspect that
> few folks would be willing to put their names on the dotted line to pay
> back
> more than a few hundred dollars.
>
> If you don`t have a project that requires at least $50K it doesn`t fit our
> criteria. It clearly isn`t of substantial scale and maturity.
>
> Slide 16 -- If the folks who are planning to put up the money are unwilling
> or unable to put equity into these "investments," it doesn't seem like they
> believe very strongly in the concept.  Why wouldn't they want to be a part
> of something in which they believe?  In my view, the last thing that many
> folks should be considering in these economic times is more deficit
> spending
> (i.e., debt financing).  I certainly don't plan to take on any debt, but I
> definitely do plan to continue contributing my time and effort to the
> pursuit of objectives in which I believe.
>
> This comment reflects a clear lack of understanding of theoretical finance
> as well as how social ventures are funded.
>
> The primary goal with a social venture is *to deliver social value *and not
> financial return. As we all know most organizations that deliver social
> returns are called *not-for-profits. *There is a reason they are referred
> to
> as such - *they don`t deliver profits*. If there is no profit, there is no
> possibility of a return for equity investors.
>
> Social ventures are projects that have a dual mission; deliver social value
> and some financial value. The primary mission, however, is to deliver
> social
> value. In doing so they trade off financial return.
>
> What we are are looking for are projects that deliver social value but, at
> the least, break even financially. Projects that break even do not generate
> any profit and hence do not generate any equity. This makes the
> uninvestable
> for equity investors.
>
> Quite simply there is not enough financial upside in social venture
> investments for investors to assume the equity risk.
>
> Furthermore, a preliminary investigation of existing social venture funds
> will make it pretty clear that the structure that investors are comfortable
> with is a debt or quasi-debt structure. Equity upside can be structured
> into
> the deal by offering convertible notes, royalty payments tied to profits
> and
> the like.
>
> I am in personal contact with most of the major social venture funding
> sources in the US and the UK and this is the preferred structure from an
> investment perspective.
>
>
> If any of those in this group have projects with a revenue model and a
> foreseeable break-even point and are interested in exploring this further,
> please let us know.
>
>
>
> I hope this helps to make things clearer.
>
