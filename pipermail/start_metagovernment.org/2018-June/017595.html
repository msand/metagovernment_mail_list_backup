On Sun, Jun 3, 2018, 11:58 AM Scott Raney, <metamerman at gmail.com> wrote:

> The results of the GCF have been posted:
> https://globalchallenges.org/our-work/the-new-shape-prize/awardees
>
> I've read the winning proposals, and skimmed several others, and
> unfortunately they all make the "Next System" competition winners look
> like models of precision by comparison: Such frantic handwaving I've
> never seen, at least outside postmodernist screed. I was particularly
> disappointed by the "AI" entry, expecting it to flesh out exactly
> *how* AI would be used to refine proposals (a tantalizing promise in
> the abstract). Unfortunately the full submission contains exactly zero
> additional information as to how any of it would work. The other two
> winners are just UN tweaks (Argh!). None of the entries address the
> fundamental flaws in misrepresentative-based decisionmaking, and yet
> the 3 winners were awarded $600K each to spend on their Tahitian
> vacations. But at least that means most of the prize money was
> withheld and so they'll be able to run the contest again at some
> point. With that in mind I sent them the following critique about a
> month ago, a submission they acknowledged but I haven't heard anything
> back from them since.
>
> But I have to say overall I'm very encouraged: If that's the best of
> the competition, the members of this group have a much better chance
> of achieving disruption than anyone else working in this area because
> at least we have *some* idea of what works and what doesn't. We also
> have a definite preference for engineering over handwaving, which
> seems to be relatively rare if the entrants in all of these
> competitions is any guide. I recommend we redouble our efforts because
> if we don't come up with a better system and get it implemented, it's
> abundantly clear that no one else will either...
>   Regards,
>     Scott
>
> To whom it may concern:
> While I was not terribly surprised that my GCF entry (Matched Proxy
> Augmented Direct Democracy, or MPADD) was not selected as a finalist
> (I didn't follow the rules!), I was surprised and very disappointed in
> the quality of the entries that were selected. My analysis of them
> just confirms my assessment going into this process that there are
> serious flaws in both the rules as stated and the process the GCF used
> to judge the entries, flaws that virtually guarantee that none of the
> finalists will be capable of eliminating, or even partially
> correcting, the serious flaws in our existing decisionmaking systems.
> Hopefully you can use this criticism to improve the process for the
> next time the contest is held.
>
> 1) There is a fundamental flaw in the rules that ensures that you
> never received (or at least could not accept) the only proposals that
> actually could effect any change in public policy decisionmaking. The
> problematic paragraph is flawed in so many ways that I must quote the
> whole thing:
>
> "The governance model must also be such that it can be implemented
> within the foreseeable future. This requires that it be acceptable to
> major states and the wider international community. A significant
> measure of civic acceptance is also required. This requirement
> eliminates models that rely on time-consuming and controversial
> changes in the political system of individual states, e.g. models that
> postulate that all states should be democracies."
>
> This insistence on deferring to those with an inherent strong
> preference for the status quo is fundamentally incompatible with the
> expectation that the decisionmaking process can be changed. My entry
> explicitly rejected this requirement (likely leading to its
> disqualification), but I note that many of the finalists implicitly
> plan to replace/bypass the existing decisionmakers without specifying
> how they'd achieve this. While I acknowledge that this is a
> politically sensitive area it my considered opinion is that attempt to
> hide the fact that it is our goal to replace existing governments or
> at least substantially reduce their power is not only dishonest but
> leads to handwaving and poor design because it eliminates the need to
> define how and when the transition in power will occur. Should any of
> these proposals actually accrue any power leaving this transition
> unspecified is also risking repeating the mistakes of Nazism and
> Marxism/Leninism that led to the deaths of millions.
>
> This rule is also based on a naive interpretation of political power
> ("a significant measure of civic acceptance"? Seriously?): A system
> capable of replacing our existing public policy decisionmaking systems
> globally and at all levels could easily be built by a small group of
> volunteers (it would be a small fraction of scale of the Linux
> project, for example), would require cloud computing resources that
> would cost significantly less than the GCF prize amount to operate
> each year, and could take over these duties within months of its
> release. What remains is merely a marketing problem: Once a majority
> of The People *believe* that the new system is the legitimate
> government, it simply becomes that. No need for the drama of a violent
> overthrow of any existing governments or even having to ask for their
> approval (though it would of course be wise to have a plan for how to
> deal with the minority that will resist the transition).
>
> 2) The rules specify that the contest is about decisionmaking, but
> many accepted entries are actually economic models.
>
> It might be sufficient to just specify this distinction more clearly
> in the rules, but the judges too should brush up on this difference:
> It seems reasonable to me to just completely reject any proposal that
> emphasizes economic models, particularly those designed merely to
> *fund* projects, to effect change when they leave the decisionmaking
> model underspecified or in some cases even completely unspecified. It
> should be obvious as to why: A functioning decisionmaking system can
> bring unimaginable economic resources to bear on a problem whereas
> even the most powerful economic system can be shut down with a simple
> vote by a decisionmaking system that considers the new system to be a
> threat to its power.
>
> 3) Many entries include proposals that have already been shown to be
> unworkable or irrelevant (e.g. anything relying on blockchain
> technology or Liquid Democracy).
>
> These issues have been debated many places (slashdot, reddit, etc.),
> but the most recent debates I've participated in are on the
> Metagovernment mailing list (see the February and March 2018
> archives):
> http://metagovernment.org/pipermail/start_metagovernment.org/
>
> And on the DemocracyEarth whitepaper GitHub project:
> https://github.com/DemocracyEarth/paper/issues
>
> They are also discussed in some of my own projects, including the Help
> system for proxyfor.me (https://www.proxyfor.me/) and in the Matchism
> manifesto (http://www.matchism.org/system)
>
> 4) The judges are academics and organizational experts rather than
> social engineers or visionaries and so are biased toward things that
> seem familiar to them.
>
> While one might think that having experience in government or in an
> NGO would qualify one as a judge, in my considered opinion the
> opposite is actually the case: These individuals have devoted their
> careers to figuring out ways of gaming the system rather than having
> the freedom to design a new one from scratch and so can only see new
> proposals from the very narrow perspective of their own hardened
> appraisal of how difficult it is to change the system from within.
> Disruption *always* comes from outside, and that's also where you'll
> find the best judges of technology that is designed to disrupt (e.g.,
> next time ask those who have successfully instigated disruptions in
> other industries to be your judges).
>
> 5) Biased toward handwaving and against functioning systems.
>
> My guess is that the anonymity requirement was an attempt to acquire
> legitimacy by imitating blind peer reviews in academia, but this is a
> bad idea for two reasons. First, academia doesn't *actually* work that
> way (authors and reviewers can usually figure out who everyone is
> because, being experts in the field, they know who's working on what).
> Secondly, these are not experiments or theories, they're supposed to
> be *social engineering* proposals. And as anyone who's worked in
> engineering, or technology in general, can tell you, a working
> prototype is worth a mountain of handwaving proposals. By excluding
> this information from the judging, particularly when you only have
> managers and academics as judges, you've virtually guaranteed that the
> winning proposals will be those that superficially appear to be
> solutions but have fundamental flaws that make them completely
> unworkable (which is my assessment of *all* of the finalists in this
> round). Next time, you should *encourage* the entrants to submit
> working prototypes or at least published work that fleshes out the
> models and then bias the judging toward those ideas that have proven
> to be workable or at least implementable.
>
> 6) No public debate forum available during entry preparation, leading
> to obviously unworkable entries because they never received feedback
> that would correct obvious errors and oversights (e.g., as specified
> in #3 above).
>
> Even the GCF web site seems to operate as a closed system with no
> opportunity for discussion or even feedback (the email this message
> was sent to is the only personal email address I found on the entire
> site!) Though public debate does provide the opportunity for
> time-wasting trolling, it is also by far the most efficient way to
> identify deeply flawed and other crackpot ideas. If a proposal can
> survive a debate in a public forum it has a vastly better chance of
> being viable and implementable. And the time to do this is during
> proposal preparation, not after judging as you seem to propose now.
>
> 7) It kind of bothered me that the rules seem to indicate that winner
> could just take the 5 mil and retire to Tahiti. I would have liked to
> see the "prize" be more like funding for a grant proposal, with a
> specific work product promised. It would seem reasonable to me to do
> that for this year's "winners" too: Have them prepare a proposal for
> how much they need and what they'll produce if they get that, then
> fund based on this additional information. I would recommend against
> what seems to be the current plan which looks to me like attempting to
> create ad-hoc groups to make mash-ups of the proposals and then try to
> implement those Frankenproposals...
>
> If anyone would like to discuss any of these items I remain at your
