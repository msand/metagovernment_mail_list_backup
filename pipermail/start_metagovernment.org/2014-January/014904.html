    Marc> It is the scalability of the decision rule that makes it act

Ned> I suspect that this is a key sentence, which causes me to regret
that I don't understand what you are saying. When you say "the decision

"encountered" ... "emergence" ... (I'm lost).

------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Ned> The AG_MFT model separates the democratic process into:

        * opinion forming
        * position forming
        * decision making
        * (and now "coordination system")

        The Isonomea/Blinap model contains no such separation of the
        democratic process into three parts corresponding to these three
        aspects. More particularly, there is no distinction between
        "position forming" (discussion system) and "decision-making"
        (resolution system).

    Marc> Just for clarification: The AG-MFT model just states that
    there are different aspects of the decision-making process that a
    system *could* focus on in general. In an ideal world those
    different aspects might be represented by different and seperate
    systems that interact with each other. But in reality each existing
    theory or system will be part of at least one of those aspects.

        Unfortunately I don't know much about Blinap in this regard, so
        I can't say which aspects of the AG-MFT model it really covers.
        But IMHO the seperation just exists; independently from any
        existing theory or system.

Ned> It may be that seeing discussion and resolution as separate aspects
(and having separate systems for discussion and resolution) has seemed
inevitable because there has not up to this point ever been a (NUO)
system such as Blinap that could successfully operate at the global scale.

Now that such (NUO) systems are possible, use of (CV) must be justified
(if it can be) for other reasons than just because it is "the only game
in town" for decision-making at the scale of large populations.
