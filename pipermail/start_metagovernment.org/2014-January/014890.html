> On Jan 15, 2014, at 3:52 PM, Ned Conner wrote:
>
> > (I keep posting this quote only because no one has so far even tried to
> counter it. We really need to "grok in fullness" that "count votes" systems
> have been around for thousands of years, and have *NEVER* shown themselves
> to be sustainable at the scale of large populations -- for reasons that are
> intrinsic to voting, irrespective of the technical particulars of the
> vote-count system.)
>

I believe its the only system that ever worked, e.g. money.
money is a special voting system and it makes the world spin.



> >
> >   A democracy [powered by popular voting] is always temporary in
> >   nature; it simply cannot exist as a permanent form of government. A
> >   democracy will continue to exist up until the time that voters
> >   discover they can vote themselves generous gifts from the public
> >   treasury. From that moment on, the majority always vote for the
> >   candidates who [and directly for the initiatives that] promise the
> >   most benefits from the public treasury, with the result that every
> >   democracy will finally collapse due to loose fiscal policy, which is
> >   always followed by a dictatorship. The average age of the world's
> >   greatest civilizations from the beginning of history, has been about
> >   200 years. -- Alexander Tyler (1787)
>
> I hadn't noticed you posting this before, but I thought you knew better
> than to characterize advanced democracy systems (i.e., some Metagov
> projects) as having anything to do with this antiquated, broken, primitive
> form of voting. In philosophical terms, that's an inductive fallacy or
> perhaps a definist fallacy.
>
> The above quote assumes that democracy is equivalent to majority rule. Our
> form of democracy is about consensus. You will never find a consensus of
> the voters "voting themselves generous gifts from the public treasury."
> That's absurd. It is their treasury, and at least a few people will be
> smart enough to understand that.* I cannot possibly imagine any
> collaborative governance system where any proposal can be voted on without
> the funding being a part of the proposal and the dialog. In a sense, most
> government proposing is in fact about funding.
>
>
> *I boldly make the assertion that if consensus is defined as a massive
> majority, such as 80-90% of participants, then there will always be 10-20%
> of the people reasonable enough to stop stupidity from reigning.
> Additionally, I do not think any past system can be compared to
> collaborative governance, because in even current "advanced democracies"
> most people are disenfranchised almost all the time: so their level of
> interest and participation is nothing like what can be expected if they
> have the power to participate in any decision as they please.
