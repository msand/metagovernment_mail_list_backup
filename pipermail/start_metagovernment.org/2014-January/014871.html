Hi marc, hi everyone.
i dont believe "counting votes" vs. "no unresolved objections" is an
either-or-question.
i feel they converge if you bring in the dimension of time and repeated
voting.
=> voting creates a snapshot of the current "status quo".
if you use "permanent voting", that means you can vote for something or
withdraw your vote at anytime and switch it to something else, then voting
is "always on" and between switching your vote from one thing to another,
there is discussion and resolving "unresolved objections"

... so if you think about time and continuous permanent voting, then i dont
see the difference between those, more that they complement each other

> Ned Conner wrote:
>
>> My answer: To choose between the abstract "No Unresolved
>> Objections" decision rule and the abstract "Count Votes"
>> decision rule is (in Thomas Kuhn terms) to choose between
>> two entirely distinct decision making paradigms; two distinct
>> ways to do democracy. In my view, there is no more
>> fundamental choice that we Metagovernment Mavens could
>> be discussing.
>>
>
> Well, is this really the one-million-dollar-question: Which decision rule
> to choose?
>
>    A) "No Unresolved Objections"
>    B) "Count Votes"
>
> IMHO there is no need to choose one rule over the other!
>
> Because both rules not only typify different paradigms for
> decision-making, but they focus on entirely different aspects of the
> democratic process also! Hence in the end they are not comparable to each
> other. Thus no decision possible. (This reminds me of the Mission
> Impossible tune - really no clue why! ;o)
>
> From my point of view there are two serious differences; a qualitative and
> a quantitative one:
>
>    (1) While (A) focuses on equity, (B) strives for majority.
>    (2) While (A) solely suits well for small groups, (B) can also handle
> large scale effectively.
>
> This leads me to the assumption that we should implement *both* rules and
> choose which one to use depending on the current context of a decision to
> be made on demand.
>
> What do you think?
