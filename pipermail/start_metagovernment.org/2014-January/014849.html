Choosing between abstract decision rules is more like choosing among 
programming languages -- the choice does not speak to specific 
application designs, or to specific implementation choices and 
logistics, or to what the specific applications will be used to do.

The abstract decision rule can be applied to existing designs such as 
SettleIt in any number of concrete ways, just as the abstract idea of 
vote-counting can be implemented in any number of concrete ways.

I take it from your response that you do not see SettleIt as a decision 
making system, and do not see any way to make it into one using the "no 
unresolved objections" decision rule. If this is so, I would be very 
interested to learn your thoughts about the obstacles to doing so.

(Off the top of my head, adding a "not resolved" button to each item, 
that prompted for explanation of why not when pressed, might be a start. 
But, the design of SettleIt already substantially does that, so the 
implementation of the decision rule might only involve an extension to 
the scoring algorithm that the program also already has. But these are 
just guesses until I have spent some hands-on time with the new version.)

>             In making public decisions, we need a decision rule.
>         Abstractly,
>             two choices are here now on the table: count votes, or
>         deliberate
>             to the point of "no unresolved objections".
>
>             The choice to count votes to decide has been around for
>         thousands
>             of years. Those who have studied its efficacy over all of
>         those
>             years have generally found that it does not work very well:
>
>                A democracy [powered by popular voting] is always
>         temporary in
>                nature; it simply cannot exist as a permanent form of
>         government. A
>                democracy will continue to exist up until the time that
>         voters
>                discover they can vote themselves generous gifts from
>         the public
>                treasury. From that moment on, the majority always vote
>         for the
>                candidates who [and directly for the initiatives that]
>         promise the
>                most benefits from the public treasury, with the result
>         that every
>                democracy will finally collapse due to loose fiscal policy,
>             which is
>                always followed by a dictatorship. The average age of
>         the world's
>                greatest civilizations from the beginning of history,
>         has been
>             about
>                200 years. -- Alexander Tyler (1787)
>
>             Now that we have computers and global electronic
>         communications,
>             another decision rule option is on the table (even at the
>         scale of
>             large populations). The option is to keep proposing and
>             counter-proposing until the point is reached that, among
>         all those
>             participating (which includes all those who wish to
>         participate),
>             there are no unresolved objections to implementing the
>         proposal.
>             That marks the point at which the decision has been made to
>             implement the proposal.
>
>             Which sort of decision rule shall we adopt?
