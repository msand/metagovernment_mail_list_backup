On Jan 17, 2014, at 8:45 PM, Alexander Praetorius wrote:

> its not open, its final all the time.
> if 50 people vote against death penalty and 51 vote for death penalty, than "death penalty" is put into practice ...UNTIL at least one person changes its mind. ...sometimes this could lead to: 5:31pm "death penalty is an option", 5:43pm "death penalty is not an option" 5:51pm "death penalty is an option", 6:13pm "death penalty is not an option".
> I believe for almost everything, vote proportions would be somehow stable for most active laws or whatever proposals could be.
> For those which are currently heavy discussed in public, they would fluctuate a lot until they become stable... after a long public discussion.

I'm not fond of this process, since it leads to uncertainty. In my vision, there are two things preventing this sort of "flapping" on/off of decisions.

First, I strongly oppose any system where 51% of the votes results in a decision. Majority rule is the bane of democracy. The United States is the best example: we have a pure majority rule system (or really plurality-rule, but let's not get into that), which has funneled us into a 2-party system where every decision is framed as for-or-against. In a consensus system (in which I include a huge majority, such as 80-90%), nothing can be decided until a solution is developed that can get broad acceptance, and so it's not a question of if you support or oppose, but rather of what can everyone support. That sort of consensus rule should inherently prevent flapping. Once a proposal has been crafted so well that almost everyone is onboard with it, chances are it's not going to flap unless the community of voting participants expands significantly.

Second, decisions can have a simple waiting period. For example, in the Rules page, we defined waiting periods (W) of one and three weeks for two kinds of decisions:
http://www.metagovernment.org/wiki/Rules
That solves a simple problem. If three people all agree on a new proposal and it passes; it still doesn't go into effect until the larger community has had time to mull it over. If a fourth person notices that those three proposed something ridiculous, person #4 can jump in, break their consensus, and effectively reset the timer. Now those three have to get #4's buy-in by modifying their proposal to make more sense. And the process can continue to scale in that way indefinitely until there is a proposal that works for the whole community.
