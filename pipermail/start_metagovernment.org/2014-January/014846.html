> accomplishing the mission.

>>     In making public decisions, we need a decision rule. Abstractly,
>>     two choices are here now on the table: count votes, or deliberate
>>     to the point of "no unresolved objections".
>>
>>     The choice to count votes to decide has been around for thousands
>>     of years. Those who have studied its efficacy over all of those
>>     years have generally found that it does not work very well:
>>
>>        A democracy [powered by popular voting] is always temporary in
>>        nature; it simply cannot exist as a permanent form of government. A
>>        democracy will continue to exist up until the time that voters
>>        discover they can vote themselves generous gifts from the public
>>        treasury. From that moment on, the majority always vote for the
>>        candidates who [and directly for the initiatives that] promise the
>>        most benefits from the public treasury, with the result that every
>>        democracy will finally collapse due to loose fiscal policy,
>>     which is
>>        always followed by a dictatorship. The average age of the world's
>>        greatest civilizations from the beginning of history, has been
>>     about
>>        200 years. -- Alexander Tyler (1787)
>>
>>     Now that we have computers and global electronic communications,
>>     another decision rule option is on the table (even at the scale of
>>     large populations). The option is to keep proposing and
>>     counter-proposing until the point is reached that, among all those
>>     participating (which includes all those who wish to participate),
>>     there are no unresolved objections to implementing the proposal.
>>     That marks the point at which the decision has been made to
>>     implement the proposal.
>>
>>     Which sort of decision rule shall we adopt?
