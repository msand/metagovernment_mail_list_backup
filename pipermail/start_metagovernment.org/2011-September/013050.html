A few random thoughts:


- I agree that collaborative governance has to start from the bottom up.

- c.g. has to be based on open source software,and must be provable as mathematically correct, i.e. as simple as possible (see some of Alan Kay's recent work e.g. http://www.tele-task.de/de/archive/video/flash/14029/), and inherently bug-free.


- The system must deal with sets of simple issues at a granular level, i.e. things that can be resolved to a single change in a process or resource allocation.  These could be grouped into larger things but the granularity has to be there to allow the full extent of any proposal to be completely clear, with references/sources provided, etc.  That way people can refine and comment on individual things as needed, but it won't turn into a huge blob of text and subjective interpretations.


- A network-oriented architecture must be taken in the software, which manages complexity by modeling things as networks of nodes (resources, people, groups, organizations) and dynamic relationships.  In other words it has to model the real world.  (Kind of like object-oriented programming, but without the emphasis on programming, without the limitations of typical OO structures, and with an equal emphasis on the dynamic relationships between objects.)  If a system does not accurately capture existing reality, most people will not be able to understand the existing reality, what changes or optimizations are therefore appropriate, or what effects those changes would have.

(I see this above point as the biggest issue, and am now developing a web information system that makes it easy for non-programmers to construct and share any type of data structures and relations to model those kinds of things.  It's probably many ~6 months away from being useful however.)

- I think the biggest barrier to collaborative governance of any sort is complexity management.  If a framework does not exist that allows reality to be accurately visualized and understood by average (non-technical) people, for potential changes to be simulated, and for users to provide input on a granular basis, then runaway complexity and chaos quickly results.  Which I believe is the biggest reason we have the political systems we have today.  98% of people don't understand the subtle details of how things really work with complex government-related systems, and thus emotion is all they are left with for a decision framework.

One of the biggest deceptions of law is to introduce a faux brand of complexity into affairs such that only lawyers and politicians are "qualified" to administer things, and the rest of the people are left with no sufficiently powerful logical framework from which to counter.  Only when science and technology are able to rise to the occasion, surmount their own complexities, and provide an inarguably solid framework, will we be able to replace the old system.


I have lots of other ideas too but just thought I'd throw those out there for now as I see the information system part of the equation as key.  The web as it is today is
 in many ways just an extension of the concepts of print media which came before it and I think there needs to be a radical shift in that.  Email chains, blogs, articles, etc. 99% of the time just contribute to long blobs of static information that quickly become unmanageable, unbounded, and obsolete.  I think that with a dynamic, interlinked view of information, an appropriate framework could enable information to be built, maintained, and refined as a cohesive interlinked structure.

For example in science or in formal logic, the processes for expanding knowledge are set up so you 
start with known conditions, and add to existing knowledge incrementally, i.e. in quantum steps, which over time creates a network topology of tightly coupled information.  Which is 
why science is so powerful, because it's 
all one interlinked set of concepts and information which essentially create solid matter out of billions of quantum 
information particles - and in that way mirror reality and the structure of the universe.  So far there has been no real 
way to represent this data in it's natural form - it has been 
forced into the serialized constructs of human language such as books, pictures, tables, etc., which results in a lot of overspecialization and inefficiency in the sciences.  Anyhow these limitations and 'impedance mismatches' between reality and its digital representation also 
extend to most other areas - i.e. politics and economics, and thus a 
huge set of separate pseudo-scientific views result, none of which have the depth or continuity to be adopted on a wider scale.  It's easy to take the 
current ways of the world for granted and not see the significance of 
aligning information systems with reality, but I see it as being no 
less significant than the invention of  
computers themselves.  Anyhow just wanted to mention these things and hopefully I'll have some more concrete contributions over the next few months :)


-David
