On Sep 21, 2011, at 9:33 PM, Matteo Martini wrote:

> I call that tyranny of the majority. Having rule by 50%+1 of the  
> voting populace is a great prescription for oppression of minorities.
> It is playing out here in the U.S. now with oppressive anti-  
> immigration laws.
> Reply: Tyranny of the majority IS what democracy is all about. There  
> is no better system than the tyranny of the majority. What other  
> system do you propose? Tyranny of the minority? There is no way to  
> get around this. Even if you propose a law, allow people to discuss  
> this law a thousand of times and amend it a thousand times, there  
> will always be a majority that will like it one way and a minority  
> taht likes it the other way.

There is no natural requirement that all governance must be tyrannical  
or oppressive. Democracy does not have to be majority-based. That is  
simply how it has usually been done.

It is in fact possible for people to come to consensus, if they work  
hard, have the right tools, and want it enough. Collaborative  
governance offers people only two options: find consensus on a new  
law, or do not pass a law. I think either one is better than passing a  
law that is oppressive.

> In conclusion, I think the main difference between your approach and  
> my ideas is that you are working to create a potentially  
> revolutionary system almost from scratch while I think that a more  
> pragmatical approach has more chances to success.

I agree with the difference, but not the conclusion. Mixdemocracy is  
pragmatic, but I think it is not possible to do it well. I believe  
that if it starts to gain enough success that it begins to scare the  
powerful elite, they will turn their attention to it, and it will be  
overwhelmed by malicious vandals, outrageous news stories (even if  
they are not true: that never stops these people), and other attacks.

Collaborative governance is "revolutionary" in that it is an entirely  
new way of doing things, but it is not an attempt to usurp the current  
power structures. Rather, the plan is for it to take hold in small  
decision-making environments, prove itself and adapt to larger and  
larger ones. As people get used to it, they will start to use the  
tools of the status quo to replace those tools with ours.

That last step is very much a good place for mixdemocracy to come in:  
E2D "politicians" can be the actors of converting systems to  
collaborative governance. This is a great opportunity for the two  
initiatives to work together (among others, since our software is  
similar). But I don't know if the timing will be right if E2D groups  
are already taking office while collaborative governance software is  
all still pretty much in alpha or pre-alpha.
