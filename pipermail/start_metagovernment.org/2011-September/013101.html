I was referring to the representation of information in a clear, complete, intuitive way, which would mostly fall under "(b)".  And although this is maybe is not critical for simple issues, or for issues relating more to morals, or highly subjective areas, for other things that relate more to resource allocation options with many variables, or for example to the history of a political candidate's campaign contributions and voting history, I think some clever ways of representing large data sets would be a big value add to many types of collaboration systems. As an example, one helpful visual tool is often a timeline - e.g. http://simile.mit.edu/timeline/examples/jfk/jfk.html - which can be quite helpful in understanding a complex issue that otherwise would take 10 times longer if you were to sort through dozens of sources to attempt to assemble all the information into a cohesive form with links to all the original sources.  Another example of a visual
 aid might be in illustrating the net economic effects of large public works proposals, using something like a Sankey diagram or other charts showing the various inputs and outputs.  Anyone who has taken economics 101 probably would agree that optimizing economics equations is usually not intuitive and usually requires some visualization to find the optimum inflection point or points where various curves cross.  Charts and visual tools are not my primary goal however, the first thing I'm focusing on is making it very easy for average people to get data into a system in a way that allows that data to be easily shared, verified, updated, etc.  The visual tools should then be relatively easy to add as a UI layer.


So yes my project is now focused on (a) - getting information from the real world, and representing it in a natural, flexible, and dynamic way.  It will be awhile before I get too far into (b), and (c) is a step I have not put much thought into yet.  Hopefully I'll have some more to say on my progress in a few months.  The basic idea though is to provide essentially a distributed CMS platform which is extremely simple in a formal mathematical sense, and which can be easily extended for purposes of data visualization, simulation, and collaboration.  (Which of course is all a fairly vague concept at the moment, and I apologize for taking up everyone's time with my explanation of this idealistic system before I actually have anything to show for it.  But maybe someone else has thought about the same thing, or seen something similar, and might have some good input for me, thus my reason for mentioning it.)

>> Maybe I'm asking too much of a collaborative governance system, and

>> voting system, and it may be no more effective against corruption

>
>You already *do* expect tight integration... but perhaps you give your
>own tools too foundational a role.  There is theory that the
>rationality of public decisions cannot be cognitive-instrumental
>alone.  The objective world is not the only world involved in the
>decision space, but that space also includes the social and subjective
>worlds, which require moral-practical and aesthetic-practical forms of
>reasoning [2 p.238].  All three forms of reasoning must be employed
>simultaneously by persons oriented to mutual understanding and
>agreement as a means of deciding what to do.  This combination of
>rationalities is called (I think) commmunicative reason and the
>overall decision + execution process, "communicative action".
>Fortunately it all happens more-or-less naturally when people are
>engaged in frank discussion with their peers.  This is not to say that
>they won't be needing reason-tools, but it does suggest that and
>objective orientation and cognitive-instrumental rationality cannot be
>foundational, as the rational foundation is necessarily tri-partite.
>All of this might figure into the design task (c).

Agreed.  I'm definitely not trying to cover every use case - just those which relate more to complex issues that are now not being served well by existing tools.

>There *is* a gaping hole in the current structure of society, but it
>seems not so much a complexity-handling deficit *in the first
>instance*.  The fact is not generally known yet, but I believe it is a
>fact that a fault (or breakage) exists between these two structural
>elements of society:
>
>    * individual person
>    * individual vote
>
>The individual person matters greatly in a modern society (subject of
>human rights), while the individual vote matters not at all.  It
>matters not at all because the course and outcome of the election are
>absolutely unaffected by it.  The individual person (as a voter) is
>thus disengaged from the electoral system, which is his/her only
>formal connection to politics.  Politics is about power.  The vote is
>the only power granted to the individual, and hence the only means of
>exercising power.  There are many voters in society, and the sum of
>their disengagement therefore amounts to a massive power vacuum.  This
>power vacuum cannot be filled by the individual voters as such owing
>to the fault in the electoral system; but a vacuum is not too picky
>about what fills it, so something other than the individual voters
>rushes in and does the job. [1]
>
>I mention all this in order to suggest that, because the ordinary
>individual *as such* has been exluded from participation in politics,
>we may safely conclude that the lifeworld has also been excluded.
>This may well account for the deficit in complexity handling that you
>diagnose.
>

Yes, there has indeed been quite a disconnect as you describe, which is certainly quite convenient for the oligarchs as it lets them do whatever they want while the people remain under the illusion that they have "democracy".

And I believe the only practical solution is - as R. Buckminster Fuller said, "You never change things by fighting the existing reality.  To change something, build a new model that makes the existing model obsolete".  So let's create a great platform and start using it at the local and community level and then over time it can expand to do bigger things.  The key to this however I feel is that is has to be not just a technology platform, but a formal, provable "mathematically correct" system which does not have the inherent bugs, complexity, and inconsistency of current approaches.

By the way I really like a lot of what I'm seeing on zelea and votorola, haven't read into it too much yet but the documentation is great and everything seems intuitive and well thought out, definitely looking forward to see how things evolve.
