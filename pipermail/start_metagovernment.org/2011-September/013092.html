Hi Paul, thank you for answering.

> >  1. When an [initiative] is prepared and presented, what effective
> >     means do I have of influencing its content, or amendments, or
> >     likelihood of passage?
 
Paul Nollen wrote:
> An itinitiative is prepared by a citizen, a group of citizens or an
> existing organisation. They can launch a call to help to prepare the
> initiative. The more help , debate and critisism while developing an
> initiative the more chanche that it will appeal to a great number of
> people and that most critics are already met.
> That is why we hope to incorporate this developing stadium in our
> website system.

That makes sense, as far as it goes.  If the initiative has little
support as yet, then the initial supporters will have considerable
leverage with which to negotiate amendments in return for their
support.  So far, so good.

However, by the time the initiative comes to my own attention, it will
be a take-it-or-leave it affair.  Even if I had time to spend, my
personal efforts at negotiation and revision would be unlikely to make
any head-way against the dead weight those who already support it.  My
effective means of influencing its content, or amendments, or
likelihood of passage, would therefore be nil.  I feel you must
address this problem, if you wish to use terms like "direct
democracy".

> >  2. How do these means differ in effect from those provided in the
> >     case of ordinary legislation?
> 
> It is a citizens initiative and the citizens decide how they develop
> the initiative. They have only to gain if they prepare it well with
> the broadest consensus possible because once launched it can't be
> altered anymore.  Therfore surveys must give the proposal a great
> majority (70% for example) before it starts because opposition has
> to start yet. If you start with 70% without organised opposition it
> is maybe possible to get the 50% needed.  Ordinary legislation need
> only a very small majority to agree (some tens of people?)

To me (the citizen) this appears to be no different than ordinary
legislation.  I have no influence over it.  Regardless of what I
happen to think, and regardless of any reasonable effort of mine to
voice objections and request amendments, the initiative will be
imposed upon me.
 
> >  3. How therefore does the initiative offer me an opportunity of
> >     political freedom, as opposed to a threat (as I might take it)
> >     of oppression?
> 
> It is a continuous development to a more acceptable society. The
> question is "what is better if I compare system A (representative
> democracy) with system B (direct democracy)". Is system B better,
> fine I accept it with the freedom of the possibility of developping
> a system C. Even that is only possible in system C in our case.

And for me the citizen, how is this better?  I already live in a
democracy and I am unable to participate as an equal citizen in
political affairs.  Now they will give me direct democracy and again I
will be unable to participate.  In this regard, the name "direct
democracy" merely adds insult to the original injury.

If the stakes were anything less crucial than political freedom, then
it would be easier to accept an incremental improvement as a side
benefit.  But the only credible way to make up for the broken promise
of democracy is to redeem that promise.  Before we can redeem it with
technology, however, we must know exactly what the promise was, and
how (in a technical sense) it got broken.

I will post my own thoughts in the "Freedom" thread, please critique
freely.
