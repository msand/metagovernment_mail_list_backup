you have to make a distinction between "plebiscite" who is a referendum on 
government  initiative and the citizens initiated "initiatives and 
referendum". Plebiscites are prohibited in Switzerland.

> I have several questions.  I'm less familiar with plebiscite systems,
> or referenda, or petitions, since we don't use them here.  I know they
> are meant to provide a means to participate in a legislative process
> that is ordinarily off limits to me as a citizen.  So, for instance,
> when a conventional bill is prepared in committee, I have no effective
> means of influencing its content, or amendments, or likelihood of
> passage.  Nor do I have any influence in the selection of law makers
> who sit in the assembly and vote on it.  Yet I must obey the law if
> they pass it.  Consequently, I have no freedom in regard to that law,
> but rather (it follows) I am in bondage to it.
>
> My questions concern the case of a plebiscite system:
>
>  1. When a plebiscite is prepared and presented, what effective means
>     do I have of influencing its content, or amendments, or
>     likelihood of passage?
>
>  2. How do these means differ in effect from those provided in the
>     case of ordinary legislation?
>
>  3. How therefore does the plebiscite offer me an opportunity of
>     political freedom, as opposed to a threat (as I might take it) of
>     oppression?
>

sorry, plebiscites don't belong to the tools of "direct democracy".
