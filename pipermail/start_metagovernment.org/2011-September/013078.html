p.s. - This email chain is a good example of what I referred to as runaway complexity in an earlier post. To rewind a bit, this following paragraph is from my original post in this thread, and kind of sums everything up:


"in my opinion, perhaps the first goal of any kind of 
collaborative governance / eDemocracy system should be to enable the 
public to understand what the complex reality is behind issues, and what the subtleties and side effects may be, and provide features that allow the community to document issues/platforms in a clear and formal way 
(i.e. with references, and with a mechanism to ensure objectivity, e.g. 
allowing subjective statements to be flagged, like the "citation needed" flags that exist in many wikipedia articles)."

That's all there was to it.  (And to clarify, when I say community above, that just means those in the community (or their delegates) who wish to be involved.)  I apologize if I was unclear about that, and for the several follow on emails which turned 1 paragraph into 20.  But for the purposes of clarity I maintain that the first issue we're facing is complexity.  And the establishment knows all too well how to leverage that to their advantage.  And this is a verrry subtle thing - if you take complexity as a given, and do nothing to minimize it within a social information system such as the types we are all interested in, then - in my opinion - you'll have a much harder time achieving larger scale adoption of any such system. 


Thanks,
David

2) any effort to have many many people (millions?) participate in the law-drafting process would be undoable as the process would become too confused (can you imagine having a million people each one with his different vision discuss about issues) and people do not have ten hours of time per day to dedicate in writing laws.


Again, not sure where this is coming from, I did not say that all people should spend hours writing and amending laws.

Anyhow, whether it is 100 or 100,000 people participating in writing and amending laws, I don't think a voting system alone is not sufficient to meet the aims of collaborative governance.  I think there should be a new type of information management system as a layer underneath - as I described in earlier posts (and which I can understand that not everyone will understand as it's a bit of a subtle concept) - to support the interaction of those people, and the complexities of the proposals being collaborated on.
