Not at all, my mistake.  You were referring to direct democracy in
California and Switzerland so maybe the correct word is "initiative".
I restate my questions (I hope) in correct form:

I'm not familiar with [initiative] systems, since we don't use them
here.  I know they are meant to provide a means to participate in a
legislative process that is ordinarily off limits to me as a citizen.
So, for instance, when a conventional bill is prepared in committee, I
have no effective means of influencing its content, or amendments, or
likelihood of passage.  Nor do I have any influence in the selection
of law makers who sit in the assembly and vote on it.  Yet I must obey
the law if they pass it.  Consequently, I have no freedom in regard to
that law, but rather (it follows) I am in bondage to it.

My questions concern the case of an [initiative] system as it operates
in California, Switzerland and other direct democracies (as you say):

  1. When an [initiative] is prepared and presented, what effective
     means do I have of influencing its content, or amendments, or
     likelihood of passage?

  2. How do these means differ in effect from those provided in the
     case of ordinary legislation?

  3. How therefore does the initiative offer me an opportunity of
     political freedom, as opposed to a threat (as I might take it) of
     oppression?
