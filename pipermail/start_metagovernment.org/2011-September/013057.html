> Not at all, my mistake.  You were referring to direct democracy in

> I restate my questions (I hope) in correct form:

The initiative is a law proposal on citizens initiative and the referendum 
is a submission to vote on a law that is proposed by the government.
About the "50% majority" , also this is no "natural law". The Swiss 
developed the "double majority" system for federal referendum. (50 % of all 
swiss + 50% in half of the cantons, that way inserting some kind of 
geografic consent also)

>
> I'm not familiar with [initiative] systems, since we don't use them
> here.  I know they are meant to provide a means to participate in a
> legislative process that is ordinarily off limits to me as a citizen.
> So, for instance, when a conventional bill is prepared in committee, I
> have no effective means of influencing its content, or amendments, or
> likelihood of passage.  Nor do I have any influence in the selection
> of law makers who sit in the assembly and vote on it.  Yet I must obey
> the law if they pass it.  Consequently, I have no freedom in regard to
> that law, but rather (it follows) I am in bondage to it.
>
> My questions concern the case of an [initiative] system as it operates
> in California, Switzerland and other direct democracies (as you say):
>
>  1. When an [initiative] is prepared and presented, what effective
>     means do I have of influencing its content, or amendments, or
>     likelihood of passage?

An itinitiative is prepared by a citizen, a group of citizens or an existing 
organisation. They can launch a call to help to prepare the initiative. The 
more help , debate and critisism while developing an initiative the more 
chanche that it will appeal to a great number of people and that most 
critics are already met.
That is why we hope to incorporate this developing stadium in our website 
system.
>
>  2. How do these means differ in effect from those provided in the
>     case of ordinary legislation?

It is a citizens initiative and the citizens decide how they develop the 
initiative. They have only to gain if they prepare it well with the broadest 
consensus possible because once launched it can't be altered anymore. 
Therfore surveys must give the proposal a great majority (70% for example) 
before it starts because opposition has to start yet. If you start with 70% 
without organised opposition it is maybe possible to get the 50% needed.
Ordinary legislation need only a very small majority to agree (some tens of 
people?)

>
>  3. How therefore does the initiative offer me an opportunity of
>     political freedom, as opposed to a threat (as I might take it) of
>     oppression?

It is a continuous development to a more acceptable society. The question is 
"what is better if I compare system A (representative democracy) with system 
B (direct democracy)". Is system B better, fine I accept it with the freedom 
of the possibility of developping a system C. Even that is only possible in 
system C in our case.
