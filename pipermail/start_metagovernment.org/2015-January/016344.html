>     > Main problem with proxies is that it's empowering certain

>
>     I think the difference is the context: The main problem with
>     representative government isn't representation per se, it's that the
>     type of people who *want* to be representatives are generally
>     inherently unfit to do so (because almost all of them are SDAPs).  So
>     we don't need to eliminate representation from our repertoire, we just
>     need to find a way to ensure that those representatives are chosen by
>     true merit and representativeness rather than because they crave power
>     and know how to game the system to get it.
>
>     > Proxies make people more susceptible to
>     > corruption, they become the obvious target for bribery if they
>     have a large
>     > following. I see this as dangerous and think that individuals
>     should always
>     > be held accountable for their own votes, never passing the
>     responsibility
>     > off to someone else. While this might lesson the overall votes
>     cast it
>     > ensures that the votes are truly theirs.
>
>     But by imposing this burden you're right back at the SDAP problem:
>     Most people are just too busy or are uninterested in the details to
>     cast an informed vote on most issues, so you end up with government by
>     the self-selected few.  I actually don't have any data to back up my
>     claim that this self-selected sample is highly correlated with SDAP
>     characteristics (e.g., no one's done a study I can find on personality
>     characteristics of voters vs. non-voters), but I do know that it is
>     correlated with income and that income is highly correlated with SDAP
>     characteristics.
>
>     Don't know if you've read the "Matchism" upgrade of "The Plan", but it
>     includes a couple of additional features that address this. The first
>     is a two-phase voting process, where the closing date for proxies is
>     the day before the final tally and everyone who's designated a proxy
>     gets an email that says "here's the way you're going to vote unless
>     you go in and manually change it".  This seems to me solve the "stand
>     behind your vote" legitimacy problem, and has the additional benefit
>     that that email will also contain a running tally of the votes up to
>     that point so that these semi-passive voters will get a clue that it
>     is a tight decision and that it's probably worth their time to do a
>     little more research to make an informed vote.  The second features is
>     a weighted list of proxies, which will distribute the load and so make
>     it harder to game the system by bribing/influencing a few key people.
>
>     I'm currently working on the design for selecting proxies. Certainly
>     people will be able to pick them directly from posts they've made or
>     by comparing a potential proxy's voting record with their own, but I'm
>     also considering running a mini-Big Five personality test and matching
>     based on that.  I'd use something like the survey on
>     http://www.politicalcompass.org/ (which is interesting and I highly
>     recommend exploring that site), but don't want someone picking a proxy
>     using a rule like "he's a conservative and I'm a conservative too"
>     because "representativeness" is a little more nuanced than that.
>
>     The current version is up now at matchism.org
>     <http://matchism.org>, and the mailing lists
>     are even (finally) working and I invite people to join them.  But
>     unfortunately I'm going back to Taiwan on Wednesday for 3 weeks and
>     won't be able to fully participate until I get back...
>
>     > Priority listings as I understand it. The general public
>     proposes goals that
>     > they would like to see accomplished and rank them in order of
>     importance for
>     > them personally. Can anyone submit a goal? How are they
>     published if you get
>     > 100,000 asking for things. Then the managers, that the public
>     has previously
>     > hired, come up with solutions to those requests of how to
>     achieve best
>     > results. Then the public choose which plan they like best by
>     prioritizing
>     > again for each goal.
>
>     Right: I'm not exactly sure how the winnowing-down process works, but
>     certainly everyone needs to be able to add a goal.  This gets back to
>     the OP, the problem being that in that system it's all one phase
>     (establishing the list and prioritizing it are done at the same time).
>     My sense is that the refining process will be less important because
>     it will be done repeatedly and eventually a good set of goals will
>     eventually just emerge regardless of who does the refining or how it's
>     done.  At least at first, though, I'm thinking the Moderators will do
>     it.
>
>     > I'm curious where the discussion aspect comes in? Is there any
>     way for the
>     > public to alter a managers solution, tweak it slightly, or call
>     them out on
>     > some oversight? If so how does that process work?
>
>     It is definitely a two phase process, the first phase being
>     review//comment/questioning of the proposal which will be done by the
>     people who are most interested in the area.  The second is a public
>     vote that will *use* that record of commentary to help them make their
>     decision.
>
>     > Also what happens if two
>     > solutions to the same problem are presented and gain equal
>     votes, which one
>     > do we go with?
>
>     I'm not sure yet how to deal with this "forking" of proposals.
>     Certainly we don't want to pass more than one per area up to the
>     voting phase, but how the best one gets picked and indeed how any of
>     them are flagged as being ready to pass up to the voting phase I'm not
>     as clear on yet.  Some of these things I'm sure we won't be able to
>     firm up completely until I get an actual prototype up and we can start
>     trying things.
>
>     > Seems like you advocate a select group determining solutions
>     > and then letting the public vote on which ever plan they think
>     is best. I
>     > feel this gives more power to the chosen mangers to draft tricky
>     solutions
>     > that include or omit select facts that could help direct a
>     particular agenda
>     > which the public might not be able to interfere with
>     appropriately. I
>     > associate this to the bills that get passed in our current
>     government that
>     > are compromises, the bill might solve the initial problem but
>     include
>     > stipulations that we have to go along with if we want the
>     initial problem
>     > solved. How do you intend to counter that?
>
>     Smart commentator!  I don't know if you read Slashdot or Reddit but
>     I'm regularly amazed at how insightful and persuasive some of the
>     comments are.  My hunch and hope is that if a proposer (who, yes, will
>     usually be the Managers) tries to put something tricky in a proposal
>     that it will get caught and that it will never even make it out of the
>     evaluation phase.  And if it does, that a highly rated critical
>     comment or two will cause it to go down in flames at the final vote.
>
>     My fear is actually the opposite: Compromise is a necessary component
>     of governing and we may find that *nothing* gets passed because the
>     criticisms will be so much better than the proposals themselves.
>     Certainly what does pass will be vastly better that what we have for
>     laws/regulations/policies now: I don't know if you've read your state
>     statutes or municipal codes, but at least here (in Colorado) they're
>     really bad (poorly and confusingly written (by lawyers), with logical
>     flaws you could drive a bus through and lots of ambiguity because
>     they're built on common law with all of its assumptions).
>
>     > I'm much more pro public involvement at all levels to ensure no
>     punches are
>     > pulled by people who are setting up the solutions. I also think
>     there are

>     like to fully
>     > understand your vision first though Scott if you would indulge
>     me. Cheers
>
>     I'm all for the *ability* for the public to participate until the
