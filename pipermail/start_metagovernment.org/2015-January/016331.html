Scott,

I have made some notes to you paragraphs. Still I believe that the main 
importance of AllOurIdeas is that it may be applied in current political 
environment to change it gradually. In other words I do not see the 
usual unresolved loop of conditions and results as in other methods. 
That makes it better IMHO.

On 01/05/2015 05:59 PM, Scott Raney wrote:
> As I've expressed before, I'm not a big fan of wikis.  Not sure if

> tails" reported in the paper.  The problem of course being that those
> "overachievers" sometimes are ideologues (who are often just idiots!)
> and sometimes have undisclosed conflicts of interest.
Not in all cases, why would you write your book then?
  I believe that the "fat heads" (or tails) make their opinions in their 
allourideas matrix more precise. From what you say I have the notion, 
that you think that they have more votes, is it so ?
Most of us live with undisclosed conflicts IMHO, there is a talk by 
Chomsky about it.
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JwdGHCqb03g
>
> I think Figure 7 is where the rubber hits the road in this paper, and
> I think it shows that while there were a handful of ideas the public
> came up with that weren't on the "seed" list, the seed stuff on
> average was of higher quality.  Seems to me the easier solution is
> therefore to have better brainstorming up front (i.e., better "seeds",
> perhaps from pilot studies) and provide an easy way for the public to
> just rank them rather than turning the public loose on a wiki.
> Participation would therefore end up being much higher overall, and
> you'd end up with a much more balanced picture of what The People
> really want.
If you can do better brainstorming. There might be limits. Small group 
might be unable to reach the variability of public.

> Thanks for posting this as it's actually something I've been thinking
> a lot about as part of how to organize the "Goals" section of my
> system.  The way I see it there are actually two phases in the
> governing process, the first being this sort of "collect and
> prioritize everything" and the second being "write and approve the
> actual policies/rules/regulations/laws".  My conclusion is that The
> People are really only useful for the second halves of each of those
> (they set the priorities and approve the laws) and that there needs to
> be a dedicated group (in my system the Moderators and the Managers)
> who do the collating of the data and actual writing of the proposals.
> Otherwise you end with a huge amount of noise that only the
> overachievers will be willing to deal with.  You can see examples of
> this in that paper where most of the citizen proposals (and almost all
> of the "education" proposals) are fluff with few if any actionable
> items.  Allowing people to propose goals (or vote for people who state
> they share goals) like "lower taxes" or "more money for education" is
> just a bad way to organize a government.  Instead they should have be
> specific, as in "taxes should be 28% of GDP" and "Education should get
> 12% of revenue".
The problem of noise is not serious in allourideas. More over it can be 
reduced in the future. E.g. You may easily drop some last options after 
some time.
