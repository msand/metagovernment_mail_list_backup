Michael Pilling wrote:
> I would be in favor of allowing any and all methods of user registration by
> distributed groups, so long as there is a relatively easy way to index what
> votes are from what group and the quality of that registration method. which
> pretty obviously is one of three levels
> 
> 1. email only verification
> 2. CC verification (paying membership dues)
> 3. photoID / sponsor (probably includes 2)
 
This reminds me: some idea on post-count filtering of the results were
posted a few months ago.  Basically the viewer decides which
authentication method/level is sufficient for her purposes, and the
votes are filtered and re-counted for her (effectively).
 
> One observation observation made by a friend of mine is that the thing our
> democracy sorely lacks is a level between our elected representatives and
> individual voters. There is a breakdown in accountability between the person
> who (supposedly) represents 100,000 and the single voter.  So hopefully
> e-democracy is something that is amenable to and easily adopted by those
> local groups, residents associations, tenants associations, churches, etc.
> If you have registration at this group level it reinforces the local nodes
> and networks and local leaders of those nodes.

If I understand, this would mix voter representation (group) with that
of voter authentication (same group)?

> the best administrative backbone is to have these groups running an
> established open source groupware (shall we list them in the wiki). devs can
> get those installs to talk to each other in the ways that we need.

This might not be sufficient for a fully authenticated (single vote,
no sock puppets, no bots) decision system.  From a technical point of
view, we must tackle the authentication problem head on (at level 3).
Otherwise, if the votes/decisions can be fabricated, there is no point
in developing the system.  What would it prove?
