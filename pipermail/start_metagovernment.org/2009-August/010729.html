On Aug 31, 2009, at 11:01 AM, Michael Pilling wrote:

> the assumption here is that there is no IT solution for sock  
> puppets. Creating sock puppets is essentially what modern partisan  
> politics and modern marketing do highly effectively (many people  
> distributing same message) - can't prevent or detect that. I also  
> suspect that lacking a huge elections bureacracy (like the one that  
> exists now to authenticate voters) - multiple voting and userids per  
> person is not preventable.  IT should focus solely on bots and  
> preserving privacy, right?
>
> given those two huge "security holes" group affiliation is the  
> fallback, and since groups have it in their self interest to have a  
> reputation, the groups can authentiucate for themselves what the  
> system cannot. "anonynous" or groupless votes can be accepted of  
> course but are near-to-worthless in a decision making process.

This is similar to the technique Manuel and Pietro had come up with a  
while ago: that the software can evaluate a user's cloud of influence:  
ie, which users are rating that user and vice-versa. If it is found  
that, for example, a whole group of "users" are giving each other high  
rankings, but almost nobody outside that group is rating any of those  
users, then the software can collapse them all into one person.

Of course, in larger-scaled instances, this gets more complicated as  
one might have many different people using sock puppets with each  
other, but the basic idea should still convey.
