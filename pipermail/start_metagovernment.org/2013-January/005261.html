> Hi Zohar,
>
> I would like to add something to all that have been said.
>
> In my experience it is unrealistic to expect the same platform to work
> equally well for 7, 70, 700, 7000, 70.000, 700.000, 7.000.000, 70.000.000,
> 700.000.000, 7.000.000.000 people. And I listed explicitly all the scale to
> make a point on how different they are.
>
> You go from a group of friends to the whole of humanity. It's not just a
> matter of numbers, the interaction changes.
>
> The TYPE of interactions change, what can you do with them changes as well.
> In Vilfredo we analyse the vote of every person, and compare it with the
> vote of every other person, so we join together people that vote in the
> same way. We also use the way a person vote to decide who should be asked
> to rewrite a proposal. This kind of analysis would hardly be possible for
> groups that are too big (and I leave undefined what does "big" mean in this
> context). If you have 70 people you can probably let people write what the
> options are. With 700 or more you must add a filter to avoid two people
> writing the same idea. But people can still rewrite ideas. And so one as
> groups become bigger the kind of interaction that the site will permit will
> either become simpler. Or less universal. What do I mean by less universal?
> In a small group, the site can analyse all the participants vote, and
> organise groups based on this. If you did this on a site the size of
> facebook (maybe based on the "like" vote) you probably would find emerging
> groups to be too big to handle. Then you are forced (as FB does) to simply
> extend the people you know based on your choice. And suddenly the site is
> not universal. Two people can be nearer or farther apart. Their interaction
> is not im-mediate anymore. It is mediate-d. Mediated by all the people that
> are in between them.
>
>
> On the other hand if you have 7 billion people (the whole of humanity), you
> probably can easily make a system where everybody can vote. But the issues
> will be very simple. Cultural issues start to raise where people with
> different cultures, different educations will have gross difficulties to
> interact and participate in the same network. And surely will not
> participate with the same power.
>
> Now if you take a system that can handle 7 billion people and you use it
> for 7 people you will get an extremely boring and practically useless
> system. You will not use all the magic that can instead be used.
>
> This regarding the size.
>
> (Ed, maybe on the list of projects we should add a column that clarifies
> the size of the group it addresses.
>
> Then there is a matter regarding what kind of questions can be asked. Some
> questions are different than others. Some questions require a single
> solution, some other can be solved by multiple solutions. What shall we do
> to fix the economy can have multiple solutions. How shall we build this car
> looks for a single solution (or several, but mutually exclusive).
>
> Another issue has to do with the complexity of the answers. The more
> complex the answers are the more technical the people need to be. The
> smaller the group will be. The more you will need to permit to people to
> rewrite proposals (with all the mathematical difficulties involved to make
> this fair).
>
> Systems that can handle simple questions, with multiple answers, that
> cannot be rewritten. That we already have, they call them petitions. And
> they could already handle 7 billion of people. The White House uses one.
> The UK another, and so on.
>
> If you want to let people rewrite proposals you are facing a tough problem.
> Because what shall you do with all the votes that have been already given
> to the previous versions of the proposal? You can ignore them, move them
> over, make the graph of the versions of a proposals, and just keep them for
> the earlier branch, ask everybody to vote again, poll the users and if
> enough people like the new version, move everybody to it.
>
> There is no "right" answer. Each of those approach has some positive and
> some negative elements. And most of those approaches has been used for some
> projects. Of course this is also intimately tied with the size. You can ask
> 7 people to vote again. You cannot do this for 7 billion. And this is why
> the more a project addresses bigger groups, the more it become
> mathematically unfair. Fairness has a cost and the bigger a group the more
> will it cost to keep it.
>
> My main projects are Vilfredo (which addresses 5-15 people. We are moving
> to 25) and Bubbling around (adresses thousands of people but with no
> rewriting).
>
> Other projects you should consider are Votorola, Mixed Ink, White House 2
> (aka nationbuilder, the one used by Iceland now), and LiquidFeedback.
>
> Each of those projects, yes also mine, are good in some sense and weak in
> others. And it is often an inherent unavoidable weakness.
>
> Writing the "perfect" system is an illusion. But you can write a system
> that is perfect for a specific situation. And then when we have a series of
> systems, each for a very clear, different, situation we can chose which one
> to use depending on the situation we face.
>
> Cheers,
> Pietro

> Hi Zohar,
> I wrote a long message explaining how some requirements contraddicts
> others.
>
> Let me make here an example:
>
> You ask for Anonimity AND Schultz voting.
>
> But Shultz voting is not anonymous. Shultz voting can never be fully
> anonymous. To use Shultz voting users must present an ordered list of all
> the options. Ordering them from the one they like more to the one they like
> least. This kind of vote, the vote itself, will act as a signature.
>
> Suppose you strike a deal with someone that you will vote for him in
> exchange for some favours. Then that person will tell you how you should
> vote (the exact sequence). Then he checks in the voting ballot if someone
> voted in the exact way he asked. And if that pattern is missing he knows
> you did not follow your end of the deal.
>
> The Mafia used to do something like this in the south of Italy. Usually the
> deal was vote in exchange for work. Before them the fascist party would do
> something similar. Unfortunately the only anonymous type of voting are
> usually the simpler ones. The one that are more subject to strategic
> voting. I think you should read some good introductory texts on Voting
> theory.
>
> Regards,
> Pietro
