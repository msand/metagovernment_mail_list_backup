I'm watching old propaganda films from WWII.  Here's one that fits
Max's volunteer story.  It also ends with Lincoln's "by the people,
for the people".  It's part of a deliberate effort at myth making .

  http://www.nfb.ca/film/call_for_volunteers  (1941)

I've watched about 100 of these wartime films.  I wasn't too surprised
at first to notice a socialist tilt; Canada and other countries were
being pulled to the left by a combination of the depression and the
war effort, as I knew.  But I soon noticed more than just a tilt here,
and I couldn't explain it.  These films from the 1940s were painting a
picture of a society more caring than the one I grew up with in the
1970s.  I felt recharged watching them.

Later I learned about the film pioneer who ran the propaganda effort
during the war, a Scotsman by the name of John Grierson.  Once you get
past the hammed-up intro by Lorne Greene, this is a fascinating film:

  https://www.nfb.ca/film/grierson  (1973)

So I figured Grierson must be part of the answer.  But only yesterday
did I realize he's the *whole* answer.  I was re-watching some of the
wartime films and thinking about myth making, when it dawned on me:
this Scotsman created the myth of Canada that I live by today, and he
did it deliberately.

  https://www.nfb.ca/film/shameless_propaganda  (2013)

I just found the film above, which confirms the theory.  But it was a
wonderful experience discovering it on my own.  I watched over 1,000
NFB films from 1939 to 2010.  Only slowly did the message of a caring
society fade from the picture (I now recall), to be replaced by that
of the critical film maker, objective and alienated.  The message was
still there on the day I was born, when this film was shot:

  https://www.nfb.ca/film/september_five_at_saint-henri  (1962)

(You should be able to stream these films for free.  Please let me
know if they charge you money, or anything.)

Mike
