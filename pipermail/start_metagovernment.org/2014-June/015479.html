> Bentley:
> every generation some proposals of the previous generation go to the next
> generation. From a mathematical point of view, those are the Pareto Front.
> The proposal that are not-dominated by any other proposal.
>
> In our case only 4 proposals were in the Pareto Front. We then wrote 21
> (twentyone!!!) new proposals :-), and so one we have to evaluate each of
>  those 21+4=25 proposals.
>
> I have seen that someone (Ned, I gather) have started writing proposals
> expressing parts of the homepage. Unfortunately Vilfredo does not work in
> this way. Basically the domination system that makes sure that one proposal
> dominates another under certain conditions makes only sense if the two
> proposals are trying to answer the same question. Semantically speaking. So
> you can substitute one proposal for the other. When this is not the case,
> the result is much less meaningful. You might end up finding a full
> consensus over a small part of what you are working on. This is the reason
> why I explained at the beginning that Vilfredo works well for "how shall
> we" kind of question. In other words question where we are looking for a
> single answer (maybe complex). But not a list of elements more or less
> independently structure. If we want to use Vilfredo in this way, we should
> open a separate question for each element. For example if we agree to have
> a specific structure made of "mission, vision, projects, ... " then for
> each of them we can ask: "what is the mission of Vilfredo to insert in the
> homepage?". (which is not a how, but it is still a question where you are
> looking for a single winning proposal).
>
> Scott:
> There were some suggestions from you (Scott), to evaluate the proposals
> with a 10 star system. It is interesting, but first you would need to
> understand the way Vilfredo works. What you are suggesting is something
> similar to Range Voting. A slightly better version of that would be
> Majority Judgment. But regardless, Vilfredo uses a completely different
> method. We DO NOT sum the votes. Instead we declare that a proposal A
> dominates a proposal B iff (if and only if) the set of people that vote for
> B is a subset of the people that vote for A. So it is not just that A has
> more support, but it is also supported by the same people. So each person
> that like B will also be happy if we pass A. Then the Pareto Front is the
> set of the non-dominated proposals.
> In this way you can be sure that whoever votes in Vilfredo, will find at
> least one of the proposals he voted for in the Pareto Front. Because if you
> voted for B, and A dominates B, so that A is in the Pareto Front. Then you
> MUST have also voted for A, so you are still represented in the Pareto
> Front. For this reason you can say that the Pareto Front represents the
> community :-).
> The next version of Vilfredo will use a mixed system. People will vote on
> an inverted triangle to say how much they like and they understand a
> proposal. Then a first approximation will be used to find the Pareto Front.
> And in situations where several proposals are competing (especially if no
> one is presenting new ideas) the actual position in the triangle will be
> used. It is much more complex, which is why its development is so slow.
>
>
>
> Finally consider looking at the Pareto Front, looking at how you vote, and
> how by changing your vote you can slightly change the Pareto Front. We are
> releasing this information (the graph, how other people votes) specifically
> so that people can change their vote and look for a specific consensus.
>
