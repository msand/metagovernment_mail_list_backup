>> This has been a sticking point for me for an incredibly long time. If you
> go back to the first discussions we had about Vilfredo at the beginning,
> also there there was a request for the possibility to comment to proposals.

> Still it was important for me that the discussion be integrated inside the
> Vilfredo process.
>
> Vilfredo works basically by:
> let people propose
> let people vote
> filter the proposal
> use the filtered proposals as a feedback mechanism to the community
> let people propose
> ...
>
> If people can discuss proposal outside, there is the risk that this might
> take away some of the power of the filter. Which is the only thing that
> prevents the participant to drift around (like in a normal discussion
> group, where the person that writes more has more power), but be focused in
> a fair way to all participants.
>
>
> The solution to this eventually was to have the comments integrated with
> the voting. In this version, when you vote against, or that you do not
> understand, you explain your perplexities.
>
> In the next version, which is actually present, but still full of bugs
> participants can make points pro and against each proposal. As well as ask
> questions (to which the author can answer).

>>  Also, why does the *big (green) thumb* show so dominant even before I
>> vote. Seems like it should only be there after voting???
>>
>
> This is another very important element. In the first version of Vilfredo,
> each proposal was an empty tick box, and you could tick it to vote for it.
> So the default option was "against" (there was no ? at the beginning). This
> had a big fault on the fact that people would vote in a traditional way,
> where they would just tick the box of their proposal (or of one proposal)
> and submit. They were expecting the vote to be counted by being summed. But
> the votes in Vilfredo are MUCH more powerful. Basically every against you
> vote is a veto you give. No amount of me saying: "vote for all that you can
> accept" was enough. The implicit education of years of plurality voting
> would make people just ignore it. So the system would not reach a small
> Pareto Front but an unnecessary big one.
>
> For this reason we turned the voting around. The default of YES. In Italy
> we say: Silence is equivalent of assent (chi tace acconsente). And voting
> no has a cost. You must say why you are against a proposal. Indeed this
> raises the work necessary for people who participate, and makes consensus
> easier to reach. Not a bad deal.
>
> In Vilfredo2.0 the people who do not understand a proposal will be treated
> differently than now. As a result, people who did not read a proposal will
> be considered as a "?" and not as a "YES".
>
> But for now the default is YES.
