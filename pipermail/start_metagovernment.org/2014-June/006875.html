> In brief, "Economic Direct Democracy" is a book-length proposal for transforming local economies into sustainable, democratic systems. In it, I describe a novel local economic framework that represents a synthesis of approaches already in use in some cities around the world. The framework builds on ideas from buy-local, invest-local, local-currency, local-food, local-sharing, open-source, open-government, open-data, participatory democracy, and related community development, knowledge transfer, and decision-making initiatives.  Results from a computer simulation are presented. Topics in the book span a wide range of disciplines.

>>> I appreciate that you mentioned metagovernment.org in the book, but I would like to point out that we don't really fit with those other organizations. We don't seek to get citizens more involved in government: we seek government of the citizens. In a democracy, any division between citizens and government is a betrayal of the core of democracy. We (or at least many of the people on this list) don't think the citizens should have a larger voice in the ears of their leaders, but rather that either a. the leaders should be completely beholden to the wishes of the people or b. that there should be no empowered leaders at all.
>>> 
>>> What we advocate is not like traditional direct democracy, which is very susceptible to mob rule and demagoguery, but rather new forms of governance allowed by the advent of collaborative technologies. I invite you to explore our brief overview here:
