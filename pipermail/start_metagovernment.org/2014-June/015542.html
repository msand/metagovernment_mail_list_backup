I might offer to help, Craig, if it were pitched more to the electors
and candidates.  I bet that's the only approach that has legs for the
long haul, and I'd like to make a career of it.  Parties etc. have
money, but surely there are other sources?

> The big gamble for Sygnol is to attract an audience. If just a small

Definitely!  Everyone will be attentive.  It'll be big news.  I'd sure
like to be part of it when it takes off.

> ...especially during a fractured multicandidate election in a
> relatively small state where 100 vote margins could be make or break
> for a candidate's viability going into the next primary or caucus.
> I haven't said much yet about my approach for convening useful

I think electors and candidates are naturally on the same side, and
both against the party machines.  I bet the candidates would love to
become independent, if only they could.  Straw polls (when designed
properly) would give them that independence.

I don't think it requires discussion facilities.  There's enough out
there already, and no need for tight integration.  A voting service is
pretty much all that's needed.  It can be deployed stand-alone.

> The earlier projects allowed for resubmission of ranks until the
> official close of voting, and the planned systems will as well. I
> presume that's what you mean by continuous.

Sure, changeable ballots.  But then there's probably no need for
ranked choice or other complicated methods.  A changeable ballot
basically means a continuous runoff.  The best method is arguably the
simplest: plurality.

I suggested that in the thread below.  It's a list of voting reform
lobbyists, though, so naturally they're unreceptive to the idea.
