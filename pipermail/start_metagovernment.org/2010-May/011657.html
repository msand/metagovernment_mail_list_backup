Yes, my reply was further below, regarding the difference between an issue,
and the public's perception of an issue.

I have assumed what the public is willing to accept, as of today, on the
grounds that private voting is what has been practiced over the years in
election processes around the world. So my belief that public voting would
be, at the moment, a huge cultural change, seems quite reasonable.

No, I have not conducted a scientific experiment, poll or otherwise, to
arrive at this assumption, nor do I think this is practical or necessary for
our purposes. Ofcourse, the party may change its mind, although this is
unlikely. If the idea of public voting gains ground and becomes the
prevalent political culture, then that would be a different scenario, but I
do not see this happening in the near future.

Furthermore, more than 20 years of research into the area of secure (in the
literature, this term usually implies and includes _privacy_) voting systems
strongly suggests that there are powerful sociological reasons to prefer
private systems, reasons that justify this historical and ongoing research.
>From my limited experience, nobody in academia considers public voting
systems seriously for elections, which is our target functionality.

But let me restate, I am not an expert in political theory, or sociology, so
I am not qualified to argue this. I am focusing on researching and
engineering a solution that meets the criteria of a secure voting system as
defined in the literature, which I think is necessary to present a credible
alternative to the general public. I do not see our party gaining
credibility if we present a public voting system, so we'll just have to
agree to disagree, as of today.

Although I am ofcourse interested in why you think we "will fail"
