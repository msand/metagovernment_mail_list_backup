On 30 May 2010 16:11, Ed Pastore <epastore at metagovernment.org> wrote:

> On May 30, 2010, at 7:59 AM, David Ruescas wrote:
>
>  Public voting would be, at the moment, a huge cultural change that the
>> voting population is not willing to accept.
>>
>
> It depends on the scale of implementation. In clubs, for example, votes are
> held by a show of hands. At board meetings, directors vote "on the record,"
> and in legislative bodies, votes are usually recorded for each member.
>

The scale of implementation that we have in mind is that for which people
expect privacy (ie elections), hence my comment re cultural change.


>
> I think the reason elections have traditionally been held with anonymous
> votes has been to protect the people who lose the election from being
> persecuted by the winners.


And matters such as coercion and vote buying.


> However, if the voting is on policy instead of for candidates, this
> possibility doesn't really exist. Even in liquid democracy systems, it is
> not very plausible to imagine that proxy representatives could lash out at
> people who voted the opposite of their position: they won't be empowered
> with means to do anything... will they?
>
> In the Web 2.0 age, the desire for privacy is most likely centered around
> reputation and peer pressure. People may not be inclined to vote for their
> beliefs if they don't want others to know they think that way. It is then a
> matter of personal philosophy as to whether this is a good or bad thing.
> Some would say that people should have the anonymous cloak to enable them to
> vote free of outside pressure. Others would say that people should be
> required to stand by their vote: if they are embarrassed to vote a
> particular way, then they have to resolve that internal conflict themselves,
> not ask that the voting system enable it.
