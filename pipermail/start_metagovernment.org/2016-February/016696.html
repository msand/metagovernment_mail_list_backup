> > Does Amartya Sen have anything interesting to
> > say about the matter of how to decide?
>
> I've run down pretty much all the major philosophers (political and
> otherwise), including Sen, and have come to the conclusion that they
> don't really have much to offer in this debate because their proposals
> all fail to address two major points:
>
> 1) Philosophers as a whole are obsessed with what is "good" and/or
> "virtuous" but seem blind to the fact that even the definitions of
> these things depends entirely on context: What is "virtuous" in an era
> of plentiful resources and stable social and political systems may be
> unworkable or even actually be harmful in an era of scarce resources
> (war, famine, zombie apocalypse, alien invasion, etc. ;-) In
> particular, none of them I've found have have incorporated the process
> of progress in technology or scientific knowledge into their systems.
> A good example of this being the scientific determination that
> homosexuality is an anatomical/physiological phenomenon and not a
> matter of social or personal choice: From a social engineering
> perspective (which IMHO is the only one that can be reliably
> distinguished from the mental masturbation that is characteristic of
> most philosophy) whether it's acceptable to discriminate against gays
> depends not on some fuzzy feeling about whether it's "good" or
> "virtuous", but merely on whether doing so has any beneficial effect
> on individuals or society as a whole.
>
> 2) Philosophers, like politicians, are essentially proposing to do
> social engineering without having any particular knowledge of
> psychology or engineering. This is just malpractice IMHO. We ought not
> to listen to anyone's proposals in these areas unless they include at
> least some claim about how their proposals will affect individuals
> (particularly their level of stress) and how stable they would be
> given human social behavior. Marx I think is the best example here of
> what happens when you dabble in social engineering but only have a
> philosophical perspective on the issues.
>
Not persuasive, but I will think about the stress indicator.
