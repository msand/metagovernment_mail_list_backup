I've added this and additional organizations to:
http://www.metagovernment.org/wiki/Related_projects
I'd encourage others to help expand this list. And to continue  
contacting them as you see fit.

I haven't come up with many ways we can work with these national  
projects. They seem to me to be more of a transitional step, being  
what Demoex calls mixdemocracy: direct democracy through the  
mechanisms of representative democracy.

Although we may not be able to use their software or work directly  
with them, we certainly should keep an eye on what they are learning  
about consensus-building. The Australian and Swedish projects, in  
particular, seem to be getting rather advanced.

> Although I dislike that all these projects are dividing our collective
> focus, I am very encouraged that this movement is clearly gaining
> traction. It looks like we are creating a competitive marketplace of
> ideas, and it will be up to the internet to decide which of these
> ideas will gain prominence.
>
> So far, each of the other organizations we have encountered is nation-
> centric and works as a supplement to the status quo. I believe the
> Metagovernment project remains distinguished on these points: we are
> entirely global (and entirely local in our initial implementations),
> and we are an outright replacement for representative democracies, not
> a supplement. I believe that our plan of running Metascore first in
> tiny, barely-formal communities and then letting it grow organically
> is a great plan for success.
>
> But I think many of us also want to see these other types of
> organizations succeed. As long as we have representative democracies,
> at least they could have some Web 2.0 input, instead of still relying
> on the "smoke-filled rooms" of yore.
>
> In any event, we should continue to try to find synergies with these
> other groups, and continue to look for ways we can combine our  
> efforts.
