Thanks for pointing that out, Robert. When you put it that way, I also  
was concerned that I was being overly confident. It is certainly worth  
noting so that we can try to avoid that arrogance.

But I still do think that the future is going to be very, very  
different from the past. Sometimes technologies can make radical  
changes in society, particularly communications technologies. The  
telegraph and then the telephone made major changes to the world,  
creating instant links over vast distances. The internet and now Web  
2.0 technologies are making even greater changes. The fact that this  
group, scattered over a dozen or more countries, can even exist shows  
that there is something fundamentally different in the world. (And of  
course, there are larger, completely global communities, such as  
Wikipedia or Debian.) Communities are no longer bounded by geography,  
and one way or another governmental structures will have to start  
adapting to that reality.

The amazing thing about Web 2.0 is that it not only connects people,  
but it actively creates communities. It promotes collaboration and  
idea-sharing... it makes for structures which are greater than the sum  
of their parts. To compare that to old-school attempts at direct  
democracy is simply not valid. We do not know how Metascore will  
mutate when it is released into the wild, but we do know that, if we  
make it flexible enough, it has the potential to make something much,  
much more productive than a bunch of individuals screaming about their  
own personal problems.
