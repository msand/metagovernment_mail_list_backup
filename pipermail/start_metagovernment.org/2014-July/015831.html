Where I live in Ashland Oregon we have a very successful food co-op with an elected Board of Directors. (Sales for 2013 were well over $28 million.) The Board of Directors uses consensus with a  clause in their bi-laws which permits them to resort to a 3/4 majority *emergency* vote should they not be able to reach consensus on a timely and critical matter. I spoke with the president of the Board several months ago and she said that (up to that date) the BOD had not found it necessary to resort to a 3/4 majority emergency vote.   We recently had our 40th anniversary.

Even though I believe this to be exemplary, I am not suggesting in sharing this that I’m a proponent of the consensus model for every situation. I think the success of any decision making process depends solely on the level of *coherence* a group can rise to in order to achieve a productive working agreement. The greater the polarity within any given group, the less likely they are to find the requisite unity (or coherence) for consensus. And as I see it, without the possibility of consensus, democracy becomes *political*, and some form of majority voting is then employed (just to get the job done) so the group can move on.

In my mind, the preponderant question is: *What creates coherence?*

The Ashland Food Coop has a very clear vision/mission that’s passionately shared by it's general membership. There is a very high level of unified orientation and I would imagine decision making for the BOD is relatively simple, quick, and easy. 

It seems to me that with [MG], (as with many organizations), our level of coherence is relatively low. Any shared vision we may have is vague, dissembled, and amorphous. It’s striking to me that after so many years we are still trying to create a home page that resonates with even a minority of those on the list. I wonder if it’s necessary, (or even possible), for [MG] to have a mission other than, very simply, “to provide a forum to discuss, challenge, and debate various systems and practices of (e)democracy”. 

The fact that we cannot even agree on how we are going to agree is remarkable to me. I’m beginning to conclude that this list just isn't the venue for that. 

I wonder if maybe our (implicit) agreement is to continually (and relentlessly) *disagree*…..and in so doing, to force the most fundamental or primary elements of some *as yet unknown and emergent* democracy towards the surface of our contemporary human governance.  

I think I could be ok with that. However disjointed and dysfunctional our deliberations appear to be…..it seems to be working on some deeper level for me.  ;-)
