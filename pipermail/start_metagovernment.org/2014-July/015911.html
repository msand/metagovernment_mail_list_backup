 Scott Raney <scott at metacard.com> wrote:

> So if I have a majority for my position I can game
> the system by accumulating the other (roughly) 16% by adding shills?

Yes, I suppose you could. The only alternative I see to would be to go with an unmodified consensus model.
In a small group I’m not sure how one would *split the difference* between the two.

> I'd recommend leaving any mention of specific technology out of this
> document. 

Right, I’m just in the initial draft phase of things. 
I put in the Loomio reference as suggestive of where we will likely begin.

> I'm still thinking two-phase for this process: The proposal gets rated
> and approved by a special interest group during which the proposer can
> change it at any time (sparing making the determination of
> "substantially altered" and consequent squabbling about whether a
> proposal is valid or not, and the issue of quorum).  Once that group
> somehow decides it's ready (and I would leave that to the group and
> primarily the original proposer), they submit it to a vote of the full
> membership during which it can't be altered.

My sense is that proposal development is ideally a collaborative process….kinda like we’re doing here.  (Yeah, I know you don’t like that word :)
Maybe when the group increases in size, though it’s difficult to imagine ever turning that determination over to a "special interest group”.

> I still like my "25% yes votes" quorum rule.  

I don’t understand. How does that work?

> With your proposed policy, I predict people trying to game the system by holding a vote
> between Christmas and New Year's or during a power outage (or
> whatever), and passing something with a tiny fraction of the
> membership.

Hmmm. I suppose any system can be gamed, one way or the other. Trying to find the middle ground.


>> Notification:
>> Notification of a proposal must be given to all members (of the
>> Organization) immediately following introduction of the proposal. This
>> notification must be easily verifiable and recorded in an online database
>> that is readily accessible to all.
> 
> This one's tough.  Would simply sending an email be sufficient?  I'm
> inclined to use time in my system.  Maybe have all votes close on the
> 1st of the month.  Send an email to remind people, but if that email
> gets spam filtered or whatever you still have a binding vote because
> they should *know* to log in and check if something is running (I'm
> thinking Sunday evening closing, but then I'm anticipating a much
> busier government system).

A quorum isn’t possible until after one week following notification. (unless the entire membership agrees.)
And most notifications will be automatic using Loomio.

>> Proxy:
>> If a member is unable to participate in the discussion and voting of any
>> given proposal, or will be out of reach for any (reasonable) period of time,
>> and would like to be represented during their absence, they may select any
>> other member to be their proxy. The member selecting a proxy must notify the
>> Organization of their choice for proxy, as well as the proposal, or period
>> of time, for which they will be absent.
> 
> Good, although Loomio doesn't support this.  

Not yet. One of Loomio’s best features (IMO) is their openness to adding features requested by their users.
They are about to add *modifiable* buttons.  I’m thinking one could possibly be used to track the *quorum* and maybe *proxies* as well.

>> Discussion:
>> Discussion of a proposal may begin immediately following notification. Once
>> a quorum has been established, the discussion will remain open until there
>> are no unresolved objections.  If and when this occurs, any member of the
>> quorum may call for a vote of consensus.
> 
> You know my position on this one: If your (or really anyone's) purpose
> is to preserve the target property as undeveloped open space, this is
> the way to achieve it.

Grin.  yes.  such pessimism Scott! 
Though to some extent I agree. So much depends on the nature of the group (their coherence)...and their willingness to collaborate (or get beyond the individual ego).
I’ve experienced this and it’s quite empowering. I suspect it’s a phenomenon we are evolving into as a culture…maybe as a species. I’m holding the optimistic realm.  ;)

>> Exigency Voting:
>> If a majority of an established quorum decides the proposal is both urgent
>> and crucial, they may pass a proposal with an exigency vote.

> This I predict would be the default operating mode.  

Maybe so. Again, looking for the middle ground. Perhaps there needs to be some distinctive definition of “urgent and crucial”.

> Again, if your goal is to preserve that land as open space, allowing
> blocks is a great way to achieve it.

My thinking is that allowing “blocks”...along side the ability of the membership to expel someone who uses them irresponsibly should limit their abuse.

> The only thing I see missing is some mention of the "executive
> branch".  As you may know from my book, I consider how Managers are
> chosen and an enumeration of their powers to be a key feature of how
> successful organizations actually operate.  They have to be able to
> make and implement the small decisions themselves, but there there has
> to be built-in authority for the group to override and even replace
> them if the need arises.

Right, our Food Coop has a store manager who answers to the BOD. 
The hybrid seems to be working pretty well.

> Do keep us posted as to the progress of this proposal, if only to
