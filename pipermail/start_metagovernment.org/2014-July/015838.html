On Wed, Jul 16, 2014 at 11:54 AM, max stalnaker <max.stalnaker at gmail.com> wrote:
> Scott it seems to me that you started out saying consensus was impossible.
> Then we had my and Steve personal experience examples.  And my easy to
> access quaker history example.  Now it becomes for you that consensus is
> impossible except in homogenous groups.

Ever hear the term "outlier"?  In psychology, we wave a magic wand and
exclude them from the analysis (albeit usually with a disclaimer).
The fact that 1% of organizations can function with a consensus
decision rule is irrelevant to the operation of the MG web site, or
indeed 99% of the other organizations out there.

> You also say only the decision matters but not the reason.

Sort of: The reason matters to the voters, but should not be encoded
in the law itself because, as I explained previously, there's no need
to agree on the reason, only the law itself.  Requiring agreement on
the reason only hinders making the decision yet offers no benefit to
the governed afterwards (at least assuming a civil law vs. a common
law legal system).

True, although not as often as conflicting religious groups.  As an

Anyone who knows anything about Buddhism must realize that this is an

because religion to me is not so much an expression of faith as an
