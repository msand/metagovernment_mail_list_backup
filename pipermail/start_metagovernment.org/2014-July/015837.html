Scott it seems to me that you started out saying consensus was impossible.
Then we had my and Steve personal experience examples.  And my easy to
access quaker history example.  Now it becomes for you that consensus is
impossible except in homogenous groups.

You also say only the decision matters but not the reason.

I will specify quakers in new jersey in 1740 were homogeneous.  Further
they were a slave economy.  And they walked away from that.  I do not
exactly sure that that they were religious was the additional defining
factor.  Lots of homogenous religious groups end up killing each other.

I might suggest a critical element was the quakers right of conscience.  I
am not that much of a 18th century scholar but at least I read what was
easily accessible on the net when I got interested.  I doubt you did.
There is a tendency for people to come up with  the first facile
explanation that explains away inconvenient experiences.

Let me very abstract here.

The axiom of choice says that every collection is a set.  If this is
applicable to reality then every possible grouping of people is
homogeneous.

Speculative 100% consensus is inadequate because the reasons for the
electors to be in the consensus are not in synch.

Max
