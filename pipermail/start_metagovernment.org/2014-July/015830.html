OK, that sounds not so much like "gridlock" as it does "anarchy".
Certainly "gridlock" and long-term preservation of the status quo are
part of it, but you're apparently proposing an even less effective
government than that.  I can just see applying this philosophy to the
question of whether you need to leash your dog when walking on a
public street: The petless "hardliners" will insist that no dogs be
allowed on public streets at all and the pitbull owners will just let
their dogs run at large with no supervision whatsoever because that's
just the kind of hairpins they are.  And there's no one to stop them
because it will take 20 or 30 years to make a decision that's binding
on everyone.

>> Again, the purpose of government is to enable collective
>> decisionmaking.  Without decisionmaking, there is no government.
>
> Decisionmaking is meaningless without "Scope".
> What is the scope of the decision? This is what i wanted to question with
> above example...

The "scope" is obviously physical: You can't make a decision about
whether dogs must be leashed on *my* street unless you also live on
(or maybe have to use) that street.  But your "islands" unfortunately
are theoretical whereas streets and taxation districts and human
beings are physical.  Until you can explain how dogs will obey your
theoretical island boundaries, I submit that your proposal is simply
unworkable.
