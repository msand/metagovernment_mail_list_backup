On Jul 6, 2013, at 11:30 AM, Stephen Coffman wrote:

> On Jul 5, 2013, at 8:01 PM, Ed Pastore wrote:
> 
>> It looks like the IRS demands that a nonprofit company have a board of directors. And I would really like to avoid that, because I want the nonprofit company to be a model for other nonprofits to adopt collaborative governance. But we might have to compromise in order to get the application approved, and just push against the boundaries of what the US tax code will permit as opportunity allows. 
> 
> I was for several years the president of a local non-profit. I believe the minimum requirement for our board to meet was once a year. 
> I don't see why an entity couldn't set up a sort of 'puppet board' that just gave a seasonal (even yearly) report in which it essentially 'rubber stamped' the business of the larger organization. 
> Or it could be that the individuals selected by the organization to manage specific functions (like keeping track of the database and the finances) could be the board members in title only...and the meetings would simply consist of their reports on the 'business' of the entity. I don't think this would necessarily be that big of an issue.

What the IRS (and any foundation that may consider providing a grant to the organization) is looking for is accountability. There have to be individuals who can be blamed for mismanagement of funds; individuals who are responsible and accountable for actions made. I am more than willing to take on that accountability, but that means that the board has to be less than just puppets.

I am wondering if the co-op model might be the right basis for a Metagov organization. I am considering paying for some advice from the Foundation Group (an org which serves to help other orgs get started), but I would like to have as much input from this group as possible before I go down that path.

Yes, Mike, what do you think? Creating the bylaws for a Metagovernment organization would be a real-world practice in governance building. It differs from all the self-governance we have done in the past in that there will be money on the line. Some of the decisions made by the Metagov organization (if I can eventually get it formed and then get grants from foundations) will funnel money to other individuals and projects here. That is sure to cause competition and dissent... the meat and potatoes of real-world governance.
