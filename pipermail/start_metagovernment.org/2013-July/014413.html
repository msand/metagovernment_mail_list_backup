And then, once it's up and running, that *criteria* could be dropped or extended to the next scale. This is a little tricky because we don't want to discriminate...and we want to contain it so that we don't have a multitude of proposers/users/on-lookers overwhelming the system.

>> Any AFC member would be free to make pledges of support (financial and/or in

Here again, start small for the beta. Then gradually scale up. And yes, "dead proposals could be (re)moved"....perhaps at the end of each cycle those proposals without any active development taking place (need descriptors here) would be tossed.

>> Membership consensus would be attained if there are no *reasonable

> A week is a bit short, imagine you are on vacation. I would make it a month

A month seems a bit long to me. Most people stay *connected* wherever they are/go. And if they can't follow along for some reason they could possibly set up a proxy. I'm not attached to one week, but I'm concerned that if a proposal is in the wings for a month, (waiting for someone to get back from vacation), it might lose it's momentum.

>> Determining which objections are *unreasonable* would be the responsibility
>> of an elected body of the membership.....(perhaps the appraisers).
> There are legal restrictions and the coop in its role as administrator and 
> host of the platform can put constraints ofc. These should be made clear 
> indeed, e.g. binding to follow US legislation. 
> I would just say that the host of the platform can stop any proposal and 
> implementation, which is obvious, but as the software and data is open-source, 
> people can implement the proposal on their own platform. The point is imo that 
> the consensus-building process itself should filter problematic issues and the 
> technology should transcend lockins. It is enough for any user to publicly 
> vote against a proposal to veto it. 
> Here one can demand that they have to be a citizen of Ashland to start with, 
> for instance. But any such rule is already discriminating, as all issues can 
> be implicitly global.

I think you misunderstood my position. I'm referring to the ability of the Network to determine whether an *objection* (that Is holding up consensus on a proposal) is *reasonable*...or not. 

Let's say:
Mary proposes to have a separate homeless shelter for women and children because of some past safety issues resulting from combining men and women in that kind of situation.

Everyone agrees except John, who (deep inside) feels threatened and abandoned by women and doesn't want to give them any special treatment. He argues that having a *combined* shelter would instead give men and women an opportunity to *work things out*...together. He feels very strongly about this and isn't open to any gender separation...and therefore blocks consensus.

I realize this is a bit imaginative...but, in the type of process we are developing, there are likely to be situations where one individual is blocking consensus because of some personal wounding (or vested interest), and the community is held hostage because of someone's individual process or selfish interest. 

The question for me is: who decides whether an objection is *reasonable* or not? 

In my mind there are risks going both ways. On the one hand, having a *panel of elected arbiters/judges* risks introducing an element of elitism. On the other hand, to introduce a *subjective* constraint, i.e. "a proposal passes if there are no *reasonable* objections"...without some way of determining which objections are *reasonable*, runs the risk of sabotaging the process....with no way of preventing endless argument about which objection is reasonable and which is not. A determination will need to be made somewhere...somehow.  Or we just drop *reasonable* and open it up to 'objection' on any grounds whatsoever.

Ok...I don't understand the tech side of things very well. The main thing is to have all relevant info easily available to everyone.

>> Any element of this process/structure could be changed at any time by a 3/4
>> majority vote of active users of the Network.
> Why suddenly 3/4? This would allow discrimination through the backdoor, 
> although one can do so if the risk is clearly specified as is with the 
> emergency BOD rule.

My thinking here is to make the *self-organinzing* element more fluid by lowering the threshold necessary for evolving the process. Some people simply resist change on grounds that aren't very reasonable (in my mind). Again, there are risks going both ways. I'm suggesting using 3/4 majority because I can imagine some people disagreeing to any change just because they're comfortable with the status quo. My fear is that the process would stagnate and not evolve if consensus was requisite for any process change. I guess I feel there are times when the majority holds enough wisdom to be trusted. And, at least initially, there will be a lot of bugs to work out. Requiring a consensus for process changes could really slow things down, IMO.  And finally, I can easily imagine a 3/4 majority agreeing to making consensus requisite for a process change...if things were moving/changing to quickly or unwisely.
