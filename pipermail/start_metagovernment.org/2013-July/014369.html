Hi Ned,

I answer both questions here, because they are related.

> I understand that a population could choose to have these two
> separate systems, related as you suggest. Do you understand that a
> population could choose *not* to have these two separate systems,
> but rather to have only an authoritative decision system, without a
> primary guidance system?

Yes, if the decisions are reached by sortition.  Sortition decisions
cannot usefully be guided or otherwise influenced.  Guidance is only
useful when the decisions are a matter of choice (voting).  Then the
deciders will want to know what choice to make.  The source of their
knowledge is what I call "primary guidance".

> Would it be possible for you to briefly list the reasons that you
> think the two-system arrangement is better than a single-system
> arrangement?

Consider the case in which primary guidance (knowing what choice to
make) is based on reason.  By contrast, recall that decisions are
based on force (the essence of their decisiveness).  Now the problem
arises that reason and force are incompatible; the two must be kept
apart.  So we have separate systems each dedicated to its own
function, then bring them into proper relation with each other:

  (reason and knowledge) --> (force and decision)

These two cannot be combined without destroying one, or the other.
Anyway, this is my own understanding.

> I am having trouble understanding the desirability of discussions
> that go on and on.  ...  The "product" of a decision system is
> decisions made. The product of a "guidance system" is decisions
> guided. One primary aim of such systems (I would have thought) is to
> facilitate coming to agreement, as quickly and efficiently as
> possible. ...  Mike, do you think that quickness and efficiency
> don't matter?

I think it matters for both system types, but to different ends.  A
decision system should be efficient in regard to forming decisions,
while a guidance system should be efficient in regard to forming
knowledge (or what not).  The crucial thing to avoid is systematically
interlinking the two, e.g. by forcing the guidance processes to dance
to the decision-making drum.
