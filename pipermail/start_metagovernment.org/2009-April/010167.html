You make very good points, Gustavo, and I don't think we're leaning  
toward user scoring in the immediate future. But to be clear, your  
example is the same one I used to prove the opposite point...

If user scores are community-specific, then Britney Spears would have  
nearly god-like status in the community of her own fan-club, but she  
would have marginally more than zero status in the community of  
nuclear proliferation debate. A highly respected nuclear scientist  
would face the opposite situation.


On Apr 20, 2009, at 1:01 PM, Gustavo Menezes wrote:

> As I said before, I simply do not like the idea of subjective score  
> systems, particularly those socially-oriented. There are political  
> issues there which promote some of the bad habits of representative  
> democracy systems.
>
> You do not create a sense of community, but really do the opposite:  
> stimulate personal or small group gains. For instance, in this  
> scenario we could have the same political parties and lobbists we  
> observe today, still conducting the same practices for personal or  
> small group gains, because these parties could inflate their scores  
> just by voting in one another.
>
> With subjective score systems marketing would have a disproportional  
> importance on the political arena, as the way people see each other  
> would mean way more than the way people really are (or vote). This  
> is particularly important in big communities where you don't get to  
> see someone face-to-face, but through a very distorted media  
> channel. For instance, Britney Spears, with all its media hype (at  
> least in her glory days...), would have a vote on nuclear  
> proliferation control which would count more than some pre-nobel  
> nuclear scientist entrenched in his lab. I can even see the media  
> seizing control of the community, as they usually control most of  
> the general public opinion
>
> There are hundreds of bad reasons for not riding the road of  
> subjective scoring. I would really want to know the opinion of  
> others on this matter.
>
> Gustavo Menezes

> As for the value of points, I think they would have to be community- 
> defined. That is, each community sets its own point-value system  
> (and perhaps also determines a base score or an accumulation value  
> for new members). That way, communities can define exchange rates  
> between each other. The book only describes Whuffie as a seemingly  
> universal standard; but I think it would have to have a community- 
> specific element.
>
> After all, you wouldn't necessarily want someone with a  
> fantastically-high score derived from the Britney Spears fan club  
> community to therefore have a great deal of power in the nuclear  
> proliferation control community.

> On Mar 17, 2009, at 2:16 PM, Manuel Barkhau wrote:
>
> As for what initial score to hand out... I think it would be more  
> likely to
> start at zero and to gradually accumulate based on participation.
>
> Yes but where does it come from? There has to be a source from which
> it can be accumulated. If you allow each user to generate the currency
> through scoring, you have the onset of inflation only bounded by the
> largest number you can represent with a computer(unbounded for all
> practical purposes). If you hand out a fixed amount per person you
> have a situation where you must verify their identity, to prevent a
> group from creating multiple accounts and thereby gaining
> proportionally more of the currency.
>
> A possible solution is paying for services to the community with the
> currency. The community needs somebody to do some research on a topic,
> make a proposal summarize a debate (whatever may be of value to them).
> Collectively they agree to pay a certain amount. Once the system is
> running you can adjust inflation with intrest/deprication rates based
> on the total amount in circulation.
>
> Since fiat currencies have a bad history though, it would be good to
> be able to guarantee something for peoples money, so that it's a bit
> more tangible. Otherwise you have to ask what is the currency worth? I
> guess I should read on before I continue.
